<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Interplanetary contamination</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Biological contamination of a planetary body by a space probe or spacecraft</div>
<p><b>Interplanetary contamination</b> refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_contamination" title="Biological contamination">biological contamination</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_body" title="Planetary body">planetary body</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, either deliberate or unintentional.
</p><p>There are two types of interplanetary contamination:
</p>
<ul><li><i><b>Forward contamination</b></i> is the transfer of life and other forms of contamination from Earth to another celestial body.</li>
<li><i><b>Back contamination</b></i> is the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial</a> organisms and other forms of contamination into <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. It also covers infection of humans and human habitats in space and on other celestial bodies by extraterrestrial organisms, if such habitats exist.</li></ul>
<p>The main focus is on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial_life" title="Microbial life">microbial life</a> and on potentially <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>. Non-biological forms of contamination have also been considered, including contamination of sensitive deposits (such as lunar polar ice deposits) of scientific interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the case of back contamination, multicellular life is thought unlikely but has not been ruled out. In the case of forward contamination, contamination by multicellular life (e.g. lichens) is unlikely to occur for robotic missions, but it becomes a consideration in <a href="/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars" title="Human mission to Mars">crewed missions to Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHYS-20180227_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHYS-20180227-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Current space missions are governed by the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COSPAR" title="COSPAR">COSPAR</a> guidelines for <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">planetary protection</a>. Forward contamination is prevented primarily by sterilizing the spacecraft. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Sample-return_mission" title="Sample-return mission">sample-return missions</a> (back contamination) the aim of the mission is to return extraterrestrial samples to Earth, and sterilization of the samples would make them of much less interest. So, back contamination would be prevented mainly by containment, and breaking the chain of contact between the planet of origin and Earth. It would also require <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantine</a> procedures for the materials and for anyone who comes into contact with them.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> appears hostile to life as we know it. No extraterrestrial life has ever been discovered, but there are several locations outside Earth where microbial life could possibly exist, have existed, or thrive if introduced. If extraterrestrial life exists, it may be vulnerable to interplanetary contamination by foreign microorganisms. Some <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">extremophiles</a> may be able to survive space travel to another planet, and foreign life could possibly be introduced by spacecraft from Earth and transform the location from its current pristine state. This poses scientific and ethical concerns.
</p><p>Locations within the Solar System where life might exist today include the oceans of liquid water beneath the icy surface of <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a>,
and <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> (its surface has oceans of liquid <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> / <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, but it may also have liquid water below the surface and <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">ice volcanoes</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smallsolarsystembodies_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-smallsolarsystembodies-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are multiple consequences for both forward- and back-contamination. If a planet becomes contaminated with Earth life, it might then be difficult to tell whether any lifeforms discovered originated there or came from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, the organic chemicals produced by the introduced life would confuse sensitive searches for <a href="/wiki/Biosignature" title="Biosignature">biosignatures</a> of living or ancient native life. The same applies to other more complex biosignatures. Life on other planets could have a common origin with Earth life, since in the early Solar System there was much exchange of material between the planets which could have transferred life as well. If so, it might be based on <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> too (<a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> or <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>). 
</p><p>The majority of the species isolated are not well understood or characterized and cannot be cultured in labs, and are known only from DNA fragments obtained with swabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On a contaminated planet, it might be difficult to distinguish the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of extraterrestrial life from the DNA of life brought to the planet by the exploring. Most species of microorganism on Earth are not yet well understood or DNA sequenced. This particularly applies to the unculturable <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, and so are difficult to study. This can be either because they depend on the presence of other microorganisms, or are slow growing, or depend on other conditions not yet understood. In <a href="/wiki/Agar_plate" title="Agar plate">typical habitats</a>, 99% of microorganisms are not <a href="/wiki/Microbiological_culture" title="Microbiological culture">culturable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Introduced Earth life could contaminate resources of value for future human missions, such as water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-groundwatercontamination_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-groundwatercontamination-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">Invasive species</a> could outcompete native life or consume it, if there is life on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martianrabbit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-martianrabbit-9">[9]</a></sup> One argument against this is that the native life would be more adapted to the conditions there. However, the experience on Earth shows that species moved from one continent to another may be able to outcompete the native life adapted to that continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-martianrabbit_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-martianrabbit-9">[9]</a></sup> Additionally, evolutionary processes on Earth might have developed biological pathways different from extraterrestrial organisms, and so may be able to out-compete it. The same is also possible the other way around for back contamination introduced to Earth's <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>.
</p><p>In addition of science concerns, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical</a> or moral issues have also been raised on accidental and intentional interplanetary transport of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RestorationEcology_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-RestorationEcology-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_for_possible_habitats_outside_Earth">Evidence for possible habitats outside Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evidence for possible habitats outside Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> show the best evidence for current habitats, mainly due to the possibility of their hosting liquid water and organic compounds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars" title="Life on Mars">Life on Mars</a></div>
<p>There is ample evidence to suggest that Mars once offered habitable conditions for microbial life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20131209_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131209-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20131209_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20131209-15">[15]</a></sup> It is therefore possible that microbial life may have existed on Mars, although no evidence has been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biosignatures_2011_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biosignatures_2011-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parnell_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parnell-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is thought that many bacterial spores (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endospores" title="Endospores">endospores</a>) from Earth were transported on Mars spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Debus_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Debus-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaty_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beaty-24">[24]</a></sup> Some may be protected within Martian rovers and landers on the shallow surface of the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In that sense, Mars may have already been interplanetarily contaminated. 
</p><p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a> from the arctic <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> are able to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthesize" title="Photosynthesize">photosynthesize</a> and grow in the absence of any liquid water, simply by using the humidity from the atmosphere. They are also highly tolerant of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_radiation" title="UV radiation">UV radiation</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Melanin" title="Melanin">melanin</a> and other more specialized chemicals to protect their cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skymania-20120426_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skymania-20120426-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EGU-20120426_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-EGU-20120426-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although numerous studies point to resistance to some of Mars conditions, they do so separately, and none has considered the full range of Martian surface conditions, including temperature, pressure, atmospheric composition, radiation, humidity, oxidizing regolith, and others, all at the same time and in combination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Laboratory simulations show that whenever multiple lethal factors are combined, the survival rates plummet quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dust-up_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-dust-up-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other studies have suggested the potential for life to survive using <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy#Deliquescence" title="Hygroscopy">deliquescing salts</a>. These, similarly to the lichens, use the humidity of the atmosphere. If the mixture of salts is right, the organisms may obtain liquid water at times of high atmospheric humidity, with salts capture enough to be capable of supporting life.
</p><p>Research published in July 2017 shows that when irradiated with a simulated Martian UV flux, <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorates</a> become even more lethal to bacteria (<a href="/wiki/Bactericide" title="Bactericide">bactericide</a> effect). Even dormant spores lost viability within minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bacteriocidal_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-bacteriocidal-31">[31]</a></sup> In addition, two other compounds of the Martian surface, <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, act in synergy with irradiated perchlorates to cause a 10.8-fold increase in cell death when compared to cells exposed to UV radiation after 60 seconds of exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bacteriocidal_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-bacteriocidal-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxides_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxides-32">[32]</a></sup>  It was also found that abraded silicates (quartz and basalt) lead to the formation of toxic <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The researchers concluded that "the surface of Mars is lethal to vegetative cells and renders much of the surface and near-surface regions uninhabitable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This research demonstrates that the present-day surface is more uninhabitable than previously thought,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bacteriocidal_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-bacteriocidal-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall2017_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wall2017-35">[35]</a></sup> and reinforces the notion to inspect at least a few meters into the ground to ensure the levels of radiation would be relatively low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall2017_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wall2017-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enceladus">Enceladus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enceladus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens"><i>Cassini</i> spacecraft</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens#Enceladus_flybys" title="Cassini-Huygens">directly sampled</a> the plumes escaping from Enceladus. Measured data indicates that these geysers are made primarily of salt rich particles with an 'ocean-like' composition, which is thought to originate from a <a href="/wiki/Ocean#Extraterrestrial_oceans" title="Ocean">subsurface ocean</a> of liquid saltwater, rather than from the moon's icy surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Data from the geyser flythroughs also indicate the presence of organic chemicals in the plumes. Heat scans of Enceladus' surface also indicate warmer temperatures around the fissures where the geysers originate from, with temperatures reaching -93 °C (-135 °F), which is 115 °C (207 °F) warmer than the surrounding surface regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europa">Europa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Europa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europa has much indirect evidence for its sub-surface ocean. Models of how Europa is affected by <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">tidal heating</a> require a subsurface layer of liquid water in order to accurately reproduce the linear fracturing of the surface. Indeed, observations by the <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)"><i>Galileo</i> spacecraft</a> of how Europa's magnetic field interacts with Jupiter's field strengthens the case for a liquid, rather than solid, layer; an <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">electrically conductive fluid</a> deep within Europa would explain these results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Observations from the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> in December 2012 appear to show an ice plume spouting from Europa's surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> which would immensely strengthen the case for a liquid subsurface ocean. As was the case for Enceladus, vapour geysers would allow for easy sampling of the liquid layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Unfortunately, there appears to be little evidence that geysering is a frequent event on Europa due to the lack of water in the space near Europa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_protection">Planetary protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Planetary protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">Planetary protection</a></div>
<p>Forward contamination is prevented by <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)#Spacecraft" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilizing space probes</a> sent to sensitive areas of the Solar System. Missions are classified depending on whether their destinations are of interest for the search for life, and whether there is any chance that Earth life could reproduce there.
</p><p>NASA made these policies official with the issuing of Management Manual NMI-4-4-1, <i>NASA Unmanned Spacecraft Decontamination Policy</i> on September 9, 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  Prior to NMI-4-4-1 the same sterilization requirements were required on all outgoing spacecraft regardless of their target.  Difficulties in the sterilization of Ranger probes sent to the Moon are the primary reasons for NASA's change to a target-by-target basis in assessing the likelihood forward contamination.
</p><p>Some destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> need no precautions at all. Others such as the Moon require documentation but nothing more, while destinations such as Mars require sterilization of the rovers sent there. For the details, see <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">Planetary protection</a>.
</p><p>Back contamination would be prevented by containment or quarantine. However, there have been no sample-returns thought to have any possibility of a back contamination risk since the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo missions</a>. The Apollo regulations have been rescinded and new regulations have yet to be developed, see <a href="#Precautions_suggested_for_sample-return">Precautions suggested for sample-return</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crewed_spacecraft">Crewed spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crewed spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">Crewed spacecraft</a> are of particular concern for interplanetary contamination because of the impossibility to sterilize a human to the same level as a robotic spacecraft. Therefore, the chance of forward contamination is higher than for a robotic mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biospherescollide_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-biospherescollide-44">[44]</a></sup> Humans are typically <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> to a hundred trillion microorganisms in ten thousand species in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_microbiome" title="Human microbiome">human microbiome</a> which cannot be removed while preserving the life of the human. Containment seems the only option, but effective containment to the same standard as a robotic rover appears difficult to achieve with present-day technology. In particular, adequate containment in the event of a hard landing is a major challenge.
</p><p>Human explorers may be potential carriers back to Earth of microorganisms acquired on Mars, if such microorganisms exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Another issue is the contamination of the water supply by Earth microorganisms shed by humans in their stools, skin and breath, which could have a direct effect on the long-term human colonization of Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-groundwatercontamination_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-groundwatercontamination-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon_as_a_testbed">The Moon as a testbed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Moon as a testbed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> has been suggested as a testbed for new technology to protect sites in the Solar System, and astronauts, from forward and back contamination. Currently, the Moon has no contamination restrictions because it is considered to be "not of interest" for prebiotic chemistry and <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">origins of life</a>.  Analysis of the contamination left by the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> astronauts could also yield useful ground truth for planetary protection models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-contaminating_exploration_methods">Non-contaminating exploration methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-contaminating exploration methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="318" data-file-width="436" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg/220px-Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg/330px-Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Teleroboitcs_image2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Telerobotics exploration on Mars and Earth</div></div></div>
<p>One of the most reliable ways to reduce the risk of forward and back contamination during visits to extraterrestrial bodies is to use only <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">Robotic spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biospherescollide_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-biospherescollide-44">[44]</a></sup> Humans in close orbit around the target planet could control equipment on the surface in real time via telepresence, so bringing many of the benefits of a surface mission, without its associated increased forward and back contamination risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Back_contamination_issues">Back contamination issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Back contamination issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the Moon is now generally considered to be free from life, the most likely source of contamination would be from Mars during either a <a href="/wiki/Mars_sample-return_mission" title="Mars sample-return mission">Mars sample-return mission</a> or as a result of a <a href="/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars" title="Human mission to Mars">crewed mission to Mars</a>. The possibility of new human pathogens, or environmental disruption due to back contamination, is considered to be of extremely low probability but cannot yet be ruled out.
</p><p>There are no immediate plans for a Mars sample-return, but it remains a high priority for NASA and the ESA because of its great potential biological and geological interest. The European Space Foundation report cites many advantages of a Mars sample-return. In particular, it would permit extensive analyses on Earth, without the size and weight constraints for instruments sent to Mars on rovers. These analyses could also be carried out without the communication delays for experiments carried out by Martian rovers. It would also make it possible to repeat experiments in multiple laboratories with different instruments to confirm key results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esf2012_PP-advanntages_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-esf2012_PP-advanntages-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> was first to publicise back contamination issues that might follow from a Mars sample-return. In <i>Cosmic Connection</i> (1973) he wrote:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Precisely because Mars is an environment of great potential biological interest, it is possible that on Mars there are pathogens, organisms which, if transported to the terrestrial environment, might do enormous biological damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmicconnection_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-cosmicconnection-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Later in <i>Cosmos</i> (1980) Carl Sagan wrote:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Perhaps Martian samples can be safely returned to Earth. But I would want to be very sure before considering a returned-sample mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>NASA and ESA views are similar. The findings were that with present-day technology, Martian samples can be safely returned to Earth provided the right precautions are taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_precautions_for_sample-returns">Suggested precautions for sample-returns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Suggested precautions for sample-returns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NASA has already had experience with returning samples thought to represent a low back contamination risk when samples were returned for the first time by <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>. At the time, it was thought that there was a low probability of life on the Moon, so the requirements were not very stringent. The precautions taken then were inadequate by current standards, however. The regulations used then have been rescinded, and new regulations and approaches for a sample-return would be needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-race_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-race-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chain_of_contact">Chain of contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chain of contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A sample-return mission would be designed to break the chain of contact between Mars and the exterior of the sample container, for instance, by sealing the returned container inside another larger container in the vacuum of space before it returns to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mepag_ppt_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-mepag_ppt-57">[57]</a></sup> In order to eliminate the risk of parachute failure, the capsule could fall at terminal velocity and the impact would be cushioned by the capsule's thermal protection system. The sample container would be designed to withstand the force of the impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mepag_ppt_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-mepag_ppt-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Receiving_facility">Receiving facility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Receiving facility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg/220px-NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg/330px-NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg/440px-NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility_-_Positive_Pressure_Personnel_Suit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Working inside a BSL-4 laboratory with air hoses providing positive air pressure to their suits</div></div></div>
<p>To receive, analyze and curate extraterrestrial soil samples, NASA has proposed to build a biohazard containment facility, tentatively known as the Mars Sample Return Receiving Facility (MSRRF).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samplereceivingfacility_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-samplereceivingfacility-58">[58]</a></sup> This future facility must be rated <a href="/wiki/Biosafety_level" title="Biosafety level">biohazard level</a> 4 (<b>BSL-4</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samplereceivingfacility_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-samplereceivingfacility-58">[58]</a></sup> While existing BSL-4 facilities deal primarily with fairly well-known organisms, a BSL-4 facility focused on extraterrestrial samples must pre-plan the systems carefully while being mindful that there will be unforeseen issues during sample evaluation and curation that will require independent thinking and solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bass_2020_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bass_2020-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The facility's systems must be able to contain unknown biohazards, as the sizes of any putative Martian microorganisms are unknown. In consideration of this, additional requirements were proposed. Ideally it should filter particles of 0.01 µm or larger, and release of a particle 0.05 µm or larger is unacceptable under any circumstance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reason for this extremely small size limit of 0.01 µm is for consideration of <a href="/wiki/Gene_transfer_agent" title="Gene transfer agent">gene transfer agents</a> (GTAs) which are virus-like particles that are produced by some microorganisms that package random segments of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> capable of <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer" title="Horizontal gene transfer">horizontal gene transfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup> These randomly incorporate segments of the host genome and can transfer them to other evolutionarily distant hosts, and do that without killing the new host. In this way many archaea and bacteria can swap DNA with each other. This raises the possibility that Martian life, if it has a common origin with Earth life in the distant past, could swap DNA with Earth microorganisms in the same way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup> In one experiment reported in 2010, researchers left GTAs (DNA conferring antibiotic resistance) and marine bacteria overnight in natural conditions and found that by the next day up to 47% of the bacteria had incorporated the genetic material from the GTAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naturegta_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-naturegta-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Another reason for the 0.05 µm limit is because of the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Ultramicrobacteria" title="Ultramicrobacteria">ultramicrobacteria</a> as small as 0.2 µm across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-4"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The BSL-4 containment facility must also double as a <a href="/wiki/Cleanroom" title="Cleanroom">cleanroom</a> to preserve the science value of the samples. A challenge is that, while it is relatively easy to simply contain the samples once returned to Earth, researchers would also want to remove parts of the sample and perform analyses. During all these handling procedures, the samples would need to be protected from Earthly contamination. A <a href="/wiki/Cleanroom" title="Cleanroom">cleanroom</a> is normally kept at a higher pressure than the external environment to keep contaminants out, while a biohazard laboratory is kept at a lower pressure to keep the biohazards in. This would require to compartmentalize the specialized rooms in order to combine these in a single building. Solutions suggested include a triple walled containment facility, and one of the suggestions include extensive robotic handling of the samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSRRF-clean-room-quote_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSRRF-clean-room-quote-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>The facility would be expected to take 7 to 10 years from design to completion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessment_2009_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment_2009-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSRtaskgroup_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSRtaskgroup-67">[67]</a></sup> and an additional two years is recommended for the staff to become accustomed to the facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessment_2009_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Assessment_2009-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTA_2013_56-5"><a href="#cite_note-GTA_2013-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissenting_views_on_back_contamination">Dissenting views on back contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dissenting views on back contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Robert Zubrin, from the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Society" title="Mars Society">Mars Society</a>, maintains that the risk of back contamination is negligible. He supports this using an argument based on the possibility of transfer of life from Earth to Mars on meteorites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubrin_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-zubrin-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubrin-interview_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-zubrin-interview-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_process_of_approval_for_Mars_sample-return">Legal process of approval for Mars sample-return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legal process of approval for Mars sample-return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Margaret Race has examined in detail the legal process of approval for a MSR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-race_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-race-55">[55]</a></sup> She found that under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) (which did not exist in the Apollo era) a formal environment impact statement is likely to be required, and public hearings during which all the issues would be aired openly. This process is likely to take up to several years to complete.
</p><p>During this process, she found, the full range of worst accident scenarios, impact, and project alternatives would be played out in the public arena. Other agencies such as the Environment Protection Agency, Occupational Health and Safety Administration, etc., may also get involved in the decision making process.
</p><p>The laws on quarantine will also need to be clarified as the regulations for the Apollo program were rescinded. In the Apollo era, NASA delayed announcement of its quarantine regulations until the day Apollo was launched, so bypassing the requirement for public debate - something that would be unlikely to be tolerated today.
</p><p>It is also probable that the presidential directive NSC-25 will apply which requires a review of large scale alleged effects on the environment and is carried out subsequent to the other domestic reviews and through a long process, leads eventually to presidential approval of the launch.
</p><p>Then apart from those domestic legal hurdles, there are numerous international regulations and treaties to be negotiated in the case of a Mars sample-return, especially those relating to environmental protection and health. She concluded that the public of necessity has a significant role to play in the development of the policies governing Mars sample-return.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_sample-returns">Alternatives to sample-returns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alternatives to sample-returns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several exobiologists have suggested that a Mars sample-return is not necessary at this stage, and that it is better to focus more on in situ studies on the surface first. Although it is not their main motivation, this approach of course also eliminates back contamination risks.
</p><p>Some of these exobiologists advocate more in situ studies followed by a sample-return in the near future. Others go as far as to advocate in situ study instead of a sample-return at the present state of understanding of Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JeffreyBada_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-JeffreyBada-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paige_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-paige-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Their reasoning is that life on Mars is likely to be hard to find. Any present day life is likely to be sparse and occur in only a few niche habitats. Past life is likely to be degraded by cosmic radiation over geological time periods if exposed in the top few meters of the Mars surface. Also, only certain special deposits of salts or clays on Mars would have the capability to preserve organics for billions of years. So, they argue, there is a high risk that a Mars sample-return at our current stage of understanding would return samples that are no more conclusive about the origins of life on Mars or present day life than the Martian meteorite samples we already have.
</p><p>Another consideration is the difficulty of keeping the sample completely free from Earth life contamination during the return journey and during handling procedures on Earth. This might make it hard to show conclusively that any biosignatures detected does not result from contamination of the samples.
</p><p>Instead they advocate sending more sensitive instruments on Mars surface rovers. These could examine many different rocks and soil types, and search for biosignatures on the surface and so examine a wide range of materials which could not all be returned to Earth with current technology at reasonable cost.
</p><p>A sample-return to Earth would then be considered at a later stage, once we have a reasonably thorough understanding of conditions on Mars, and possibly have already detected life there, either current or past life, through biosignatures and other <i>in situ</i> analyses.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments_under_development_for_in_situ_analyses">Instruments under development for <i>in situ</i> analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Instruments under development for in situ analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>NASA Marshall Space Flight Center is leading a research effort to develop a Miniaturized Variable Pressure Scanning Electron Microscope (MVP-SEM) for future lunar and Martian missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Several teams, including Jonathan Rothberg, and J. Craig Venter, are separately developing solutions for sequencing alien DNA directly on the Martian surface itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li>Levin is working on updated versions of the Labeled release instrument flown on Viking. For instance versions that rely on detecting chirality. This is of special interest because it can enable detection of life even if it is not based on standard life chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li>The Urey Mars Organic and Oxidant Detector instrument for detection of biosignatures has been descoped, but was due to be flown on <a href="/wiki/ExoMars" title="ExoMars">ExoMars</a> in 2018. It is designed with much higher levels of sensitivity for biosignatures than any previous instruments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JeffreyBada_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-JeffreyBada-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Study_and_analyses_from_orbit">Study and analyses from orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Study and analyses from orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the “Exploration	Telerobotics	Symposium" in 2012 experts on telerobotics from industry, NASA and academics met to discuss telerobotics, and its applications to space exploration. Amongst other issues, particular attention was given to Mars missions and a Mars sample-return.
</p><p>They came to the conclusion that telerobotic approaches could permit direct study of the samples on the Mars surface via telepresence from Mars orbit, permitting rapid exploration and use of human cognition to take advantage of chance discoveries and feedback from the results obtained so far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi.usra.edu_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-lpi.usra.edu-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>They found that telepresence exploration of Mars has many advantages. The astronauts have near real-time control of the robots, and can respond immediately to discoveries. It also prevents contamination both ways and has mobility benefits as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>Return of the sample to orbit has the advantage that it permits analysis of the sample without delay, to detect volatiles that may be lost during a voyage home. This was the conclusion of a meeting of researchers at the NASA Goddard Space Flight Center in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpi.usra.edu_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-lpi.usra.edu-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Similar methods could be used to directly explore other biologically sensitive moons such as <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a>, once the human presence in the vicinity becomes possible.
</p>

<li id="cite_note-biospherescollide-44"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-biospherescollide_44-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-biospherescollide_44-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"> <a class="external text" href="http://www.nasa.gov/connect/ebooks/when_biospheres_collide_detail.html#.U_uVh_mwJcQ" rel="nofollow">When Biospheres Collide - a history of NASA'S Planetary Protection Programs</a>, Michael Meltzer, May 31, 2012. See Chapter 7, Return to Mars. Quote: "One of the most reliable ways to reduce the risk of forward contamination during visits to extraterrestrial bodies is to make those visits only with robotic spacecraft. Sending a person to Mars would be, for some observers, more exciting. But in the view of much of the space science community, robotic missions are the way to accomplish the maximum amount of scientific inquiry since valuable fuel and shipboard power do not have to be expended in transporting and operating the equipment to keep a human crew alive and healthy. And very important to planetary protection goals, robotic craft can be thoroughly sterilized, while humans cannot. Such a difference can be critical in protecting sensitive targets, such as the special regions of Mars, from forward contamination." "Perhaps a change in the public's perspective as to just what today's robotic missions really are would be helpful in deciding what types of missions are important to implement. In the opinion of Terence Johnson, who has played a major role in many of NASA's robotic missions, including serving as the project scientist for the Galileo mission and the planned Europa Orbiter mission, the term "robotic exploration" misses the point. NASA is actually conducting human exploration on these projects.  The mission crews that sit in the control panel at JPL, "as well as everyone else who can log on to the Internet" can observe in near real-time what is going on. The spacecraft instruments, in other words, are becoming more like collective sense organs for humankind. Thus, according to Johnson, when NASA conducts its so-called robotic missions, people all around the world are really "all standing on the bridge of Starship Enterprise". The question must thus be asked, when, if ever, is it necessary for the good of humankind to send people rather than increasingly sophisticated robots to explore other worlds." </span>

A Draft Test Protocol for Detecting Possible Biohazards in Martian Samples Returned to Earth was published in October 2002 [7]. In 2003, three architectural design teams independently examined the scope, approach, cost, and technology required for the SRF, using the Draft Test Protocol for requirements. The approaches varied from allrobotic handling of samples to more traditional glove box implementations. The studies indicated that the principles and techniques required are generally mature. Biosafety laboratories, the NASA Lunar Sample Facility, pharmaceutical laboratories, and electronic fabrication cleanrooms perform most of the required individual functions. However, there are some areas needing early development, such as ensuring sample preservation and bio-safety together, representing new challenges that were addressed by techniques like dual-walled containers (and gloves) with positive pressure clean inert gas in between the walls. This, as well as some further development in ultra-clean sample manipulation, safe and pure transport of samples, and sample sterilization techniques, are planned in the technology program.</q></cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=2010+Mars+Sample+Return+Orbiter+decadal+survey&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.nap.edu%2Freports%2F13117%2FApp%2520G%252008_Mars-Sample-Return-Orbiter.pdf&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AInterplanetary+contamination"></span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r879151008" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></span>
