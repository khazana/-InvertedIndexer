<p>The <b>Red Sea</b> (also the <b>Erythraean Sea</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">البحر الأحمر</span>‎) is a <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, lying between <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The connection to the ocean is in the south through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bab_el_Mandeb" title="Bab el Mandeb">Bab el Mandeb</a> strait and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a>. To the north lie the <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai Peninsula</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aqaba" title="Gulf of Aqaba">Gulf of Aqaba</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Suez" title="Gulf of Suez">Gulf of Suez</a> (leading to the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a>). The Red Sea is a <a href="/wiki/Global_200" title="Global 200">Global 200</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>. The sea is underlain by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_Rift" title="Red Sea Rift">Red Sea Rift</a> which is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Rift_Valley_(geographical_concept)" title="Great Rift Valley (geographical concept)">Great Rift Valley</a>.
</p><p>The Red Sea has a surface area of roughly 438,000 km<sup>2</sup> (169,100 mi<sup>2</sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is about 2250 km (1398 mi) long and, at its widest point, 355 km (220.6 mi) wide. It has a maximum depth of 3,040 m (9,970 ft) in the central <i>Suakin Trough</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and an average depth of 490 m (1,608 ft). However, there are also extensive shallow shelves, noted for their marine life and <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>. The sea is the habitat of over 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> species, and 200 soft and hard corals. It is the world's northernmost <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> sea.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha,_Yemen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2072" data-file-width="3319" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg/220px-Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg/330px-Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg/440px-Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha%2C_Yemen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tihama_on_the_Red_Sea_near_Khaukha,_Yemen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tihama" title="Tihama">Tihama</a> on the Red Sea near Khaukha, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a></div></div></div>
<p><i>Red Sea</i> is a direct translation of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>Erythra Thalassa</i> (<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">Ερυθρὰ Θάλασσα</span>), <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Mare Rubrum</i> (alternatively <i>Sinus Arabicus</i>, literally "Arabian Gulf"), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">البحر الأحمر</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">translit.</a> </small><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language transliteration">Al-Baḥr Al-Aḥmar</i> (alternatively بحر القلزم <i>Baḥr Al-Qulzum</i>, literally "the Sea of <a href="/wiki/Clysma" title="Clysma">Clysma</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Somali_language" title="Somali language">Somali</a> <i>Badda Cas</i> and <a href="/wiki/Tigrinya_language" title="Tigrinya language">Tigrinya</a> <i>Qeyyiḥ bāḥrī</i> (ቀይሕ ባሕሪ). The name of the sea may signify the seasonal blooms of the red-coloured <i><a href="/wiki/Trichodesmium" title="Trichodesmium">Trichodesmium erythraeum</a></i> near the water's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  A theory favoured by some modern scholars is that the name <i>red</i> is referring to the direction south, just as the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>'s name may refer to north. The basis of this theory is that some Asiatic languages used colour words to refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5_cardinal_point" title="5 cardinal point">cardinal directions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChiTra_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChiTra-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> on one occasion uses Red Sea and Southern Sea interchangeably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, it was also known to western geographers as <i>Mare Mecca</i> (Sea of Mecca), and <i>Sinus Arabicus</i> (Gulf of Arabia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some ancient geographers called the Red Sea the Arabian Gulf<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or Gulf of Arabia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The association of the Red Sea with the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> account of the Israelites <a href="/wiki/Crossing_the_Red_Sea" title="Crossing the Red Sea">crossing the Red Sea</a> is ancient, and was made explicit in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> translation of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Book of Exodus</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koine" title="Koine">Koine Greek</a> in approximately the third century B.C.  In that version, the <i><a href="/wiki/Yam_Suph" title="Yam Suph">Yam Suph</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he">ים סוף</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a> </small>'Sea of Reeds') is translated as <i>Erythra Thalassa</i> (Red Sea). The Red Sea is one of four seas named in English after common <a href="/wiki/Color_term" title="Color term">color terms</a> — the others being the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White_Sea" title="White Sea">White Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a>. The direct rendition of the Greek <i>Erythra thalassa</i> in Latin as <a href="/wiki/Mare_Erythraeum" title="Mare Erythraeum">Mare Erythraeum</a> refers to the north-western part of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and also to a region on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="714" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG/250px-C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG/375px-C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG/500px-C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C%2BB-Ship-Fig1-HatshepsuSailingBoat.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptian</a> expedition to the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Punt" title="Land of Punt">Land of Punt</a> on the Red Sea coast during the reign of Queen <a href="/wiki/Hatshepsut" title="Hatshepsut">Hatshepsut</a></div></div></div>
<p>The earliest known exploration of the Red Sea was conducted by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptians</a>, as they attempted to establish commercial routes to <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Punt" title="Land of Punt">Punt</a>. One such expedition took place around 2500 BC, and another around 1500 BC (by <a href="/wiki/Hatshepsut" title="Hatshepsut">Hatshepsut</a>). Both involved long voyages down the Red Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Historically, scholars argued whether these trips were possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The biblical <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Book of Exodus</a> tells the account  of the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a>' <a href="/wiki/Crossing_the_Red_Sea" title="Crossing the Red Sea">crossing of a body of water</a>, which the Hebrew text calls <i><a href="/wiki/Yam_Suph" title="Yam Suph">Yam Suph</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r867359662">.mw-parser-output .script-hebrew,.mw-parser-output .script-Hebr{font-size:1.15em;font-family:"Ezra SIL","Ezra SIL SR","Keter Aram Tsova","Taamey Ashkenaz","Taamey David CLM","Taamey Frank CLM","Frank Ruehl CLM","Keter YG","Shofar","David CLM","Hadasim CLM","Simple CLM","Nachlieli","SBL BibLit","SBL Hebrew",Cardo,Alef,"Noto Serif Hebrew","Noto Sans Hebrew","David Libre",David,"Times New Roman",Gisha,Arial,FreeSerif,FreeSans}</style><span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%;">יַם סוּף</span>‬). <i>Yam Suph</i> was traditionally identified as the Red Sea. Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Saadia_Gaon" title="Saadia Gaon">Saadia Gaon</a> (882‒942), in his Judeo-Arabic translation of the Pentateuch, identifies the crossing place of the Red Sea as <i>Baḥar al-Qulzum</i>, meaning the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Suez" title="Gulf of Suez">Gulf of Suez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="903" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg/400px-Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg/600px-Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg/800px-Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Periplous_of_the_Erythraean_Sea.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Settlements and commercial centers in the vicinity of the Red Sea involved in the <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a>, as described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>In the 6th century BC, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great_of_Persia" title="Darius the Great of Persia">Darius the Great of Persia</a> sent reconnaissance missions to the Red Sea, improving and extending navigation by locating many hazardous rocks and currents. A canal was built between the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> and the northern end of the Red Sea at <a href="/wiki/Suez" title="Suez">Suez</a>. In the late 4th century BC, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> sent Greek naval expeditions down the Red Sea to the Indian Ocean. Greek navigators continued to explore and compile data on the Red Sea. <a href="/wiki/Agatharchides" title="Agatharchides">Agatharchides</a> collected information about the sea in the 2nd century BC. The <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythraean Sea">Periplus of the Erythraean Sea</a></i> ("Periplus of the Red Sea"), a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">periplus</a> written by an unknown author around the 1st century AD, contains a detailed description of the Red Sea's ports and sea routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Periplus also describes how <a href="/wiki/Hippalus" title="Hippalus">Hippalus</a> first discovered the direct route from the Red Sea to India.
</p><p>The Red Sea was favored for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_trade_with_India" title="Roman trade with India">Roman trade with India</a> starting with the reign of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> gained control over the Mediterranean, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and the northern Red Sea. The route had been used by previous states but grew in the volume of traffic under the Romans. From Indian ports goods from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> were introduced to the Roman world. Contact between Rome and China depended on the Red Sea, but the route was broken by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aksumite_Empire" title="Aksumite Empire">Aksumite Empire</a> around the 3rd century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_and_modern_era">Middle Ages and modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages and modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the Red Sea was an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a> route. In 1513, trying to secure that channel to Portugal, <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> laid <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Aden" title="Siege of Aden">siege to Aden</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but was forced to retreat. They cruised the Red Sea inside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bab_al-Mandab" title="Bab al-Mandab">Bab al-Mandab</a>, as the first European fleet to have sailed these waters.
</p><p>In 1798, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> ordered General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon</a> to invade <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and take control of the Red Sea. Although he failed in his mission, the engineer <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lep%C3%A8re" title="Jean-Baptiste Lepère">Jean-Baptiste Lepère</a>, who took part in it, revitalised the plan for a canal which had been envisaged during the reign of the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">Pharaohs</a>. Several canals were built in ancient times from the Nile to the Red Sea along or near the line of the present <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Water_Canal" title="Sweet Water Canal">Sweet Water Canal</a>, but none lasted for long. The <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a> was opened in November 1869. At the time, the British, French, and Italians shared the trading posts but these were gradually dismantled following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>. After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the Americans and Soviets exerted their influence whilst the volume of oil tanker traffic intensified. However, the <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Six-Day War</a> culminated in the closure of the Suez Canal from 1967 to 1975. Today, in spite of patrols by the major maritime fleets in the waters of the Red Sea, the Suez Canal has never recovered its supremacy over the Cape route, which is believed to be less vulnerable to piracy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanography">Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ISS036-E-011050.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" decoding="async" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ISS036-E-011050.jpg/290px-ISS036-E-011050.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ISS036-E-011050.jpg/435px-ISS036-E-011050.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/ISS036-E-011050.jpg/580px-ISS036-E-011050.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ISS036-E-011050.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Annotated view of the Nile and Red Sea, with a dust storm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Red Sea is between arid land, <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-desert" title="Semi-desert">semi-desert</a>. Reef systems are better developed along the Red Sea mainly because of its greater depths and an efficient water circulation pattern. The Red Sea water mass-exchanges its water with the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a>. These physical factors reduce the effect of high salinity caused by evaporation in the north and relatively hot water in the south.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of the Red Sea is the result of two monsoon seasons; a northeasterly monsoon and a southwesterly monsoon. Monsoon winds occur because of differential heating between the land and the sea. Very high surface temperatures and high salinities make this one of the warmest and saltiest bodies of seawater in the world. The average surface water temperature of the Red Sea during the summer is about 26 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (79 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) in the north and 30 °C (86 °F) in the south, with only about 2 °C (3.6 °F) variation during the winter months. The overall average water temperature is 22 °C (72 °F). Temperature and visibility remain good to around 200 m (656 ft). The sea is known for its strong winds and unpredictable local currents.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> over the Red Sea and its coasts is extremely low, averaging 0.06 m (2.36 in) per year. The rain is mostly short showers, often with thunderstorms and occasionally with dust <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>. The scarcity of rainfall and no major source of fresh water to the Red Sea result in excess evaporation as high as 205 cm (81 in) per year and high salinity with minimal seasonal variation. A recent underwater expedition to the Red Sea offshore from <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> found surface water temperatures 28 °C in winter and up to 34 °C in the summer, but despite that extreme heat the coral was healthy with much fish life with very little sign of <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>, with only 9% infected by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thalassomonas_loyana" title="Thalassomonas loyana">Thalassomonas loyana</a></i>, the 'white plague' agent. <a href="/wiki/Favia" title="Favia"><i>Favia favus</i></a> coral there harbours a virus, BA3, which kills <i>T. loyana</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
Plans are afoot to use samples of these corals' apparently heat-adapted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commensal" title="Commensal">commensal</a> <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> to salvage bleached coral elsewhere.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salinity">Salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Red Sea is one of the saltiest bodies of water in the world, owing to high evaporation. <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">Salinity</a> ranges from between ~36 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_mil" title="Per mil">‰</a> in the southern part because of the effect of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a> water and 41 ‰ in the northern part, owing mainly to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Suez" title="Gulf of Suez">Gulf of Suez</a> water and the high evaporation. The average salinity is 40 ‰.  (Average salinity for the world's <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> is ~35 ‰ on the Practical Salinity Scale, or PSU; that translates to 3.5% of actual dissolved salts.)
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> of the Red Sea is greater than the world average, by approximately 5‰. This is due to several factors:
</p>
<ol><li>High rate of evaporation and very little precipitation.</li>
<li>Lack of significant rivers or streams draining into the sea.</li>
<li>Limited connection with the Indian Ocean, which has lower water salinity.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_range">Tidal range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tidal range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general tide ranges between 0.6 m (2.0 ft) in the north, near the mouth of the Gulf of Suez and 0.9 m (3.0 ft) in the south near the Gulf of Aden but it fluctuates between 0.20 m (0.66 ft) and 0.30 m (0.98 ft) away from the nodal point. The central Red Sea (Jeddah area) is therefore almost tideless, and as such the annual water level changes are more significant. Because of the small tidal range the water during high tide inundates the coastal <a href="/wiki/Sabkha" title="Sabkha">sabkhas</a> as a thin sheet of water up to a few hundred metres rather than flooding the <a href="/wiki/Sabkha" title="Sabkha">sabkhas</a> through a network of channels. However, south of Jeddah in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shoiaba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shoiaba (page does not exist)">Shoiaba</a> area the water from the lagoon may cover the adjoining sabkhas as far as 3 km (2 mi), whereas, north of Jeddah in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Kharrar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Al-Kharrar (page does not exist)">Al-Kharrar</a> area the sabkhas are covered by a thin sheet of water as far as 2 km (1.2 mi). The prevailing north and northeast winds influence the movement of water in the coastal inlets to the adjacent sabkhas, especially during storms. Winter mean sea level is 0.5 m (1.6 ft) higher than in summer. Tidal velocities passing through constrictions caused by reefs, sand bars and low islands commonly exceed 1–2 m/s (3–6.5 ft/s). Coral reefs in the Red Sea are near Egypt, Eritrea, Israel, Saudi Arabia, and Sudan.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Detailed information regarding current data is lacking, partially because the currents are weak and both spatially and temporally variable. The variation of temporal and spatial currents is as low as 0.5 m (1.6 ft) and are governed all by wind. During the summer, NW winds drive surface water south for about four months at a velocity of 15–20 cm/s (6–8 in/s), whereas in winter the flow is reversed resulting in the inflow of water from the Gulf of Aden into the Red Sea. The net value of the latter predominates, resulting in an overall drift to the north end of the Red Sea. Generally, the velocity of the tidal current is between 50–60 cm/s (20–23.6 in/s) with a maximum of 1 m/s (3.3 ft/s) at the mouth of the al-Kharrar Lagoon. However, the range of the north-northeast current along the Saudi coast is 8–29 cm/s (3–11.4 in/s).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_regime">Wind regime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wind regime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The north part of the Red Sea is dominated by persistent north-west <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winds" title="Winds">winds</a>, with speeds ranging between 7 km/h (4.3 mph) and 12 km/h (7.5 mph). The rest of the Red Sea and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a> are subjected to regular and seasonally reversible winds. The wind <a href="/wiki/Regime" title="Regime">regime</a> is characterized by seasonal and regional variations in <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry,_geography)" title="Direction (geometry, geography)">direction</a> with average speed generally increasing northward.
</p><p>Wind is the driving force in the Red Sea to transport material as suspension or as bedload. Wind-induced currents play an important role in the Red Sea in resuspending bottom sediments and transferring materials from sites of dumping to sites of burial in quiescent environment of deposition. Wind-generated current <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> is therefore important in order to determine the sediment dispersal pattern and its role in the erosion and accretion of the coastal rock exposure and the submerged coral beds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dust_red_sea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="540" decoding="async" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Dust_red_sea.jpg/170px-Dust_red_sea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Dust_red_sea.jpg/255px-Dust_red_sea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Dust_red_sea.jpg/340px-Dust_red_sea.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dust_red_sea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">Dust storm</a> over the Red Sea</div></div></div>
<p>The Red Sea was formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a> being split from the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> by movement of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_Rift" title="Red Sea Rift">Red Sea Rift</a>. This split started in the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> and accelerated during the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene" title="Oligocene">Oligocene</a>. The sea is still widening (in 2005, following a three-week period of tectonic activity it had grown by 8m),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and it is considered that it will become an ocean in time (as proposed in the model of <a href="/wiki/John_Tuzo_Wilson" title="John Tuzo Wilson">John Tuzo Wilson</a>). In 1949, a deep water survey reported anomalously hot brines in the central portion of the Red Sea. Later work in the 1960s confirmed the presence of hot, 60 °C (140 °F), saline brines and associated metalliferous muds. The hot solutions were emanating from an active subseafloor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_(geology)" title="Rift (geology)">rift</a>. Lake Asal in Djibouti is eligible as an experimental site to study the evolution of the deep hot brines of the Red Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> Indeed, by observing the strontium isotope composition of the Red Sea brines, it is easy to deduce how these salt waters found at the bottom of the Red Sea could have evolved in a similar way to Lake Asal, which ideally represents their compositional extreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> The high salinity of the waters was not hospitable to living organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometime during the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> period, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bab_el_Mandeb" title="Bab el Mandeb">Bab el Mandeb</a> closed and the Red Sea evaporated to an empty hot dry salt-floored sink. Effects causing this would have been:
</p>
<ul><li>A "race" between the Red Sea widening and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perim_Island" title="Perim Island">Perim Island</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">erupting</a> filling the Bab el Mandeb with <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>.</li>
<li>The lowering of world <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Ages</a> because of much water being locked up in the <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>.</li></ul>
<p>A number of volcanic islands rise from the center of the sea. Most are dormant. However, in 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jabal_al-Tair_island" title="Jabal al-Tair island">Jabal al-Tair island</a> in the Bab el Mandeb strait erupted violently. Two new islands were formed in 2011 and 2013 in the <a href="/wiki/Zubair_Group" title="Zubair Group">Zubair Archipelago</a>, a small chain of islands owned by Yemen. The first island, Sholan Island, emerged in an eruption in December 2011, the second island, Jadid, emerged in September 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Durwara 2 Field was discovered in 1963, while the Suakin 1 Field and the Bashayer 1A Field were discovered in 1976, on the Egyptian side of the Red Sea.  The Barqan Field was discovered in 1969, and the Midyan Field in 1992, both within the Midyan Basin on the Saudi Arabian side of the Red Sea.  The 20 m thick <a href="/wiki/Middle_Miocene" title="Middle Miocene">Middle Miocene</a> Maqna <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(geology)" title="Formation (geology)">Formation</a> is an oil source rock in the basin.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_seep" title="Oil seep">Oil seeps</a> occur near the <a href="/wiki/Farasan_Islands" title="Farasan Islands">Farasan Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dahlak_Archipelago" title="Dahlak Archipelago">Dahlak Archipelago</a>, along the coast of Eritrea, and in the southeastern Red Sea along the coasts of Saudi Arabia and Yemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_resources">Mineral resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mineral resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg/220px-Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg/330px-Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg/440px-Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_sea_stony_beach_taba_egypt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red Sea coast in <a href="/wiki/Taba,_Egypt" title="Taba, Egypt">Taba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a></div></div></div>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef,_Red_Sea,_Egypt_(35150034493).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1697" data-file-width="3017" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg/220px-Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg/330px-Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg/440px-Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef%2C_Red_Sea%2C_Egypt_%2835150034493%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hawksbill_turtle_at_Elphinstone_Reef,_Red_Sea,_Egypt_(35150034493).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hawksbill_sea_turtle" title="Hawksbill sea turtle">Hawksbill sea turtle</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Elphinstone_Reef" title="Elphinstone Reef">Elphinstone Reef</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3462" data-file-width="2597" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG/220px-Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG/330px-Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG/440px-Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nudibranch_egg_ribbon_at_Shaab_Mahmoud.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nudibranch egg ribbon at Shaab Mahmoud</div></div></div>
<p>The Red Sea is a rich and diverse <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. More than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_of_the_Red_Sea" title="Fish of the Red Sea">1200 species of fish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-28">[28]</a></sup> have been recorded in the Red Sea, and around 10% of these are found nowhere else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This also includes 42 species of <a href="/wiki/List_of_deep_water_fish_of_the_Red_Sea" title="List of deep water fish of the Red Sea">deepwater fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fishbase_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-fishbase-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="573" data-file-width="850" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg/220px-Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg/330px-Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg/440px-Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_sea_coral_reef.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red Sea coral and marine fish</div></div></div>
<p>The rich diversity is in part due to the 2,000 km (1,240 mi) of <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> extending along its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">coastline</a>; these <a href="/wiki/Fringing_reef" title="Fringing reef">fringing reefs</a> are 5000–7000 years old and are largely formed of stony <a href="/wiki/Acropora" title="Acropora">acropora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porites" title="Porites">porites</a> corals. The reefs form platforms and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a> along the coast and occasional other features such as cylinders (such as the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Hole_(Red_Sea)" title="Blue Hole (Red Sea)">Blue Hole (Red Sea)</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dahab" title="Dahab">Dahab</a>). These coastal reefs are also visited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> species of Red Sea fish, including some of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_sharks_in_the_Red_Sea" title="List of sharks in the Red Sea">44 species of shark</a>.
</p><p>It contains 175 species of <a href="/wiki/Nudibranch" title="Nudibranch">nudibranch</a>, many of which are only found in the Red Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAW_Yonow_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAW_Yonow-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Red Sea also contains many offshore reefs including several true atolls.  Many of the unusual offshore reef formations defy classic (i.e., Darwinian) coral reef classification schemes, and are generally attributed to the high levels of tectonic activity that characterize the area.
</p><p>The special <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> of the area is recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> government, who set up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ras_Mohammed" title="Ras Mohammed">Ras Mohammed National Park</a> in 1983. The rules and regulations governing this area protect local marine life, which has become a major draw for <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">diving</a> enthusiasts.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">Divers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snorkeller" title="Snorkeller">snorkellers</a> should be aware that although most Red Sea species are innocuous, a few are hazardous to humans: see <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_species_hazardous_to_humans" title="Red Sea species hazardous to humans">Red Sea species hazardous to humans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-book2-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other marine habitats include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_grass" title="Sea grass">sea grass</a> beds, <a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">salt pans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">salt marshes</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Desalination_plants">Desalination plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Desalination plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is extensive demand for <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalinated</a> water to meet the needs of the population and the industries along the Red Sea.
</p><p>There are at least 18 desalination plants along the Red Sea coast of Saudi Arabia which discharge warm <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> and treatment chemicals (<a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-fouling_agent" title="Anti-fouling agent">anti-scalants</a>) that <a href="/wiki/Bleach" title="Bleach">bleach</a> and kill <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and cause diseases in the fish. This is only localized, but it may intensify with time and  profoundly impact the fishing industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The water from the Red Sea is also used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_refineries" title="Oil refineries">oil refineries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> factories for cooling. Used water drained back into the coastal zones may harm the nearshore environment of the Red Sea.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Red Sea is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_road" title="Sea road">sea roads</a> between <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, and as such has heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">shipping traffic</a>. Government-related bodies with responsibility to police the Red Sea area include the <a href="/wiki/Port_Said_Port_Authority" title="Port Said Port Authority">Port Said Port Authority</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal_Authority" title="Suez Canal Authority">Suez Canal Authority</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_Ports_Authority" title="Red Sea Ports Authority">Red Sea Ports Authority</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Maritime_Authority" title="Jordan Maritime Authority">Jordan Maritime Authority</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel_Port_Authority" title="Israel Port Authority">Israel Port Authority</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Ports_Authority" title="Saudi Ports Authority">Saudi Ports Authority</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_Ports_Corporation,_Sudan" title="Sea Ports Corporation, Sudan">Sea Ports Corporation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facts_and_figures">Facts and figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Facts and figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Length:  ~2,250 km (1,398.1 mi) - 79% of the eastern Red Sea with numerous coastal inlets</li>

<li>Minimum Width:   ~ 26–29 km (16–18 mi)- <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bab_el_Mandeb" title="Bab el Mandeb">Bab el Mandeb</a> Strait (Yemen)</li>

<li>Maximum Depth:  ~ 3,040 m (9,970 ft)</li>
<li>Surface Area: 438-450 x 10<sup>2</sup> km<sup>2</sup> (16,900–17,400 sq mi)</li>
<li>Volume: 215–251 x 10<sup>3</sup> km<sup>3</sup> (51,600–60,200 cu mi)</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Approximately 40% of the Red Sea is quite shallow (under 100 m/330 ft), and about 25% is under 50 m (164 ft) deep.</li>
<li>About 15% of the Red Sea is over 1,000 m (3,300 ft) depth that forms the deep axial trough.</li>
<li>Shelf breaks are marked by coral reefs</li>
<li>Continental slope has an irregular profile (series of steps down to  ~500 m or 1,640 ft)</li>
<li>Centre of Red Sea has a narrow trough (<i>Suakin Trough</i>) (~ 1,000 m or 3,281 ft; with maximum depth 3,040 m or 9,974 ft)</li></ul></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="242" data-file-width="350" decoding="async" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg/220px-Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg/330px-Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elath_Eilat_Israel_Strand_Hotel_datafox.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hotels in <a href="/wiki/Eilat" title="Eilat">Eilat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a></div></div></div>
<p>The sea is known for its <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diving" title="Recreational diving">recreational diving</a> sites, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ras_Mohammed" title="Ras Mohammed">Ras Mohammed</a>, <a href="/wiki/SS_Thistlegorm" title="SS Thistlegorm">SS Thistlegorm</a> (shipwreck), <a href="/wiki/Elphinstone_Reef" title="Elphinstone Reef">Elphinstone Reef</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Brothers,_Egypt" title="The Brothers, Egypt">The Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daedalus_Reef" title="Daedalus Reef">Daedalus Reef</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St.John%27s_Reef&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="St.John's Reef (page does not exist)">St.John's Reef</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rocky_Island_(Egypt)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rocky Island (Egypt) (page does not exist)">Rocky Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and less known sites in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sanganeb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sanganeb (page does not exist)">Sanganeb</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Abington,_Sudan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Abington, Sudan (page does not exist)">Abington</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Angarosh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Angarosh (page does not exist)">Angarosh</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shaab_Rumi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shaab Rumi (page does not exist)">Shaab Rumi</a>.
</p><p>The Red Sea became a popular destination for diving after the expeditions of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Hass" title="Hans Hass">Hans Hass</a> in the 1950s, and later by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques-Yves_Cousteau" title="Jacques-Yves Cousteau">Jacques-Yves Cousteau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cousteau_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cousteau-34">[34]</a></sup> Popular tourist resorts include <a href="/wiki/El_Gouna" title="El Gouna">El Gouna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurghada" title="Hurghada">Hurghada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safaga" title="Safaga">Safaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsa_Alam" title="Marsa Alam">Marsa Alam</a>, on the west shore of the Red Sea, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharm-el-Sheikh" title="Sharm-el-Sheikh">Sharm-el-Sheikh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dahab" title="Dahab">Dahab</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taba,_Egypt" title="Taba, Egypt">Taba</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> side of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sina%C3%AF" title="Sinaï">Sinaï</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Aqaba" title="Aqaba">Aqaba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eilat" title="Eilat">Eilat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in an area known as the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea_Riviera" title="Red Sea Riviera">Red Sea Riviera</a>.
</p><p>The popular tourist beach of Sharm el-Sheikh was closed to all swimming in December 2010 due to several serious <a href="/wiki/2010_Sharm_El_Sheikh_shark_attacks" title="2010 Sharm El Sheikh shark attacks">shark attacks</a>, including a fatality. As of December 2010, scientists are investigating the attacks and have identified, but not verified, several possible causes including over-fishing which causes large sharks to hunt closer to shore, tourist boat operators who chum offshore for shark-photo opportunities, and reports of ships throwing dead livestock overboard. The sea's narrowness, significant depth, and sharp drop-offs, all combine to form a geography where large deep-water sharks can roam in hundreds of meters of water, yet be within a hundred meters of swimming areas.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/2016_Hurghada_attack" title="2016 Hurghada attack">2016 Hurghada attack</a>, <a href="/wiki/2017_Hurghada_attack" title="2017 Hurghada attack">2017 Hurghada attack</a>, <a href="/wiki/2006_Dahab_bombings" title="2006 Dahab bombings">2006 Dahab bombings</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Sharm_El_Sheikh_bombings" title="2005 Sharm El Sheikh bombings">2005 Sharm El Sheikh bombings</a>, <a href="/wiki/2004_Sinai_bombings" title="2004 Sinai bombings">2004 Sinai bombings</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metrojet_Flight_9268" title="Metrojet Flight 9268">Metrojet Flight 9268</a></div>
<p>Tourism to the region has been threatened by occasional terrorist attacks, and by incidents related to food safety standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalshKaraszDeath_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-WalshKaraszDeath-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RegevCriticalBlow_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-RegevCriticalBlow-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bordering_countries">Bordering countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Bordering countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Red Sea may be geographically divided into three sections: the Red Sea proper, and in the north, the Gulf of Aqaba and the Gulf of Suez. The six countries bordering the Red Sea proper are:
</p>
<ul><li>Eastern shore:

<li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="450" decoding="async" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Flag_of_Yemen.svg/23px-Flag_of_Yemen.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Flag_of_Yemen.svg/35px-Flag_of_Yemen.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Flag_of_Yemen.svg/45px-Flag_of_Yemen.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Western shore:
<ul><li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Flag_of_Egypt.svg/23px-Flag_of_Egypt.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Flag_of_Egypt.svg/35px-Flag_of_Egypt.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Flag_of_Egypt.svg/45px-Flag_of_Egypt.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a></li>
<li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="12" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Flag_of_Sudan.svg/23px-Flag_of_Sudan.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Flag_of_Sudan.svg/35px-Flag_of_Sudan.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Flag_of_Sudan.svg/46px-Flag_of_Sudan.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a></li>
<li><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="12" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Eritrea.svg/23px-Flag_of_Eritrea.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Eritrea.svg/35px-Flag_of_Eritrea.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Eritrea.svg/46px-Flag_of_Eritrea.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a></li>

<p>The Gulf of Suez is entirely bordered by Egypt. The Gulf of Aqaba borders Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> and Saudi Arabia.
</p><p>In addition to the standard geographical definition of the six countries bordering the Red Sea cited above, areas such as <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> are sometimes also described as Red Sea territories. This is primarily due to their proximity to and geological similarities with the nations facing the Red Sea and/or political ties with said areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barth_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barth-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Makinda_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Makinda-38">[38]</a></sup>
