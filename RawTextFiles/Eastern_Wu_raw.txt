<p><b>Wu</b> (222–280), commonly known as <b>Dong Wu</b> (<b>Eastern Wu</b>) or <b>Sun Wu</b>, was one of the three major states that competed for supremacy over <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period (220–280). It previously existed from 220–222 as a <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a> kingdom nominally under <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>, its rival state, but declared independence from Wei and became a sovereign state in 222. It became an empire in 229 after its founding ruler, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a>, declared himself <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">emperor</a>. Its name was derived from the place it was based in — the <a href="/wiki/Jiangnan" title="Jiangnan">Jiangnan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River_Delta" title="Yangtze River Delta">Yangtze River Delta</a>) region, which was also historically known as "<a href="/wiki/Wu_(region)" title="Wu (region)">Wu</a>". It was referred to as "Dong Wu" ("Eastern Wu") or "Sun Wu" by historians to distinguish it from other Chinese historical states with similar names which were also located in that region, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wu_(state)" title="Wu (state)">Wu state</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wuyue" title="Wuyue">Wuyue</a> kingdom in the <a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period" title="Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period">Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period</a>. It was called "Eastern Wu" because it occupied most of eastern China in the Three Kingdoms period, and "Sun Wu" because the family name of its rulers was "<a href="/wiki/Sun_(surname)" title="Sun (surname)">Sun</a>". During its existence, Wu's capital was at <a href="/wiki/Jiankang" title="Jiankang">Jianye</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a>), but at times it was also at Wuchang (<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">武昌</span>; present-day <a href="/wiki/Ezhou" title="Ezhou">Ezhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:244px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WuJar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1387" data-file-width="967" decoding="async" height="347" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WuJar.jpg/242px-WuJar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WuJar.jpg/363px-WuJar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/WuJar.jpg/484px-WuJar.jpg 2x" width="242"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WuJar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A jar made in Eastern Wu dating to the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_and_founding">Beginnings and founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings and founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chibi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="416" decoding="async" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chibi.jpg/150px-Chibi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chibi.jpg/225px-Chibi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chibi.jpg/300px-Chibi.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chibi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Before the dynasty of Eastern Wu was established, the territory was defended by the Sun clan in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Cliffs" title="Battle of Red Cliffs">Battle of Red Cliffs</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Towards the <a href="/wiki/End_of_the_Han_dynasty" title="End of the Han dynasty">end of the Han dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Ce" title="Sun Ce">Sun Ce</a>, the eldest son of the warlord <a href="/wiki/Sun_Jian" title="Sun Jian">Sun Jian</a>, and his followers borrowed troops from the warlord <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Shu" title="Yuan Shu">Yuan Shu</a> and embarked on <a href="/wiki/Sun_Ce%27s_conquests_in_Jiangdong" title="Sun Ce's conquests in Jiangdong">a series of military conquests</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Jiangnan" title="Jiangnan">Jiangdong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_(region)" title="Wu (region)">Wu</a> regions between 194 and 199, seizing several territories previously occupied by warlords such as <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yao_(warlord)" title="Liu Yao (warlord)">Liu Yao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yan_Baihu" title="Yan Baihu">Yan Baihu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Lang" title="Wang Lang">Wang Lang</a>. Sun Ce broke off relations with Yuan Shu around 196-197 after the latter declared himself emperor — an act deemed as treason against <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xian_of_Han" title="Emperor Xian of Han">Emperor Xian</a>, the figurehead ruler of the Han dynasty. The warlord <a href="/wiki/Cao_Cao" title="Cao Cao">Cao Cao</a>, who was the <i>de facto</i> head of government in the Han imperial court, asked Emperor Xian to grant Sun Ce the title of "Marquis of Wu" (<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">吳侯</span>).
</p><p>Sun Ce was assassinated in the summer of 200 and was succeeded by his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a>. Sun Quan, like his elder brother, also paid nominal allegiance to Emperor Xian while maintaining autonomous rule over the Wu territories. In 208, Sun Quan allied with the warlord <a href="/wiki/Liu_Bei" title="Liu Bei">Liu Bei</a> and they combined forces to defeat Cao Cao at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Cliffs" title="Battle of Red Cliffs">Battle of Red Cliffs</a>. Sun Quan and Liu Bei maintained their alliance against Cao Cao after the battle for the next ten years or so, despite having some territorial disputes over <a href="/wiki/Jingzhou_(ancient_China)" title="Jingzhou (ancient China)">Jing Province</a>. In 219, Sun Quan severed ties with Liu Bei when he sent his general <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BC_Meng" title="Lü Meng">Lü Meng</a> to <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BC_Meng%27s_invasion_of_Jing_Province" title="Lü Meng's invasion of Jing Province">invade Liu's territories in Jing Province</a>. <a href="/wiki/Guan_Yu" title="Guan Yu">Guan Yu</a>, who was defending Liu Bei's assets in Jing Province, was captured and executed by Sun Quan's forces. After that, the boundaries of Sun Quan's domain extended from beyond the Jiangdong region to include the southern part of Jing Province, which covered roughly present-day <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a>.
</p><p>In 220, Cao Cao's son and successor, <a href="/wiki/Cao_Pi" title="Cao Pi">Cao Pi</a>, ended the Han dynasty by forcing Emperor Xian to abdicate in his favour and established the state of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Cao Wei</a>. Sun Quan agreed to submit to Wei and was granted the title of a vassal king, "King of Wu" (<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">吳王</span>), by Cao Pi. A year later, Liu Bei declared himself emperor and founded the state of <a href="/wiki/Shu_Han" title="Shu Han">Shu Han</a>. In 222, Liu Bei launched a military campaign against Sun Quan to take back Jing Province and avenge Guan Yu, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Xiaoting" title="Battle of Xiaoting">Battle of Xiaoting</a>. However, Liu Bei suffered a crushing defeat at the hands of Sun Quan's general <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Lu Xun (Three Kingdoms)">Lu Xun</a> and was forced to retreat to <a href="/wiki/Baidicheng" title="Baidicheng">Baidicheng</a>, where he died a year later.
</p><p>Liu Bei's successor, <a href="/wiki/Liu_Shan" title="Liu Shan">Liu Shan</a>, and his regent, <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang" title="Zhuge Liang">Zhuge Liang</a>, made peace with Sun Quan later and reaffirmed their previous alliance. Sun Quan declared independence from Wei in 222, but continued to rule as "King of Wu" until 229, when he declared himself "Emperor of Wu". His legitimacy was recognised by Shu.
</p>
<h3><span id="Sun_Quan.27s_reign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sun_Quan's_reign">Sun Quan's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sun Quan's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sun Quan ruled for over 30 years and his long reign resulted in stability in southern China. During his reign, Wu engaged Wei in numerous wars, including the battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ruxu_(222%E2%80%93223)" title="Battle of Ruxu (222–223)">Ruxu</a> (222–223), <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Shiting" title="Battle of Shiting">Shiting</a> (228), and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hefei_(234)" title="Battle of Hefei (234)">Hefei</a> (234). However, Wu never managed to gain any territory north of the Yangtze River while Wei also never succeeded in conquering the lands south of the Yangtze.
</p><p>A succession struggle broke out between Sun Quan's sons in the later part of his reign — Sun Quan instated <a href="/wiki/Sun_He_(Zixiao)" title="Sun He (Zixiao)">Sun He</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">crown prince</a> in 242 after his former heir apparent, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Deng_(Eastern_Wu)" title="Sun Deng (Eastern Wu)">Sun Deng</a>, died in 241, but Sun He soon became involved in a rivalry with his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Ba" title="Sun Ba">Sun Ba</a>. The conflict resulted in the emergence of two rivalling factions, each supporting either Sun He or Sun Ba, in Sun Quan's imperial court. Sun Quan eventually deposed Sun He and forced Sun Ba to commit suicide, while <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Lu Xun (Three Kingdoms)">Lu Xun</a> and many other ministers who took either Sun He's or Sun Ba's side in the struggle met with unhappy ends. Sun Quan appointed his youngest son, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Liang" title="Sun Liang">Sun Liang</a>, as the crown prince after the incident.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reigns_of_Sun_Liang_and_Sun_Xiu">Reigns of Sun Liang and Sun Xiu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reigns of Sun Liang and Sun Xiu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sun Quan died in 252 and was succeeded by Sun Liang, with <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Ke" title="Zhuge Ke">Zhuge Ke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sun_Jun_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Sun Jun (Three Kingdoms)">Sun Jun</a> serving as regents. In 253, Zhuge Ke was assassinated in a coup launched by Sun Jun, and the state power of Wu fell into Sun Jun's hands and was passed on to his cousin, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Chen" title="Sun Chen">Sun Chen</a>, after his death. During Sun Liang's reign, <a href="/wiki/Three_Rebellions_in_Shouchun" title="Three Rebellions in Shouchun">two rebellions</a> broke out in the Wei garrison at Shouchun (around present-day <a href="/wiki/Shou_County" title="Shou County">Shou County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>) in 255 and 257–258. Sun Jun and Sun Chen led Wu forces to support the rebels in the first and second rebellions respectively in the hope of making some territorial gains in Wei, but both revolts were suppressed and the Wu forces retreated after suffering many losses.
</p><p>Sun Liang was deposed in 258 by Sun Chen, who installed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_Xiu_(emperor)" title="Sun Xiu (emperor)">Sun Xiu</a>, another son of Sun Quan, on the throne. Sun Xiu killed Sun Chen later in a coup with the help of <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Bu" title="Zhang Bu">Zhang Bu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ding_Feng_(general)" title="Ding Feng (general)">Ding Feng</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_of_Wu">Fall of Wu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fall of Wu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sun Xiu died of illness in 264, a year after <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Shu_by_Wei" title="Conquest of Shu by Wei">Shu was conquered by Wei</a>. At the time, Wu was experiencing internal turmoil because rebellions had broken out in <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhi#Three_Kingdoms" title="Jiaozhi">Jiaozhi</a> (<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">交趾</span>) in the south. The ministers <a href="/wiki/Puyang_Xing" title="Puyang Xing">Puyang Xing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wan_Yu" title="Wan Yu">Wan Yu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Bu" title="Zhang Bu">Zhang Bu</a> decided to install Sun He's son, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Hao" title="Sun Hao">Sun Hao</a>, on the throne.
</p><p>In the beginning of Sun Hao's reign, the emperor reduced taxes, gave relief to the poor, and granted freedom to a large number of palace maids. However, Sun Hao gradually became more cruel and superstitious and started indulging in wine and women instead of finding ways to revive his declining state. Sun Hao's tyranny caused widespread anger and hatred towards him in Wu, but it was due to the efforts of officials such as <a href="/wiki/Lu_Kai" title="Lu Kai">Lu Kai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lu_Kang_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Lu Kang (Three Kingdoms)">Lu Kang</a> that Wu was able to remain relatively stable and peaceful.
</p><p>In February 266, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Wu of Jin">Sima Yan</a> ended the state of Cao Wei by forcing its last ruler, <a href="/wiki/Cao_Huan" title="Cao Huan">Cao Huan</a>, to abdicate in his favour, and then established the <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265–420)">Jin dynasty</a>. In 279, Jin forces led by <a href="/wiki/Du_Yu" title="Du Yu">Du Yu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Jun_(Jin_dynasty)" title="Wang Jun (Jin dynasty)">Wang Jun</a> and others attacked Wu from six directions. Sun Hao attempted to put up resistance by sending his armies to fight the Jin invaders, but the Wu forces suffered several consecutive defeats and even the Wu chancellor, <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Ti" title="Zhang Ti">Zhang Ti</a>, was killed in action. Seeing that Wu was doomed to fall, Sun Hao surrendered to the Jin dynasty on 31 May 280,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dardess_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dardess-1">[1]</a></sup> marking the end of Wu and the end of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> period.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_military">Government and military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government and military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite Wu and its court becoming imperial in 229, Sun Quan had kept Wu in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warlordism" title="Warlordism">warlordism</a> reflected state.  When Wu was initially founded its military was dominated by famed generals who had gained their positions through prowess and pluck.  These generals were celebrated for their <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Politics within the court were often influenced by conflicts between powerful families and individuals.  Positions within the court were inherited from one generation to the next unlike the Han dynasty's <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>.  However, over time, the influence ultimately would move away from the <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">central government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Outside of the court, families displayed their own independent authority. Wu, at times, was to a certain extent run for the protection of particular families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_economy">Culture and economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Culture and economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CottonPlant.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2100" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CottonPlant.JPG/220px-CottonPlant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CottonPlant.JPG/330px-CottonPlant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/CottonPlant.JPG/440px-CottonPlant.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CottonPlant.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shu Han exported cotton into Eastern Wu.</div></div></div>

Wu Kingdom.</div></div></div>
<p>The culture of Wu was most solidified under the reign of Sun Quan from 229 to 252. Migrations from the north and the needed settlement from the <a href="/wiki/Shanyue" title="Shanyue">Shanyue</a> barbarians made it possible for the increase in manpower, agriculture, and settling the lower most parts of Wu. Along with that, river transportation became a huge factor and flourished as the <a href="/wiki/Jiangnan" title="Jiangnan">Jiangnan</a> and Zhedong canals were finished with construction. After the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Xiaoting" title="Battle of Xiaoting">Battle of Xiaoting</a> and during the <a href="/wiki/Cao_Pi%27s_invasions_of_Eastern_Wu" title="Cao Pi's invasions of Eastern Wu">invasions of Wu</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Wei</a> in the 220s, Shu was able to reestablish their trade and relationships with Wu. Shu's cotton was a great influx for Wu, and the development of shipbuilding, salt,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and metal industries was greatly increased.
</p><p>The fact of inflation and economic problems still were in existence since the Han dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=24_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=24-10">[10]</a></sup> Sun Quan tried to start a currency of large coins manufactured by copper. He also tried to prohibit private minting. This policy was terminated in 246 due to ineffectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=24_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=24-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[a]</a></sup>
</p><p>Eastern Wu was able to make close overseas trade with countries such as Vietnam and Cambodia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Wu also traded with India and the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_matters">Civil matters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Wu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Civil matters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Personages with clerical or scholarly abilities had roles to play within the state, but the policies were more determined by those of military command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cres4i-4_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cres4i-4-14">[13]</a></sup> Nevertheless, every Wu army was in need of administrative support and, according to <a href="/wiki/Rafe_de_Crespigny" title="Rafe de Crespigny">Rafe de Crespigny</a>, certain scholars were "recognised as practical counsellors, regardless of their fighting prowess or their ability to command troops in the field."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cres4i-4_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-cres4i-4-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a>, he needed a strong role of advisors and secretaries in order to keep his link of power in a maintained level. Sun Quan's prestige in dealing with hostiles and friendly relations called for the establishment of a controlled form of an imperial government for the empire of Wu. Sun Quan also created the opportunity for people residing within Wu to gain prestige and influence throughout the empire and the surrounding establishments with the duty of being an envoy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cres4i-4_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-cres4i-4-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the death of <a href="/wiki/Cao_Pi" title="Cao Pi">Cao Pi</a> in 226, Sun Quan strongly promoted his kingdom to focus on agriculture because the threat from Wei was lifted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">Lu Xun</a> suggested to Sun Quan that military commanders should become involved in the colonization of land. Sun Quan quickly accepted and he, along with his sons would execute the memorial presented by Lu Xun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> However, in 240, Sun Quan restrained Lu Xun's idea and refocused on agricultural works, because Wu came to suffer a severe famine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> In 234, when <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Ke" title="Zhuge Ke">Zhuge Ke</a> was in control of affairs in the south, he strongly ignored the colonisation order and viciously ordered the agriculture factor, often starving enemies into submission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Under the rule of Wu, the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River_Delta" title="Yangtze River Delta">Yangtze River Delta</a> region, regarded in early history as a barbaric "jungle", developed into one of the commercial, cultural, and political centres of China. The achievements of Wu in the south marked the coming of Chinese civilization to the farthest southern reaches of the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 230, the island of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> was reached by the Chinese during the Three Kingdoms period under the reign of Sun Quan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Contact with the native population and the dispatch of officials to an island named "Yizhou" (<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">夷州</span>) by the Wu navy might have been to Taiwan, but the location of Yizhou is open to dispute; some historians believe it was Taiwan, while others believe it was the <a href="/wiki/Ryukyu_Islands" title="Ryukyu Islands">Ryukyu Islands</a>. Wu merchants also may have reached <a href="/wiki/Southern_Vietnam" title="Southern Vietnam">Southern Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. Failed protection of <a href="/wiki/Gongsun_Yuan" title="Gongsun Yuan">Gongsun Yuan</a> also was in existence when the latter <a href="/wiki/Sima_Yi%27s_Liaodong_campaign" title="Sima Yi's Liaodong campaign">rebelled against Wei</a>. This was because of the waterway's difficulties. Such things cost Wu, and the achievements supposedly gained within Taiwan did not cover this problem and Sun Quan lost his vassal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Later on in the existence of Wu, the once great military was turned to an unimpressive military. It was most likely an easy task to take <a href="/wiki/Hefei" title="Hefei">Hefei</a> from Wei, but Wu could not do so. Since the 230s, this task was made harder due to the "New City", a heavily fortified castle built at Hefei by Wei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10-21">[20]</a></sup> One of the greatest failures to accomplish something later on in Wu's reign was during 255 and during the last few years of the 250s. When <a href="/wiki/Guanqiu_Jian" title="Guanqiu Jian">Guanqiu Jian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wen_Qin" title="Wen Qin">Wen Qin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Rebellion_in_Shouchun" title="Second Rebellion in Shouchun">rebelled</a> against Wei, Wu promised to help the two in Shouchun (around present-day <a href="/wiki/Shou_County" title="Shou County">Shou County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>). However, the Wu forces never made it in time before the rebellion was quashed by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Shi" title="Sima Shi">Sima Shi</a> and the Wei forces. When <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Dan" title="Zhuge Dan">Zhuge Dan</a> launched a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Rebellion_in_Shouchun" title="Third Rebellion in Shouchun">massive full-scale rebellion</a>, the Wu forces suffered a great defeat as they lent a great quantity of manpower to Zhuge Dan's cause. Shouchun was quickly regained by Wei under <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhao" title="Sima Zhao">Sima Zhao</a>'s command.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-de_Crespigny_1990_loc=10-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Shu_by_Wei" title="Conquest of Shu by Wei">conquest of Shu by Wei</a> in 263, Wu could not fully lend support to their allies due to a revolt in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The decline of Wu was long in existence since the death of <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Lu Xun (Three Kingdoms)">Lu Xun</a> in 245 and the death of Sun Quan in 252. Sun Quan's successors could do little for the empire. <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Ke" title="Zhuge Ke">Zhuge Ke</a> was assassinated by <a href="/wiki/Sun_Jun_(Three_Kingdoms)" title="Sun Jun (Three Kingdoms)">Sun Jun</a> in 253 after a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hefei_(253)" title="Battle of Hefei (253)">failed invasion of Hefei</a> following the Wu victory over an invading Wei force at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dongxing" title="Battle of Dongxing">Dongxing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ding_Feng_(general)" title="Ding Feng (general)">Ding Feng</a> also ended up killing <a href="/wiki/Sun_Chen" title="Sun Chen">Sun Chen</a> under orders from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_Xiu_(emperor)" title="Sun Xiu (emperor)">Sun Xiu</a>. Corruption plagued Wu, which led to an easy <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wu_by_Jin" title="Conquest of Wu by Jin">conquest of Wu</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin dynasty (265-420)">Jin dynasty</a> in 280.
</p>
