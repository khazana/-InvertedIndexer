<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dynamo</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Dynamometer" title="Dynamometer">dynamometer</a>.</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="502" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png/220px-DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png/330px-DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png/440px-DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DynamoElectricMachinesEndViewPartlySection_USP284110.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Dynamo Electric Machine" (end view, partly section, <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US284110" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 284,110</a></span>)</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>dynamo</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a> that creates <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutator</a>. Dynamos were the first electrical generators capable of delivering power for industry, and the foundation upon which many other later <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_conversion" title="Electric power conversion">electric-power conversion</a> devices were based, including the <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating-current</a> <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rotary_converter" title="Rotary converter">rotary converter</a>. Today, the simpler alternator dominates large scale <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">power generation</a>, for efficiency, reliability and cost reasons. A dynamo has the disadvantages of a mechanical commutator. Also, converting alternating to direct current using power rectification devices (such as <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> or more recently via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_(electronics)" title="Solid state (electronics)">solid state</a> technology) is effective and usually economical.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>dynamo</i> (from the Greek word dynamis (δύναμις), meaning force or power) was originally another name for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a>, and still has some regional usage as a replacement for the word generator. The word "dynamo" was coined in 1831 by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>, who utilized his invention toward making many discoveries in <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> (Faraday discovered electrical induction) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The original "dynamo principle" of Wehrner von Siemens or <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Siemens" title="Werner von Siemens">Werner von Siemens</a> referred only the direct current generators which use exclusively the <a href="/wiki/Excitation_(magnetic)" title="Excitation (magnetic)">self-excitation</a> (self-induction) principle to generate DC power. The earlier DC generators which used permanent magnets were not considered "dynamo electric machines".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The invention of the dynamo principle (self-induction) was a huge technological leap over the old traditional permanent magnet based DC generators. The discovery of the dynamo principle made industrial scale electric power generation technically and economically feasible.
After the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a> and that <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> can be used as a power supply, the word <i>dynamo</i> became associated exclusively with the <i><a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutated</a> <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> electric generator</i>, while an AC electrical generator using either <a href="/wiki/Slip_ring" title="Slip ring">slip rings</a> or rotor magnets would become known as an <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a>.
</p><p>A small electrical generator built into the hub of a bicycle wheel to power lights is called a <a href="/wiki/Hub_dynamo" title="Hub dynamo">hub dynamo</a>, although these are invariably AC devices,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and are actually <a href="/wiki/Magneto" title="Magneto">magnetos</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The  electric dynamo uses rotating coils of wire and magnetic fields to convert mechanical rotation into a pulsing direct electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">current</a> through <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">Faraday's law of induction</a>. A dynamo machine consists of a stationary structure, called the <a href="/wiki/Stator" title="Stator">stator</a>, which provides a constant <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, and a set of rotating windings called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armature_(electrical_engineering)" title="Armature (electrical engineering)">armature</a> which turn within that field.  Due to <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">Faraday's law of induction</a> the motion of the wire within the magnetic field creates an <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">electromotive force</a> which pushes on the electrons in the metal, creating an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> in the wire. On small machines the constant magnetic field may be provided by one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">permanent magnets</a>; larger machines have the constant magnetic field provided by one or more <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnets</a>, which are usually called <i><a href="/wiki/Field_coil" title="Field coil">field coils</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commutation">Commutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commutation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">Commutator (electric)</a></div>
<p>The <i>commutator</i> is needed to produce <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a>. When a loop of wire rotates in a magnetic field, the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a> through it, and thus the potential induced in it, reverses with each half turn, generating an <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a>. However, in the early days of electric experimentation, <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> generally had no known use. The few uses for electricity, such as <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a>, used direct current provided by messy liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>. Dynamos were invented as a replacement for batteries. The commutator is essentially a rotary <a href="/wiki/Switch" title="Switch">switch</a>. It consists of a set of contacts mounted on the machine's shaft, combined with graphite-block stationary contacts, called "brushes", because the earliest such fixed contacts were metal brushes. The commutator reverses the connection of the windings to the external circuit when the potential reverses, so instead of alternating current, a pulsing direct current is produced.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Excitation">Excitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Excitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Excitation_(magnetic)" title="Excitation (magnetic)">Excitation (magnetic)</a></div>
<p>The earliest dynamos used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">permanent magnets</a> to create the magnetic field. These were referred to as "magneto-electric machines" or <a href="/wiki/Magneto" title="Magneto">magnetos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockwood_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lockwood-4">[4]</a></sup> However, researchers found that stronger magnetic fields, and so more power, could be produced by using <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnets</a> (field coils) on the stator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schellen_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schellen-5">[5]</a></sup> These were called "dynamo-electric machines" or dynamos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lockwood_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lockwood-4">[4]</a></sup> The field coils of the stator were originally <i>separately excited</i> by a separate, smaller, dynamo or magneto. An important development by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wilde_(engineer)" title="Henry Wilde (engineer)">Wilde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Siemens" title="Werner von Siemens">Siemens</a> was the discovery (by 1866) that a dynamo could also <a href="/wiki/Bootstrapping" title="Bootstrapping">bootstrap</a> itself to be <i>self-excited</i>, using current generated by the dynamo itself. This allowed the growth of a much more powerful field, thus far greater output power.
</p><p>Self-excited direct current dynamos commonly have a combination of series and parallel (shunt) field windings which are directly supplied power by the rotor through the commutator in a regenerative manner. They are started and operated in a manner similar to modern portable alternating current electric generators, which are not used with other generators on an electric grid.
</p><p>There is a weak residual magnetic field that persists in the metal frame of the device when it is not operating, which has been imprinted onto the metal by the field windings. The dynamo begins rotating while not connected to an external load. The residual magnetic field induces a very small electrical current into the rotor windings as they begin to rotate. Without an external load attached, this small current is then fully supplied to the field windings, which in combination with the residual field, cause the rotor to produce more current. In this manner the self-exciting dynamo <i>builds up</i> its internal magnetic fields until it reaches its normal operating voltage. When it is able to produce sufficient current to sustain both its internal fields and an external load, it is ready to be used.
</p><p>A self-excited dynamo with insufficient residual magnetic field in the metal frame will not be able to produce any current in the rotor, regardless of what speed the rotor spins. This situation can also occur in modern self-excited portable generators, and is resolved for both types of generators in a similar manner, by applying a brief direct current battery charge to the output terminals of the stopped generator. The battery energizes the windings just enough to imprint the residual field, to enable building up the current. This is referred to as <i>flashing the field</i>.
</p><p>Both types of self-excited generator, which have been attached to a large external load while it was stationary, will not be able to build up voltage even if the residual field is present. The load acts as an energy sink and continuously drains away the small rotor current produced by the residual field, preventing magnetic field buildup in the field coil.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Induction_with_permanent_magnets">Induction with permanent magnets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Induction with permanent magnets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Faraday_disk_generator.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="487" decoding="async" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/220px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/330px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Faraday_disk_generator.jpg/440px-Faraday_disk_generator.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Faraday_disk_generator.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faraday_disk" title="Faraday disk">Faraday disk</a> was the first electric generator. The horseshoe-shaped magnet <i>(A)</i> created a magnetic field through the disk <i>(D)</i>. When the disk was turned, this induced an electric current radially outward from the center toward the rim.  The current flowed out through the sliding spring contact <i>m</i> (connected to <i>B'</i>) through the external circuit, and back through <i>B</i> to the center of the disk through the axle.</div></div></div>
<p>The operating principle of electromagnetic generators was discovered in the years 1831–1832 by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>. The principle, later called <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction" title="Faraday's law of induction">Faraday's law</a>, is that an <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">electromotive force</a> is generated in an electrical conductor which encircles a varying <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a>.
</p><p>He also built the first electromagnetic generator, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faraday_disk" title="Faraday disk">Faraday disk</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Homopolar_generator" title="Homopolar generator">homopolar generator</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> disc rotating between the poles of a horseshoe <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a>.  It produced a small <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC voltage</a>. This was not a dynamo in the current sense, because it did not use a <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutator</a>.
</p><p>This design was inefficient, due to self-cancelling counterflows of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a> in regions of the disk that were not under the influence of the magnetic field. While current was induced directly underneath the magnet, the current would circulate backwards in regions that were outside the influence of the magnetic field. This counterflow limited the power output to the pickup wires, and induced waste heating of the copper disc. Later homopolar generators would solve this problem by using an array of magnets arranged around the disc perimeter to maintain a steady field effect in one current-flow direction.
</p><p>Another disadvantage was that the output <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> was very low, due to the single current path through the magnetic flux. Faraday and others found that higher, more useful voltages could be produced by winding multiple turns of wire into a coil. Wire windings can conveniently produce any voltage desired by changing the number of turns, so they have been a feature of all subsequent generator designs, requiring the invention of the commutator to produce direct current.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_dynamos">The first dynamos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The first dynamos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="958" data-file-width="603" decoding="async" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg/200px-Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg/300px-Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg/400px-Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wechselstromerzeuger_Crop_LevelAdj.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Pixii" title="Hippolyte Pixii">Hippolyte Pixii</a>'s dynamo. The commutator is located on the shaft below the spinning magnet.</div></div></div>
<p>The first dynamo based on Faraday's principles was built in 1832 by <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Pixii" title="Hippolyte Pixii">Hippolyte Pixii</a>, a French instrument maker. It used a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">permanent magnet</a> which was rotated by a crank. The spinning magnet was positioned so that its north and south poles passed by a piece of iron wrapped with insulated wire.
</p><p>Pixii found that the spinning magnet produced a pulse of current in the wire each time a pole passed the coil. However, the north and south poles of the magnet induced currents in opposite directions. To convert the alternating current to DC, Pixii invented a <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutator</a>, a split metal cylinder on the shaft, with two springy metal contacts that pressed against it.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="839" data-file-width="1295" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg/220px-Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg/330px-Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg/440px-Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pacinotti_dynamo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Pacinotti" title="Antonio Pacinotti">Pacinotti</a> dynamo, 1860</div></div></div>
<p>This early design had a problem: the electric current it produced consisted of a series of "spikes" or pulses of current separated by none at all, resulting in a low average power output. As with electric motors of the period, the designers did not fully realize the seriously detrimental effects of large air gaps in the magnetic circuit.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Pacinotti" title="Antonio Pacinotti">Antonio Pacinotti</a>, an Italian physics professor, solved this problem around 1860 by replacing the spinning two-pole <a href="/wiki/Axial_symmetry" title="Axial symmetry">axial</a> coil with a multi-pole <a href="/wiki/Toroid" title="Toroid">toroidal</a> one, which he created by wrapping an iron ring with a continuous winding, connected to the commutator at many equally spaced points around the ring; the commutator being divided into many segments. This meant that some part of the coil was continually passing by the magnets, smoothing out the current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Woolrich_Electrical_Generator" title="Woolrich Electrical Generator">Woolrich Electrical Generator</a> of 1844, now in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thinktank,_Birmingham" title="Thinktank, Birmingham">Thinktank, Birmingham Science Museum</a>, is the earliest electrical generator used in an industrial process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was used by the firm of <a href="/wiki/Elkington_Silver_Electroplating_Works" title="Elkington Silver Electroplating Works">Elkingtons</a> for commercial <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thomas_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-thomas-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044(1).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg/220px-Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg/330px-Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg/440px-Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044%281%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thinktank_Birmingham_-_object_1889S00044(1).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Woolrich_Electrical_Generator" title="Woolrich Electrical Generator">Woolrich Electrical Generator</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thinktank,_Birmingham" title="Thinktank, Birmingham">Thinktank, Birmingham</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamo_self_excitation">Dynamo self excitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamo self excitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Independently of Faraday, the Hungarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anyos_Jedlik" title="Anyos Jedlik">Anyos Jedlik</a> started experimenting in 1827 with the electromagnetic rotating devices which he called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jedlik%27s_dynamo" title="Jedlik's dynamo">electromagnetic self-rotors</a>. In the prototype of the single-pole electric starter, both the stationary and the revolving parts were electromagnetic.
</p><p>About 1856 he formulated the concept of the dynamo about six years before <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Siemens" title="Werner von Siemens">Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wheatstone" title="Charles Wheatstone">Wheatstone</a> but did not patent it as he thought he was not the first to realize this. His dynamo used, instead of permanent magnets, two electromagnets placed opposite to each other to induce the magnetic field around the rotor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon,_1998_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simon,_1998-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was also the discovery of the principle of dynamo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-excitation" title="Self-excitation">self-excitation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which replaced permanent magnet designs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_designs">Practical designs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Practical designs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1953" data-file-width="2439" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg/300px-High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg/450px-High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg/600px-High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:High-Current_Copper-Brush_Commutated_Dynamo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This large belt-driven high-current dynamo from around 1917 produced 310 amperes at 7 volts DC.  The huge complicated <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutator</a> <i>(left)</i> was needed to handle the large current.  Dynamos are no longer used due to the size and complexity of commutators needed for high power applications.</div></div></div>
<p>The dynamo was the first electrical generator capable of delivering power for industry. The modern dynamo, fit for use in industrial applications, was invented independently by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Charles_Wheatstone" title="Sir Charles Wheatstone">Sir Charles Wheatstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Siemens" title="Werner von Siemens">Werner von Siemens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Alfred_Varley" title="Samuel Alfred Varley">Samuel Alfred Varley</a>. Varley took out a patent on 24 December 1866, while Siemens and Wheatstone both announced their discoveries on 17 January 1867, the latter delivering a paper on his discovery to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>.
</p><p>The "dynamo-electric machine" employed self-powering electromagnetic field coils rather than permanent magnets to create the stator field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Wheatstone's design was similar to Siemens', with the difference that in the Siemens design the stator electromagnets were in series with the rotor, but in Wheatstone's design they were in parallel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The use of electromagnets rather than permanent magnets greatly increased the power output of a dynamo and enabled high power generation for the first time. This invention led directly to the first major industrial uses of electricity. For example, in the 1870s Siemens used electromagnetic dynamos to power <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnaces</a> for the production of metals and other materials.
</p><p>The dynamo machine that was developed consisted of a stationary structure, which provides the magnetic field, and a set of rotating windings which turn within that field. On larger machines the constant magnetic field is provided by one or more electromagnets, which are usually called field coils.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gramme_dynamo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="658" decoding="async" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Gramme_dynamo.png/220px-Gramme_dynamo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Gramme_dynamo.png/330px-Gramme_dynamo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Gramme_dynamo.png/440px-Gramme_dynamo.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gramme_dynamo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gramme_dynamo" title="Gramme dynamo">Gramme dynamo</a>, around 1878.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Z%C3%A9nobe_Gramme" title="Zénobe Gramme">Zénobe Gramme</a> reinvented Pacinotti's design in 1871 when designing the first commercial power plants operated in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. An advantage of Gramme's design was a better path for the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a>, by filling the space occupied by the magnetic field with heavy iron cores and minimizing the air gaps between the stationary and rotating parts. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gramme_dynamo" title="Gramme dynamo">Gramme dynamo</a> was one of the first machines to generate commercial quantities of power for industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Further improvements were made on the Gramme ring, but the basic concept of a spinning endless loop of wire remains at the heart of all modern dynamos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Brush" title="Charles F. Brush">Charles F. Brush</a> assembled his first dynamo in the summer of 1876 using a horse-drawn <a href="/wiki/Treadmill" title="Treadmill">treadmill</a> to power it. Brush's design modified the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gramme_dynamo" title="Gramme dynamo">Gramme dynamo</a> by shaping the ring armature like a disc rather than a cylinder shape. The field electromagnets were also positioned on the sides of the armature disc rather than around the circumference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotary_converters">Rotary converters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rotary converters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After dynamos and motors were found to allow easy conversion back and forth between mechanical or electrical power, they were combined in devices called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_converters" title="Rotary converters">rotary converters</a>, rotating machines whose purpose was not to provide mechanical power to loads but to convert one type of electric current into another, for example <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> into <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a>. They were multi-field single-rotor devices with two or more sets of rotating contacts (either commutators or sliprings, as required), one to provide power to one set of armature windings to turn the device, and one or more attached to other windings to produce the output current.
</p><p>The rotary converter can directly convert, internally, any type of electric power into any other. This includes converting between direct current (DC) and alternating current (AC), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_phase" title="Three phase">three phase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Single-phase_electric_power" title="Single-phase electric power">single phase</a> power, 25 Hz AC and 60 Hz AC, or many different output voltages at the same time. The size and mass of the rotor was made large so that the rotor would act as a <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a> to help smooth out any sudden surges or dropouts in the applied power.
</p><p>The technology of rotary converters was replaced in the early 20th century by <a href="/wiki/Mercury-arc_valve" title="Mercury-arc valve">mercury-vapor rectifiers</a>, which were smaller, did not produce vibration and noise, and required less maintenance. The same conversion tasks are now performed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_(electronics)" title="Solid state (electronics)">solid state</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_semiconductor_device" title="Power semiconductor device">power semiconductor devices</a>.  Rotary converters remained in use in the West Side <a href="/wiki/Interborough_Rapid_Transit_Company" title="Interborough Rapid Transit Company">IRT subway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> into the late 1960s, and possibly some years later. They were powered by 25 Hz AC, and provided DC at 600 volts for the trains.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_uses">Historical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_power_generation">Electric power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electric power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dynamos, usually driven by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_engines" title="Steam engines">steam engines</a>, were widely used in <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> to generate electricity for industrial and domestic purposes. They have since been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternators</a>.
</p><p>Large industrial dynamos with series and parallel (shunt) windings can be difficult to use together in a power plant, unless either the rotor or field wiring or the mechanical drive systems are coupled together in certain special combinations.  It seems theoretically possible to run dynamos in parallel to create induction and self sustaining system for electrical power.  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dynamos were used in motor vehicles to generate electricity for battery charging. An early type was the <a href="/wiki/Third-brush_dynamo" title="Third-brush dynamo">third-brush dynamo</a>. They have, again, been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Alternator_(automotive)" title="Alternator (automotive)">alternators</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_uses">Modern uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dynamos still have some uses in low power applications, particularly where low voltage <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> is required, since an <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> <a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">rectifier</a> can be inefficient in these applications.
</p><p>Hand <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">cranked</a> dynamos are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clockwork_radio" title="Clockwork radio">clockwork radios</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanically-powered_flashlight" title="Mechanically-powered flashlight">hand powered flashlights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> chargers, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-powered_equipment" title="Self-powered equipment">human powered equipment</a> to recharge <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">batteries</a>.
</p>
