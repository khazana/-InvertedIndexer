<p><b>Samuel de Champlain</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[samyɛl də ʃɑ̃plɛ̃]</a></span> born <b>Samuel Champlain</b>; on August 13, 1567<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birth_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-birth-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birth2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-birth2-3">[Note 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[Note 2]</a></sup> – December 25, 1635), known as "The Father of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>", was a French settler, navigator, cartographer, draftsman, soldier, explorer, geographer, ethnologist, diplomat, and chronicler. He made from 21-29 trips across the Atlantic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup>, and founded New France and <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a> on July 3, 1608. He is an important figure in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_history" title="Canadian history">Canadian history</a> due to his creation of the first accurate coastal map and the role he played in the foundation of the settlements.
</p><p>Born into a family of mariners, Champlain, while still a young boy, began exploring <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in 1603 under the guidance of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Grav%C3%A9_Du_Pont" title="François Gravé Du Pont">François Gravé Du Pont</a>, his uncle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davignon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davignon-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> From 1604 to 1607, Champlain participated in the exploration and settlement of the first permanent European settlement north of Florida, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a> (1605) as well as the first European settlement that would become <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> (1604). Then, in 1608, he established the French settlement that is now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quebec_City,_Canada" title="Quebec City, Canada">Quebec City, Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[Note 3]</a></sup> Champlain was the first European to explore and describe the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, and published maps of his journeys and accounts of what he learned from the natives and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" title="Métis people (Canada)">French living among the Natives</a>. He formed relationships with local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innu_people" title="Innu people">Montagnais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innu" title="Innu">Innu</a> and later with others farther west (<a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River" title="Ottawa River">Ottawa River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nipissing" title="Lake Nipissing">Lake Nipissing</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Bay" title="Georgian Bay">Georgian Bay</a>), with <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_people" title="Algonquin people">Algonquin</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Wendat</a>, and agreed to provide assistance in the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">Beaver Wars</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a>.
</p><p>In 1620, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII of France</a> ordered Champlain to cease exploration, return to Quebec, and devote himself to the administration of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[Note 4]</a></sup> In every way but formal title, Samuel <i>de</i> Champlain served as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_France" title="Governor of New France">Governor of New France</a>, a title that may have been formally unavailable to him owing to his non-noble status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honorifics_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-honorifics-10">[Note 5]</a></sup> He established trading companies that sent goods, primarily fur, to France, and oversaw the growth of New France in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> valley until his death in 1635.
</p><p>Champlain is memorialized as the "Father of New France" and "Father of <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a>", and many places, streets, and structures in northeastern North America bear his name, or have monuments established in his memory. The most notable of these is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a>, which straddles the border between northern <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>, extending slightly across the border into <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. In 1609 he led an expedition up the <a href="/wiki/Richelieu_River" title="Richelieu River">Richelieu River</a> and explored a long, narrow lake situated between the <a href="/wiki/Green_Mountains" title="Green Mountains">Green Mountains</a> of present-day <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Mountains" title="Adirondack Mountains">Adirondack Mountains</a> of present-day <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>; he named the lake after himself as the first European to map and describe it.
</p>

<h2><span id="Birth_year.2C_location_and_family"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_year,_location_and_family">Birth year, location and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth year, location and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samuel-de-champlain-s.jpg"><img alt="A half-length portrait of a man, set against a background that is a red curtain to the left and a landscape scene to the right. <p>Champlain was born to Antoine Champlain (also written "Anthoine Chappelain" in some records) and Marguerite Le Roy, in either <a href="/wiki/Hiers-Brouage" title="Hiers-Brouage">Hiers-Brouage</a>, or the port city of <a href="/wiki/La_Rochelle" title="La Rochelle">La Rochelle</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Aunis" title="Aunis">Aunis</a>. He was born on or before August 13, 1574, according to a recent baptism record found by Jean-Marie Germe, French genealogist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birth_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-birth-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birth2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-birth2-3">[Note 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Germe_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Germe-12">[7]</a></sup> Although in 1870, the Canadian Catholic priest Laverdière, in the first chapter of his <i>Œuvres de Champlain</i>, accepted Pierre-Damien Rainguet's<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[8]</a></sup> estimate and tried to justify it, his calculations were based on assumptions now believed, or proven, to be incorrect. Although Léopold Delayant (member, secretary, then president of <i>l'Académie des belles-lettres, sciences et arts de La Rochelle</i>) wrote as early as 1867 that Rainguet's estimate was wrong, the books of Rainguet and Laverdière have had a significant influence. The 1567 date was carved on numerous monuments dedicated to Champlain and is widely regarded as accurate. In the first half of the 20th century, some authors disagreed, choosing 1570 or 1575 instead of 1567. In 1978 Jean Liebel published groundbreaking research about these estimates of Champlain's birth year and concluded, "Samuel Champlain was born about 1580 in Brouage, France."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[9]</a></sup> Liebel asserts that some authors, including the Catholic priests Rainguet and Laverdière, preferred years when Brouage was under Catholic control (which include 1567, 1570, and 1575).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[10]</a></sup>
Champlain claimed to be from Brouage in the title of his 1603 book and to be <i>Saintongeois</i> in the title of his second book (1613). He belonged to either a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> family, or a tolerant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> one, since Brouage was most of the time a Catholic city in a Protestant region, and his <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> first name (<a href="/wiki/Samuel" title="Samuel">Samuel</a>) was not usually given to Catholic children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laberge_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laberge-16">[Note 6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richelieu_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richelieu-17">[Note 7]</a></sup> The exact location of his birth is thus also not known with certainty, but at the time of his birth his parents were living in <a href="/wiki/Hiers-Brouage" title="Hiers-Brouage">Brouage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[Note 8]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="854" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg/220px-ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg/330px-ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg/440px-ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChamplianStoneDingleTowerHalifaxNovaScotia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sir_Sandford_Fleming_Park" title="Sir Sandford Fleming Park">Sir Sandford Fleming Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a> – Stone from Samuel de Champlain's birthplace in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brouage" title="Brouage">Brouage</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (1574)</div></div></div>
<p>Born into a family of mariners (both his father and uncle-in-law were sailors, or navigators), Samuel Champlain learned to navigate, draw, make <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical charts</a>, and write practical reports. His education did not include <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, so he did not read or learn from any ancient literature. As each French fleet had to assure its own defense at sea, Champlain sought to learn to fight with the firearms of his time: he acquired this practical knowledge when serving with the army of <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">King Henry IV</a> during the later stages of <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">France's religious wars</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> from 1594 or 1595 to 1598, beginning as a quartermaster responsible for the feeding and care of horses. During this time he claimed to go on a "certain secret voyage" for the king,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[11]</a></sup> and saw combat (including maybe the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_Crozon" title="Siege of Fort Crozon">Siege of Fort Crozon</a>, at the end of 1594).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F65_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-F65-20">[12]</a></sup> By 1597 he was a "capitaine d'une compagnie" serving in a garrison near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quimper,_Finist%C3%A8re" title="Quimper, Finistère">Quimper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F65_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-F65-20">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_travels">Early travels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early travels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG/220px-SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG/330px-SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG/440px-SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SamuelDeChamplainStatueILMVT.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Champlain and guide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[13]</a></sup> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_La_Motte,_Vermont" title="Isle La Motte, Vermont">Isle La Motte, Vermont</a>, at the site Champlain is said to have first set foot in <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> (and encamped) in 1609. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> is in the background. <small>(Sculptor E.L.Weber, 1967; Photo by Matt Wills, 2009)</small></div></div></div>
<p>In 1598, his uncle-in-law, a navigator whose ship <i>Saint-Julien</i> was chartered to transport Spanish troops to <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">Cádiz</a> pursuant to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Vervins" title="Treaty of Vervins">Treaty of Vervins</a>, gave Champlain the opportunity to accompany him. After a difficult passage, he spent some time in Cadiz before his uncle, whose ship was then chartered to accompany a large Spanish fleet to the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, again offered him a place on the ship. His uncle, who gave command of the ship to Jeronimo de Valaebrera, instructed the young Champlain to watch over the ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[14]</a></sup> This journey lasted two years, and gave Champlain the opportunity to see or hear about Spanish holdings from the Caribbean to <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. Along the way he took detailed notes, and wrote an illustrated report on what he learned on this trip, and gave this secret report to King Henry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[Note 9]</a></sup> who rewarded Champlain with an annual pension. This report was published for the first time in 1870, by Laverdière, as <i>Brief Discours des Choses plus remarquables que Sammuel Champlain de Brouage a reconneues aux Indes Occidentalles au voiage qu'il en a faict en icettes en l'année 1599 et en l'année 1601, comme ensuite</i> (and in English as <i>Narrative of a Voyage to the West Indies and Mexico 1599–1602</i>). The authenticity of this account as a work written by Champlain has frequently been questioned, due to inaccuracies and discrepancies with other sources on a number of points; however, recent scholarship indicates that the work probably was authored by Champlain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[Note 10]</a></sup>
</p><p>On Champlain's return to Cadiz in August 1600, his uncle, who had fallen ill, asked him to look after his business affairs. This Champlain did, and when his uncle died in June 1601, Champlain inherited his substantial estate. It included an estate near <a href="/wiki/La_Rochelle" title="La Rochelle">La Rochelle</a>, commercial properties in Spain, and a 150-ton merchant ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[15]</a></sup> This inheritance, combined with the king's annual pension, gave the young explorer a great deal of independence, as he was not dependent on the financial backing of merchants and other investors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[16]</a></sup> From 1601 to 1603 Champlain served as a geographer in the court of King Henry IV. As part of his duties he traveled to French ports and learned much about <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> from the fishermen that seasonally traveled to coastal areas from <a href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a> to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> to capitalize on the rich fishing grounds there. He also made a study of previous French failures at colonization in the area, including that of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Chauvin_de_Tonnetuit" title="Pierre de Chauvin de Tonnetuit">Pierre de Chauvin</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tadoussac" title="Tadoussac">Tadoussac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[17]</a></sup> When Chauvin forfeited his monopoly on fur trade in North America in 1602, responsibility for renewing the trade was given to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aymar_de_Chaste" title="Aymar de Chaste">Aymar de Chaste</a>. Champlain approached de Chaste about a position on the first voyage, which he received with the king's assent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Champlain's first trip to North America was as an observer on a fur-trading expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Grav%C3%A9_Du_Pont" title="François Gravé Du Pont">François Gravé Du Pont</a>. Du Pont was a navigator and merchant who had been a ship's captain on Chauvin's expedition, and with whom Champlain established a firm lifelong friendship. He educated Champlain about navigation in North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a>, and in dealing with the natives there (and in <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a> after).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davignon_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davignon-6">[4]</a></sup> The <i>Bonne-Renommée</i> (the <i>Good Fame</i>) arrived at Tadoussac on March 15, 1603. Champlain was anxious to see for himself all of the places that <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier" title="Jacques Cartier">Jacques Cartier</a> had seen and described about sixty years earlier, and wanted to go even further than Cartier, if possible. Champlain created a map of the Saint Lawrence on this trip and, after his return to France on September 20, published an account as <i>Des Sauvages: ou voyage de Samuel Champlain, de Brouages, faite en la France nouvelle l'an 1603</i> ("Concerning the Savages: or travels of Samuel Champlain of Brouages, made in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> in the year 1603").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[Note 11]</a></sup> Included in his account were meetings with <a href="/wiki/Begourat" title="Begourat">Begourat</a>, a chief of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innu_people" title="Innu people">Montagnais</a> at Tadoussac, in which positive relationships were established between the French and the many Montagnais gathered there, with some <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_people" title="Algonquin people">Algonquin</a> friends.
</p><p>Promising to King Henry to report on further discoveries, Champlain joined a second expedition to New France in the spring of 1604. This trip, once again an exploratory journey without women and children, lasted several years, and focused on areas south of the St. Lawrence River, in what later became known as <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a>. It was led by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Dugua,_Sieur_de_Mons" title="Pierre Dugua, Sieur de Mons">Pierre Dugua de Mons</a>, a noble and Protestant merchant who had been given a fur trading monopoly in New France by the king. Dugua asked Champlain to find a site for winter settlement. After exploring possible sites in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">Bay of Fundy</a>, Champlain selected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Croix_Island,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint Croix Island, New Brunswick">Saint Croix Island</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Croix_River_(Maine_%E2%80%93_New_Brunswick)" title="Saint Croix River (Maine – New Brunswick)">St. Croix River</a> as the site of the expedition's first winter settlement. After enduring a harsh winter on the island the settlement was relocated across the bay where they established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a>. Until 1607, Champlain used that site as his base, while he explored the Atlantic coast. Dugua was forced to leave the settlement for France in September 1605, because he learned that his monopoly was at risk. His monopoly was rescinded by the king in July 1607 under pressure from other merchants and proponents of free trade, leading to the abandonment of the settlement.
</p><p>In 1605 and 1606, Champlain explored the North American coast as far south as <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>, searching for sites for a permanent settlement. Minor skirmishes with the resident <a href="/wiki/Nauset" title="Nauset">Nausets</a> dissuaded him from the idea of establishing one near present-day <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Massachusetts" title="Chatham, Massachusetts">Chatham, Massachusetts</a>. He named the area Mallebar ("bad bar").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-map-31">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the spring of 1608, Dugua wanted Champlain to start a new French colony and fur trading center on the shores of the St. Lawrence. Dugua equipped, at his own expense, a fleet of three ships with workers, that left the French port of <a href="/wiki/Honfleur" title="Honfleur">Honfleur</a>. The main ship, called the <a href="/wiki/Don_de_Dieu_(ship)" title="Don de Dieu (ship)"><i>Don-de-Dieu</i></a> (French for the <i>Gift of God</i>), was commanded by Champlain. Another ship, the <i>Lévrier</i> (the <i>Hunt Dog</i>), was commanded by his friend Du Pont. The small group of male settlers arrived at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadoussac,_Quebec" title="Tadoussac, Quebec">Tadoussac</a> on the lower St. Lawrence in June. Because of the dangerous strength of the <a href="/wiki/Saguenay_River" title="Saguenay River">Saguenay River</a> ending there, they left the ships and continued up the "Big River" in small boats bringing the men and the materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ships_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ships-32">[Note 12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>On July 3, 1608, Champlain landed at the "point of Quebec" and set about fortifying the area by the erection of three main wooden buildings, each two stories tall, that he collectively called the "<a href="/wiki/Habitation_de_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Habitation de Québec">Habitation</a>", with a wooden <a href="/wiki/Stockade" title="Stockade">stockade</a> and a moat 12 feet (4 m) wide surrounding them. This was the very beginning of <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a>. Gardening, exploring, and fortifying this place became great passions of Champlain for the rest of his life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In the 1620s, the <i>Habitation</i> at Quebec was mainly a store for the <i>Compagnie des Marchands</i> (Traders Company), and Champlain lived in the wooden <i>Fort Saint Louis</i> newly built up the hill (south from the present-day <i>Château Frontenac</i> Hotel), near the only two houses built by the two settler families (the ones of <a href="/wiki/Louis_H%C3%A9bert" title="Louis Hébert">Louis Hébert</a> and Guillaume Couillard, his son-in-law).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One route Champlain may have chosen to improve his access to the court of the regent was his decision to enter into marriage with the twelve-year-old <a href="/wiki/Helene_Boull%C3%A9" title="Helene Boullé">Hélène Boullé</a>. She was the daughter of Nicolas Boullé, a man charged with carrying out royal decisions at court. The marriage contract was signed on December 27, 1610 in presence of Dugua, who had dealt with the father, and the couple was married three days later. The terms of the contract called for the marriage to be consummated two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[21]</a></sup> Champlain sought permission from her parents to consummate the marriage before that: "Many of those who entered into such relationships, such as Samuel de Champlain (d. 1635), the first governor of French Canada, agreed that they would not have sex with a 12-year-old bride until she was 14, as Champlain did unless he consulted with her family and received their permission to do so earlier. Apparently, he did."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bullough_on_Champlain_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bullough_on_Champlain-34">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Champlain's marriage was initially quite troubled, as Hélène rallied against joining him in August 1613. Their relationship, while it apparently lacked any physical connection, recovered and was apparently good for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[23]</a></sup> Hélène lived in Quebec for several years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[24]</a></sup> but returned to Paris and eventually decided to enter a convent. The couple had no children, although Champlain did adopt three Montagnais girls named Faith, Hope, and Charity in the winter of 1627–28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_and_war_with_Native_Americans">Relations and war with Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relations and war with Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="459" data-file-width="746" decoding="async" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg/220px-DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg/330px-DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg/440px-DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DefeatOfIroquoisByChamplain.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engraving based on a drawing by Champlain of his 1609 voyage. It depicts a battle between <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algonquian_people" title="Algonquian people">Algonquian</a> tribes near Lake Champlain</div></div></div>
<p>During the summer of 1609, Champlain attempted to form better relations with the local <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">native tribes</a>. He made alliances with the <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Wendat</a> (derogatorily called <i>Huron</i> by the French) and with the <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_people" title="Algonquin people">Algonquin</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innu_people" title="Innu people">Montagnais</a> and the Etchemin, who lived in the area of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a>. These tribes demanded that Champlain help them in their war against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haudenosaunee" title="Haudenosaunee">Iroquois</a>, who lived farther south. Champlain set off with nine French soldiers and 300 natives to explore the <i>Rivière des Iroquois</i> (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Richelieu_River" title="Richelieu River">Richelieu River</a>), and became the first European to map <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a>. Having had no encounters with the Haudenosaunee at this point many of the men headed back, leaving Champlain with only 2 Frenchmen and 60 natives.
</p><p>On July 29, somewhere in the area near <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga,_New_York" title="Ticonderoga, New York">Ticonderoga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Point,_New_York" title="Crown Point, New York">Crown Point, New York</a> (historians are not sure which of these two places, but <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Fort Ticonderoga">Fort Ticonderoga</a> historians claim that it occurred near its site), Champlain and his party encountered a group of Haudenosaunee. In a battle that began the next day, two hundred and fifty Haudenosaunee advanced on Champlain's position, and one of his guides pointed out the three chiefs. In his account of the battle, Champlain recounts firing his <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebus</a> and killing two of them with a single shot, after which one of his men killed the third. The Haudenosaunee turned and fled. This action set the tone for <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">poor French-Iroquois relations</a> for the rest of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[Note 13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sorel" title="Battle of Sorel">Battle of Sorel</a> occurred on June 19, 1610, with Samuel de Champlain supported by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">Kingdom of France</a> and his allies, the <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Wendat people</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_people" title="Algonquin people">Algonquin people</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innu_people" title="Innu people">Innu people</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_people" title="Mohawk people">Mohawk people</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> at present-day <a href="/wiki/Sorel-Tracy" title="Sorel-Tracy">Sorel-Tracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>. Champlain's forces armed with the <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebus</a> engaged and slaughtered or captured nearly all of the Mohawks.  The battle ended major hostilities with the Mohawks for twenty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration_of_New_France">Exploration of New France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exploration of New France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="310" data-file-width="507" decoding="async" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG/220px-Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG/330px-Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG/440px-Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baie_des_Chaleurs_1612.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Chaleur_Bay" title="Chaleur Bay">Chaleur Bay</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence" title="Gulf of Saint Lawrence">Gulf of Saint Lawrence</a> — extract of Champlain 1612 map</div></div></div>
<p>On March 29, 1613, arriving back in New France, he first ensured that his new royal commission be <a href="/wiki/Proclamation" title="Proclamation">proclaimed</a>. Champlain set out on May 27 to continue his exploration of the Huron country and in hopes of finding the "northern sea" he had heard about (probably <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>). He traveled the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River" title="Ottawa River">Ottawa River</a>, later giving the first description of this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[Note 14]</a></sup> Along the way, he apparently dropped or left behind a cache of silver cups, copper kettles, and a brass astrolabe dated 1603 <a class="external text" href="https://www.historymuseum.ca/cmc/vmnf/champlain/expl5_en.shtml" rel="nofollow">(Champlain's Astrolabe)</a>, which was later found by a farm boy named Edward Lee near <a href="/wiki/Cobden,_Ontario" title="Cobden, Ontario">Cobden, Ontario</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[27]</a></sup> It was in June that he met with <a href="/wiki/Tessouat" title="Tessouat">Tessouat</a>, the Algonquin chief of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27Isle-aux-Allumettes,_Quebec" title="L'Isle-aux-Allumettes, Quebec">Allumettes Island</a>, and offered to build the tribe a fort if they were to move from the area they occupied, with its poor soil, to the locality of the Lachine Rapids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-map-31">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>By August 26, Champlain was back in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Malo" title="Saint-Malo">Saint-Malo</a>. There, he wrote an account of his life from 1604 to 1612 and his journey up the Ottawa river, his <i>Voyages</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[28]</a></sup> and published another map of New France. In 1614, he formed the "Compagnie des Marchands de Rouen et de Saint-Malo" and "Compagnie de Champlain", which bound the Rouen and Saint-Malo merchants for eleven years. He returned to New France in the spring of 1615 with four <a href="/wiki/Recollects" title="Recollects">Recollects</a> in order to further religious life in the new colony. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> was eventually given <i><a href="/wiki/Seigneurial_system_of_New_France" title="Seigneurial system of New France">en seigneurie</a></i> large and valuable tracts of land, estimated at nearly 30% of all the lands granted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_France" title="King of France">French Crown</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roydalton_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-roydalton-43">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Champlain continued to work to improve relations with the natives, promising to help them in their struggles against the Iroquois. With his native guides, he explored further up the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River" title="Ottawa River">Ottawa River</a> and reached <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nipissing" title="Lake Nipissing">Lake Nipissing</a>. He then followed the <a href="/wiki/French_River_(Ontario)" title="French River (Ontario)">French River</a> until he reached the freshwater sea he called Lac Attigouautau (now <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 1615, Champlain was escorted through the area that is now <a href="/wiki/Peterborough,_Ontario" title="Peterborough, Ontario">Peterborough, Ontario</a> by a group of Wendat. He used the ancient portage between <a href="/wiki/Chemong_Lake" title="Chemong Lake">Chemong Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Little_Lake_(Peterborough)" title="Little Lake (Peterborough)">Little Lake</a> (now Chemong Road) and stayed for a short period of time near what is now Bridgenorth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_expedition">Military expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Military expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Champlain_statue,_Nepean_Point,_Ottawa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2015" data-file-width="1657" decoding="async" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg/220px-Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg/330px-Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg/440px-Champlain_statue%2C_Nepean_Point%2C_Ottawa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Champlain_statue,_Nepean_Point,_Ottawa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Samuel de Champlain, <a href="/wiki/Nepean_Point" title="Nepean Point">Nepean Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_MacCarthy" title="Hamilton MacCarthy">Hamilton MacCarthy</a></div></div></div>
<p>On September 1, 1615, at Cahiagué (a Wendat community on what is now called <a href="/wiki/Lake_Simcoe" title="Lake Simcoe">Lake Simcoe</a>), he and the northern tribes started a military expedition against the Iroquois. The party passed <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a> at its eastern tip where they hid their canoes and continued their journey by land. They followed the <a href="/wiki/Oneida_River" title="Oneida River">Oneida River</a> until they arrived at the main Onondaga fort on October 10. The exact location of this place is still a matter of debate. Although the traditional location, Nichols Pond, is regularly disproved by professional and amateur archaeologists, many still claim that Nichols Pond is the location of the battle. 10 miles (16 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Canastota,_New_York" title="Canastota, New York">Canastota, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiskotten,_1998_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiskotten,_1998-45">[31]</a></sup> Champlain attacked the stockaded Oneida village. He was accompanied by 10 Frenchmen and 300  Wendat. Pressured by the Huron Wendat to attack prematurely, the assault failed. Champlain was wounded twice in the leg by arrows, one in his knee. The conflict ended on October 16 when the French Wendat were forced to flee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Although he did not want to, the Wendat insisted that Champlain spend the winter with them. During his stay, he set off with them in their great deer hunt, during which he became lost and was forced to wander for three days living off game and sleeping under trees until he met up with a band of First Nations people by chance. He spent the rest of the winter learning "their country, their manners, customs, modes of life". On May 22, 1616, he left the Wendat country and returned to Quebec before heading back to France on July 2.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improving_administration_in_New_France">Improving administration in New France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Improving administration in New France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1468" data-file-width="2491" decoding="async" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg/220px-Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg/330px-Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg/440px-Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Samuel_de_Champlain_Carte_geographique_de_la_Nouvelle_France.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> (Champlain, 1612). A more precise map was drawn by Champlain in 1632.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="626" data-file-width="573" decoding="async" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg/250px-Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg/375px-Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg/500px-Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Samuel_de_Champlain_by_Ronjat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>19th century artist's conception of Champlain by E. Ronjat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[32]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Champlain returned to New France in 1620 and was to spend the rest of his life focusing on administration of the territory rather than exploration. Champlain spent the winter building Fort Saint-Louis on top of Cape Diamond. By mid-May, he learned that the fur trading monopoly had been handed over to another company led by the Caen brothers. After some tense negotiations, it was decided to merge the two companies under the direction of the Caens. Champlain continued to work on relations with the natives and managed to impose on them a chief of his choice. He also negotiated a peace treaty with the Iroquois.
</p><p>Champlain continued to work on the fortifications of what became Quebec City, laying the first stone on May 6, 1624. On August 15 he once again returned to France where he was encouraged to continue his work as well as to continue looking for a passage to China, something widely believed to exist at the time. By July 5 he was back at Quebec and continued expanding the city.
</p><p>In 1627 the Caen brothers' company lost its monopoly on the fur trade, and <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Cardinal Richelieu">Cardinal Richelieu</a> (who had joined the Royal Council in 1624 and rose rapidly to a position of dominance in French politics that he would hold until his death in 1642) formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compagnie_des_Cent-Associ%C3%A9s" title="Compagnie des Cent-Associés">Compagnie des Cent-Associés</a> (the Hundred Associates) to manage the fur trade. Champlain was one of the 100 investors, and its first fleet, loaded with colonists and supplies, set sail in April 1628.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Champlain had overwintered in Quebec. Supplies were low, and English merchants pillaged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_Tourmente" title="Cap Tourmente">Cap Tourmente</a> in early July 1628.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anglo-French_War_(1627%E2%80%931629)" title="Anglo-French War (1627–1629)">A war</a> had broken out between France and England, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a> had issued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letters_of_marque" title="Letters of marque">letters of marque</a> that authorized the capture of French shipping and its colonies in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[35]</a></sup> Champlain received a summons to surrender on July 10 from some heavily armed, English based Scottish merchants, the <a href="/wiki/David_Kirke" title="David Kirke">Kirke brothers</a>. Champlain refused to deal with them, misleading them to believe that Quebec's defenses were better than they actually were (Champlain had only 50 pounds of gunpowder to defend the community). Successfully bluffed, they withdrew, but encountered and captured the French supply fleet, cutting off that year's supplies to the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[36]</a></sup> By the spring of 1629 supplies were dangerously low and Champlain was forced to send people to <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé</a> and into Indian communities to conserve rations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[37]</a></sup> On July 19, the Kirke brothers arrived before Quebec after intercepting Champlain's plea for help, and Champlain was forced to surrender the colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[38]</a></sup> Many colonists were taken first to England and then to France by the Kirkes, but Champlain remained in London to begin the process of regaining the colony. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Susa" title="Treaty of Susa">peace treaty had been signed</a> in April 1629, three months before the surrender, and, under the terms of that treaty, Quebec and other prizes were taken by the Kirkes after the treaty was supposed to be returned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[39]</a></sup> It was not until the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Germain-en-Laye_(1632)" title="Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1632)">1632 Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye</a> that Quebec was formally given back to France. (David Kirke was rewarded when Charles I knighted him and gave him a charter for <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.) Champlain reclaimed his role as commander of New France on behalf of Richelieu on March 1, 1633, having served in the intervening years as commander in New France "in the absence of my Lord the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armand_Jean_du_Plessis,_Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Armand Jean du Plessis, Cardinal Richelieu">Cardinal de Richelieu</a>" from 1629 to 1635.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trudel_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trudel-54">[40]</a></sup> In 1632 Champlain published <i>Voyages de la Nouvelle France</i>, which was dedicated to Cardinal Richelieu, and <i>Traitté de la marine et du devoir d'un bon marinier</i>, a treatise on leadership, seamanship, and navigation. (Champlain made more than twenty-five round-trip crossings of the Atlantic in his lifetime, without losing a single ship.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Last_return.2C_and_last_years_working_in_Quebec"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_return,_and_last_years_working_in_Quebec">Last return, and last years working in Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Last return, and last years working in Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Champlain returned to Quebec on May 22, 1633, after an absence of four years. Richelieu gave him a commission as <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General_of_New_France" title="Lieutenant General of New France">Lieutenant General of New France</a>, along with other titles and responsibilities, but not that of <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_France" title="Governor of New France">Governor</a>. Despite this lack of formal status, many colonists, French merchants, and Indians treated him as if he had the title; writings survive in which he is referred to as "our governor".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[42]</a></sup> On August 18, 1634, he sent a report to Richelieu stating that he had rebuilt on the ruins of Quebec, enlarged its fortifications, and established two more habitations. One was 15 leagues upstream, and the other was at <a href="/wiki/Trois-Rivi%C3%A8res" title="Trois-Rivières">Trois-Rivières</a>. He also began <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_and_Iroquois_Wars" title="French and Iroquois Wars">an offensive</a> against the Iroquois, reporting that he wanted them either wiped out or "brought to reason".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_burial">Death and burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_de_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death and burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Champlain had a severe <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> in October 1635, and died on December 25, leaving no immediate heirs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> records state he died in the care of his friend and confessor <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lallemant" title="Charles Lallemant">Charles Lallemant</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Although his will (drafted in November 17, 1635) gave much of his French property to his wife Hélène, he made significant bequests to the Catholic missions and to individuals in the colony of Quebec. However, Marie Camaret, a cousin on his mother's side, challenged the will in Paris and had it successfully overturned. It is unclear exactly what happened to his estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samuel de Champlain was temporarily buried in the church while a standalone chapel was built to hold his remains in the upper part of the city. Unfortunately, this small building, along with many others, was destroyed by a large fire in 1640. Though immediately rebuilt, no traces of it exist anymore: his exact burial site is still unknown, despite much research since about 1850, including several archaeological digs in the city. There is general agreement that the previous Champlain chapel site, and the remains of Champlain, should be somewhere near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Qu%C3%A9bec_Cathedral" title="Notre-Dame de Québec Cathedral">Notre-Dame de Québec Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>The search for Champlain's remains supplies a key plot-line in the crime writer <a href="/wiki/Louise_Penny" title="Louise Penny">Louise Penny</a>'s 2010 novel, <i>Bury Your Dead</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[48]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Many sites and landmarks have been named to honour Champlain, who remains, to this day, a prominent historical figure in many parts of <a href="/wiki/Acadia" title="Acadia">Acadia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>. They include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Champlain_Valley" title="Champlain Valley">Champlain Valley</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Champlain_Trail_Lakes" title="Champlain Trail Lakes">Champlain Trail Lakes</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Champlain_Sea" title="Champlain Sea">Champlain Sea</a>: a past inlet of the Atlantic Ocean in North America, over the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">St. Lawrence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Saguenay_River" title="Saguenay River">Saguenay</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Richelieu_River" title="Richelieu River">Richelieu</a> rivers, to over <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a>, which inlet disappeared many thousands years before Champlain was born.</li>
<li>Champlain Mountain, <a href="/wiki/Acadia_National_Park" title="Acadia National Park">Acadia National Park</a> – which he first observed in 1604.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champlain_(town),_New_York" title="Champlain (town), New York">town</a> and <a href="/wiki/Champlain_(village),_New_York" title="Champlain (village), New York">village</a> in New York, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Champlain,_Ontario" title="Champlain, Ontario">township in Ontario</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Champlain,_Quebec" title="Champlain, Quebec">municipality in Quebec</a>.</li>
<li>The provincial electoral district of <a href="/wiki/Champlain_(provincial_electoral_district)" title="Champlain (provincial electoral district)">Champlain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, and several defunct electoral districts elsewhere in Canada.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain_Provincial_Park" title="Samuel de Champlain Provincial Park">Samuel de Champlain Provincial Park</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Provincial_park" title="Provincial park">provincial park</a> in northern Ontario near the town of <a href="/wiki/Mattawa,_Ontario" title="Mattawa, Ontario">Mattawa</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champlain_Bridge_(Montreal)" title="Champlain Bridge (Montreal)">Champlain Bridge</a>, which connects the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Montreal" title="Island of Montreal">island of Montreal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Brossard" title="Brossard">Brossard</a>, Quebec across the St. Lawrence.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Champlain_Bridge_(Ottawa)" title="Champlain Bridge (Ottawa)">Champlain Bridge</a>, which connects the cities of <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, Ontario and <a href="/wiki/Gatineau" title="Gatineau">Gatineau</a>, Quebec.</li>
<li>Champlain College, one of six colleges at <a href="/wiki/Trent_University" title="Trent University">Trent University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peterborough,_Ontario" title="Peterborough, Ontario">Peterborough, Ontario</a>, is named in his honour.</li>
<li>Fort Champlain, a <a href="/wiki/Dormitory" title="Dormitory">dormitory</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College_of_Canada" title="Royal Military College of Canada">Royal Military College of Canada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">Kingston, Ontario</a>; named in his honour in 1965, it houses the 10th cadet squadron.</li>
<li>A French school in <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a>; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89cole_Champlain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="École Champlain (page does not exist)">École Champlain</a>, an elementary school in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moncton,_New_Brunswick" title="Moncton, New Brunswick">Moncton, New Brunswick</a>; <a href="/wiki/Champlain_College" title="Champlain College">Champlain College</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Vermont" title="Burlington, Vermont">Burlington, Vermont</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Champlain_Regional_College" title="Champlain Regional College">Champlain Regional College</a>, a <a href="/wiki/CEGEP" title="CEGEP">CEGEP</a> with three campuses in Quebec.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_Champlain" title="Château Champlain">Marriott Château Champlain</a> hotel, in Montreal.</li>
<li>Streets named Champlain in numerous cities, including Quebec, <a href="/wiki/Shawinigan" title="Shawinigan">Shawinigan</a>, the city of <a href="/wiki/Dieppe,_New_Brunswick" title="Dieppe, New Brunswick">Dieppe</a> in the province of New Brunswick, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plattsburgh" title="Plattsburgh">Plattsburgh</a>, and no less than eleven communities in northwestern Vermont.</li>

<li>A statue in <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga,_New_York" title="Ticonderoga, New York">Ticonderoga, New York</a>, unveiled in 2009 to commemorate the 400th anniversary of Champlain's exploration of Lake Champlain.</li>
<li>A statue in <a href="/wiki/Orillia" title="Orillia">Orillia</a>, Ontario at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Couchiching_Beach_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Couchiching Beach Park (page does not exist)">Couchiching Beach Park</a> on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Couchiching" title="Lake Couchiching">Lake Couchiching</a>.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/HMCS_Champlain" title="HMCS Champlain">HMCS <i>Champlain</i></a>, a <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Forces_Naval_Reserve" title="Canadian Forces Naval Reserve">Canadian Forces Naval Reserve</a> division based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicoutimi,_Quebec" title="Chicoutimi, Quebec">Chicoutimi, Quebec</a> since activation in 1985.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champlain_Place" title="Champlain Place">Champlain Place</a>, a shopping centre located in <a href="/wiki/Dieppe,_New_Brunswick" title="Dieppe, New Brunswick">Dieppe, New Brunswick</a>, Canada.</li>
