<p><b>Erich Rudolf Alexander Regener</b> (12 November 1881 – 27 February 1955) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> known primarily for the design and construction of instruments to measure <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> intensity at various altitudes. He is also known for predicting a <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000279999999999999♠</span>2.8 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation" title="Cosmic background radiation">cosmic background radiation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> for the invention of the scintillation counter which contributed to the discovery of the structure of the atom, for his calculation of the charge of an electron and for his early work on atmospheric ozone. He is also credited with the first use of rockets for scientific research.
</p><p>Regener was born in <a href="/wiki/Wilczak" title="Wilczak">Schleusenau (Wilczak)</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bydgoszcz" title="Bydgoszcz">Bromberg (Bydgoszcz)</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Prussia" title="West Prussia">West Prussia</a>.  He studied physics from 1900 to 1905 at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a> under <a href="/wiki/Emil_Warburg" title="Emil Warburg">Emil Warburg</a> and from 1909 worked with <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rubens" title="Heinrich Rubens">Heinrich Rubens</a>. In 1911 he became professor of experimental physics and meteorology at the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_University_of_Berlin" title="Agricultural University of Berlin">Agricultural University of Berlin</a>. In 1920 he became the professor in experimental physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stuttgart" title="University of Stuttgart">University of Stuttgart</a> working alongside the theoretical physicist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Peter_Ewald" title="Paul Peter Ewald">Paul Peter Ewald</a>. During this time, he developed instruments to measure cosmic rays at various altitudes. Regener's leadership in this field is not always fully recognised as he was persecuted during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> era because his wife was of Jewish ancestry. It has been argued that the naming of terms such as <i>Pfotzer maximum</i> after his student <a href="/wiki/Georg_Pfotzer" title="Georg Pfotzer">Georg Pfotzer</a> is misleading as Regener was the principal scientist for this work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bruno_Rossi" title="Bruno Rossi">Bruno Rossi</a> wrote of this period that <i>"In the late 1920s and early 1930s the technique of self-recording electroscopes carried by balloons into the highest layers of the atmosphere or sunk to great depths under water was brought to an unprecedented degree of perfection by the German physicist Erich Regener and his group. To these scientists we owe some of the most accurate measurements ever made of cosmic-ray ionization as a function of altitude and depth."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> stated in 1931 that <i>"thanks to the fine experiments of Professor Millikan and the even more far-reaching experiments of Professor Regener, we have now got for the first time, a curve of absorption of these radiations in water which we may safely rely upon."</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1937 Regener was forced into "provisional retirement" by the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">National Socialists</a>.  He then founded the private "Research Laboratory for the Physics of the Stratosphere" in <a href="/wiki/Friedrichshafen" title="Friedrichshafen">Friedrichshafen</a> on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a>, which later became a part of the <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Wilhelm_Society" title="Kaiser Wilhelm Society">Kaiser Wilhelm Society</a>. In 1939 he was invited to work at the German Army-Air Force rocket research station in <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenemünde">Peenemünde</a> where he developed a spectrograph protected by a steel casing.  This instrument, later called the <i><a href="/wiki/Regener-Tonne" title="Regener-Tonne">Regener-Tonne</a></i> was the first scientific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> designed to reach high altitude. Regener overcame the problems of getting the parachute canopy to open at extreme high altitudes by inflating the parachute with compressed air. After a successful test flight the project was cancelled in September 1944 and the rockets were used as long-range missiles against <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>. The capsule disappeared after Peenemunde tests in 1944 but later resurfaced in the United States.
</p><p>In 1948 Regener was appointed the first vice president of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Society" title="Max Planck Society">Max Planck Society</a>. He was also cofounder of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Solar_System_Research" title="Max Planck Institute for Solar System Research">Max Planck Institute for Solar System Research</a> with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Dieminger" title="Walter Dieminger">Walter Dieminger</a> and was instrumental in attracting physicists back to post-war Germany.
</p><p>Regener was the father of Victor H. Regener <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070312060248/http://panda.unm.edu/operations/RegenerTribute/VictorHRegener_AMemoir-corrected%20version%20-%20Swinson.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> and Erika Regener. Erika Regener later married one of Erich Regener's students, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Daniel_Rathgeber" title="Henri Daniel Rathgeber">Henri Daniel Rathgeber</a>.
</p>
