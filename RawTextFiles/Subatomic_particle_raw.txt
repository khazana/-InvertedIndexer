<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical sciences</a>, <b>subatomic particles</b> are <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particles</a> much smaller than <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The two types of subatomic particles are: <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a>, which according to current theories are not made of other particles; and <i>composite</i> particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">Particle physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> study these particles and how they <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interact</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntroQM1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IntroQM1-3">[3]</a></sup>
The idea of a particle underwent serious rethinking when experiments showed that light could behave like a stream of particles (called <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>) as well as exhibiting wave-like properties. This led to the new concept of <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave–particle duality">wave–particle duality</a> to reflect that quantum-scale "particles" behave like both particles and waves (they are sometimes described as <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave–particle duality">wavicles</a> to reflect this). Another new concept, the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">uncertainty principle</a>, states that some of their properties taken together, such as their simultaneous <a href="/wiki/Position_(vector)" title="Position (vector)">position</a> and <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>, cannot be measured exactly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In more recent times, wave–particle duality has been shown to apply not only to photons but to increasingly massive particles as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Interactions of particles in the framework of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> are understood as creation and annihilation of <i><a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a></i> of corresponding <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a>. This blends particle physics with <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">field theory</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_statistics">By statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: By statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:394px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="375" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg/392px-Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg/588px-Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg/784px-Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg.png 2x" width="392"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> classification of particles</div></div></div>
<p>Any subatomic particle, like any particle in the three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> that obeys the laws of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, can be either a <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">boson</a> (with integer <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>) or a <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a> (with odd half-integer spin).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_composition">By composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The elementary particles of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntroSM1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IntroSM1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Six "<a href="/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics)" title="Flavour (particle physics)">flavors</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>: <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up</a>, <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottom_quark" title="Bottom quark">bottom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Top_quark" title="Top quark">top</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strange_quark" title="Strange quark">strange</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charm_quark" title="Charm quark">charm</a>;</li>
<li>Six types of <a href="/wiki/Lepton" title="Lepton">leptons</a>: <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">electron neutrino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muon_neutrino" title="Muon neutrino">muon neutrino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_(particle)" title="Tau (particle)">tau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_neutrino" title="Tau neutrino">tau neutrino</a>;</li>
<li>Twelve <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">gauge bosons</a> (force carriers): the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, the three <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons">W and Z bosons</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak force</a>, and the eight <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_force" title="Strong force">strong force</a>;</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Physics_beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Physics beyond the Standard Model">extensions of the Standard Model</a> predict the existence of an elementary <a href="/wiki/Graviton" title="Graviton">graviton</a> particle and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elementary_particles#Hypothetical_particles" title="List of elementary particles">many other elementary particles</a>.
</p><p>Composite subatomic particles (such as <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> or atomic <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bound states</a> of two or more <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a>. For example, a proton is made of two <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up quarks</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down quark</a>, while the atomic nucleus of <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a> is composed of two protons and two <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>. The neutron is made of two down quarks and one up quark. Composite particles include all <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>: these include <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaon" title="Kaon">kaons</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_mass">By mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: By mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence" title="Mass–energy equivalence">energy of a particle at rest equals its mass times the speed of light squared</a>, <span class="nowrap"><i>E</i> = <i>mc</i><sup>2</sup></span>. That is, <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> can be expressed in terms of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> and vice versa. If a particle has a <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> in which it lies <a href="/wiki/Rest_(physics)" title="Rest (physics)">at rest</a>, then it has a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> and is referred to as <i>massive</i>.
</p><p>All composite particles are massive. Baryons (meaning "heavy") tend to have greater mass than mesons (meaning "intermediate"), which in turn tend to be heavier than leptons (meaning "lightweight"), but the heaviest lepton (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tau_particle" title="Tau particle">tau particle</a>) is heavier than the two lightest flavours of baryons (<a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a>). It is also certain that any particle with an <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> is massive.
</p><p>All <a href="/wiki/Massless_particle" title="Massless particle">massless particles</a> (particles whose <a href="/wiki/Invariant_mass" title="Invariant mass">invariant mass</a> is zero) are elementary. These include the photon and gluon, although the latter cannot be isolated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_properties">Other properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Through the work of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose" title="Satyendra Nath Bose">Satyendra Nath Bose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie" title="Louis de Broglie">Louis de Broglie</a>, and many others, current scientific theory holds that <i>all</i> particles also have a wave nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This has been verified not only for elementary particles but also for compound particles like atoms and even molecules. In fact, according to traditional formulations of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, wave–particle duality applies to all objects, even macroscopic ones; although the wave properties of macroscopic objects cannot be detected due to their small wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Interactions between particles have been scrutinized for many centuries, and a few simple laws underpin how particles behave in collisions and interactions. The most fundamental of these are the laws of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">conservation of momentum</a>, which let us make calculations of particle interactions on scales of magnitude that range from stars to <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These are the prerequisite basics of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics" title="Newtonian mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>, a series of statements and equations in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophiae_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i>, originally published in 1687.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dividing_an_atom">Dividing an atom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dividing an atom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The negatively charged electron has a mass equal to ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>1837 or 1836</sub></span> of that of a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atom. The remainder of the hydrogen atom's mass comes from the positively charged <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> of an element is the number of protons in its nucleus. Neutrons are neutral particles having a mass slightly greater than that of the proton. Different <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of the same element contain the same number of protons but differing numbers of neutrons. The <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> of an isotope is the total number of <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a> (neutrons and protons collectively).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">Chemistry</a> concerns itself with how electron sharing binds atoms into structures such as crystals and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">Nuclear physics</a> deals with how protons and neutrons arrange themselves in nuclei. The study of subatomic particles, atoms and molecules, and their structure and interactions, requires <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. Analyzing processes that change the numbers and types of particles requires <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>. The study of subatomic particles <i>per se</i> is called <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>. The term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-energy_physics" title="High-energy physics">high-energy physics</a></i> is nearly synonymous to "particle physics" since creation of particles requires high energies: it occurs only as a result of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(particle_physics)" title="Phenomenology (particle physics)">Particle phenomenology</a> systematizes the knowledge about subatomic particles obtained from these experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subatomic_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_subatomic_physics" title="History of subatomic physics">History of subatomic physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_particle_discoveries" title="Timeline of particle discoveries">Timeline of particle discoveries</a></div>
<p>The term "<i>subatomic</i> particle" is largely a <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a> of the 1960s, used to distinguish a large number of <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> (which comprise <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>) from particles that are now thought to be <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">truly elementary</a>. Before that hadrons were usually classified as "elementary" because their composition was unknown.
