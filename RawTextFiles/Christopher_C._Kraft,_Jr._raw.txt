<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Christopher C. Kraft Jr.</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Christopher Columbus Kraft Jr.</b> (born February 28, 1924) is an American <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineer</a> and retired <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> engineer and manager who was instrumental in establishing the agency's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control" title="Mission Control">Mission Control</a> operation. Following his graduation from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Polytechnic_Institute_and_State_University" title="Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University">Virginia Tech</a> in 1944, Kraft was hired by the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> (NACA), the predecessor organization to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA). He worked for over a decade in aeronautical research before being asked in 1958 to join the <a href="/wiki/Space_Task_Group" title="Space Task Group">Space Task Group</a>, a small team entrusted with the responsibility of putting America's first man in space. Assigned to the flight operations division, Kraft became NASA's first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_Director" title="Flight Director">flight director</a>. He was on duty during such historic missions as America's <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_3" title="Mercury-Redstone 3">first manned spaceflight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_6" title="Mercury-Atlas 6">first manned orbital flight</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">first spacewalk</a>.
</p><p>At the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo program</a>, Kraft retired as a flight director to concentrate on management and mission planning. In 1972, he became director of the Manned Spacecraft Center (later <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a>), following in the footsteps of his mentor <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Gilruth" title="Robert R. Gilruth">Robert R. Gilruth</a>. He held the position until his 1982 retirement from NASA. During his retirement, Kraft has consulted for numerous companies including <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a>, and he published an autobiography entitled <i>Flight: My Life in Mission Control</i>.
</p><p>More than any other person, Kraft was responsible for shaping the organization and culture of NASA's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control" title="Mission Control">Mission Control</a>. As his protégé <a href="/wiki/Glynn_Lunney" title="Glynn Lunney">Glynn Lunney</a> commented, "the Control Center today ... is a reflection of Chris Kraft."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunney_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lunney-1">[1]</a></sup>  In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">Mission Control Center building</a> was named after him.  When Kraft received the National Space Trophy from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_Club" title="Rotary Club">Rotary Club</a> in 1999, the organization described him as "a driving force in the U.S. human space flight program from its beginnings to the Space Shuttle era, a man whose accomplishments have become legendary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruffner_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ruffner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Phoebus,_Virginia" title="Phoebus, Virginia">Phoebus, Virginia</a>, on February 28, 1924, Kraft was named after his father, Christopher Columbus Kraft, who was born in New York City in 1892 near the newly renamed <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Circle" title="Columbus Circle">Columbus Circle</a>. Kraft's father, the son of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> immigrants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, had found his name an embarrassment, but passed it along to his son nonetheless. In later years, Kraft—as well as other commentators—would consider it peculiarly appropriate. Kraft commented in his autobiography that, with the choice of his name, "some of my life's direction was settled from the start."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a boy, Kraft played in an <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a> drum-and-bugle corps and became the state champion bugler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was also a keen baseball player and continued to play baseball in college; one year he had a batting average of .340.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giant_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-giant-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1942, Kraft began his studies at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Polytechnic_Institute_and_State_University" title="Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University">Virginia Tech</a> and became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech_Corps_of_Cadets" title="Virginia Tech Corps of Cadets">Corps of Cadets</a>. During his freshman year, he attempted to enlist in the military as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> cadet but was rejected because of a burned right hand that he had suffered at age three. Because of wartime demands, Virginia Tech was operating on a twelve-month schedule, and Kraft finished his degree in only two years. He graduated in December 1944 with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_Engineering" title="Aeronautical Engineering">Aeronautical Engineering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NACA_career">NACA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NACA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On graduation, Kraft accepted a job with the <a href="/wiki/Vought" title="Vought">Chance Vought</a> aircraft company in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. He had also sent an application to the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> (NACA), a government agency whose <a href="/wiki/Langley_Research_Center" title="Langley Research Center">Langley Research Center</a> was located in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton, Virginia</a>; Kraft considered it to be too close to home, but applied as insurance. On arrival at Chance Vought he was told that he could not be hired without his birth certificate, which he had not brought with him. Annoyed by the bureaucratic mindset of the company, he decided to accept the offer from NACA instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1940s, NACA was a research and development organization, devoted to cutting-edge aeronautical research. At the Langley Research Center, advanced <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnels</a> were used to test new aircraft designs, and studies were taking place on new concepts such as the <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-1" title="Bell X-1">X-1</a> rocket plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Kraft was assigned to the flight research division, where <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Gilruth" title="Robert R. Gilruth">Gilruth</a> was then head of research. His work with NACA included the development of an early example of gust alleviation system for an aircraft flying in turbulent air.  This involved correcting for variations in the atmosphere by automatically deflecting the control surfaces. He also discovered that <a href="/wiki/Wingtip_vortices" title="Wingtip vortices">wingtip vortices</a>, and not prop-wash, are responsible for most of the <a href="/wiki/Wake_turbulence" title="Wake turbulence">turbulence</a> in the air that trails flying aircraft. This finding was forgotten and later rediscovered independently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-flight-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although he enjoyed his work, Kraft found it increasingly stressful, especially since he did not consider himself to be a strong theoretician. In 1956, he was diagnosed with an <a href="/wiki/Ulcer" title="Ulcer">ulcer</a> and started thinking about a change of career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_director">Flight director</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flight director">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_operations">Flight operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flight operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1957, the Russian flight of <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> prompted the United States to accelerate its fledgling space program. On July 29, 1958, President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> signed the <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Act" title="National Aeronautics and Space Act">National Aeronautics and Space Act</a>, which established NASA and subsumed NACA within this newly created organization. <a href="/wiki/Langley_Research_Center" title="Langley Research Center">Langley Research Center</a> became a part of NASA, as did Langley employees such as Kraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Even before NASA began its official existence in October, Kraft was invited by Gilruth to become a part of a new group that was working on the problems of putting a man into orbit. Without much hesitation, he accepted the offer. When the <a href="/wiki/Space_Task_Group" title="Space Task Group">Space Task Group</a> was officially formed on November 5, Kraft became one of the original thirty-five engineers to be assigned to <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>, America's man-in-space program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-flight-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a member of the Space Task Group, Kraft was assigned to the flight operations division, which made plans and arrangements for the operation of the Mercury spacecraft during flight and for the control and monitoring of missions from the ground. Kraft became the assistant to Chuck Mathews, the head of the division, and was given the responsibility of putting together a mission plan. Given Mathews' casual analysis of the problem, it almost sounded simple:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Chris, you come up with a basic mission plan. You know, the bottom-line stuff on how we fly a man from a launch pad into space and back again. It would be good if you kept him alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft68_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft68-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>However, when Kraft began to plan NASA's flight operations, no human being had yet flown in space. In fact, the task before him was vast, requiring attention to flight plans, timelines, procedures, mission rules, spacecraft tracking, <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a>, ground support, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> networks and contingency management.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Control.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2300" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mercury_Control.jpg/220px-Mercury_Control.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mercury_Control.jpg/330px-Mercury_Control.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mercury_Control.jpg/440px-Mercury_Control.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Control.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control" title="Mission Control">Mission Control</a> as it was during <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a></div></div></div>
<p>One of Kraft's most important contributions to manned spaceflight would be his origination of the concept of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control_Center" title="Mission Control Center">Mission Control Center</a>. Many of the engineers in Project Mercury had previously worked on the <a href="/wiki/Flight_test" title="Flight test">flight test</a> of aircraft, where the role for ground support was minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Kraft soon realized that an astronaut could only do so much, particularly during the fast-moving launch phase; the Mercury spacecraft would require real time monitoring and support from specialist engineers.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I saw a team of highly skilled engineers, each one an expert on a different piece of the Mercury capsule. We'd have a flow of accurate telemetry data so the experts could monitor their systems, see and even predict problems, and pass along instructions to the astronaut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft68_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft68-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>These concepts shaped the Mercury Control Center, which was located at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral</a> in Florida. Another important concept pioneered by Kraft was the idea of the flight director, the man who would coordinate the team of engineers and make real-time decisions about the conduct of the mission. As Mathews later recalled, Kraft came to him one day saying, "There needs to be someone in charge of the flights while they're actually going on, and I want to be that person."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In this informal way, the position of flight director was born.
</p><p>A pivotal experience for Kraft was the flight of <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_5" title="Mercury-Atlas 5">Mercury-Atlas 5</a>, which sent a <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzee</a> named <a href="/wiki/Enos_(chimpanzee)" title="Enos (chimpanzee)">Enos</a> on the first American orbital spaceflight carrying a live passenger. Coverage of these early missions that carried non-human passengers could often be tongue-in-cheek; a <i>Time Magazine</i> article on the flight, for example, was titled "Meditative Chimponaut".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chimponaut_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-chimponaut-15">[15]</a></sup> Yet Kraft viewed them as important tests for the men and procedures of Mission Control, and as rehearsals for the manned missions that would follow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Originally, the flight of Mercury-Atlas 5 had been intended to last for three orbits. However, the failure of one of the hydrogen peroxide jets controlling the spacecraft's attitude forced Kraft to make the decision to bring the capsule back to Earth after two orbits. After the flight, astronaut <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a> stated that he believed a human passenger would have been able to bring the capsule under control without the need for an early re-entry, thus (in the words of <i>Time Magazine</i>) "affirming the superiority of astronauts over chimponauts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chimponaut_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-chimponaut-15">[15]</a></sup> Yet for Kraft, the flight of Enos represented proof of the importance of real-time decision-making in Mission Control. It also gave him his first experience of the responsibility that he as flight director would have for the life of another, whether human or chimpanzee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury">Mercury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mercury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kraft served as flight director during all six of the manned Mercury missions. Only during the final flight—<a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_9" title="Mercury-Atlas 9">Mercury-Atlas 9</a>, which lasted for over a day—did he share responsibility with his deputy <a href="/wiki/John_Hodge_(engineer)" title="John Hodge (engineer)">John Hodge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christopher_Kraft,_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury,_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2216" data-file-width="2800" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg/220px-Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg/330px-Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg/440px-Christopher_Kraft%2C_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury%2C_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christopher_Kraft,_flight_director_during_Project_Mercury,_works_at_his_console_inside_the_Flight_Control_area_at_Mercury_Mission_Control.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kraft works at his console inside the Flight Control area of the Mercury Control Center.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_6" title="Mercury-Atlas 6">Mercury-Atlas 6</a>, the flight of <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a>, proved to be a testing experience both for Mission Control and for Kraft. One book on the history of the Apollo program calls it "the single event that decisively shaped Flight Operations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The mission, the first <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital</a> flight by an American, unfolded normally until Glenn began his second orbit. At that point Kraft's systems controller, Don Arabian, reported that telemetry was showing a "Segment 51" indicator. This suggested that the capsule's landing bag, which was meant to deploy upon splashdown in order to provide a cushion, might have deployed early. Kraft believed that the Segment 51 indicator was due to faulty instrumentation rather than to an actual early deployment. However, if he was wrong, it would mean that the capsule's heat shield, which fitted on top of the landing bag, was now loose. A loose heat shield could cause the capsule to burn up during re-entry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>On consulting with his flight controllers, Kraft became convinced that the indication was false, and that no action was needed. However, his superiors, including Mercury capsule designer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Faget" title="Max Faget">Max Faget</a>, felt differently. They overruled Kraft, telling him to instruct Glenn to leave the capsule's retrorocket package on during re-entry. The reasoning was that the package, which was strapped over the heatshield, would hold the heatshield in place if it was loose. Kraft, however, felt that this was an unacceptable risk. "I was aghast," he remembered. "If any of three retrorockets had solid fuel remaining, an explosion could rip everything apart."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Yet he agreed to follow the plan advocated by Faget and by Walt Williams, his superior in the flight operations division. The retrorockets would be kept on.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2256" data-file-width="1776" decoding="async" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg/220px-Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg/330px-Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg/440px-Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chris_Kraft_and_Walt_Williams.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chris Kraft (seated) confers with Walt Williams and others during <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_9" title="Mercury-Atlas 9">Mercury-Atlas 9</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Glenn landed safely, but an inspection of his capsule revealed that one of the landing bag switches had been faulty. Kraft was right; the heat shield had not been loose after all. The lessons that he drew from this experience were clear.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>My flight controllers and I were a lot closer to the systems and to events than anyone in top management. From now on, I swore, they'd pay hell before they overruled any decision I made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft161_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft161-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>His assistant on the mission, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kranz" title="Gene Kranz">Gene Kranz</a>, considered Glenn's flight "the turning point ... in Kraft's evolution as a flight director."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the flight of <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_7" title="Mercury-Atlas 7">Mercury-Atlas 7</a>, Kraft had objected to the choice of <a href="/wiki/Scott_Carpenter" title="Scott Carpenter">Scott Carpenter</a> as the astronaut for the mission, telling Walt Williams that Carpenter's lack of engineering skills might put the mission or his own life in danger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The mission suffered from problems including an unusually high rate of fuel usage, a malfunctioning horizon indicator, a delayed retrofire for re-entry, and a splashdown that was 250 nautical miles (460 km) downrange from the target area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Throughout the mission, Kraft found himself frustrated by the vagueness of Carpenter's communications with Mission Control, and what he perceived as Carpenter's inattention to his duties. "Part of the problem," he recalled, "was that Carpenter either didn't understand or was ignoring my instructions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>While some of these problems were due to mechanical failures, and responsibility for some of the others is still being debated, Kraft did not hesitate to assign blame to Carpenter, and continued to speak out about the mission for decades afterwards. His autobiography, written in 2001, reopened the issue; the chapter that dealt with the flight of Mercury-Atlas 7 was titled "The Man Malfunctioned." In a letter to <i>The New York Times</i>, Carpenter called the book "vindictive and skewed," and offered a different assessment of the reasons for Kraft's frustration: "in space things happen so fast that only the pilot knows what to do, and even ground control can't help. Maybe that's why he is still fuming after all these years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of the Mercury program, Kraft was invited to attend a ceremony in the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Rose_Garden" title="White House Rose Garden">White House Rose Garden</a>, where he received the NASA Outstanding Leadership Medal. It was awarded by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James Webb</a>. "None of us have many days in our lives like that one," Kraft remembered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gemini">Gemini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gemini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini program</a>, Kraft's role changed again. He was now the head of mission operations, in charge of a team of flight directors, although still also serving as a flight director himself. Due to the greater length of Gemini missions, Mission Control was now manned on a three-shift basis. "Clearly, with flight control facing a learning curve," space historian David Harland has said, "these arrangements were an experiment in their own right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Yet Kraft proved to be remarkably successful at passing on responsibility to his fellow flight directors—arguably too successful, as Gene Kranz found during his first shift handover on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">Gemini 4</a>. As Kranz recalled, "He just said, 'You're in charge,' and walked out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apollo284_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apollo284-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg/220px-Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg/330px-Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg/440px-Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kranz_on_Console_-_GPN-2000-001406.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>During <a href="/wiki/Gemini_5" title="Gemini 5">Gemini 5</a>, Kraft (seated at center of console) confers with flight controllers and astronauts. Malfunctioning fuel cells nearly forced the early end of the mission.</div></div></div>
<p>The Gemini program represented a string of firsts for NASA—the first flight with two astronauts, the first rendezvous in space, the first spacewalk—and Kraft was on duty during many of these historic events. America's first spacewalk happened during the Gemini 4 mission; Kraft, at his console, found that he had to force himself to concentrate on his work, distracted by <a href="/wiki/Ed_White_(astronaut)" title="Ed White (astronaut)">Ed White</a>'s "mesmerizing" descriptions of the Earth below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He could easily understand the euphoria that White felt at the spectacle, yet he also was mindful of the discipline required to keep the flight safe. White delayed his return to the capsule, and a communication problem prevented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capsule_communicator" title="Capsule communicator">capsule communicator</a> <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a> from getting the crew to hear the order to terminate the spacewalk. When contact was finally re-established, Kraft expressed his frustration on his ground loop to Grissom:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Flight Director says "Get back in!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a>, Kraft stepped back from his work at Mission Control, allowing other flight directors to take charge of the remaining missions so that he could devote more time to planning for the Apollo program. He served on two review boards at <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aviation" title="North American Aviation">North American Aviation</a>, the contractor responsible for the Apollo capsule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Yet Kraft still felt pangs at not being at the center of the action, particularly after the emergency re-entry of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_8" title="Gemini 8">Gemini 8</a>. Both astronauts and mission controllers had made the right decisions, but, as Kraft confessed to Robert Gilruth, he found himself wishing that he had been the one on the spot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_1_fire">Apollo 1 fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Apollo 1 fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo program</a>, Kraft expected to return to his role in Mission Control. He would have been lead flight director on the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">first manned Apollo mission</a> (later known as Apollo 1), which was scheduled to launch in early 1967. However, on January 27, 1967, the three crew members were killed in a fire during a countdown test on the pad. At the time of the fire, Kraft was in Mission Control, but under the circumstances there was little he could do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> He was asked by Betty Grissom, the widow of astronaut <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Gus Grissom</a>, to be one of the pallbearers at Grissom's funeral at <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_profile">Public profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Public profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the sixties, Kraft was a household name in America. He appeared on the cover of the August 27, 1965 issue of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a></i>, in which he was profiled as the "Conductor in a Command Post". In his interview with <i>Time</i>, Kraft compared himself with namesake <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, displaying what the magazine described as "an almost angry pride" in his work. "We know a lot more about what we have to do than he did," Kraft said. "And we know where we're going."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conductor_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-conductor-37">[37]</a></sup> The article described Kraft's role in the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_5" title="Gemini 5">Gemini 5</a> mission, and drew on Kraft's frequent comparisons of his position as flight director with that of an orchestra conductor.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The conductor can't play all the instruments--he may not even be able to play any one of them. But he knows when the first violin should be playing, and he knows when the trumpets should be loud or soft, and when the drummer should be drumming. He mixes all this up and out comes music. That's what we do here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conductor_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-conductor-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Kraft had originally been surprised at <i>Time Magazine</i>'s decision to put him on the cover, telling the NASA public affairs officer that "they've got the wrong guy. It should be <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Gilruth" title="Robert R. Gilruth">Bob Gilruth</a> ... not me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> However, he eventually came to terms with the idea, and the portrait that was painted for the cover became one of his prized possessions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_astronaut_corps">Relations with astronaut corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relations with astronaut corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After John Glenn's flight, Kraft had vowed that he would no longer allow his decisions as flight director to be overruled by anyone outside Mission Control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft161_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft161-22">[22]</a></sup> The mission rules, whose drafting had been overseen by Kraft, stated that "the flight director may, after analysis of the flight, choose to take any necessary action required for the successful completion of the mission."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> For Kraft, the power that the flight director held over every aspect of the mission extended to his control over the actions of the astronauts. In his 1965 interview with <i>Time Magazine</i>, he stated:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[T]he guy on the ground ultimately controls the mission. There's no question about that in my mind or in the astronauts' minds. They are going to do what he says.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conductor_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-conductor-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Occasionally, Kraft intervened in order to ensure that his conception of the flight director's authority was maintained. By the time that the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_7" title="Apollo 7">Apollo 7</a> mission flew, he had been promoted to head of the flight operations division; thus, it was <a href="/wiki/Glynn_Lunney" title="Glynn Lunney">Glynn Lunney</a> who served as lead flight director and had to deal directly with behavior by the crew that Kraft considered "insubordinate". As Kraft commented in his memoirs, "it was like having a ringside seat at the Wally Schirra Bitch Circus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft289_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft289-41">[41]</a></sup> Mission commander <a href="/wiki/Wally_Schirra" title="Wally Schirra">Wally Schirra</a>, annoyed by last-minute changes in the crew's schedule and suffering from a bad cold, repeatedly refused to accept orders from the ground. Although Schirra's actions were successful in the short term, Kraft decreed in consultation with astronaut chief <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a> that none of the Apollo 7 crew would fly again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> However, Schirra had decided before the flight that he would retire after Apollo 7.
</p><p>Kraft had made a similar pronouncement before, in the case of astronaut <a href="/wiki/Scott_Carpenter" title="Scott Carpenter">Scott Carpenter</a>. After Carpenter's <a href="/wiki/Scott_Carpenter#NASA_career" title="Scott Carpenter">troubled Mercury mission</a>, Kraft wrote, "I swore an oath that Scott Carpenter would never again fly in space." The result: "He didn't."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manager_and_mentor">Manager and mentor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Manager and mentor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_mission_planning">Apollo mission planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Apollo mission planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">the Apollo 1 fire</a> in 1967, Kraft had reluctantly concluded that his responsibilities as a manager would keep him from serving as a flight director on the next manned mission, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_7" title="Apollo 7">Apollo 7</a>, and on missions thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kraft289_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft289-41">[41]</a></sup>  Henceforth his involvement in the Apollo program would be at a higher level.
</p><p>As the director of Flight Operations, Kraft was closely involved in planning the broad outlines of the program. He was one of the first NASA managers to become involved in the decision to send <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> on a circumlunar flight. Due to problems with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_module" title="Lunar module">lunar module</a> development in 1968, NASA faced the possibility of a full Apollo test mission being delayed until 1969. As a substitute, <a href="/wiki/George_Low" title="George Low">George Low</a>, the manager of the Apollo Spacecraft Program Office, came up with the idea of assigning a new mission profile to Apollo 8, one that could be flown without the lunar module.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chariots11.2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-chariots11.2-45">[45]</a></sup> The idea was discussed in early August at a meeting between Low, Kraft, Gilruth and Deke Slayton:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The four of us ... had become an unofficial committee that got together often in Bob's office to discuss problems, plans and off-the-wall ideas. Not much happened in Gemini or Apollo that didn't either originate with us or with our input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Low's plan was to fly the mission in December, which left little time for the flight operations division to train and prepare. After agreeing that the mission was possible in principle, Kraft went to his mission planners and flight directors in order to determine whether they and their teams could be ready within the tight schedule that was projected.  "My head was abuzz with the things we'd have to do," remembered Kraft. "But it was one hell of a challenge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 9, Gilruth, Low, Kraft and Slayton flew to <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">Marshall Space Flight Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>, where they briefed NASA managers including <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocco_Petrone" title="Rocco Petrone">Rocco Petrone</a> on the planned mission. On August 14, they, along with the Huntsville group, traveled to NASA Headquarters in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> to brief Deputy Administrator <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine" title="Thomas O. Paine">Thomas O. Paine</a>. In turn, Paine recommended the mission to Administrator <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James E. Webb</a>, who gave Kraft and his colleagues the authority to begin preparations for the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chariots11.2_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-chariots11.2-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In planning for Apollo 8, one of the responsibilities Kraft faced was ensuring that a fleet would be waiting to recover the crew when they splashed down at the end of the mission. This proved an unusual challenge, because much of the Pacific fleet of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a> would be on leave over the Christmas and New Year's Eve period. Kraft had to personally meet with Admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_S._McCain,_Jr." title="John S. McCain, Jr.">John McCain</a> in order to persuade him to make the requisite resources available to NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_missions">Apollo missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Apollo missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S-65-44212.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="206" data-file-width="296" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/S-65-44212.jpg/220px-S-65-44212.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bb/S-65-44212.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S-65-44212.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chris Kraft and <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Gilruth" title="Robert R. Gilruth">Robert R. Gilruth</a> pictured in Mission Control</div></div></div>
<p>On Christmas Eve, 1968, Apollo 8 went into orbit around the Moon. Only ten years earlier, Kraft had joined Gilruth's newly founded Space Task Group. Now, the two men sat together in Mission Control, reflecting on how far they had come. Around them, the room was filled with cheers, but Kraft and Gilruth celebrated more quietly.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It was glorious pandemonium, and through the mist in my own eyes, I saw Bob Gilruth wiping at his and hoping that no one saw him crying. I put my hand on his arm and squeezed. [...] He lifted my hand from his arm and shook it strongly. There were no words from either of us. The lumps in our throats held them back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Kraft again found himself a spectator during the landing of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>, which he viewed from Mission Control, sitting with Gilruth and George Low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> He played a more active role in events during the unfolding of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> crisis. Called into Mission Control by Gene Kranz almost immediately after the accident,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Kraft chaired the meeting of senior managers which decided the mode that Apollo 13 would use to return to Earth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mentor">Mentor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mentor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Apollo engineers, later to become top managers themselves, considered Kraft to have been one of the best managers in the program. He personally hand-picked and trained an entire generation of NASA flight directors, including <a href="/wiki/John_Hodge_(engineer)" title="John Hodge (engineer)">John Hodge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glynn_Lunney" title="Glynn Lunney">Glynn Lunney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kranz" title="Gene Kranz">Gene Kranz</a>, the last of whom referred to Kraft simply as "The Teacher."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In the words of the space historians Murray and Cox, Kraft "set the tone for one of the most striking features of Flight Operations: unquestioning trust—not of superiors by subordinates, but the other way around."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apollo284_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Apollo284-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="366" data-file-width="483" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg/220px-Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg/330px-Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg/440px-Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gemini-4_NASA_Mission_Control_Center.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kraft with his new flight directors before the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">Gemini 4</a> mission. (Clockwise from lower right: Kraft, Gene Kranz, Glynn Lunney and John Hodge.)</div></div></div>
<p>The principles that Kraft had inculcated continued to have an impact at Johnson Space Center long after he retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honeycutt14_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-honeycutt14-53">[53]</a></sup> As Glynn Lunney reflected in 1998:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>He ... instilled a sense of what was right, what was wrong, what you had to do, how good you had to be, and those standards that he kind of inbred into everybody, by his own example, and by what he did with us, continue today. The Control Center today ... is a reflection of Chris Kraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunney_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lunney-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Kraft could, however, be a tough taskmaster, making it clear that there was no place in the flight operations division for those who failed to live up to his exacting standards. "To err is human," went one of his favorite sayings, "but to do so more than once is contrary to Flight Operations Directorate policy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murray_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Murray-18">[18]</a></sup> Subordinates who seriously displeased Kraft could find themselves deprived of the opportunity to make it up to him. Kraft possessed the power to end careers at Johnson Space Center; as mission controller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sy_Liebergot" title="Sy Liebergot">Sy Liebergot</a> recalled, "if he was behind you, you had as much leverage as you needed; if he was against you, you were dead meat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Center_director">Center director</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Center director">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2292" data-file-width="3056" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg/220px-Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg/330px-Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg/440px-Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ronald_Reagan_and_Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kraft shows President Ronald Reagan around Mission Control during the <a href="/wiki/STS-2" title="STS-2">STS-2</a> mission in 1981.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1969, Kraft was named deputy director of the Manned Spacecraft Center (MSC). On January 14, 1972, he became the director of MSC, replacing Gilruth, for whom Kraft had worked since his arrival at Langley in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Space commentator Anthony Young has described Kraft as a "superb successor" to Gilruth, second only to him in the history of center directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kraft was eligible to retire in the early 1980s, but he chose not to take the option. He remained as center director in the status of a "reemployed annuitant," receiving his government pension but still employed by NASA. In 1981 he had been involved in a conflict with the NASA Administrator and other top officials over the conduct of the <a href="/wiki/STS-2" title="STS-2">STS-2</a> mission, and over issues relating to NASA organization and management. This contributed to making his position at NASA more tenuous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1982, Kraft made what newspaper reports called a "surprise announcement" that he intended to step down as center director at the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> He denied that his resignation had anything to do with the threatened possibility of Johnson Space Center losing its leading role in space shuttle operations or in the development of NASA's Space Station Freedom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giant_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-giant-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consultant">Consultant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Consultant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After his retirement, Kraft served as a consultant for companies including <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, and as director-at-large of the Houston Chamber of Commerce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruffner_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ruffner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, Kraft was appointed chairman of the space shuttle management independent review team, a panel made up of leading aerospace experts, whose remit was to investigate ways in which NASA could make its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle_program" title="Space shuttle program">space shuttle program</a> more cost effective. The panel's report, known as the <i>Kraft report</i>, was published in February 1995. It recommended that NASA's space shuttle operations should be outsourced to a single private contractor, and that "NASA should consider ... progression towards the privatization of the space shuttle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> It also criticized the effect of safety changes made by NASA after the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster"><i>Challenger</i> accident</a>, saying that they had "created a safety environment that is duplicative and expensive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Fundamental to the report was the idea that the shuttle had become "a mature and reliable system ... about as safe as today's technology will provide."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The report was controversial even at the time of its publication. John Pike, space policy director for the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_American_Scientists" title="Federation of American Scientists">Federation of American Scientists</a>, commented that "the <i>Kraft report</i> is a recipe for disaster. They are basically saying dismantle the safety and quality assurance mechanisms set in place after the Challenger accident."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> NASA's Aerospace Safety Advisory Panel also took issue with the report, saying in May 1995 that "the assumption that the Space Shuttle systems are now 'mature' smacks of a complacency which may lead to serious mishaps."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Nonetheless, NASA accepted the recommendations of the report, and in November 1995, responsibility for shuttle operations was turned over to the <a href="/wiki/United_Space_Alliance" title="United Space Alliance">United Space Alliance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caib108_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-caib108-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nine years later, the <i>Kraft report</i> was again criticized, this time by the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Accident_Investigation_Board" title="Columbia Accident Investigation Board">Columbia Accident Investigation Board</a> (CAIB) as part of its consideration of the organizational and cultural causes of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster"><i>Columbia</i> accident</a>. "The report," it said, "characterized the Space Shuttle program in a way that the Board judges to be at odds with the realities of the Shuttle Program."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caib108_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-caib108-65">[65]</a></sup> According to the CAIB, the <i>Kraft report</i> had contributed to the undesirable safety culture within NASA, allowing NASA to view the shuttle as an operational—rather than experimental—vehicle, and distracting attention from continuing engineering anomalies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autobiography">Autobiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Autobiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Kraft published his autobiography, <i>Flight: My Life in Mission Control</i>. It dealt with his life up until the end of the Apollo program, only briefly mentioning his time as center director in the epilogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The book was generally well-reviewed. In a <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> review, space writer <a href="/wiki/Henry_S._F._Cooper_Jr." title="Henry S. F. Cooper Jr.">Henry S. F. Cooper Jr.</a> called it a "highly readable memoir,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooper_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-cooper-68">[68]</a></sup> while the <i>Kirkus Review</i> summed it up as a "[s]nappy, highly detailed account of ... 20th century America's most dramatic technological achievement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Reviewers almost unanimously commented on the outspokenness of Kraft's storytelling, and his readiness to personally criticize those with whom he had disagreed. Cooper noted that Kraft "pull[s] no punches about some of [his colleagues'] shortcomings,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooper_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-cooper-68">[68]</a></sup> and <i>Kliatt</i> magazine said that he "isn't afraid to name names."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1950, Kraft has been married to Betty Anne Kraft, formerly Turnbull, whom he met in high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> They have two children, Gordon and Kristi-Anne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In his autobiography, Kraft recognizes the sacrifices that his family made as a result of his work for NASA, saying that "I was ... more of a remote authority figure to Gordon and Kristi-Anne than a typical American father."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kraft is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_in_the_United_States_of_America" title="Episcopal Church in the United States of America">Episcopalian</a>, serving as a <a href="/wiki/Lay_reader" title="Lay reader">lay reader</a> at his local church. During the sixties, the Kraft family was deeply involved in church activities: Betty Anne taught Sunday school and served on the altar guild; Gordon was an <a href="/wiki/Acolyte" title="Acolyte">Acolyte</a>; and Kristi-Anne sang in the choir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In addition to his duties as a lay reader, Kraft spent some time teaching a class in adult Bible study. As he recounts, however:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I lacked the fundamentalist verve and drove people away when I tried too hard to relate the early church to more modern interpretations. It was hard not to be modern when I spent my working days sending men into space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Kraft has been an avid golfer ever since he was introduced to the game in the 1940s by his friend and NASA colleague Sig Sjoberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> He cited the good golfing as a reason for staying in Houston after his retirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giant_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-giant-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Kraft has received numerous awards and honors for his work. These include the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Outstanding_Leadership_Medal" title="NASA Outstanding Leadership Medal">NASA Outstanding Leadership Medal</a>; four <a href="/wiki/NASA_Distinguished_Service_Medal" title="NASA Distinguished Service Medal">NASA Distinguished Service Medals</a>; the Distinguished Citizen Award, given to him by the city of Hampton, Virginia in 1966; the <a href="/wiki/John_J._Montgomery_Award" title="John J. Montgomery Award">John J. Montgomery Award</a> in 1963; and the Goddard Memorial Trophy, awarded by the National Space Club in 1979. In 1999, Kraft received the National Space Trophy from the <a class="external text" href="http://rnasa.org/1999files/winner1999.html" rel="nofollow">Rotary National Award for Space Achievement Foundation</a>, which described him as "a driving force in the U.S. human space flight program from its beginnings to the Space Shuttle era, a man whose accomplishments have become legendary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruffner_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ruffner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, NASA gave Kraft the Ambassador of Exploration Award, which carried with it a sample of lunar material brought back by Apollo 11. Kraft in turn presented the award to his alma mater, <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Tech" title="Virginia Tech">Virginia Tech</a>, for display in its College of Engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, the Johnson Space Center renamed its Mission Control Center the <i>Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center</i> in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kraft Elementary School, located in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton, Virginia</a> near Kraft's hometown, was named for him.
</p><p>It was announced that Kraft would be inducted into the <a href="/wiki/National_Aviation_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Aviation Hall of Fame">National Aviation Hall of Fame</a>, on October 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAHF_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAHF-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_films">In films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kraft was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Root" title="Stephen Root">Stephen Root</a> in the 1998 miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(miniseries)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (miniseries)">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>. He has been interviewed in numerous documentaries about the space program, including <i>Apollo 13: To the Edge and Back</i> (<a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a>), <i>Failure is Not an Option</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="History (U.S. TV channel)">History Channel</a>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/When_We_Left_Earth" title="When We Left Earth">When We Left Earth</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>), <i>Live From The Moon</i> (Spacecraft Films), and <i>Mission Control: The Unsung Heroes of Apollo</i> (Haviland Digital). In 2018, he will be portrayed in the film <i><a href="/wiki/First_Man_(film)" title="First Man (film)">First Man</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/J._D._Evermore" title="J. D. Evermore">J. D. Evermore</a>.
</p>
