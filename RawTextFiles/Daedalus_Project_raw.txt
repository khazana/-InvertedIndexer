<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Project Daedalus</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Project Daedalus</b> was a study conducted between 1973 and 1978 by the <a href="/wiki/British_Interplanetary_Society" title="British Interplanetary Society">British Interplanetary Society</a> to design a plausible <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_spacecraft" title="Unmanned spacecraft">unmanned</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_spacecraft" title="Interstellar spacecraft">interstellar spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-study_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-study-1">[1]</a></sup> Intended mainly as a scientific probe, the design criteria specified that the spacecraft had to use existing or near-future technology and had to be able to reach its destination within a human lifetime. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bond_(engineer)" title="Alan Bond (engineer)">Alan Bond</a> led a team of scientists and engineers who proposed using a <a href="/wiki/Fusion_rocket" title="Fusion rocket">fusion rocket</a> to reach <a href="/wiki/Barnard%27s_Star" title="Barnard's Star">Barnard's Star</a> 5.9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a> away.  The trip was estimated to take 50 years, but the design was required to be flexible enough that it could be sent to any other target star.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_Daedalus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Daedalus would be constructed in Earth orbit and have an initial mass of 54,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> including 50,000 tonnes of fuel and 500 tonnes of scientific payload. Daedalus was to be a two-stage spacecraft. The first stage would operate for two years, taking the spacecraft to 7.1% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_speed" title="Light speed">light speed</a> (0.071 <i>c</i>), and then after it was jettisoned, the second stage would fire for 1.8 years, taking the spacecraft up to about 12% of light speed (0.12 <i>c</i>), before being shut down for a 46-year cruise period. Due to the extreme temperature range of operation required, from near <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a> to 1600 K, the <a href="/wiki/Bell_nozzle" title="Bell nozzle">engine bells</a> and support structure would be made of <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> alloyed with <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, which retains strength even at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_temperature" title="Cryogenic temperature">cryogenic temperatures</a>. A major stimulus for the project was <a href="/wiki/Friedwardt_Winterberg" title="Friedwardt Winterberg">Friedwardt Winterberg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement fusion</a> drive concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-study_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-study-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> for which he received the Hermann Oberth gold medal award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>This velocity is well beyond the capabilities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_rocket" title="Chemical rocket">chemical rockets</a> or even the type of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion" title="Nuclear pulse propulsion">nuclear pulse propulsion</a> studied during <a href="/wiki/Project_Orion_(nuclear_propulsion)" title="Project Orion (nuclear propulsion)">Project Orion</a>. According to Dr. <a href="/wiki/Donald_A._Martin" title="Donald A. Martin">Tony Martin</a>, controlled-fusion engine and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_electric_rocket" title="Nuclear electric rocket">nuclear–electric systems</a> have very low <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a>, equipment to convert nuclear energy into electrical has a large mass, which results in small <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a>, which would take a century to achieve the desired speed; thermodynamic nuclear engines of the <a href="/wiki/NERVA" title="NERVA">NERVA</a> type require a great quantity of fuel, <a href="/wiki/Photon_rocket" title="Photon rocket">photon rockets</a> have to generate power at a rate of 3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">9</span></sup> W per kg of vehicle mass and require mirrors with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molar_absorptivity" title="Molar absorptivity">absorptivity</a> of less than 1 part in 10<sup>6</sup>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_ramjet" title="Interstellar ramjet">interstellar ramjet</a>'s problems are tenuous interstellar medium with a density of about 1 atom/cm<sup>3</sup>, a large diameter funnel, and high power required for its electric field. Thus the only suitable propulsion method for the project was the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion" title="Nuclear pulse propulsion">nuclear pulse rocket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Daedalus would be propelled by a <a href="/wiki/Fusion_rocket" title="Fusion rocket">fusion rocket</a> using pellets of a <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">helium-3</a> mix that would be ignited in the reaction chamber by <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam" title="Electron beam">electron beams</a>. The electron beam system would be powered by a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Induction_coils" title="Induction coils">induction coils</a> trapping energy from the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> exhaust stream. 250 pellets would be detonated per second, and the resulting plasma would be directed by a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_nozzle" title="Magnetic nozzle">magnetic nozzle</a>. The computed burn-up fraction for the fusion fuels was 0.175 and 0.133 producing exhaust velocities of 10,600 km/s and 9,210 km/s respectively. Due to scarcity of helium-3 on Earth, it was to be mined from the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> by large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot-air_balloon" title="Hot-air balloon">hot-air balloon</a> supported robotic factories over a 20-year period, or from a less distant source, such as the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The second stage would have two 5-metre <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescopes</a> and two 20-metre <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a>. About 25 years after launch these telescopes would begin examining the area around Barnard's Star to learn more about any accompanying planets. This information would be sent back to Earth, using the 40-metre diameter second stage <a href="/wiki/Bell_nozzle" title="Bell nozzle">engine bell</a> as a communications dish, and targets of interest would be selected. Since the spacecraft would not decelerate, upon reaching Barnard's Star, Daedalus would carry 18 autonomous sub-probes that would be launched between 7.2 and 1.8 years before the main craft entered the target system. These sub-probes would be propelled by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_electric_rocket" title="Nuclear electric rocket">nuclear-powered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_drive" title="Ion drive">ion drives</a> and would carry cameras, <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometers</a>, and other sensory equipment. The sub-probes would fly past their targets, still traveling at 12% of the speed of light, and transmit their findings back to the Daedalus' second stage, mothership, for relay back to Earth.
</p><p>The ship's payload bay containing its sub-probes, telescopes, and other equipment would be protected from the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> during transit by a <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> disk, up to 7 mm thick, weighing up to 50 tonnes.  This erosion shield would be made from beryllium due to its lightness and high latent heat of vaporisation. Larger obstacles that might be encountered while passing through the target system would be dispersed by an artificially generated cloud of particles, ejected by support vehicles called dust bugs about 200 km ahead of the vehicle. The spacecraft would carry a number of <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robot</a> wardens capable of autonomously repairing damage or malfunctions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Project_Daedalus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A quantitative engineering analysis of a <a href="/wiki/Self-replicating_spacecraft" title="Self-replicating spacecraft">self-replicating</a> variation on Project Daedalus was published in 1980 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Freitas" title="Robert Freitas">Robert Freitas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The non-replicating design was modified to include all subsystems necessary for self-replication. Use the probe to deliver a seed factory, with a mass of about 443 metric tons, to a distant site. Have the seed factory replicate many copies of itself on-site, to increase its total manufacturing capacity, then use the resulting automated industrial complex to construct probes, with a seed factory on board, over a 1,000-year period. Each REPRO would weigh over 10 million tons due to the extra fuel needed to decelerate from 12% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightspeed" title="Lightspeed">lightspeed</a>.
</p><p>Another possibility is to equip the Daedalus with a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_sail" title="Magnetic sail">Magnetic sail</a> similar to the magnetic scoop on a <a href="/wiki/Bussard_ramjet" title="Bussard ramjet">Bussard ramjet</a> to use the destination star <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">heliosphere</a> as a brake, making carrying deceleration fuel unnecessary, allowing a much more in-depth study of the star system chosen.
</p>
