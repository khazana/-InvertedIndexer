<p>The <b>Belt of Venus</b>, <b>Venus's Girdle</b>, or <b>antitwilight arch</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_phenomenon" title="Atmospheric phenomenon">atmospheric phenomenon</a> visible shortly before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> or after <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_twilight" title="Civil twilight">civil twilight</a>, when a pinkish glow extending roughly <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system" title="Horizontal coordinate system">10–20°</a> above the horizon surrounds the observer.
</p><p>In a way, the Belt of Venus is actually <a href="/wiki/Alpenglow" title="Alpenglow">alpenglow</a> visible above the horizon during twilight, near the <a href="/wiki/Antisolar_point" title="Antisolar point">antisolar point</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/Alpenglow" title="Alpenglow">alpenglow</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">backscatter</a> of reddened <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> also creates the Belt of Venus. Unlike alpenglow, the sunlight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refracted" title="Refracted">refracted</a> by the fine <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a> that cause the rosy arch of the Belt hovers high in the atmosphere and persists long after sunset or before sunrise. 
</p><p>As twilight progresses, the glow is separated from the horizon by the dark band of <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_shadow" title="Earth's shadow">Earth's shadow</a>, or "dark segment." The arch's light pink color is due to the <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">backscatter</a> of reddened light from the rising or setting <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. A very similar effect can be seen during a total <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">zodiacal light</a>, which is caused by the <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffuse reflection</a> of sunlight from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust" title="Interplanetary dust">interplanetary dust</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, is also a similar phenomenon.
</p><p>The name of the phenomenon alludes to the <i>cestus</i>, a girdle or breast-band, of the Ancient Greek goddess <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>, customarily equated with the Roman goddess <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a>. Since the greatest elongation (separation) between Venus and the Sun is only about 46 degrees, the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, even when visible, is never located opposite the sun and hence is never located in the Belt of Venus.
</p>
