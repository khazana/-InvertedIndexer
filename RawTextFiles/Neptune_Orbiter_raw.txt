<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Neptune Orbiter</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

The orbiter would then deploy a lander on the surface of Triton (bottom).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Neptune Orbiter</b> was a proposed <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> unmanned planetary spacecraft to explore the planet <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. It was envisioned that it would be launched sometime around 2016 and take 8 to 12 years to reach the planet; however, NASA's website no longer lists any possible launch date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Neptune Orbiter concept would have answered many questions about the nature of the planet.
</p><p>Its objective was to study Neptune's atmosphere and weather, its ring system, and its moons, particularly <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>. The <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> proposed one mission plan in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Idaho" title="University of Idaho">University of Idaho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> proposed an alternative approach in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_summary_and_status">Mission summary and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission summary and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neptune Orbiter's primary mission was to go into orbit and perform scientific studies of the planet. The mission concept was first proposed to NASA in 2005. It was proposed to use a launch rocket similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_IV_rocket" title="Delta IV rocket">Delta IV</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>. The orbiter's trajectory was to use one Venus gravity assist, and a Jupiter gravity assist before arriving at Neptune. The length of time from launch until Neptune arrival was estimated to be 10.25 years.
</p><p>Just prior to arriving, the orbiter would release its two atmospheric probes, which would transmit data before <a href="/wiki/Aerocapture" title="Aerocapture">aerocapture</a>. Then, it would begin Neptune orbit insertion by aerocapture. After adjusting its orbit to its planned science orbit, the orbiter would carry out studies of Neptune, its rings, atmosphere, weather, and its natural satellites. The main phase of the science operations would have taken from 3 to 5 years with a possible extension lasting 3 more years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the mission was removed from <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090805113110/http://solarsystem.nasa.gov/missions/future1.cfm" rel="nofollow">NASA's possible future missions</a> list. According to NASA's 2010 budget, funding to missions to the outer Solar System was aimed at the future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa_Jupiter_System_Mission" title="Europa Jupiter System Mission">Europa Jupiter System Mission</a>. The remaining budget was allocated to ongoing undertakings such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">Juno</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i>, with the Neptune system not being part of any official considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, NASA's Decadal Survey considered a mission to an ice giant—either Uranus or Neptune—but for feasibility reasons recommended a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranus_orbiter_and_probe" title="Uranus orbiter and probe">Uranus orbiter and probe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_sources">Power sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Power sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generators">Radioisotope thermoelectric generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radioisotope thermoelectric generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Caltech mission concept would have been similar in design to previous NASA outer Solar System missions. It would use <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generators</a> for electrical power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Based on this proposal, the spacecraft design would be similar to <i><a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i>, with conventional thrusters for propulsion. Another proposal were to power the spacecraft with a nuclear powered Stirling engine, which NASA were developing at that time (this project later cancelled) together with Ion propulsion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_panels">Solar panels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar panels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another proposal called for using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a> to provide electrical power to the spacecraft. The panels would be inflatable, reducing their mass. Although advancements in solar panel technology means it should be possible to achieve sufficient power at the distance of Neptune, the high cost of providing the solar panels would possibly eliminate the idea of using solar cells. This design would presumably also use conventional thrusters, because the dim sunlight in the outer Solar System would probably be insufficient to power an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_propulsion" title="Ion propulsion">ion propulsion</a> system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbiter">Orbiter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orbiter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The orbiter would have carried out the main mission objectives. Some proposed instruments were a multispectral imaging system to image the planet from <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> to <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to investigate why the Neptune's <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> is oriented so far from the planet's axis of rotation.
</p>
<h3><span id="Triton_lander.28s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Triton_lander(s)">Triton lander(s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Triton lander(s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NASA scientists and engineers have considered to send one or two mini-landers to Triton's surface and analyze the composition of the surface, the interior, and the very rarefied nitrogen atmosphere. Various landing sites were suggested, among those the area with Nitrogen geysers that had been discovered by the Voyager spacecraft, but other locations were also proposed such as either the north or south poles of Triton. No final decision were however made since this were only a proposed mission. The landers' operational lifetime could have been anything between days to a month depending on power source and instrumentation.
</p>
<h3><span id="Atmospheric_probe.28s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_probe(s)">Atmospheric probe(s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptune_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Atmospheric probe(s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with Triton landers, at least two atmospheric probes were proposed to descend through Neptune's atmosphere and study the climate and weather of the stormy planet. Similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_probe#Galileo's_atmospheric_entry_probe" title="Galileo probe">Galileo probe</a> that descended into Jupiter's atmosphere, the descent of a probe into the atmosphere of Neptune would take about 2–3 hours until the planet's atmospheric heat and pressure would destroy it – the data transmitted during the descent would allow a detailed analysis of Neptune's atmosphere.
</p>
