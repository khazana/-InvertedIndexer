<p><b>Óc Eo</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>: <span lang="km">អូរកែវ</span>, <i>O Keo</i>, "Glass Canal") is an archaeological site in <a href="/wiki/Tho%E1%BA%A1i_S%C6%A1n_District" title="Thoại Sơn District">Thoại Sơn District</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/An_Giang_Province" title="An Giang Province">An Giang Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_River_Delta" title="Mekong River Delta">Mekong River Delta</a>. Óc Eo may have been a busy port of the kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Funan</a> between the 1st and 7th centuries.
</p><p>Scholars use the term "Óc Eo Culture" to refer to the archaeological culture of the Mekong Delta region that is typified by the artifacts recovered at Óc Eo through archeological investigation.
</p><p>Óc Eo is also one of the modern day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communes_of_Vietnam" title="Communes of Vietnam">communes of Vietnam</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_site">Archaeological site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Archaeological site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg" title="This map shows the locations of archeological sites associated with Oc Eo culture. It is located at the Museum of Vietnamese History, Ho Chi Minh City."><img alt="This map shows the locations of archeological sites associated with Oc Eo culture. It is located at the Museum of Vietnamese History, Ho Chi Minh City." data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/150px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/225px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/300px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div>
<p>Excavation at Óc Eo began on February 10, 1942, after French archaeologists had discovered the site through the use of aerial photography. The first excavations were led by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Malleret" title="Louis Malleret">Louis Malleret</a>.  The site covers 450 ha.
</p><p>Óc Eo is situated within a network of ancient canals that crisscross the low flatland of the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a>.  One of the canals connects Óc Eo to  the town's seaport while another goes 68 kilometres (42 mi) north-northeast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angkor_Borei" title="Angkor Borei">Angkor Borei</a>.  Óc Eo is longitudinally bisected by a canal, and there are four transverse canals along which pile-supported houses were perhaps ranged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Archaeological sites reflecting the material culture of Óc Eo are spread throughout southern Vietnam, but are most heavily concentrated in the area of the Mekong Delta to the south and west of <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>.  The most significant site, aside from Óc Eo itself, is at Tháp Muời north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tien_Giang" title="Tien Giang">Tien Giang</a> River, where among other remains a stele with a 6th-century <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> text has been discovered.
</p><p>Aerial photography in 1958 revealed that during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Funan</a> period a distributary of the Mekong entered the Gulf of Thailand in the vicinity of Ta Keo, which was then on the shore but since then become separated by some distance from the sea as a result of siltation. At that time, Ta Keo was connected by a canal with Oc Eo, allowing it access to the Gulf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The distributary of the Mekong revealed in the aerial photography was probably <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Sinus" title="Magnus Sinus">the <i>Saenus</i></a> mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>’s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_Geography" title="Ptolemy's Geography">Geography</a></i> as the western branch of the Mekong, which Ptolemy called the <i>Cottiaris</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Cattigara in Ptolemy's Geography could be derived from a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_grammar#Phonology" title="Sanskrit grammar">Sanskrit word</a>, either <i>Kottinagara</i> (Strong City) or <i>Kirtinagara</i> (Renowned City).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remains">Remains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Remains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg/220px-Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg/330px-Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg/440px-Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Visnu_Oc_Eo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This statue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visnu" title="Visnu">Visnu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> deity of Indian-origin religion, from the 6th or 7th century AD was found in Óc Eo and is now housed in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Vietnamese_History" title="Museum of Vietnamese History">Museum of Vietnamese History</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The remains found at Óc Eo include pottery, tools, jewelry, casts for making jewelry, coins, and religious statues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Among the finds are gold jewellery imitating coins from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonine" title="Antonine">Antonine</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>279</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Roman golden <a href="/wiki/Medal" title="Medal">medallions</a> from the reign of <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a>, and possibly his successor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, have been discovered at Óc Eo, which was near Chinese-controlled <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhou_(region)" title="Jiaozhou (region)">Jiaozhou</a> and the region where <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">Chinese historical texts</a> claim <a href="/wiki/Daqin" title="Daqin">the Romans</a> first landed <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">before venturing further into China</a> to conduct diplomacy in 166.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_2001_p_29_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-young_2001_p_29-8">[8]</a></sup> Many of the remains have been collected and are on exhibition in <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Vietnamese_History" title="Museum of Vietnamese History">Museum of Vietnamese History</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>.
</p><p>Among the coins found at Óc Eo by Malleret were eight made of silver bearing the image of the <a href="/wiki/Hamsa_(bird)" title="Hamsa (bird)">hamsa</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crested_argus" title="Crested argus">crested argus</a>, apparently minted in Funan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".C3.93c_Eo_and_Funan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Óc_Eo_and_Funan">Óc Eo and Funan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Óc Eo and Funan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Kingdom of Funan</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg/220px-Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg/330px-Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg/440px-Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The archeological site of Go Cay Thi, Ba The Oc Eo</div></div></div>
<p>Óc Eo has been regarded as belonging to the historical kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Funan</a> (扶南) that flourished in the Mekong Delta between the 1st and the 6th century CE.  The kingdom of Funan is known to us from the works of ancient Chinese historians, especially writers of dynastic histories, who in turn drew from the testimony of Chinese diplomats and travellers, and of foreign (including Funanese) embassies to the Chinese imperial courts.  Indeed, the name "Funan" itself is an artifact of the Chinese histories, and does not appear in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleography" title="Paleography">paleographic</a> record of ancient Vietnam or Cambodia.  From the Chinese sources, however, it can be determined that a polity called "Funan" by the Chinese was the dominant polity located in the Mekong Delta region.  As a result, archeological discoveries in that region that can be dated to the period of Funan have been identified with the historical polity of Funan.  The discoveries at Óc Eo and related sites are our primary source for the <a href="/wiki/Material_culture" title="Material culture">material culture</a> of Funan.
</p><p>The Vietnamese archaeologist and historian <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_V%C4%83n_T%E1%BA%A5n" title="Hà Văn Tấn">Hà Văn Tấn</a> has written that at the present stage of knowledge, it was impossible to demonstrate the existence of a Funan culture, widely spread from the Mekong Delta through the Chao Praya delta to Burma, with Oc Eo as the typical representative: the presence of similar artefacts such as jewelry and seals from sites in those areas was simply the result of trade and exchange, while each of the sites bore the signs of their own separate cultural development. He supported the view of Claude Jacques that, in view of the complete lack of any <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Khmer</a> records relating to a kingdom by the name of Funan, use of this name should be abandoned in favour of the names, such as <i>Aninditapura</i>, <i>Bhavapura</i>, <i>Shresthapura</i> and <i>Vyadhapura</i>, which are known from inscriptions to have been used at the time for cities in the region and provide a more accurate idea of the true geography of the ancient Khmer territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Hà Văn Tấn argued that, from the late neolithic or early metal age, Óc Eo gradually emerged as an economic and cultural centre of the Mekong Delta and, with an important position on the Southeast Asian sea routes, became a meeting place for craftsmen and traders, which provided adequate conditions for urbanization, receiving foreign influences, notably from India, which in turn stimulated internal development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Funan was part of the region of Southeast Asia referred to in ancient Indian texts as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suwannaphum" title="Suwannaphum">Suvarnabhumi</a>, and may have been the part to which the term was first applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kattigara_of_Ptolemy">Kattigara of Ptolemy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kattigara of Ptolemy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oceo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="756" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Oceo.jpg/230px-Oceo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Oceo.jpg/345px-Oceo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Oceo.jpg/460px-Oceo.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oceo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ancient canal linking Óc Eo to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angkor_Borei" title="Angkor Borei">Angkor Borei</a></div></div></div>
<p>Óc Eo may have been the port known to the Romans as Kattigara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Kattigara was the name given by the 2nd-century <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_geographers" title="Greek geographers">geographer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> to the land on the easternmost shore of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Sea</a> at (due to a scribal error) 8½° south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "Kattigara" was probably derived from the Sanskrit <i>Kirti-nagara</i> कीर्ति- नगर “Renowned City” or <i>Kotti-nagara</i> कोटि-नगर “Strong City”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scholarship has now determined that Ptolemy's Kattigara was at 8½° north of the Equator and was the forerunner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> as the main port and entrepot at the mouth of the Mekong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Monthly_Review,_Or,_Literary_Journal,_Volume_40,_1769.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1362" data-file-width="2447" decoding="async" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG/220px-The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG/330px-The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG/440px-The_Monthly_Review%2C_Or%2C_Literary_Journal%2C_Volume_40%2C_1769.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Monthly_Review,_Or,_Literary_Journal,_Volume_40,_1769.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mr Caverhill "proves" the ancient Cattigara to be the same with the modern Ponteamass (Banteaymeas), <i>The Monthly Review, Or, Literary Journal,</i> Volume 40, 1769, p.98.</div></div></div>
<p>John Caverhill deduced in 1767 that Cattigara was the Mekong Delta port Banteaymeas (now <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_Ti%C3%AAn" title="Hà Tiên">Hà Tiên</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> not far from Óc Eo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The plea in 1979 by Jeremy H.C.S. Davidson for  “a thorough study of Hà-tiên in its historical context and in relation to Óc-eo” as indispensable for accurate understanding and interpretation of the site, still remains unanswered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The eighteenth-century French geographer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Bourguignon_d%27Anville" title="Jean-Baptiste Bourguignon d'Anville">Jean-Baptiste Bourguignon d'Anville</a>, located Cattigara at the mouth of the Mekong (<i>Cottiaris</i>) River, where it is shown on his map, <i>Orbis Veteribus Notus</i> (The World Known to the Ancients).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="978" data-file-width="885" decoding="async" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg/220px-DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg/330px-DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg/440px-DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cattigara located at the mouth of the Mekong (Cottiaris) River, by d' Anville, Orbis Veteribus Notus (The World Known to the Ancients).</div></div></div>
<p>The Swedish yachtsman and writer Bjorn Landström also concluded, from the sailing directions given by the ancient merchant and seafarer Alexander, that Cattigara lay at the mouth of the Mekong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The “father of Early Southeast Asian History”, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Coed%C3%A8s" title="George Coedès">George Coedès</a>, has said: “By the middle of the 3rd century Fu-nan had already established relations with China and India, and it is doubtless on the west coast of the Gulf of Siam that the furthest point reached by Hellenistic navigators is to be found, that is the harbour of Kattigara mentioned by Ptolemy”.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The quotation near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source to ensure that it is accurate, not taken out of context, and/or actually exists. (June 2018)">verify</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A.H. Christie said in 1979 that “the presence of objects, however few in number, from the Roman Orient” added some weight to the conjecture that Óc-eo was the Ptolemaic Kattigara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The distinguished German classical scholar, Albrecht Dihle, supported this view, saying:
</p>
<blockquote><p>From the account of the voyage of Alexander referred to by Ptolemy, Kattigara can actually be located only in the Mekong delta, because Alexander went first along the east coast of the Malacca peninsula, northward to Bangkok, from thence likewise only along the coast toward the south east, and so came to Kattigara. We hear nothing of any further change of course. In addition, at Oc Eo, an emporium excavated in the western Mekong delta, in the ancient kingdom of Fu-nan, Roman finds from the 2nd century after Christ have come to light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span id="Columbus.27_search_for_Ciamba"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Columbus'_search_for_Ciamba">Columbus' search for Ciamba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Columbus' search for Ciamba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guided by Ptolemy, the discoverers of the New World were initially trying to find their way to Kattigara. On the 1489 map of the world made by <a href="/wiki/Henricus_Martellus_Germanus" title="Henricus Martellus Germanus">Henricus Martellus Germanus</a>, revising Ptolemy’s work, Asia terminated in its southeastern point in a cape, the Cape of Cattigara. Writing of his 1499 voyage, <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> said he had hoped to reach <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> (Melaka) by sailing westward from Spain across the Western Ocean (the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>) around the Cape of Cattigara into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinus_Magnus" title="Sinus Magnus">Sinus Magnus</a> ("Great Gulf") that lay to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Chersonese" title="Golden Chersonese">Golden Chersonese</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>), of which the Cape of  Cattigara formed the southeastern point. The Sinus Magnus was the actual <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, on his fourth and last voyage of 1502–1503, planned to follow the coast of <a href="/wiki/Champa" title="Champa">Champa</a> southward around the Cape of Cattigara and sail through the strait separating Cattigara from the New World, into the Sinus Magnus to Malacca. This was the route he thought <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> had gone from China to India in 1292.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Columbus planned to meet up with the expedition sent at the same time from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> under <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>, and carried letters of credence from the Spanish monarchs to present to da Gama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On reaching Cariay on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, Columbus thought he was close to the gold mines of Champa. On July 7, 1503, he wrote from <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>: “I reached the land of Cariay...Here I received news of the gold mines of Ciamba [Champa] which I was seeking”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
