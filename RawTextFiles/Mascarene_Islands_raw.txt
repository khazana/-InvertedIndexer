<p>The <b>Mascarene Islands</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>English: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Mascareignes</i>) or <b>Mascarenes</b> or <b>Mascarenhas Archipelago</b> is a group of <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> east of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues" title="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a>. Their name derives from the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people"> Portuguese</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mascarenhas" title="Pedro Mascarenhas">Pedro Mascarenhas</a>, who first visited them in April 1512. The islands share a common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic" title="Geologic">geologic</a> origin in the volcanism of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_hotspot" title="Réunion hotspot">Réunion hotspot</a> beneath the <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau" title="Mascarene Plateau">Mascarene Plateau</a> and form a distinct <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> with a unique flora and fauna.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_description">Location and description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location and description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> comprises three large islands, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues" title="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a>, plus a number of volcanic remnants in the tropics of the southwestern Indian Ocean, generally between 700 and 1500 kilometres east of Madagascar. The <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> includes a variety of <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a>, and small islands. They present various <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edaphology" title="Edaphology">edaphic</a> regions. On the largest islands these gave rise to unusual biodiversity. The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">climate is oceanic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG/220px-Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG/330px-Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG/440px-Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piton_de_la_Fournaise_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Piton_de_la_Fournaise" title="Piton de la Fournaise">Piton de la Fournaise</a></div></div></div>
<p>The islands are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> in origin; <a href="/wiki/Saya_de_Malha_Bank" title="Saya de Malha Bank">Saya de Malha</a> (35 mya) was the first of the Mascarene islands to rise out of the Indian Ocean due to the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_hotspot" title="Réunion hotspot">Réunion hotspot</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Nazareth_Bank" title="Nazareth Bank">Nazareth Bank</a> (some 2,000 years later), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soudan_Bank&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soudan Bank (page does not exist)">Soudan Bank</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cargados_Carajos" title="Cargados Carajos">Cargados Carajos</a>. The youngest islands to form were Mauritius (7–10 mya), the oldest of the existing islands, created along with the undersea Rodrigues ridge. The islands of Rodrigues and Réunion were created in the last two million years. Réunion is the largest of the islands (2,500 km<sup>2</sup>), followed by Mauritius (1,900 km<sup>2</sup>) and Rodrigues (110 km<sup>2</sup>). Eventually, Saya de Malha, Nazareth and Soudan were completely submerged, Cargados Carajos remaining as a coral atoll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Réunion hotspot was beginning to cool and Rodrigues came out as a small island.
</p><p>Réunion is home to the highest peaks in the Mascarenes, the <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">shield volcanoes</a> <a href="/wiki/Piton_des_Neiges" title="Piton des Neiges">Piton des Neiges</a> (3,069 m) and <a href="/wiki/Piton_de_la_Fournaise" title="Piton de la Fournaise">Piton de la Fournaise</a> (2,525 m). Piton de la Fournaise, on the southeastern corner of Réunion, is one of the most active volcanoes in the world, erupting last in 2017. <a href="/wiki/Piton_de_la_Petite_Rivi%C3%A8re_Noire" title="Piton de la Petite Rivière Noire">Piton de la Petite Rivière Noire</a> (828 m) is the highest peak on Mauritius, and the gentle hills of Rodrigues rise to only 390 m.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau" title="Mascarene Plateau">Mascarene Plateau</a> is an undersea plateau that extends approximately 2000  km, from the <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a> to Réunion. The plateau covers an area of over 115,000 km<sup>2</sup> of shallow water, with depths ranging from 8 to 150 meters, plunging to 4000 m to the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a> at its edges. The southern part of the plateau, including the <a href="/wiki/Saya_de_Malha_Bank" title="Saya de Malha Bank">Saya de Malha Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazareth_Bank" title="Nazareth Bank">Nazareth Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soudan_Banks" title="Soudan Banks">Soudan Banks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cargados_Carajos" title="Cargados Carajos">Cargados Carajos</a> Shoals (Saint Brandon) (then one large island), was formed by the Réunion hotspot. These were once volcanic islands, much like Mauritius and Réunion, which have now sunk or eroded to below sea level or, in the case of the Cargados Carajos, to low coral islands. The Saya de Malha Bank formed 35 million years ago, and the Nazareth Bank and the Cargados Carajos shoals after that. <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">Limestone</a> banks found on the plateau are the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>, indicating that the plateau was a succession of islands. Some of the banks may have been islands as recently as 18,000–6,000 years ago, when sea levels were as much as 130 meters lower during the most recent <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:170px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg/168px-Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg/252px-Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg/336px-Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg 2x" width="168"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phyllanthus_tenellus_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Phyllanthus tenellus</i> (Mascarenes leaf-flower)</div></div></div>
<p>The habitats of the islands differ in size, topography, age, and in proximity to the nearest major land mass, namely (Madagascar). As is common among remote islands, the Mascarene fauna and flora display a high degree of <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a>; they include over a thousand species of which several hundred are endemic.  There also are many introduced species but until Europeans first settled the islands in the sixteenth century the Mascarenes are not known to have harbored any human populations, so much of the island's wildlife, which would have gone extinct much earlier had any native people lived there, was still flourishing during the early days of settlement.
</p><p>In prehistoric times the islands were covered in a diverse range of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_moist_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical moist broadleaf forests">tropical moist broadleaf forest</a>. At present, however, though the Mascarene islands form a distinct <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>, known as the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity_of_the_Mascarene_islands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biodiversity of the Mascarene islands (page does not exist)">Mascarene forests</a></i>, the ecoregion is not homogeneous, and comprises at least five fairly distinct vegetation zones that reflect variations in altitude and in moisture regime.
</p><p>The freshwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a> includes coastal <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swamp_forest" title="Swamp forest">swamp forests</a>, grading into rainforest to windward and to lowland dry forest to leeward. Dryland areas include palm <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane_ecology" title="Montane ecology">montane</a> deciduous forests and heathlands on the highest peaks of Réunion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_dry_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical dry broadleaf forests">dry lowland forests</a> range from sea level to elevations of some 200 metres. They occur in regions with less than 1000 mm average annual rainfall. These dry lowland forests are dominated by palms such as <a href="/wiki/Latania" title="Latania">Latania</a> species and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictyosperma_album" title="Dictyosperma album">Dictyosperma album</a>, and by the palm-like screw-pines (<a href="/wiki/Pandanus" title="Pandanus">Pandanus</a> species). There also are sclerophyllous trees such as species in the family <a href="/wiki/Combretaceae" title="Combretaceae">Combretaceae</a>, for example <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terminalia_bentzoe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terminalia bentzoe (page does not exist)">Terminalia bentzoe</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Semi-dry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclerophyllous" title="Sclerophyllous">sclerophyllous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">forests</a> occur between the coastal areas and an altitude of 360 metres on Mauritius and Rodrigues. On Réunion only small relict patches remain at altitudes up to 750 metres on the western slopes. This semi-dry ecosystem occurs where the average annual rainfall is about 1000–1500 mm. Characteristic families of the flora in the include <a href="/wiki/Ebenaceae" title="Ebenaceae">Ebenaceae</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Ebony" title="Ebony">ebony</a> species in the genus <a href="/wiki/Diospyros" title="Diospyros">Diospyros</a>. Other tree families are occurring such as <a href="/wiki/Pleurostylia" title="Pleurostylia">Pleurostylia</a> spp. (<a href="/wiki/Celastraceae" title="Celastraceae">Celastraceae</a>), <a href="/wiki/Foetidia" title="Foetidia">Foetidia</a> spp.  (<a href="/wiki/Lecythidaceae" title="Lecythidaceae">Lecythidaceae</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olea_europea" title="Olea europea">Olea europea</a> subsp. africana (<a href="/wiki/Oleaceae" title="Oleaceae">Oleaceae</a>), Cossinia pinnata (<a href="/wiki/Sapindaceae" title="Sapindaceae">Sapindaceae</a>), Dombeya spp.  (Sterculiaceae), and a variety of Sapotaceae species <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sideroxylon_boutonianum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sideroxylon boutonianum (page does not exist)">Sideroxylon boutonianum</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sideroxylon_borbonicum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sideroxylon borbonicum (page does not exist)">Sideroxylon borbonicum</a> spp. and <a href="/wiki/Mimusops" title="Mimusops">Mimusops</a>. The ecosystem is also home to several spectacular endemic species of <a href="/wiki/Hibiscus" title="Hibiscus">Hibiscus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Malvaceae</a>) <a href="/wiki/Zanthoxylum" title="Zanthoxylum">Zanthoxylum</a>  spp. (<a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>),  Obetia ficifolia (<a href="/wiki/Urticaceae" title="Urticaceae">Urticaceae</a>), and Scolopia heterophylla (<a href="/wiki/Flacourtiaceae" title="Flacourtiaceae">Flacourtiaceae</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lowland_rainforests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lowland rainforests (page does not exist)">lowland rainforests</a> are characterized by dense <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> forests, with a canopy exceeding 30 meters. With an average annual rainfall of 1500–6000 mm, occur on Mauritius island above 360 m and all over the eastern lowlands from the coast to 800–900 m and, on the western side. On Réunion island the Lowland rainforests is present from 750 to 1100 m. These
forests have a canopy of tall trees up to 30 m high and represent the more diversified plant communities of the Mascarene Islands. Characteristic plants include trees in the plant family <a href="/wiki/Sapotaceae" title="Sapotaceae">Sapotaceae</a> e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mimusops" title="Mimusops">Mimusops</a> spp. <a href="/wiki/Labourdonnaisia" title="Labourdonnaisia">Labourdonnaisia</a> spp., <a href="/wiki/Hernandiaceae" title="Hernandiaceae">Hernandiaceae</a> <a href="/wiki/Hernandia_mascarenensis" title="Hernandia mascarenensis">Hernandia mascarenensis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clusiaceae" title="Clusiaceae">Clusiaceae</a> <a href="/wiki/Calophyllum" title="Calophyllum">Calophyllum</a> spp., and <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a> <a href="/wiki/Syzygium" title="Syzygium">Syzygium</a> spp., <a href="/wiki/Eugenia" title="Eugenia">Eugenia</a> spp., <a href="/wiki/Sideroxylon" title="Sideroxylon">Sideroxylon</a> spp., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monimiastrum" title="Monimiastrum">Monimiastrum</a> spp.; shrubs in the plant family Rubiaceae (Gaertnera spp., Chassalia spp., Bertiera spp., Coffea spp.); <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboos</a> as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nastus_borbonicus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nastus borbonicus (page does not exist)">Nastus borbonicus</a>, numerous species of orchids (e.g.,  Angraecum spp.,  Bulbophyllum spp.) and ferns e.g., Asplenium spp., Hymenophyllum spp., Trichomanes spp., Elaphoglossum spp., Marattia fraxinea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forests</a>, a dense type of hygrophilous rainforest, occur on Réunion between 800 and 1900 m on eastern slopes with an average annual rainfall 2000–10,000 mm, and between 1100 and 2000 m on western slopes with an average annual rainfall 2000–3000 mm and are also restricted to a small area of Mauritius around the montane area "Montagne Cocotte" above 750 m on Mauritius with an average annual rainfall 4500–5500 mm.  These type of forests is present on both islands with a canopy of 6 to 10 m high. They are rich in <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytes</a> (orchids, ferns, mosses, lichens), emergent <a href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">tree ferns</a> (Cyathea spp.), and, originally, palms (<a href="/wiki/Acanthophoenix_rubra" title="Acanthophoenix rubra">Acanthophoenix rubra</a>), but these now survive only in areas of Réunion where <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> has not wiped them out. Untransformed cloud forests still cover large areas on Réunion with 44,000 ha in 2005.
These forests are characterized by trees such as Dombeya spp., on the Réunion island only, and species in the plant family Monimiaceae (Monimia spp., Tambourissa spp.) as canopy species, with small trees and shrubs sucth as Psiadia spp. (Asteraceae) and Melicope spp. (Rutaceae) in the understory. They also include large areas of three monodominant plant communities, forests with <a href="/wiki/Acacia_heterophylla" title="Acacia heterophylla">Acacia heterophylla</a> (Fabaceae) as canopy species that are very similar to Acacia koa forests in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, thickets dominated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erica_reunionensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erica reunionensis (page does not exist)">Erica reunionensis</a> (Ericaceae), or hyperhumid screw-pine forest (<a href="/wiki/Pandanus_montanus" title="Pandanus montanus">Pandanus montanus</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Subalpine_scrub&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Subalpine scrub (page does not exist)">subalpine scrub</a> with an average annual rainfall 2000–6000 mm is above the tree line to 1800–2000 m, at elevations where frosts occur regularly in winter, dominated by shrubs in the plant families of <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">Ericaceae</a> (Erica spp.), <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a> (Hubertia spp., Psiadia spp.,  Stoebe passerinoides), and <a href="/wiki/Rhamnaceae" title="Rhamnaceae">Rhamnaceae</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylica_nitida" title="Phylica nitida">Phylica nitida</a>), with some notable endemic species suchs Heterochaenia rivalsii (Campanulaceae), Eriotrix commersonii (<a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a>), and Cynoglossum borbonicum (<a href="/wiki/Boraginaceae" title="Boraginaceae">Boraginaceae</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The summits of the <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> are covered by large mineral areas with sparse grasslands rich in endemic grasses (Poaceae, e.g., Festuca borbonica, Agrostis salaziensis, Pennisetum caffrum) and orchids (Orchidaceae, e.g.,  Disa borbonica), ericoid thickets, or thickets of the small tree Sophora denudata (<a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a>), depending on substrate texture and age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally the Mascarene Islands are surrounded by approximately 750 square km of coral reef. Rodrigues has nearly continuous fringing reefs bounding an extensive lagoon with deep channels, whereas Mauritius is surrounded by a discontinuous fringing reef and a small barrier reef. In contrast, Réunion has very short stretches of narrow fringing reefs along the western and southwestern coasts only. The islets of the Cargados Carajos Shoals, which have a very depauperate terrestrial biota owing to being so low-lying and swamped during cyclones, are bound to the east by an extensive arc of fringing reef, which accounts for ∼30% of the reefs of the Mascarene Islands. Lagoon reefs and reef flats are dominated by scleractinian corals such as branching and tabular Acropora,  Porites massives, foliaceous Montipora and Pavona, and sand consolidated with beds of seagrass such Halophila spp. (<a href="/wiki/Hydrocharitaceae" title="Hydrocharitaceae">Hydrocharitaceae</a>).
Among coral reef fishes, wrasses (Labridae), damselfish (Pomacentridae), carnivorous groupers (<a href="/wiki/Serranidae" title="Serranidae">Serranidae</a>), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Surgeonfishes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Surgeonfishes (page does not exist)">surgeonfishes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acanthuridae" title="Acanthuridae">Acanthuridae</a>) have many species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ups-tlse1_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-ups-tlse1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the indigenous Mascarene flora and fauna are thought to have descended originally from <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> ancestors.   The Mascarenes are home to many endemic species of <a href="/wiki/Dombeyoideae" title="Dombeyoideae">Dombeyoideae</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">monotypic</a> genus <a href="/wiki/Psiloxylon" title="Psiloxylon">Psiloxylon</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Psiloxylon" title="Psiloxylon">Psiloxylon mauritianum</a></i>), and members of the family <a href="/wiki/Monimiaceae" title="Monimiaceae">Monimiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Escalloniaceae" title="Escalloniaceae">Escalloniaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foetidia" title="Foetidia">Foetidia</a>.  Indigenous trees for example include species in the genera <a href="/wiki/Ocotea" title="Ocotea">Ocotea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erythrina" title="Erythrina">Erythrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sideroxylon" title="Sideroxylon">Sideroxylon</a> and several species of palms in the genus <a href="/wiki/Hyophorbe" title="Hyophorbe">Hyophorbe</a>.  Ferns are prominent components of the biotas of the islands, especially in the tropical forest. Most ferns disperse easily via <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal" title="Seed dispersal">ornithochory</a> of their spores, allowing fairly frequent colonization from Madagascar and exchange among the Mascarene islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other endemic families include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteropeiaceae" title="Asteropeiaceae">Asteropeiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarcolaenaceae" title="Sarcolaenaceae">Sarcolaenaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sphaerosepalaceae" title="Sphaerosepalaceae">Sphaerosepalaceae</a> (with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psiloxylaceae" title="Psiloxylaceae">psiloxylaceae</a> ). The four largest families present in the Mascarene Islands are: <a href="/wiki/Myrtaceae" title="Myrtaceae">Myrtaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubiaceae" title="Rubiaceae">Rubiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orchidaceae" title="Orchidaceae">Orchidaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Euphorbiaceae" title="Euphorbiaceae">Euphorbiaceae</a>, they comprise between 193 and 223 species each, for a total of 831 species, or 26.9% of the flowering plant flora. Another seven families contain 80 or more species each: <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apocynaceae" title="Apocynaceae">Apocynaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyperaceae" title="Cyperaceae">Cyperaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cunoniaceae" title="Cunoniaceae">Cunoniaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Araliaceae" title="Araliaceae">Araliaceae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sapotaceae" title="Sapotaceae">Sapotaceae</a>, representing an additional ca. 660 species in all, 21.3% of the angiosperm flora. Some families that have few species, but are distributed too in the Americas, showing its ancient related origin, are typically in wet areas and occur as relict species in wet Islands wideworld, with several families including <a href="/wiki/Phyllanthaceae" title="Phyllanthaceae">Phyllanthaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hernandiaceae" title="Hernandiaceae">Hernandiaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauraceae" title="Lauraceae">Lauraceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ebenaceae" title="Ebenaceae">Ebenaceae</a> with Mauritius ebony, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diospyros_tesselaria" title="Diospyros tesselaria">Diospyros tesselaria</a>.
</p><p>The increasing disappearance of young calvaria trees, is suggested due to <i><a href="/wiki/Cylindraspis" title="Cylindraspis">Cylindraspis</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tortoises" title="Tortoises">tortoises</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_bat" title="Fruit bat">fruit bats</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Broad-billed_parrot" title="Broad-billed parrot">broad-billed parrot</a> could have been dispersing the seeds.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tambalacoque" title="Tambalacoque">Tambalacoque</a> (<i>Sideroxylon grandiflorum</i>), often called the dodo tree, is also threatened with extinction, although this is principally as a result of unripened seed destruction by the introduced crab-eating macaque (<i>Macaca fascicularis</i>) rather than any connection to a reliance on the dodo to assist with seed germination after the seeds passed through the extinct bird's digestive tract.
</p><p>In the Mascarenes, the angiosperms with 22 species have 21 endemic. The endemic <a href="/wiki/Dombeyoideae" title="Dombeyoideae">Dombeyoideae</a> from the Mascarenes are polyphyletic and split into nine clades. <a href="/wiki/Trochetia" title="Trochetia">Trochetia</a> appears monophyletic and more closely related to <a href="/wiki/Eriolaena" title="Eriolaena">Eriolaena</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helmiopsis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helmiopsis (page does not exist)">Helmiopsis</a> than to <a href="/wiki/Dombeya" title="Dombeya">Dombeya</a>. All Dombeya taxa are included in a clade together with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruizia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruizia (page does not exist)">Ruizia</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Astiria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Astiria (page does not exist)">Astiria</a>, this means that Dombeya is paraphyletic. In terms of breeding systems the Malagasy Dombeyoideae are hermaphroditic, whereas those of the Mascarenes are considered dioecious. The polyphyly of the Mascarene Dombeyoideae suggests that dioecy has been acquired several times. At least five colonization events from Madagascar to the Mascarene archipelago were produced. The evolutionary history of two lineages of Mascarene Domeyoideae seems to be related to adaption to xeric habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

Painted by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Hoefnagel" title="Jacob Hoefnagel">Jacob Hoefnagel</a>, c. 1602</div></div></div>
<p>Indeed, much of the Mascarenes' native fauna has become endangered or extinct since the human settlement of the islands in the 17th century. Settlers cleared most of the forests for agriculture and grazing, and introduced many exotic species, including <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongooses</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Many of the Mascarene birds evolved into flightless forms; the most famous of which was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> flightless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon">pigeon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dodo" title="Dodo">dodo</a> of Mauritius. As well as the dodo thirteen additional species of birds became extinct including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_flightless_ibis" title="Réunion flightless ibis">Réunion flightless ibis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broad-billed_parrot" title="Broad-billed parrot">broad-billed parrot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_rail" title="Red rail">red rail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_rail" title="Rodrigues rail">Rodrigues rail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_solitaire" title="Rodrigues solitaire">Rodrigues solitaire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldovesociety1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldovesociety1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-internationaldovesociety1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-internationaldovesociety1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Today sixteen endemic bird species survive on the islands.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Contemporary sources state that the dodo used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gizzard_stones" title="Gizzard stones">gizzard stones</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tripod1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-tripod1-7">[7]</a></sup> and could swallow <a href="/wiki/Ocotea" title="Ocotea">ocotea</a> fruits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tripod1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-tripod1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Seed distribution of at least some <i>Ocotea</i> species is performed by <a href="/wiki/Frugivore" title="Frugivore">frugivorous</a> birds and there exist a few reports of "solitaires" from the Mascarenes without mention of which island these came from, and the term was also used for other <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> with "solitary" habits, such as the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_swamphen" title="Réunion swamphen">Réunion blue swamphen</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_sacred_ibis" title="Réunion sacred ibis">Réunion sacred ibis</a>. At one point it was even believed that Réunion was the home of not only a white dodo, but also a white solitaire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1786, sub-fossil bones were discovered in a cave which confirmed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leguat" title="Leguat">Leguat</a>'s descriptions, but at this time no living residents of Rodrigues remembered having seen living birds. The star <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Turdus_Solitarius" title="Turdus Solitarius">Turdus Solitarius</a> was named after this bird.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Mauritius, Réunion, and Rodrigues were also once each home to one or more species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_tortoises" title="Giant tortoises">giant tortoises</a>, now extinct, which comprised the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Cylindraspis" title="Cylindraspis">Cylindraspis</a></i>. There are thirteen living endemic reptile species, including a number of species of day <a href="/wiki/Gecko" title="Gecko">geckoes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Phelsuma" title="Phelsuma">Phelsuma</a></i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The islands have no native <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, except for <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Mauritian_flying_fox" title="Mauritian flying fox">Mauritian flying fox</a>.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Wildlife.2C_threats_and_preservation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife,_threats_and_preservation">Wildlife, threats and preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wildlife, threats and preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today Mauritius has one of the highest population densities in the world and on all of the islands, there has been a great loss of habitat and many of the surviving endemic species are still threatened with extinction with little protection. Less than 40 percent of Réunion is covered with natural vegetation, only about 5 percent of Mauritius, and almost none of Rodrigues. On Réunion, forest has been cleared for agriculture and then overtaken by introduced plants. Mauritius was largely converted to sugar cane, tea, and conifer plantations. On Rodrigues the damage has been done by shifting cultivation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands,_Reunion,_Mauritius,_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1747" data-file-width="2500" decoding="async" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg/220px-1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg/330px-1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg/440px-1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands%2C_Reunion%2C_Mauritius%2C_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1780_Raynal_and_Bonne_Map_of_Mascarene_Islands,_Reunion,_Mauritius,_Bourbon_-_Geographicus_-_GeneralesIsles-bonne-1780.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1780 map of Reunion, Mauritius, and Rodrigues</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Mauritius" title="History of Mauritius">History of Mauritius</a></div>
<p>The early colonial history of the islands, like that of the Caribbean, is a confusing story of takeovers between the rival Portuguese, Dutch, French and British colonizers, usually separate or in varying combinations, sometimes even with distant other colonies, e.g. in the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>.
</p><p>Around 1507, the explorer <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira" title="Diogo Fernandes Pereira">Diogo Fernandes Pereira</a> discovered the island group. The area remained under nominal Portuguese rule until <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_de_Flacourt" title="Étienne de Flacourt">Étienne de Flacourt</a> arrived with a French naval squadron and took possession in 1649.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> From 4 June 1735 to 23 March 1746, a single French Mascarene Islands chartered colony under one <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gouverneur_g%C3%A9n%C3%A9ral" title="Gouverneur général">gouverneur général</a></i> (governor general) contained <a href="/wiki/Isle_de_France_(Mauritius)" title="Isle de France (Mauritius)">Isle de France</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_Bourbon" title="Île Bourbon">Île Bourbon</a> (Réunion) and <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Séchelles</a> (Seychelles). On 14 July 1767 this became a French crown colony, still under one governor general. From 3 February 1803 till 2 September 1810 the French colony of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Indes-Orientales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Indes-Orientales (page does not exist)">Indes-Orientales</a>, under a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitaine_g%C3%A9n%C3%A9ral" title="Capitaine général">capitaine général</a></i> (captain-general), included Réunion and (nominally) the Seychelles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mauritius">Mauritius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mauritius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> was formed 8–10 million years ago, and is geographically one of the oldest remaining islands in the group. It was discovered in the 10th century by the Arabs and was first named Dina Harobi, but the first permanent settlement was by the Dutch in 1638. It was seized in 1715 by France, who remained in control of it until the British took over in 1810. Mauritius gained independence in 1968.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rodrigues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodrigues_(island)" title="Rodrigues (island)">Rodrigues</a> was formed at around the same time as Reunion. It was first discovered by the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> but named after Portuguese navigator <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Rodrigues" title="Diogo Rodrigues">Diogo Rodrigues</a>. It was under Dutch control in 1601 and settled by the French in 1691. Britain took possession of Rodrigues in 1809. When Mauritius gained independence in 1968, Rodrigues was forcefully joined to it.<sup class="noprint selfreference">[<i><a href="/wiki/Talk:Mascarene_Islands" title="Talk:Mascarene Islands">discuss</a></i>]</sup> Rodrigues remains an autonomous region of Mauritius.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="R.C3.A9union"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Réunion">Réunion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Réunion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> was discovered first by the Arabs then by the Portuguese, who named it Santa Apolónia. It was then occupied by the French as part of Mauritius. It was first inhabited by French mutineers who arrived on the island between 1646 and 1669.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was given its current name in 1793. From 1810 to 1815 it was held by the British, before being returned to France. Réunion became an overseas <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> of France in 1946.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargados_Carajos">Cargados Carajos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cargados Carajos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cargados_Carajos" title="Cargados Carajos">Cargados Carajos</a> is the remnant of one or more large volcanic islands which sank with the rising tides. Today it is administered by Mauritius.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saya_de_Malha">Saya de Malha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Saya de Malha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Saya_de_Malha_Bank" title="Saya de Malha Bank">Saya de Malha Bank</a> is a large, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submerged_bank" title="Submerged bank">submerged bank</a>. Prehistorically it was a group of volcanic islands, and was joined to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Chagos_Bank" title="Great Chagos Bank">Great Chagos Bank</a> until continental drift pushed them apart.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soudan_Banks">Soudan Banks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Soudan Banks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Soudan_Banks" title="Soudan Banks">Soudan Banks</a> are a group of low-lying banks on the <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau" title="Mascarene Plateau">Mascarene Plateau</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazareth_Bank">Nazareth Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nazareth Bank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nazareth_Bank" title="Nazareth Bank">Nazareth Bank</a> is located just north of Cargados Carajos, and prehistorically they were a single geological feature. Today it is a large, shallow fishing bank.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hawkins_Bank">Hawkins Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mascarene_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hawkins Bank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hawkins_Bank" title="Hawkins Bank">Hawkins Bank</a> is located on the northernmost point of the <a href="/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau" title="Mascarene Plateau">Mascarene Plateau</a>.
</p>
