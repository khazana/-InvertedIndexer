<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fahrenheit</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Fahrenheit scale</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Scale_of_temperature" title="Scale of temperature">temperature scale</a> based on one proposed in 1724 by Dutch–German–Polish <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gabriel_Fahrenheit" title="Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit">Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit</a> (1686–1736).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It uses the <b>degree Fahrenheit</b> (symbol: °F) as the unit. Several accounts of how he originally defined his scale exist. The lower defining point, 0 °F, was established as the freezing <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of a solution of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> made from equal parts of ice, water and salt (<a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Further limits were established as the melting point of ice (32 °F) and his best estimate of the average <a href="/wiki/Human_body_temperature" title="Human body temperature">human body temperature</a> (96 °F, about 2.6 °F less than the modern value due to a later redefinition of the scale).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The scale is now usually defined by two fixed points: the temperature at which <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> freezes into <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> is defined as 32 °F, and the boiling point of water is defined to be 212 °F, a 180 °F separation, as defined at sea level and standard atmospheric pressure.
</p><p>At the end of the 2010s, Fahrenheit was used as the official temperature scale only in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (including its <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Unincorporated territories of the United States">unincorporated territories</a>), its <a href="/wiki/Compact_of_Free_Association" title="Compact of Free Association">freely associated states</a> in the Western Pacific (<a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Federated States of Micronesia">Federated States of Micronesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>), the <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a> and other islands which use the same meteorological service, such as <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, use Fahrenheit and <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>. All other countries in the world officially now use the Celsius scale, named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_people" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_conversion">Definition and conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>On the Fahrenheit scale, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is 32 degrees Fahrenheit (°F) and the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> is 212 °F (at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a>). This puts the boiling and freezing points of water 180 degrees apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rank_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rank-4">[4]</a></sup> Therefore, a degree on the Fahrenheit scale is ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>180</sub></span> of the interval between the freezing point and the boiling point. On the Celsius scale, the freezing and boiling points of water are 100 degrees apart. A temperature interval of 1 °F is equal to an interval of ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>9</sub></span> degrees Celsius. The Fahrenheit and Celsius scales intersect at −40° (i.e., −40 °F = −40 °C).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">Absolute zero</a> is −273.15 °C or −459.67 °F. The <i>Rankine</i> temperature scale uses degree intervals of the same size as those of the Fahrenheit scale, except that absolute zero is 0 °R — the same way that the <i><a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">Kelvin</a></i> temperature scale matches the Celsius scale, except that absolute zero is 0 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rank_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-rank-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Fahrenheit scale uses the symbol <a href="/wiki/Degree_symbol" title="Degree symbol">°</a> to denote a point on the temperature scale (as does Celsius) and the letter F to indicate the use of the Fahrenheit scale (<i>e.g.</i> "<a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Gallium</a> melts at 85.5763 °F"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as to denote a difference between temperatures or an uncertainty in temperature (<i>e.g.</i> "The output of the heat exchanger experiences an increase of 72 °F" and "Our standard uncertainty is ±5 °F").
</p><p>For an exact conversion, the following formulas can be applied. Here, <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i></span> is the value in Fahrenheit and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> the value in Celsius:
</p>
<ul><li><i>f</i> °Fahrenheit to <i>c</i> °Celsius : <span class="texhtml">(<i>f</i> − 32) °F × <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">5°C</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">9°F</span></span> = <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">(<i>f</i> − 32)</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">1.8</span></span> °C = <i>c</i> °C</span></li>
<li><i>c</i> °Celsius to <i>f</i> °Fahrenheit : <span class="texhtml">(<i>c</i> °C × <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">9°F</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">5°C</span></span>) + 32 °F = (<i>c</i> × 1.8) °F + 32 °F = <i>f</i> °F</span></li></ul>
<p>This is also an exact conversion making use of the identity −40 °F = −40 °C. Again, <span class="texhtml"><i>f</i></span> is the value in Fahrenheit and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> the value in Celsius:
</p>
<ul><li><i>f</i> °Fahrenheit to <i>c</i> °Celsius : <span class="texhtml">((<i>f</i> + 40) ÷ 1.8) − 40 = <i>c</i></span>.</li>
<li><i>c</i> °Celsius to <i>f</i> °Fahrenheit : <span class="texhtml">((<i>c</i> + 40) × 1.8) − 40 = <i>f</i></span>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:168px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fahrenheit_small.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="166" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Fahrenheit_small.jpg" width="166"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fahrenheit_small.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Gabriel_Fahrenheit" title="Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit">Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit</a>, a pioneer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precision_thermometry" title="Precision thermometry">exact thermometry</a> (<a href="/wiki/Temperature_measurement" title="Temperature measurement">temperature measurement</a>). He invented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_thermometer" title="Mercury thermometer">mercury thermometer</a> (first practical, accurate <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a>) and Fahrenheit scale (first widely used, standardized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_scale" title="Temperature scale">temperature scale</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>Fahrenheit proposed his temperature scale in 1724, basing it on two reference points of temperature. In his initial scale (which is not the final Fahrenheit scale), the zero point was determined by placing the <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> in  a mixture "of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, of water, and of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<i>salis Armoniaci</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or even of sea salt". This combination forms a <a href="/wiki/Eutectic_system" title="Eutectic system">eutectic system</a> which stabilizes its temperature automatically: 0 °F was defined to be that stable temperature. The second point, 96 degrees, was approximately the human body's temperature (<i>sanguine hominis sani</i>, the blood of a healthy man).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a story in Germany, Fahrenheit actually chose the lowest air temperature measured in his hometown <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Danzig</a> in winter 1708/09 as 0 °F, and only later had the need to be able to make this value reproducible using brine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a letter Fahrenheit wrote to his friend <a href="/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave" title="Herman Boerhaave">Herman Boerhaave</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> his scale was built on the work of <a href="/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer" title="Ole Rømer">Ole Rømer</a>, whom he had met earlier. In <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B8mer_scale" title="Rømer scale">Rømer's scale</a>, brine freezes at zero, water freezes and melts at 7.5 degrees, body temperature is 22.5, and water boils at 60 degrees. Fahrenheit multiplied each value by four in order to eliminate fractions and make the scale more <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">fine-grained</a>. He then re-calibrated his scale using the melting point of ice and normal human body temperature (which were at 30 and 90 degrees); he adjusted the scale so that the melting point of ice would be 32 degrees and body temperature 96 degrees, so that 64 intervals would separate the two, allowing him to mark degree lines on his instruments by simply bisecting the interval six times (since 64 is 2 to the sixth power).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TMU_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TMU-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fahrenheit soon after observed that water boils at about 212 degrees using this scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The use of the freezing and boiling points of water as thermometer fixed reference points became popular following the work of <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> and these fixed points were adopted by a committee of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> in 1776.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Under this system, the Fahrenheit scale is redefined slightly so that the freezing point of water is exactly 32 °F, and the boiling point is exactly 212 °F or 180 degrees higher. It is for this reason that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_human_body_temperature" title="Normal human body temperature">normal human body temperature</a> is approximately 98° (oral temperature) on the revised scale (whereas it was 90° on Fahrenheit's multiplication of Rømer, and 96° on his original scale).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the present-day Fahrenheit scale, 0 °F no longer corresponds to the eutectic temperature of ammonium chloride brine as described above. Instead, that eutectic is at approximately 4 °F on the final Fahrenheit scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Rankine_scale" title="Rankine scale">Rankine temperature scale</a> was based upon the Fahrenheit temperature scale, with its zero representing <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a> instead.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1314" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/350px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/525px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg/700px-Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Countries_that_use_Fahrenheit.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #339933; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> Countries that use Fahrenheit.</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #cccccc; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> Countries that use Celsius.</div></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thermometer_CF.svg"><img alt="Thermometer CF.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1150" decoding="async" height="383" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/220px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/330px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Thermometer_CF.svg/440px-Thermometer_CF.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thermometer_CF.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The Fahrenheit scale was the primary temperature standard for climatic, industrial and medical purposes in English-speaking countries until the 1960s. In the late 1960s and 1970s, the Celsius scale replaced Fahrenheit in almost all of those countries—with the notable exception of the United States—typically during their <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">metrication</a> process.
</p><p>Fahrenheit is used in the United States, its territories and associated states (all served by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>), as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> for everyday applications. For example, U.S. weather forecasts, food cooking, and freezing temperatures are typically given in degrees Fahrenheit. Scientists, such as meteorologists, use Celsius or Kelvin in all countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early in the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_A._Halsey" title="Frederick A. Halsey">Halsey</a> and Dale suggested that the resistance to the use of centigrade (now Celsius) system in the U.S. included the larger size of each degree Celsius and the lower zero point in the Fahrenheit system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canada <a href="/wiki/Metrication_in_Canada" title="Metrication in Canada">has passed legislation</a> favoring the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a>, while also maintaining legal definitions for traditional Canadian imperial units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Canadian weather reports are conveyed using degrees Celsius with occasional reference to Fahrenheit especially for cross-border broadcasts. Virtually all Canadian ovens make legal use of the Fahrenheit scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Thermometers, both digital and analog, sold in Canada usually employ both the Celsius and Fahrenheit scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canadian-unit-laws_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian-unit-laws-22">[22]</a></sup>  Also, in some instances (swimming pool temperature, thermostats, or cooking temperatures for example), temperatures are still expressed in Fahrenheit.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waschen_40.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="375" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Waschen_40.svg/170px-Waschen_40.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Waschen_40.svg/255px-Waschen_40.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Waschen_40.svg/340px-Waschen_40.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waschen_40.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>European laundry symbol for "Wash at 40 °C"</div></div></div>
<p>Within the European Union, it is mandatory to use kelvins or degrees Celsius when quoting temperature for "economic, public health, public safety and administrative" purposes, though degrees Fahrenheit may be used alongside degrees Celsius as a supplementary unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Laundry_symbol" title="Laundry symbol">laundry symbols</a> used in the United Kingdom follow the recommendations of ISO 3758:2005 showing the temperature of the washing machine water in degrees Celsius only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The equivalent label in North America uses one to six dots to denote temperature with an optional temperature in degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the unregulated sector, such as journalism, the use of Fahrenheit in the United Kingdom follows no fixed pattern with degrees Fahrenheit often appearing alongside degrees Celsius. <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">The Daily Mail</a></i>, on its daily weather page, quotes Celsius first, followed by Fahrenheit in brackets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> does not mention Fahrenheit on its daily weather page<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> also has an all-metric daily weather page but has a Celsius-to-Fahrenheit conversion table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> When publishing news stories, much of the UK  press and population have adopted a convention of using degrees Celsius in headlines and discussion relating to low temperatures and Fahrenheit for high temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="noprint selfreference">[<i><a href="/wiki/Talk:Fahrenheit#Use_in_UK_media" title="Talk:Fahrenheit">discuss</a></i>]</sup> In February 2006, the writer of an article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> suggested that the rationale was one of emphasis:  "−6 °C" sounds colder than "21 °F" and "94 °F" sounds more impressive than "34 °C".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T20060223_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-T20060223-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode_representation_of_symbol">Unicode representation of symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahrenheit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unicode representation of symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> provides the Fahrenheit symbol at code point <span class="nowrap"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r861301850">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced">U+2109</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">℉</span> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872446130">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">DEGREE FAHRENHEIT</span>. However, this is a <a href="/wiki/Unicode_compatibility_characters" title="Unicode compatibility characters">compatibility character</a> encoded for <a href="/wiki/Round-trip_format_conversion" title="Round-trip format conversion">roundtrip compatibility</a> with legacy encodings. The Unicode standard explicitly discourages the use of this character: "The sequence <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+00B0</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">°</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">DEGREE SIGN</span> +<span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+0046</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">F</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">LATIN CAPITAL LETTER F</span> is preferred over <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+2109</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">℉</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">DEGREE FAHRENHEIT</span>, and those two sequences should be treated as identical for searching."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Sal Armoniac" was an impure form of ammonium chloride.  The French chemist <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Lemery" title="Nicolas Lemery">Nicolas Lémery</a> (1645–1715) discussed it in his book <i>Cours de Chymie</i> (A Course of Chemistry, 1675), describing where it occurs naturally and how it can be prepared artificially.  It occurs naturally in the deserts of northern Africa, where it forms from puddles of animal urine.  It can be prepared artificially by boiling 5 parts of urine, 1 part of sea salt, and ½ part of chimney soot until the mixture has dried.  The mixture is then heated in a sublimation pot until it sublimates ; the sublimated crystals are <i>sal Armoniac</i>.  See:
