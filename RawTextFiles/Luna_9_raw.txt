<p><i><b>Luna 9</b></i> (Луна-9), internal designation <b>Ye-6 No.13</b>, was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_space_mission" title="Unmanned space mission">unmanned space mission</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna programme</a>. On 3 February 1966 the Luna 9 spacecraft became the first spacecraft to achieve a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lander had a mass of 99 kilograms (218 lb). It used a landing bag to survive the impact speed of 22 kilometres per hour (6.1 m/s; 14 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  It was a <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">hermetically sealed</a> container with radio equipment, a program timing device, heat control systems, scientific apparatus, power sources, and a television system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_and_translunar_coast">Launch and translunar coast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch and translunar coast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Luna 9</i> was launched by a <a href="/wiki/Molniya-M" title="Molniya-M">Molniya-M</a> rocket, serial number 103-32, flying from <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_31" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 31">Site 31/6</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic">Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic</a>. Liftoff took place at 11:41:37 UTC on 31 January 1966. The first three stages of the four-stage carrier rocket injected the payload and fourth stage into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, at an altitude of 168 by 219 kilometres (104 by 136 mi) and 51.8 degrees <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satcat_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-satcat-1">[1]</a></sup> The fourth stage, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blok-L&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blok-L (page does not exist)">Blok-L</a>, then fired to raise the orbit's <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perigee</a> to a new <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apogee</a> approximately 500,000 kilometres (310,000 mi), before deploying <i>Luna 9</i> into a <a href="/wiki/Highly_elliptical_orbit" title="Highly elliptical orbit">highly elliptical</a> <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">geocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satcat_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-satcat-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The spacecraft then spun itself up to 0.67 rpm using nitrogen jets. On 1 February at 19:29 UT, a mid-course correction took place involving a 48-second burn and resulting in a delta-V of 71.2 metres per second (234 ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At an altitude of 8,300 kilometres (5,200 mi) from the Moon, the spacecraft was oriented for the firing of its <a href="/wiki/Retrorocket" title="Retrorocket">retrorockets</a> and its spin was stopped in preparation for landing. From this moment the orientation of the spacecraft was supported by measurements of directions to the Sun and the Earth using an opto-mechanical system. At 74.885 kilometres (46.531 mi) above the lunar surface, the <a href="/wiki/Radar_altimeter" title="Radar altimeter">radar altimeter</a> triggered the jettison of the side modules, the inflation of the air bags and the firing of the retro rockets. Approximately at 250 metres (820 ft) from the surface, the main retrorocket was turned off by the integrator of an acceleration having reached the planned velocity of the braking manoeuver. The four outrigger engines were used to slow the craft. Approximately 5 metres (16 ft) above the lunar surface, a contact sensor touched the ground triggering the engines to be shut down and the landing capsule to be ejected. The craft landed at 22 kilometres per hour (6.1 m/s; 14 mph)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The spacecraft bounced several times before coming to rest in <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">Oceanus Procellarum</a> west of <a href="/wiki/Reiner_(crater)" title="Reiner (crater)">Reiner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marius_(crater)" title="Marius (crater)">Marius</a> craters at approximately 7.08 N, 64.37 W on 3 February 1966 at 18:45:30 UT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The spacecraft was developed in the design bureau then known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">OKB-1</a>, under Chief Designer <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a> (who had died before the launch). The first 11 Luna missions were unsuccessful for a variety of reasons. At that time the project was transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Lavochkin" title="NPO Lavochkin">Lavochkin</a> design bureau since OKB-1 was busy with a manned expedition to the Moon. <i>Luna 9</i> was the twelfth attempt at a soft-landing by the Soviet Union; it was also the first successful deep space probe built by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Lavochkin" title="NPO Lavochkin">Lavochkin</a> design bureau, which ultimately would design and build almost all Soviet (later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian</a>) lunar and interplanetary spacecraft. All operations prior to landing occurred without fault, and the 58-centimetre (23 in) spheroid ALS capsule landed on the Moon at 18:45:30 UT on 3 February 1966 west of the craters <a href="/wiki/Reiner_(crater)" title="Reiner (crater)">Reiner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marius_(crater)" title="Marius (crater)">Marius</a> in the Ocean of Storms (at 7°8' north latitude and 64°22' west longitude). Approximately five minutes after touchdown, Luna 9 began transmitting data to Earth, but it was seven hours (after the Sun had climbed to 7° elevation) before the probe began sending the first of nine images (including five panoramas) of the surface of the Moon.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_operations">Surface operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Approximately 250 seconds after landing in the <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">Oceanus Procellarum</a>, four petals which covered the top half of the spacecraft opened outward for increased stability. The television camera system began a photographic survey of the lunar environment. Seven radio sessions with a total of 8 hours and 5 minutes were transmitted, as well as three series of TV pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>After assembly the photographs gave a panoramic view of the immediate lunar surface, comprising views of nearby rocks and of the horizon, 1.4 kilometres (0.87 mi) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pictures from <i>Luna 9</i> were not released immediately by the Soviet authorities, but scientists at <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory" title="Jodrell Bank Observatory">Jodrell Bank Observatory</a> in England, which was monitoring the craft, noticed that the signal format used was identical to the internationally agreed <a href="/wiki/Radiofax" title="Radiofax">Radiofax</a> system used by newspapers for transmitting pictures. The <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i> rushed a suitable receiver to the Observatory and the pictures from <i>Luna 9</i> were decoded and published worldwide. The <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> speculated that the spacecraft's designers deliberately fitted the probe with equipment conforming to the standard, to enable reception of the pictures by Jodrell Bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The radiation detector, the only scientific instrument on board, measured a dosage of 30 millirads (0.3 <a href="/wiki/Gray_(unit)" title="Gray (unit)">milligrays</a>) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The mission also determined that a spacecraft would not sink into the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">lunar dust</a>; that the ground could support a lander. Last contact with the spacecraft was at 22:55 UTC on 6 February 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC1966-006A_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC1966-006A-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
