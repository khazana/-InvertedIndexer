<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Scientific priority</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <b>priority</b> is the credit given to the individual or group of individuals who first made the discovery or propose the theory. Fame and honours usually go to the first person or group to publish a new finding, even if several researchers arrived at the same conclusion independently and at the same time. Thus between two or more independent discoverers, the first to make formal publication is the legitimate winner. Hence, the tradition is often referred to as the <b>priority rule</b>, the procedure of which is nicely summed up in a phrase "<a href="/wiki/Publish_or_perish" title="Publish or perish">publish or perish</a>", because there are no second prizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In a way, the race to be first inspires risk-taking that can lead to scientific breakthroughs which is beneficial to the society (such as discovery of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria transmission</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>, etc.); on the other hand, it can create an unhealthy competition, thus, becoming detrimental to scientific progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fang_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fang-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Priority_disputes">Priority disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_priority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Priority disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Priority becomes a difficult issue usually in the context of priority disputes, where the priority for a given theory, understanding, or discovery comes into question. In most cases historians of science disdain retrospective priority disputes as enterprises which generally lack understanding about the nature of scientific change and usually involve gross misreadings of the past to support the idea of a long-lost priority claim. Historian and biologist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> once remarked that "debates about the priority of ideas are usually among the most misdirected in the history of science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a> told <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a> that he avoided priority disputes by "Always giv[ing] the bastards more credit than they deserve." Dyson remarked that he also follows this rule, and that this practice is "remarkably effective for avoiding quarrels and making friends." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_priority&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The priority rule came into existence before or as soon as modern scientific methods were established. For example, the earliest documented controversy was a bitter claim between <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> in the 17th century about priority in the invention of <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>. This particular incidence clearly shows human biases and prejudice. It has become unanimously accepted that both the mathematicians independently developed calculus. Since then priority has caused a number of historical maladies in the history of science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fang_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fang-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
In the cases of scientists who have since achieved incredible levels of popularity, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, priority questions are often rooted in taking too seriously the myth of the "<a href="/wiki/Heroic_theory_of_invention_and_scientific_development" title="Heroic theory of invention and scientific development">lone genius</a>" which is often cultivated around such quasi-mythic figures (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Man_theory" title="Great Man theory">Great Man theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whig_history" title="Whig history">Whig history</a>). In an attempt to laud such scientists as visionaries, the context in which they worked is often neglected by popularizers, making it appear as if they worked without assistance or without reference to other work, something which is rarely the case.
</p>
