<p><b>Venera 13</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Венера-13</span> meaning <i>Venus 13</i>) was a probe in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_program" title="Venera program">Venera program</a> for the exploration of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.
</p><p>Venera 13 and 14 were identical spacecraft built to take advantage of the 1981 Venus launch opportunity and launched 5 days apart, Venera 13 on 30 October 1981 at 06:04 UTC and <a href="/wiki/Venera_14" title="Venera 14">Venera 14</a> on 4 November 1981 at 05:31 UTC, both with an on-orbit dry mass of 760 kg (1,680 lb).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each mission consisted of a cruise stage and an attached descent craft.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cruise_stage">Cruise stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cruise stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the cruise stage flew by Venus the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacecraft_bus" title="Spacecraft bus">bus</a> acted as a data relay for the lander and then continued on into a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>. It was equipped with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">gamma-ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV" title="UV">UV</a> grating monochromator, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> spectrometers, <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray burst</a> detectors, <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> plasma detectors, and two-frequency transmitters which made measurements before, during, and after the Venus flyby.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent_lander">Descent lander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Descent lander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The descent lander was a hermetically-sealed pressure vessel, which contained most of the instrumentation and electronics, mounted on a ring-shaped landing platform and topped by an antenna. The design was similar to the earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_program" title="Venera program">Venera 9–12 landers</a>. It carried instruments to take chemical and <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> measurements, monitor the spectrum of scattered sunlight, and record electric discharges during its descent phase through the Venusian atmosphere. The spacecraft utilized a camera system, an <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">X-ray fluorescence spectrometer</a>, a screw drill and surface sampler, a dynamic penetrometer, and a seismometer to conduct investigations on the surface.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landing">Landing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Landing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After launch and a four-month cruise to Venus the descent vehicle separated from the cruise stage and plunged into the Venusian atmosphere on 1 March 1982. After entering the atmosphere a parachute was deployed. At an altitude of about 50 kilometres (31 mi) the parachute was released and simple airbraking was used the rest of the way to the surface.
</p><p>Venera 13 landed at around 7-8m/s at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Venera_13&amp;params=7.5_S_303_E_globe:Venus"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">7°30′S</span> <span class="longitude">303°00′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">7.5°S 303°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-7.5; 303</span></span></span></a></span>, about 950 km (590 mi) northeast of Venera 14, just east of the eastern extension of an elevated region known as <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_Regio" title="Phoebe Regio">Phoebe Regio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The lander had cameras to take pictures of the ground and spring-loaded arms to measure the compressibility of the soil. The quartz camera windows were covered by lens caps which popped off after descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The area was composed of bedrock outcrops surrounded by dark, fine-grained soil. After landing, an imaging panorama was started and a mechanical drilling arm reached to the surface and obtained a sample, which was deposited in a hermetically sealed chamber, maintained at 30 °C (86 °F) and a pressure of about 0.05 atmosphere (5 kPa). The composition of the sample determined by the X-ray fluorescence spectrometer put it in the class of weakly differentiated melanocratic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbroids</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The lander functioned for at least 127 minutes (the planned design life was 32 minutes) in an environment with a temperature of 457 °C (855 °F) and a pressure of 89 Earth atmospheres (9.0 MPa). The descent vehicle transmitted data to the satellite, which acted as a data relay as it flew by Venus.
</p><p>Microphones on the probe recorded atmospheric wind noises in an effort to measure wind speed (they also recorded noises associated with the probe's equipment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This was the first recording of sound on another planet. The recording would be repeated with <a href="/wiki/Venera_14" title="Venera 14">Venera 14</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_photographic_evidence_of_life">Suggested photographic evidence of life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_13&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Suggested photographic evidence of life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leonid Ksanfomaliti<sup><a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D1%81%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%84%D0%BE%D0%BC%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B8,_%D0%9B%D0%B5%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B4_%D0%92%D0%B0%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" title="ru:Ксанфомалити, Леонид Васильевич">[:ru]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Research_Institute" title="Russian Space Research Institute">Space Research Institute</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a> (a contributor to the Venera mission) and Stan Karaszewski of Karas, suggested signs of life in the <i>Venera</i> images in an article published in <i><a href="/wiki/Solar_System_Research" title="Solar System Research">Solar System Research</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to Ksanfomaliti, certain objects  resembled a "disk", a "black flap" and a "scorpion" which "emerge, fluctuate and disappear", referring to their changing location on different photographs and traces on the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Engineers familiar with the probe have identified the moving "disk" as actually being the two lens caps ejected from the lander. Rather than a single object that had moved between two different places, they are simply two inanimate similar-looking objects in different places. The other "objects" are ascribed to image processing artifacts and do not appear in the original photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The editors of <i>Solar System Research</i> published an editorial comment and a number of commentary articles from other scientists in their September 2012 publication of Issue 5, Volume 46 of the journal. That issue also includes a second article from Ksanfomaliti, in which he claims to identify several other life forms and speculates regarding the apparent rich diversity of life around the landing site.
</p><p>These claims have been refuted by the Live Science website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
