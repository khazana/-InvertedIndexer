<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Radio telescope</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2657" data-file-width="2129" decoding="async" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg/250px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg/375px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg/500px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_4350_CSIROs_Parkes_Radio_Telescope_with_moon_in_the_background.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 64-meter radio telescope at <a href="/wiki/Parkes_Observatory" title="Parkes Observatory">Parkes Observatory</a> as seen in 1969, when it was used to receive live televised footage from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UTR-2_-_P3094042_(wiki).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg/220px-UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg/330px-UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg/440px-UTR-2_-_P3094042_%28wiki%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UTR-2_-_P3094042_(wiki).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Antenna of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTR-2" title="UTR-2">UTR-2</a> low frequency radio telescope, <a href="/wiki/Kharkiv" title="Kharkiv">Kharkiv</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Consists of an array of 2040 <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">cage dipole</a> elements.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>radio telescope</b> is a specialized <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">radio receiver</a> used to receive <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> from <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_radio_source" title="Astronomical radio source">astronomical radio sources</a> in the sky in <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verschuur_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verschuur-3">[3]</a></sup> Radio telescopes are the main observing instrument used in radio astronomy, which studies the <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> portion of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> emitted by astronomical objects, just as <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescopes</a> are the main observing instrument used in traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_astronomy" title="Optical astronomy">optical astronomy</a> which studies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_wave" title="Light wave">light wave</a> portion of the spectrum coming from astronomical objects. Radio telescopes are typically large <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic ("dish") antennas</a>  similar to those employed in tracking and communicating with <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> and space probes. They may be used singly or linked together electronically in an array.  Unlike optical telescopes, radio telescopes can be used in the daytime as well as at night. Since astronomical radio sources such as <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> are very far away, the radio waves coming from them are extremely weak, so radio telescopes require very large antennas to collect enough radio energy to study them, and extremely sensitive receiving equipment. Radio <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatories</a> are preferentially located far from major centers of population to avoid <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interference" title="Electromagnetic interference">electromagnetic interference</a> (EMI) from radio, <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, motor vehicles, and other manmade electronic devices.
</p><p>Radio waves from space were first detected by engineer <a href="/wiki/Karl_Guthe_Jansky" title="Karl Guthe Jansky">Karl Guthe Jansky</a> in 1932 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Telephone_Laboratories" title="Bell Telephone Laboratories">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a> in Holmdel, New Jersey using an antenna built to study noise in radio receivers.  The first purpose-built radio telescope was a 9-meter parabolic dish constructed by radio amateur <a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">Grote Reber</a> in his back yard in Wheaton, Illinois in 1937. The sky survey he did with it is often considered the beginning of the field of radio astronomy.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_radio_telescopes">Early radio telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early radio telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="773" decoding="async" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg/200px-Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg/300px-Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg/400px-Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Janksy_Karl_radio_telescope.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Full-size replica of the first radio telescope, Jansky's <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">dipole</a> array, preserved at the US <a href="/wiki/National_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory" title="National Radio Astronomy Observatory">National Radio Astronomy Observatory</a> in Green Bank, West Virginia.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="813" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif/175px-Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif/263px-Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif/350px-Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif 2x" width="175"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grote_Antenna_Wheaton.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">Reber</a>'s first "dish" radio telescope - Wheaton, IL 1937</div></div></div>
<p>The first radio antenna  used to identify an astronomical radio source was one built by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Guthe_Jansky" title="Karl Guthe Jansky">Karl Guthe Jansky</a>, an engineer with <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a>, in  1932. Jansky was assigned the job of identifying sources of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Static_(radio)" title="Static (radio)">static</a> that might interfere with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_telephone" title="Radio telephone">radio telephone</a> service. Jansky's antenna was an array of <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipoles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflector_(antenna)" title="Reflector (antenna)">reflectors</a> designed to receive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_wave" title="Short wave">short wave</a> radio signals at a <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of 20.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megahertz" title="Megahertz">MHz</a> (wavelength about 14.6 meters). It was mounted on a turntable that allowed it to rotate in any direction, earning it the name "Jansky's merry-go-round".  It had a diameter of approximately 100 ft (30 m) and stood 20 ft (6 m) tall. By rotating the antenna, the direction of the received interfering radio source (static) could be pinpointed. A small shed to the side of the antenna housed an <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">analog</a> pen-and-paper recording system. After recording signals from all directions for several months, Jansky eventually categorized them into three types of static: nearby thunderstorms, distant thunderstorms, and a faint steady hiss of unknown origin. Jansky finally determined that the "faint hiss" repeated on a cycle of 23 hours and 56 minutes. This period is the length of an astronomical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">sidereal day</a>, the time it takes any "fixed" object located on the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a> to come back to the same location in the sky. Thus Jansky suspected that the hiss originated outside of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, and by comparing his observations with optical astronomical maps, Jansky concluded that the radiation was coming from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a> and was strongest in the direction of the center of the galaxy, in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">Sagittarius</a>.
</p><p>An amateur radio operator, <a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">Grote Reber</a>, was one of the pioneers of what became known as <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>.  He built the first parabolic "dish" radio telescope, 9 metres (30 ft) in diameter, in his back yard in Wheaton, Illinois in 1937. He repeated Jansky's pioneering work, identifying the Milky Way as the first off-world radio source, and he went on to conduct the first sky survey at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">very high</a> radio frequencies, discovering other radio sources.  The rapid <a href="/wiki/History_of_radar" title="History of radar">development of radar</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> created technology which was applied to radio astronomy after the war, and radio astronomy became a branch of astronomy, with universities and research institutes constructing large radio telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The range of frequencies in the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> that makes up the <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio spectrum</a> is very large. This means that the types of antennas that are used as radio telescopes vary widely in design, size, and configuration. At wavelengths of 30 meters to 3 meters (10 MHz - 100 MHz), they are generally either <a href="/wiki/Directional_antenna" title="Directional antenna">directional antenna</a> arrays similar to "TV antennas" or large stationary reflectors with moveable focal points. Since the wavelengths being observed with these types of antennas are so long, the "reflector" surfaces can be constructed from coarse wire <a href="/wiki/Mesh" title="Mesh">mesh</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Chicken_wire" title="Chicken wire">chicken wire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-galaxy196506_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-galaxy196506-5">[5]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At shorter wavelengths <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic "dish" antennas</a> predominate. The <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">angular resolution</a> of a dish antenna is determined by the ratio of the diameter of the dish to the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the radio waves being observed. This dictates the dish size a radio telescope needs for a useful resolution. Radio telescopes that operate at wavelengths of 3 meters to 30 cm (100 MHz to 1 GHz) are usually well over 100 meters in diameter. Telescopes working at wavelengths shorter than 30 cm (above 1 GHz) range in size from 3 to 90 meters in diameter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequencies">Frequencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Frequencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The increasing use of radio frequencies for communication makes astronomical observations more and more difficult (see <a href="/wiki/Open_spectrum#Radio_astronomy_needs" title="Open spectrum">Open spectrum</a>).
Negotiations to defend the <a href="/wiki/Frequency_allocation" title="Frequency allocation">frequency allocation</a> for parts of the spectrum most useful for observing the universe are coordinated in the Scientific Committee on Frequency Allocations for Radio Astronomy and Space Science.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="780" data-file-width="1650" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/350px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/525px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/700px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plot of Earth's atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">transmittance</a> (or opacity) to various wavelengths of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Some of the more notable frequency bands used by radio telescopes include:
</p>
<ul><li>Every frequency in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Radio_Quiet_Zone" title="United States National Radio Quiet Zone">United States National Radio Quiet Zone</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Channel_37" title="Channel 37">Channel 37</a>: 608 to 614 MHz</li>
<li>The "<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_line" title="Hydrogen line">Hydrogen line</a>", also known as the "21 centimeter line": 1420.40575177 MHz, used by many radio telescopes including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Big_Ear" title="The Big Ear">The Big Ear</a> in its discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Wow!_signal" title="Wow! signal">Wow! signal</a></li>
<li>1406 MHz and 430 MHz <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterhole_(radio)" title="Waterhole (radio)">Waterhole</a>: 1,420 to 1,666 MHz</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> has several receivers that together cover the whole 1–10 GHz range.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe</a> mapped the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">Cosmic microwave background radiation</a> in 5 different frequency bands, centered on 23 GHz, 33 GHz, 41 GHz, 61 GHz, and 94 GHz.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_dishes">Big dishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Big dishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The world's largest filled-aperture (i.e. full dish) radio telescope is the <a href="/wiki/Five_hundred_meter_Aperture_Spherical_Telescope" title="Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope">Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope</a> (FAST) completed in 2016 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The 500-meter-diameter (1,600 ft) dish with an area as large as 30 football fields is built into a natural <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">Karst</a> depression in the landscape in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guizhou_province" title="Guizhou province">Guizhou province</a> and cannot move; the <a href="/wiki/Antenna_feed" title="Antenna feed">feed antenna</a> is in a cabin suspended above the dish on cables.  The active dish is composed of 4450 moveable panels controlled by a computer. By changing the shape of the dish and moving the feed cabin on its cables, the telescope can be steered to point to any region of the sky up to 40° from the zenith.  Although the dish is 500 meters in diameter, only a 300-meter circular area on the dish is illuminated by the feed antenna at any given time, so the actual effective aperture is 300 meters.   Construction was begun in 2007 and completed July 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the telescope became operational September 25, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The world's second largest filled-aperture telescope is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arecibo_radio_telescope" title="Arecibo radio telescope">Arecibo radio telescope</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Arecibo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Arecibo, Puerto Rico">Arecibo, Puerto Rico</a>. Another stationary dish telescope like FAST, whose 305 m (1,001 ft) dish is built into a natural depression in the landscape, the antenna is steerable within an angle of about 20° of the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> by moving the suspended <a href="/wiki/Antenna_feed" title="Antenna feed">feed antenna</a>. The largest individual radio telescope of any kind is the <a href="/wiki/RATAN-600" title="RATAN-600">RATAN-600</a> located near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nizhny_Arkhyz" title="Nizhny Arkhyz">Nizhny Arkhyz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, which consists of a 576-meter circle of rectangular radio reflectors, each of which can be pointed towards a central conical receiver.
</p><p>The above stationary dishes are not fully "steerable"; they can only be aimed at points in an area of the sky near the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a>, and cannot receive from sources near the horizon. The largest fully steerable dish radio telescope is the 100 meter <a href="/wiki/Green_Bank_Telescope" title="Green Bank Telescope">Green Bank Telescope</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, United States, constructed in 2000. The largest fully steerable radio telescope in Europe is the <a href="/wiki/Effelsberg_100-m_Radio_Telescope" title="Effelsberg 100-m Radio Telescope">Effelsberg 100-m Radio Telescope</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, Germany, operated by the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Radio_Astronomy" title="Max Planck Institute for Radio Astronomy">Max Planck Institute for Radio Astronomy</a>, which also was the world's largest fully steerable telescope for 30 years until the Green Bank antenna was constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridpath2012_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ridpath2012-11">[11]</a></sup> The third-largest fully steerable radio telescope is the 76-meter <a href="/wiki/Lovell_Telescope" title="Lovell Telescope">Lovell Telescope</a> at <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory" title="Jodrell Bank Observatory">Jodrell Bank Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, England, completed in 1957. The fourth-largest fully steerable radio telescopes are six 70-meter dishes: three Russian <a href="/wiki/RT-70" title="RT-70">RT-70</a>, and three in the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">NASA Deep Space Network</a>. As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the planned <a href="/wiki/Qitai_Radio_Telescope" title="Qitai Radio Telescope">Qitai Radio Telescope</a> will be the world's largest fully steerable single-dish radio telescope with a diameter of 110 m (360 ft).
</p><p>A typical size of the single antenna of a radio telescope is 25 meters. Dozens of radio telescopes with comparable sizes are operated in radio observatories all over the world.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiotelescopes_in_space">Radiotelescopes in space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radiotelescopes in space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1965, humans have launched three space-based radio telescopes. In 1965, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> sent the first one called <a href="/wiki/Zond_3" title="Zond 3">Zond 3</a>. In 1997, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> sent the second, <a href="/wiki/HALCA" title="HALCA">HALCA</a>. The last one was sent by <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> in 2011 called <a href="/wiki/Spektr-R" title="Spektr-R">Spektr-R</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_interferometry">Radio interferometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Radio interferometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_interferometer" title="Astronomical interferometer">Astronomical interferometer</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy#Radio_interferometry" title="Radio astronomy">Radio astronomy § Radio interferometry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg/250px-USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg/375px-USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg/500px-USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Array" title="Very Large Array">Very Large Array</a> in Socorro, New Mexico, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interferometer" title="Interferometer">interferometric array</a> formed of 27 parabolic dish telescopes.</div></div></div>
<p>One of the most notable developments came in 1946 with the introduction of the technique called astronomical interferometry, which means combining the signals from multiple antennas so that they simulate a larger antenna, in order to achieve greater resolution.  Astronomical radio interferometers usually consist either of arrays of parabolic dishes (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/One-Mile_Telescope" title="One-Mile Telescope">One-Mile Telescope</a>), arrays of one-dimensional antennas (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Molonglo_Observatory_Synthesis_Telescope" title="Molonglo Observatory Synthesis Telescope">Molonglo Observatory Synthesis Telescope</a>) or two-dimensional arrays of omnidirectional <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">dipoles</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Antony_Hewish" title="Antony Hewish">Tony Hewish's</a> <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Scintillation_Array" title="Interplanetary Scintillation Array">Pulsar Array</a>). All of the telescopes in the array are widely separated and are usually connected using <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a>, or other type of <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission line</a>. Recent advances in the stability of electronic oscillators also now permit interferometry to be carried out by independent recording of the signals at the various antennas, and then later correlating the recordings at some central processing facility.  This process is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Long_Baseline_Interferometry" title="Very Long Baseline Interferometry">Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI)</a>.  Interferometry does increase the total signal collected, but its primary purpose is to vastly increase the resolution through a process called <a href="/wiki/Aperture_synthesis" title="Aperture synthesis">Aperture synthesis</a>. This technique works by superposing (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">interfering</a>) the signal <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> from the different telescopes on the principle that <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> that coincide with the same <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> will add to each other while two waves that have opposite phases will cancel each other out. This creates a combined telescope that is equivalent in resolution (though not in sensitivity) to a single antenna whose diameter is equal to the spacing of the antennas furthest apart in the array.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1864" data-file-width="4353" decoding="async" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg/250px-The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg/375px-The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg/500px-The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Atacama_Compact_Array.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">Atacama Large Millimeter Array</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atacama_desert" title="Atacama desert">Atacama desert</a> consisting of 66 12-metre (39 ft), and 7-metre (23 ft) diameter radio telescopes designed to work at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submillimeter_astronomy" title="Submillimeter astronomy">sub-millimeter wavelengths</a></div></div></div>
<p>A high-quality image requires a large number of different separations between telescopes. Projected separation between any two telescopes, as seen from the radio source, is called a baseline. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Array" title="Very Large Array">Very Large Array</a> (VLA) near <a href="/wiki/Socorro,_New_Mexico" title="Socorro, New Mexico">Socorro, New Mexico</a> has 27 telescopes with 351 independent baselines at once, which achieves a resolution of 0.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_seconds" title="Arc seconds">arc seconds</a> at 3 cm wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ryle" title="Martin Ryle">Martin Ryle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Astrophysics_Group" title="Cavendish Astrophysics Group">group in Cambridge</a> obtained a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> for interferometry and aperture synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lloyd%27s_mirror" title="Lloyd's mirror">Lloyd's mirror</a> interferometer was also developed independently in 1946 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Pawsey" title="Joseph Pawsey">Joseph Pawsey</a>'s group at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">University of Sydney</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the early 1950s, the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Interferometer" title="Cambridge Interferometer">Cambridge Interferometer</a> mapped the radio sky to produce the famous <a href="/wiki/Second_Cambridge_Catalogue_of_Radio_Sources" title="Second Cambridge Catalogue of Radio Sources">2C</a> and <a href="/wiki/Third_Cambridge_Catalogue_of_Radio_Sources" title="Third Cambridge Catalogue of Radio Sources">3C</a> surveys of radio sources. An example of a large physically connected radio telescope array is the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Metrewave_Radio_Telescope" title="Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope">Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The largest array, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-Frequency_Array" title="Low-Frequency Array">Low-Frequency Array</a> (LOFAR), finished in 2012, is located in western Europe and consists of about 81,000 small antennas in 48 stations distributed over an area several hundreds of kilometers in diameter and operates between 1.25 and 30 m wavelengths. VLBI systems using post-observation processing have been constructed with antennas thousands of miles apart.  Radio interferometers have also been used to obtain detailed images of the anisotropies and the polarization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Microwave_Background" title="Cosmic Microwave Background">Cosmic Microwave Background</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager" title="Cosmic Background Imager">CBI</a> interferometer in 2004.
</p><p>The world's largest physically connected telescope, the <a href="/wiki/Square_Kilometre_Array" title="Square Kilometre Array">Square Kilometre Array</a> (SKA), is planned to start operations in 2025.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_observations">Astronomical observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Astronomical observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">Radio astronomy</a></div>
<p>Many astronomical objects are not only observable in <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light</a> but also emit <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> at <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio wavelengths</a>. Besides observing energetic objects such as <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>, radio telescopes are able to "image" most astronomical objects such as <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>, and even radio emissions from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
