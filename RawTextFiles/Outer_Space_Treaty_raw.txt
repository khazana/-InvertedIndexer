<p>The <b>Outer Space Treaty</b>, formally the <b>Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> that forms the basis of international <a href="/wiki/Space_law" title="Space law">space law</a>. The treaty was opened for signature in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> on 27 January 1967, and entered into force on 10 October 1967. As of February 2019, 108 countries are parties to the treaty, while another 23 have signed the treaty but have not completed ratification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unoda_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-unoda-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, which is currently recognized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="International recognition of the Republic of China">16 UN member states</a>, ratified the treaty prior to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758" title="United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758">vote to transfer China's seat</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unodacn_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-unodacn-5">[5]</a></sup> The Outer Space Treaty does not ban military activities or conventional weapons within space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_points">Key points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Key points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Outer Space Treaty represents the basic legal framework of international <a href="/wiki/Space_law" title="Space law">space law</a>. Among its principles, it bars states party to the treaty from placing <a href="/wiki/Weapon_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapon of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a> in <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> orbit, installing them on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> or any other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a>, or otherwise stationing them in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. It exclusively limits the use of the Moon and other <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial bodies</a> to peaceful purposes and expressly prohibits their use for testing weapons of any kind, conducting military maneuvers, or establishing military bases, installations, and fortifications (Article IV). However, the treaty does not prohibit the placement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conventional_weapons" title="Conventional weapons">conventional weapons</a> in orbit and thus some highly destructive attack strategies such as <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment" title="Kinetic bombardment">kinetic bombardment</a> are still potentially allowable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The treaty also states that the exploration of outer space shall be done to benefit all countries and that space shall be free for exploration and use by all the States.
</p><p>The treaty explicitly forbids any government to claim a celestial resource such as the Moon or a planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Article II of the treaty states that "outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, is not subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means." However, the State that launches a space object retains jurisdiction and control over that object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The State is also liable for damages caused by its space object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibility_for_activities_in_space">Responsibility for activities in space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Responsibility for activities in space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty deals with international responsibility, stating that "the activities of non-governmental entities in outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, shall require authorization and continuing supervision by the appropriate State Party to the Treaty" and that States Parties shall bear international responsibility for national space activities whether carried out by governmental or non-governmental entities.
</p><p>As a result of discussions arising from <a href="/wiki/Project_West_Ford" title="Project West Ford">Project West Ford</a> in 1963, a consultation clause was included in Article IX of the Outer Space Treaty: "A State Party to the Treaty which has reason to believe that an activity or experiment planned by another State Party in outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, would cause potentially harmful interference with activities in the peaceful exploration and use of outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, may request consultation concerning the activity or experiment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_parties">List of parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: List of parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Outer Space Treaty was opened for signature in the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Soviet Union on 27 January 1967, and entered into force on 10 October 1967. As of February 2019, 108 countries are parties to the treaty, while another 23 have signed the treaty but have not completed ratification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unoda_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-unoda-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple dates indicate the different days in which states submitted their signature or deposition, which varied by location.  This location is noted by: (L) for London, (M) for Moscow, and (W) for Washington, DC.  Also indicated is whether the state became a party by way of signing the treaty and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Ratification" title="Ratification">ratification</a>, or by <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/accession" title="wikt:accession">accession</a> to the treaty after it had closed for signature.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partially_recognized_state_abiding_by_treaty">Partially recognized state abiding by treaty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Partially recognized state abiding by treaty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (Taiwan), which is currently only recognized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="International recognition of the Republic of China">16 UN member states</a>, ratified the treaty prior to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758" title="United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758">vote to transfer China's seat</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) in 1971.  When the PRC subsequently ratified the treaty, they described the Republic of China's (ROC) ratification as "illegal".  The ROC has committed itself to continue to adhere to the requirements of the treaty, and the United States has declared that they still consider them to be "bound by its obligations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unodacn_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-unodacn-5">[5]</a></sup>
