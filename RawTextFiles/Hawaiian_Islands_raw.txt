<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hawaiian Islands</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Hawaiian Islands</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian</a>: <i lang="haw">Mokupuni o Hawai‘i</i>) are an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> of eight major <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>, several <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a>, numerous smaller <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a> in the North <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, extending some 1,500 miles (2,400 kilometers) from the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">island of Hawaiʻi</a> in the south to northernmost <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure Atoll</a>. Formerly the group was known to Europeans and Americans as the <b>Sandwich Islands</b>, a name chosen by <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> in honor of the then First Lord of the Admiralty <a href="/wiki/John_Montagu,_4th_Earl_of_Sandwich" title="John Montagu, 4th Earl of Sandwich">John Montagu, 4th Earl of Sandwich</a>. The contemporary name is derived from the name of the largest island, Hawaii Island.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> now occupies the archipelago almost in its entirety (including the uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Northwestern Hawaiian Islands">Northwestern Hawaiian Islands</a>), with the sole exception of <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">Midway Island</a>, which instead separately belongs to the United States as one of its <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_territories_of_the_United_States" title="Unincorporated territories of the United States">unincorporated territories</a> within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Minor_Outlying_Islands" title="United States Minor Outlying Islands">United States Minor Outlying Islands</a>.
</p><p>The Hawaiian Islands are the exposed peaks of a great undersea mountain range known as the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian%E2%80%93Emperor_seamount_chain" title="Hawaiian–Emperor seamount chain">Hawaiian–Emperor seamount chain</a>, formed by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a> over a <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)#Earth's_mantle" title="Mantle (geology)">Earth's mantle</a>. The islands are about 1,860 miles (3,000 km) from the nearest continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands_and_reefs">Islands and reefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Islands and reefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Hawaii" title="History of Hawaii">History of Hawaii</a></div>
<p>Captain <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> visited the islands on January 18, 1778,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and named them the "Sandwich Islands" in honor of <a href="/wiki/John_Montagu,_4th_Earl_of_Sandwich" title="John Montagu, 4th Earl of Sandwich">John Montagu, 4th Earl of Sandwich</a>, who was one of his sponsors as the <a href="/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Admiralty" title="First Lord of the Admiralty">First Lord of the Admiralty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This name was in use until the 1840s, when the local name "Hawaii" gradually began to take precedence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Hawaiian Islands have a total land area of 6,423.4 square miles (16,636.5 km<sup>2</sup>). Except for <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">Midway</a>, which is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unincorporated_territory" title="Unincorporated territory">unincorporated territory</a> of the United States, these islands and islets are administered as <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>—the 50th state of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guide_to_State_and_Local_Census_Geography_-_Hawaii_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_to_State_and_Local_Census_Geography_-_Hawaii-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii hotspot</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Hawaii_rivers" title="List of Hawaii rivers">List of Hawaii rivers</a></div>
<p>This chain of islands, or <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>, developed as the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> moved slowly northwestward over a <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">Earth's mantle</a> at a rate of approximately 32 miles (51 km) per million years. Thus, the southeast island is volcanically active, whereas the islands on the northwest end of the archipelago are older and typically smaller, due to longer exposure to <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. The age of the archipelago has been estimated using potassium-argon dating methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> From this study and others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> it is estimated that the northwesternmost island, <a href="/wiki/Kure_Atoll" title="Kure Atoll">Kure Atoll</a>, is the oldest at approximately 28 million years (Ma); while the southeasternmost island, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Hawaiʻi</a>, is approximately 0.4 Ma (400,000 years). The only active volcanism in the last 200 years has been on the southeastern island, Hawaiʻi, and on the submerged but growing volcano to the extreme southeast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loihi" title="Loihi">Loʻihi</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Volcano_Observatory" title="Hawaiian Volcano Observatory">Hawaiian Volcano Observatory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a>  documents recent volcanic activity and provides images and interpretations of the volcanism. <a href="/wiki/K%C4%ABlauea" title="Kīlauea">Kīlauea</a> has been erupting nearly continuously since 1983.
</p><p>Almost all of the <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> of the hotspot has the composition of <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>, and so the Hawaiian volcanoes are composed almost entirely of this igneous rock. There is very little coarser-grained <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">diabase</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nephelinite" title="Nephelinite">Nephelinite</a> is exposed on the islands but is extremely rare. The majority of eruptions in Hawaiʻi are <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_eruption" title="Hawaiian eruption">Hawaiian-type eruptions</a> because basaltic magma is relatively fluid compared with magmas typically involved in more explosive eruptions, such as the andesitic magmas that produce some of the spectacular and dangerous eruptions around the margins of the Pacific basin.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hawaii_hotspot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="632" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Hawaii_hotspot.jpg/300px-Hawaii_hotspot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Hawaii_hotspot.jpg/450px-Hawaii_hotspot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Hawaii_hotspot.jpg/600px-Hawaii_hotspot.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hawaii_hotspot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eruptions from the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii hotspot</a> left a trail of underwater mountains across the Pacific over millions of years, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Seamounts" title="Emperor Seamounts">Emperor Seamounts</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hawaiʻi island (the Big Island) is the biggest and youngest island in the chain, built from five volcanoes. <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a>, taking up over half of the Big Island, is the largest <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">shield volcano</a> on the Earth. The measurement from sea level to summit is more than 2.5 miles (4 km), from sea level to sea floor about 3.1 miles (5 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_earthquakes_in_Hawaii" title="List of earthquakes in Hawaii">List of earthquakes in Hawaii</a></div>
<p>The Hawaiian Islands have many <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, generally caused by volcanic activity. Most of the early earthquake monitoring took place in <a href="/wiki/Hilo,_Hawaii" title="Hilo, Hawaii">Hilo</a>, by missionaries <a href="/wiki/Titus_Coan" title="Titus Coan">Titus Coan</a>, Sarah J. Lyman and her family. From 1833 to 1896, approximately 4 or 5 earthquakes were reported per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hawaii accounted for 7.3% of the United States' reported earthquakes with a <a href="/wiki/Richter_magnitude_scale" title="Richter magnitude scale">magnitude</a> 3.5 or greater from 1974 to 2003, with a total 1533 earthquakes. Hawaii ranked as the state with the third most earthquakes over this time period, after <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/2006_Kiholo_Bay_earthquake" title="2006 Kiholo Bay earthquake">On October 15, 2006, there was an earthquake</a> with a magnitude of 6.7 off the northwest coast of the island of Hawaii, near the <a href="/wiki/Kona_District,_Hawaii" title="Kona District, Hawaii">Kona</a> area of the big island. The initial earthquake was followed approximately five minutes later by a magnitude 5.7 <a href="/wiki/Aftershock" title="Aftershock">aftershock</a>. Minor-to-moderate damage was reported on most of the Big Island. Several major roadways became impassable from rock slides, and effects were felt as far away as <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, Oahu, nearly 150 miles (240 km) from the <a href="/wiki/Epicenter" title="Epicenter">epicenter</a>. Power outages lasted for several hours to days. Several water mains ruptured. No deaths or life-threatening injuries were reported.
</p><p>On May 4, 2018 there was a 6.9 earthquake in the zone of volcanic activity from <a href="/wiki/K%C4%ABlauea" title="Kīlauea">Kīlauea</a>.
</p><p>Earthquakes are monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Volcano_Observatory" title="Hawaiian Volcano Observatory">Hawaiian Volcano Observatory</a> run by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tsunamis">Tsunamis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tsunamis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="612" data-file-width="766" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg/300px-1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg/450px-1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg/600px-1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1960-Chilean-tsunami-Hilo-HI-USGS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/1960_Valdivia_earthquake" title="1960 Valdivia earthquake">1960 Chilean tsunami</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hilo" title="Hilo">Hilo</a>, Hawaiʻi, where the tsunami left 61 people dead and 282 seriously injured. The waves reached 35 feet (11 m) high.</div></div></div>
<p>The Hawaiian Islands are subject to <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a>, great <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">waves</a> that strike the shore. Tsunamis are most often caused by <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> somewhere in the Pacific. The waves produced by the earthquakes travel at speeds of 400–500 miles per hour (600–800 km/h) and can affect coastal regions thousands of miles (kilometers) away.
</p><p>Tsunamis may also originate from the Hawaiian Islands. Explosive volcanic activity can cause tsunamis. The island of <a href="/wiki/Molokai" title="Molokai">Molokaʻi</a> had a catastrophic collapse or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debris_avalanche" title="Debris avalanche">debris avalanche</a> over a million years ago; this underwater landslide likely caused tsunamis. The <a href="/wiki/Hilina_Slump" title="Hilina Slump">Hilina Slump</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">island of Hawaiʻi</a> is another potential place for a large landslide and resulting tsunami.
</p><p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Hilo,_Hawaii" title="Hilo, Hawaii">Hilo</a> on the Big Island has been most affected by tsunamis, where the in-rushing water is accentuated by the shape of <a href="/wiki/Hilo_Bay" title="Hilo Bay">Hilo Bay</a>. Coastal cities have tsunami warning sirens.
</p><p>A tsunami resulting from an <a href="/wiki/2010_Chile_earthquake" title="2010 Chile earthquake">earthquake in Chile</a> hit the islands on February 27, 2010. It was relatively minor, but local emergency management officials utilized the latest technology and ordered evacuations in preparation for a possible major event. The Governor declared it a "good drill" for the next major event.
</p><p>A tsunami resulting from an <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">earthquake in Japan</a> hit the islands on <a href="/wiki/March" title="March">March</a> 11, 2011. It was relatively minor, but local officials ordered evacuations in preparation for a possible major event. The tsunami caused about $30.1 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_Open-File_Report_2012-1229_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_Open-File_Report_2012-1229-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_the_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Endemism in the Hawaiian Islands">Endemism in the Hawaiian Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_animal_species_introduced_to_the_Hawaiian_Islands" title="List of animal species introduced to the Hawaiian Islands">List of animal species introduced to the Hawaiian Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_invasive_plant_species_in_Hawaii" title="List of invasive plant species in Hawaii">List of invasive plant species in Hawaii</a></div>
<p>The islands are home to many <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> species. Since human settlement, first by <a href="/wiki/Polynesians" title="Polynesians">Polynesians</a>, non native trees, plants, and animals were introduced. These included species such as rats and pigs, that have preyed on native birds and invertebrates that initially evolved in the absence of such predators. The growing population of humans has also led to deforestation, forest degradation, treeless grasslands, and environmental degradation. As a result, many species which depended on forest habitats and food became extinct--with many current species facing extinction. As humans cleared land for farming, <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monocultural crop production</a> replaced <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">multi-species systems</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">Europeans</a> had a more significant impact, with the promotion of large-scale single-species export agriculture and livestock grazing. This led to increased clearing of forests, and the development of towns, adding many more species to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_the_Hawaiian_Islands" title="List of extinct animals of the Hawaiian Islands">list of extinct animals of the Hawaiian Islands</a>. As of  2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, many of the remaining endemic species are considered endangered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Monument">National Monument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: National Monument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">

<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="759" decoding="async" height="22" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/40px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/60px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Wikinews-logo.svg/80px-Wikinews-logo.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></td>
<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Wikinews has related news:
<i><b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikinews.org/wiki/Bush_proclaims_Northwestern_Hawaiian_Islands_a_National_Monument" title="wikinews:Bush proclaims Northwestern Hawaiian Islands a National Monument"> Bush proclaims Northwestern Hawaiian Islands a National Monument</a></b></i></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>On June 15, 2006, President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> issued a public proclamation creating <a href="/wiki/Papah%C4%81naumoku%C4%81kea_Marine_National_Monument" title="Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument">Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_Act" title="Antiquities Act">Antiquities Act</a> of 1906. The Monument encompasses the northwestern Hawaiian Islands and surrounding waters, forming the largest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Expansion_2016_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Expansion_2016-26">[26]</a></sup> marine wildlife reserve in the world. In August 2010, <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a> added Papahānaumokuākea to its <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Oceania" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Oceania">list</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On August 26, 2016, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> greatly expanded Papahānaumokuākea, quadrupling it from its original size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hawaiian_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Hawaii" title="Climate of Hawaii">Climate of Hawaii</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of the Hawaiian Islands is <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical</a> but it experiences many different climates, depending on altitude and weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup>  The islands receive most rainfall from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a> on their north and east flanks (the windward side) as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_precipitation" title="Orographic precipitation">orographic precipitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup> Coastal areas in general and especially the south and west flanks or <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">leeward</a> sides, tend to be drier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, the lowlands of Hawaiian Islands receive most of their precipitation during the winter months (October to April).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup> Drier conditions generally prevail from May to September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storms</a>, and occasional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a>, tend to occur from July through November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrology_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-hydrology-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
