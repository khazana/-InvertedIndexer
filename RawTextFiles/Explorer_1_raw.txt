<p><b>Explorer 1</b> was the first <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> launched by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and was part of the U.S. participation in the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>. The mission followed the first two satellites the previous year; the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">2</a>, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a> between the two nations.
</p><p>Explorer 1 was launched on January 31, 1958 at 22:48 Eastern Time (February 1, 03:48 UTC) atop the first <a href="/wiki/Juno_I" title="Juno I">Juno</a> booster from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Launch_Complex_26" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Launch Complex 26">LC-26</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Missile Annex</a>, Florida. It was the first spacecraft to detect the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> returning data until its batteries were exhausted after nearly four months. It remained in orbit until 1970, and has been followed by more than 90 scientific spacecrafts in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer_program" title="Explorer program">Explorer series</a>.
</p><p>Explorer 1 was given <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Catalog_Number" title="Satellite Catalog Number">Satellite Catalog Number</a> 4, and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard designation</a> 1958 Alpha 1,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yost_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yost-3">[3]</a></sup> the forerunner to the modern <a href="/wiki/International_Designator" title="International Designator">International Designator</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The U.S. Earth satellite program began in 1954 as a joint <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> proposal, called <a href="/wiki/Project_Orbiter" title="Project Orbiter">Project Orbiter</a>, to put a scientific satellite into <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> during the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>. The proposal, using a military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redstone_(rocket)" title="Redstone (rocket)">Redstone</a> missile, was rejected in 1955 by the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Presidency of Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower administration</a> in favor of the Navy's <a href="/wiki/Project_Vanguard" title="Project Vanguard">Project Vanguard</a>, using a booster advertised as more civilian in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the launch of the Soviet satellite <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> on October 4, 1957, the initial Project Orbiter program was revived as the Explorer program to catch up with the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_age_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-space_age-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Explorer 1 was designed and built by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> (JPL), while a <a href="/wiki/Jupiter-C" title="Jupiter-C">Jupiter-C</a> rocket was modified by the <a href="/wiki/Army_Ballistic_Missile_Agency" title="Army Ballistic Missile Agency">Army Ballistic Missile Agency</a> (ABMA) to accommodate a satellite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a>; the resulting rocket known as the <a href="/wiki/Juno_I" title="Juno I">Juno I</a>. The Jupiter-C design used for the launch had already been flight-tested in <a href="/wiki/Nose_cone" title="Nose cone">nose cone</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">reentry</a> tests for the <a href="/wiki/PGM-19_Jupiter" title="PGM-19 Jupiter">Jupiter</a> <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-range_ballistic_missile" title="Intermediate-range ballistic missile">IRBM</a>, and was modified into Juno I. Working closely together, ABMA and JPL completed the job of modifying the Jupiter-C and building Explorer 1 in 84 days. However, before work was completed, the Soviet Union launched a second satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">Sputnik 2</a>, on November 3, 1957. The U.S. Navy's attempt to put the first U.S. satellite into orbit failed with the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_TV3" title="Vanguard TV3">Vanguard TV3</a> on December 6, 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Explorer1_preparations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1401" data-file-width="1111" decoding="async" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Explorer1_preparations.jpg/220px-Explorer1_preparations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Explorer1_preparations.jpg/330px-Explorer1_preparations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Explorer1_preparations.jpg/440px-Explorer1_preparations.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Explorer1_preparations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Explorer 1 is mated to its booster at LC-26</div></div></div>
<p>Explorer 1 was designed and built by the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>'s JPL under the direction of Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Hayward_Pickering" title="William Hayward Pickering">William H. Pickering</a>. It was the second satellite to carry a mission payload (Sputnik 2 was the first).
</p><p>The total mass of the satellite was 13.37 kilograms (30.80 lb), of which 8.3 kg (18.3 lb) were instrumentation. In comparison, the mass of the first Soviet satellite Sputnik 1 was 83.6 kg (184 lb). The instrument section at the front end of the satellite and the empty scaled-down <a href="/wiki/MGM-29_Sergeant" title="MGM-29 Sergeant">fourth-stage rocket casing</a> orbited as a single unit, spinning around its long axis at 750 revolutions per minute.
</p><p>Data from the scientific instruments was transmitted to the ground by two <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a>. A 60 milliwatt transmitter fed a <a href="/wiki/Dipole_antenna" title="Dipole antenna">dipole antenna</a> consisting of two fiberglass <a href="/wiki/Slot_antenna" title="Slot antenna">slot antennas</a> in the body of the satellite operating on 108.03 <a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">MHz</a>, and four flexible whips forming a <a href="/wiki/Turnstile_antenna" title="Turnstile antenna">turnstile antenna</a> were fed by a 10 milliwatt transmitter operating on 108.00 MHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data_sheet_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-data_sheet-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of the limited space available and the requirements for low weight, the payload instrumentation was designed and built with simplicity and high reliability in mind, using <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> in its electronics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-semiconductormuseum_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-semiconductormuseum-10">[10]</a></sup> A total of 29 transistors were used in Explorer 1, plus additional ones in the Army's micrometeorite amplifier. Electrical power was provided by <a href="/wiki/Mercury_battery" title="Mercury battery">mercury chemical batteries</a> that made up approximately 40 percent of the payload weight.
</p><p>
The external skin of the instrument section was sandblasted stainless steel with white stripes.  Several other color schemes had been tested, resulting in backup articles, models, and photographs showing different configurations, including alternate white and green striping and blue stripes alternating with copper.  The final coloration was determined by studies of shadow–sunlight intervals based on firing time, <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a>.</p><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:722px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Explorer1_sketch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="720" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/Explorer1_sketch.jpg" width="720"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><i>Explorer 1 schematic</i></div></div></div></div>

<p>The Explorer 1 payload consisted of the Iowa <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Ray" title="Cosmic Ray">Cosmic Ray</a> Instrument without a tape data recorder which was not modified in time to make it onto the spacecraft. The real-time data received on the ground was therefore very sparse and puzzling showing normal counting rates and no counts at all. The later Explorer 3 mission, which included a tape data recorder in the payload, provided the additional data for confirmation of the earlier Explorer 1 data.
</p><p>The scientific instrumentation of Explorer 1 was designed and built under the direction of Dr. <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a> containing:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data_sheet_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-data_sheet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Anton 314 omnidirectional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geiger-M%C3%BCller_tube" title="Geiger-Müller tube">Geiger-Müller tube</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/George_H._Ludwig" title="George H. Ludwig">Dr. George Ludwig</a> of Iowa's Cosmic Ray Laboratory, to detect <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. It could detect <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> with E &gt; 30 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> with E &gt; 3 MeV. Most of the time the instrument was <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saturation" title="wikt:saturation">saturated</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmic_ray_detector_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cosmic_ray_detector-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Five <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">temperature sensors</a> (one internal, three external and one on the nose cone);</li>
<li>Acoustic detector (crystal <a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">transducer</a> and solid-state <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifier</a>) to detect <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">micrometeorite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a>) impacts. It responded to micrometeorite impacts on the spacecraft skin in such a way that each impact would be a function of <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>. Its effective area was 0.075 m<sup>2</sup> and the average threshold sensitivity was 2.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−3</sup> g cm/s;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micrometeorite_detector_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-micrometeorite_detector-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manring_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-manring-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Wire grid detector, also to detect micrometeorite impacts. It consisted of 12 parallel connected cards mounted in a <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> supporting ring. Each card was wound with two layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enameled_wire" title="Enameled wire">enameled</a> <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> wire with a diameter of 17 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a> (21 µm with the enamel insulation included) in such way that a total area of 1 cm by 1 cm was completely covered. If a micrometeorite of about 10 µm impacted, it would fracture the wire, destroy the electrical connection, and thus record the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micrometeorite_detector_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-micrometeorite_detector-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manring_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-manring-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After a <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>-related delay on 28 January 1958, at 10:48:16 PM <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Time" title="Eastern Time">Eastern Time</a> on 31 January<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196810_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196810-14">[14]</a></sup> the Juno I rocket was launched, putting Explorer 1 into orbit with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> of 358 kilometers (222 mi) and an <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apogee</a> of 2,550 kilometers (1,580 mi) having a period of 114.8 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-facts-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trajectory_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-trajectory-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarsystem_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-solarsystem-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldstone_Tracking_Station" title="Goldstone Tracking Station">Goldstone Tracking Station</a> could not report after 90 minutes as planned whether the launch had succeeded because the orbit was larger than expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196810_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ley196810-14">[14]</a></sup> At about 1:30 a.m. ET, after confirming that Explorer 1 was indeed in orbit, a news conference was held in the Great Hall at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in Washington, DC to announce it to the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovering_Earth's_Radiation_Belts:_Remembering_Explorer_1_and_3_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovering_Earth's_Radiation_Belts:_Remembering_Explorer_1_and_3-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The original expected lifetime of the satellite before <a href="/wiki/Orbital_decay" title="Orbital decay">orbital decay</a> was three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196810_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-ley196810-14">[14]</a></sup> Mercury batteries powered the high-power transmitter for 31 days and the low-power transmitter for 105 days. Explorer 1 stopped transmission of data on May 23, 1958<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadunaisky_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zadunaisky-19">[19]</a></sup> when its batteries died, but remained in orbit for more than 12 years. It reentered the atmosphere over the Pacific Ocean on March 31, 1970 after more than 58,000 orbits.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Explorer1_people.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2830" data-file-width="2231" decoding="async" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Explorer1_people.jpg/220px-Explorer1_people.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Explorer1_people.jpg/330px-Explorer1_people.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Explorer1_people.jpg/440px-Explorer1_people.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Explorer1_people.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Hayward_Pickering" title="William Hayward Pickering">William Hayward Pickering</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> display a full-scale model of Explorer 1 at a crowded news conference in Washington, DC after confirmation the satellite was in orbit.</div></div></div>
<p>Explorer 1 changed <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation axis</a> after launch. The elongated body of the spacecraft had been designed to spin about its long (least-<a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>) axis but refused to do so, and instead started <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precessing</a> due to energy <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> from flexible structural elements. Later it was understood that on general grounds, the body ends up in the spin state that minimizes the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">rotational energy</a> for a fixed angular momentum (this being the maximal-inertia axis). This motivated the first further development of the <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Eulerian</a> theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rigid_body_kinetics" title="Rigid body kinetics">rigid body dynamics</a> after nearly 200 years—to address this kind of momentum-preserving energy dissipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometimes the instrumentation reported the expected cosmic ray count (approximately 30 counts per second) but other times it would show a peculiar zero counts per second. The University of Iowa (under <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a>) observed that all of the zero counts per second reports were from an altitude of more than 2,000 kilometres (1,200 mi) over South America, while passes at 500 km (310 mi) would show the expected level of cosmic rays. Later, after <a href="/wiki/Explorer_3" title="Explorer 3">Explorer 3</a>, it was concluded that the original Geiger counter had been overwhelmed ("saturated") by strong radiation coming from a belt of charged particles trapped in space by the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>. This belt of charged particles is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>. The discovery was considered to be one of the outstanding discoveries of the International Geophysical Year.
</p><p>The acoustic micrometeorite detector detected 145 impacts of cosmic dust in 78,750 seconds. This calculates to an average impact rate of 8.0<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−3</sup> impacts m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> over the twelve-day period (29 impacts per hour per square meter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dubin_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dubin-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Explorer 1 was the first of the long-running <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer_program" title="Explorer program">Explorer program</a>. A follow-up to the first mission, <a href="/wiki/Explorer-1_Prime" title="Explorer-1 Prime">Explorer-1 [PRIME]</a>, was successfully launched aboard a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> rocket in late October, 2011. The PRIME was built using modern satellite construction techniques.  The orbiting satellite was a backup, since the initial Explorer-1 PRIME, launched on March 4, 2011, failed to reach orbit due to a problem in the launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>An identically constructed flight backup of Explorer 1 is on display in the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>, Milestones of Flight Gallery in Washington, DC.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Launch_Complex_26" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Launch Complex 26">Launch Pad 26</a> was deactivated in 1963, and was designated for use as a museum in 1964, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Space_and_Missile_Museum" title="Air Force Space and Missile Museum">Air Force Space and Missile Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  Here too, a full-scale Explorer 1 is on display, but this one is a mockup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Explorer 1 was the first orbital object to return scientific data.  Its launch happened 100 years to the day after the <a href="/wiki/SS_Great_Eastern" title="SS Great Eastern">SS <i>Great Eastern</i></a> was launched.  The ship was larger than any that came before it, and accomplished laying the first lasting <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">transatlantic telegraph cable</a>.  Both events served to more closely knit the world together.
</p>
