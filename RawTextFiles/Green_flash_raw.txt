<p><b>Green flashes</b> and <b>green rays</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_optics" title="Meteorological optics"> meteorological optical phenomena</a> that sometimes occur just after <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> or right before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>. When the conditions are right, a distinct green spot is briefly visible above the upper rim of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s disk; the green appearance usually lasts for no more than a second or two. Rarely, the green flash can resemble a green ray shooting up from the sunset (or sunrise) point.
</p><p>Green flashes occur because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> can cause the light from the sun to separate out into different colors. Green flashes are a group of similar phenomena which stem from slightly different causes, and therefore some types of green flashes are more common than others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-young-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observing">Observing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green flashes may be observed from any altitude. They usually are seen at an unobstructed <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, such as over the ocean, but are possible over cloud tops and mountain tops as well. They may occur at any latitude, although at the equator the flash rarely lasts longer than a second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lits_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lits-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A green flash also may be observed in association with the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and bright planets at the horizon, including <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lits_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-lits-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With an unrestricted view of the horizon, green flashes are regularly seen by airline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilot_(aircraft)" title="Pilot (aircraft)">pilots</a>, particularly when flying westwards as the sunset is slowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lits_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-lits-2">[2]</a></sup> If the atmosphere is layered the green flash may appear as a series of flashes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lits_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-lits-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>While observing at the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Observatory" title="Vatican Observatory">Vatican Observatory</a> in 1960, D.K.J. O'Connell produced the first color photographs of a green flash at sunset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lits_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-lits-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green flashes are enhanced by <a href="/wiki/Mirage_of_astronomical_objects" title="Mirage of astronomical objects">mirages</a>, which increase <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a>. A green flash is more likely to be seen in stable, clear air, when more of the light from the setting sun reaches the observer without being scattered. One might expect to see a blue flash, since blue light is refracted most of all, and the blue component of the sun's light is therefore the very last to disappear below the horizon, but the blue is preferentially scattered out of the line of sight, and the remaining light ends up appearing green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Explaining_Green_Flashes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Explaining_Green_Flashes-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>With slight <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnification</a>, a green rim on the top of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar disk</a> may be seen on most clear-day sunsets, although the flash or ray effects require a stronger layering of the atmosphere and a mirage, which serves to magnify the green from a fraction of a second to a couple of seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Explaining_Green_Flashes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Explaining_Green_Flashes-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "green flash" description relates to a group of optical phenomena, some of which are listed below:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-young-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<table class="wikitable">

<th>Type</th>
<th>Characteristics</th>
<th>Conditions</th>
<th>Best seen from...
</th></tr>
<tr style="background:mintcream">
<td>Inferior-mirage flash</td>
<td>Joule's "last glimpse"; oval, flattened below; lasts 1 or 2 seconds</td>
<td>Surface warmer than the overlying air</td>
<td>Close to sea level
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Mock-mirage flash</td>
<td>Indentations seem to "pinch off" a thin, pointy strip from the upper rim of the Sun; lasts 1 or 2 seconds</td>
<td>Atmospheric inversion layer below eye level; surface colder than air</td>

</td></tr>
<tr style="background:mintcream">
<td>Sub-<a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_duct" title="Atmospheric duct">duct</a> flash</td>

<td>Sea level
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The majority of flashes observed are inferior-mirage or mock-mirage effects, with the others constituting only 1% of reports. Some types not listed in the table above, such as the cloud-top flash (seen as the sun sinks into a coastal <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, or at distant <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus clouds</a>), are not understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-young-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blue_flashes">Blue flashes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blue flashes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_glow" title="Blue glow">Blue glow</a>.</div>
<p>Rarely, the amount of blue light is sufficient to be visible as a "blue flash".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_rim">Green rim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Green rim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="945" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg/220px-Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg/330px-Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg/440px-Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green_rim_of_the_setting_sun.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The upper rim is green while the lower one is red, as the sun sets behind the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2533" data-file-width="3800" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg/220px-Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg/330px-Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg/440px-Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green_rim_and_green_flashes_in_SF.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The green rim and flashes of a setting sun</div></div></div>
<p>As an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> sets or rises in relation to the horizon, the light it emits travels through Earth's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, which works as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prism</a> separating the light into different colors. The color of the upper rim of an astronomical object could go from green to blue to violet depending on the decrease in concentration of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">pollutants</a>, as they spread throughout an increasing volume of atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dispersive_refraction_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dispersive_refraction-8">[8]</a></sup> The lower rim of an astronomical object is always red.<br/>A green rim is very thin and is difficult or impossible to see with the naked eye. In usual conditions, a green rim of an astronomical object gets fainter when an astronomical object is very low above the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> because of atmospheric reddening,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_and_red_rims_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green_and_red_rims-9">[9]</a></sup> but sometimes the conditions are right to see a green rim just above the horizon. The following quote describes what was probably the longest observation of a green rim, which at times could have been a green flash. It was seen on and off for 35 minutes by members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Evelyn_Byrd" title="Richard Evelyn Byrd">Richard Evelyn Byrd</a> party from the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Little_America_(exploration_base)" title="Little America (exploration base)">Little America exploration base</a> in 1934:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There was a rush for the surface and as eyes turned southward, they saw a tiny but brilliant green spot where the last ray of the upper rim of the sun hung on the skyline. It lasted an appreciable length of time, several seconds at least, and no sooner disappeared than it flashed forth again. Altogether it remained on the horizon with short interruptions for thirty-five minutes.<br/>When it disappeared momentarily it seemed to have been shut off by a tiny spurt, an inequality in the skyline caused by the barrier surface.<br/>Even by moving the head up a few inches it would disappear and reappear again and after it had finally disappeared from view it could be recaptured by climbing up the first few steps of the <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antanea</a> [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-byrd_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-byrd-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>For the explorers to have seen a green rim on and off for 35 minutes there must have been some mirage effect present.
</p><p>A green rim is present at every sunset but it is too thin to be seen with the naked eye. Often a green rim changes to a green flash and back again during the same sunset. The best time to observe a green rim is about 10 minutes before sunset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_and_red_rims_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Green_and_red_rims-9">[9]</a></sup> That is too early to use any <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnification</a> like <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> to look directly at the Sun without potential harm to the eyes. (Of course, a magnified image might be projected onto a sheet of paper for safe viewing.) As the sun gets closer to the horizon, the green rim becomes fainter due to atmospheric reddening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_and_red_rims_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Green_and_red_rims-9">[9]</a></sup> According to the above, it is probably correct to conclude that although a green rim is present during every sunset, a green flash is rarer because of the required <a href="/wiki/Mirage_of_astronomical_objects" title="Mirage of astronomical objects">mirage</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_flash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s 1882 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Ray" title="The Green Ray">The Green Ray</a></i> helped to popularize the green flash phenomenon. A 1986 film also called <a href="/wiki/The_Green_Ray_(film)" title="The Green Ray (film)"><i>The Green Ray</i></a> uses the green flash and Verne's book as a plot device. Additionally, the green flash has inspired or been mentioned in:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_Holt" title="Victoria Holt">Victoria Holt</a>'s 1976 novel <i>Pride of the Peacock</i>, which uses both the green flash and an <a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a> named for the phenomenon as plot devices</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>' 1981 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Cities_of_the_Red_Night" title="Cities of the Red Night">Cities of the Red Night</a></i></li>
<li><i>The Green Ray</i>, a piece of orchestral music composed by <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Bryars" title="Gavin Bryars">Gavin Bryars</a> and first performed in 1991<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_At_World%27s_End" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End">Pirates of the Caribbean: At World's End</a></i>, a 2007 film that features green flashes as a plot point.</li>
<li>In the Tacita Dean's video "The green ray", a shoot of the phenomenon to an artproject in Tate Modern Gallery.</li></ul>
