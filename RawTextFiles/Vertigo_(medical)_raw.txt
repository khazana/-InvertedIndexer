<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Vertigo</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Vertigo</b> is a symptom where a person feels as if they or the objects around them are moving when they are not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> Often it feels like a spinning or swaying movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> This may be associated with <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, sweating, or difficulties walking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> It is typically worse when the head is moved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Vertigo is the most common type of <a href="/wiki/Dizziness" title="Dizziness">dizziness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common diseases that result in vertigo are <a href="/wiki/Benign_paroxysmal_positional_vertigo" title="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo">benign paroxysmal positional vertigo</a> (BPPV), <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9ni%C3%A8re%27s_disease" title="Ménière's disease">Ménière's disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Labyrinthitis" title="Labyrinthitis">labyrinthitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Less common causes include <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_tumors" title="Brain tumors">brain tumors</a>, brain injury, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestibular_migraine" title="Vestibular migraine">migraines</a>, trauma, and <a href="/wiki/Alternobaric_vertigo" title="Alternobaric vertigo">uneven pressures between the middle ears</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Physiologic vertigo may occur following being exposed to motion for a prolonged period such as when on a ship or simply following spinning with the eyes closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Other causes may include toxin exposures such as to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Vertigo typically indicates a problem in a part of the <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">vestibular system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> Other causes of dizziness include <a href="/wiki/Presyncope" title="Presyncope">presyncope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disequilibrium_(medicine)" title="Disequilibrium (medicine)">disequilibrium</a>, and non-specific dizziness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo is more likely in someone who gets repeated episodes of vertigo with movement and is otherwise normal between these episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> The episodes of vertigo should last less than one minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dix-Hallpike_test" title="Dix-Hallpike test">Dix-Hallpike test</a> typically produces a period of rapid eye movements known as <a href="/wiki/Nystagmus" title="Nystagmus">nystagmus</a> in this condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> In Ménière's disease there is often <a href="/wiki/Tinnitus" title="Tinnitus">ringing in the ears</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hearing_loss" title="Hearing loss">hearing loss</a>, and the attacks of vertigo last more than twenty minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> In labyrinthitis the onset of vertigo is sudden and the nystagmus occurs without movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> In this condition vertigo can last for days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup> More severe causes should also be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> This is especially true if other problems such as weakness, headache, <a href="/wiki/Diplopia" title="Diplopia">double vision</a>, or numbness occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hog2015_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Hog2015-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dizziness affects approximately 20–40% of people at some point in time, while about 7.5–10% have vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bre2011_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2011-3">[3]</a></sup> About 5% have vertigo in a given year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> It becomes more common with age and affects women two to three times more often than men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> Vertigo accounts for about 2–3% of emergency department visits in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">developed world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vertigo is classified into either peripheral or central depending on the location of the dysfunction of the vestibular pathway,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hearing_loss_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hearing_loss-11">[11]</a></sup>  although it can also be caused by psychological factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlChapter_14:_Evaluation_of_the_Dizzy_Patient_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlChapter_14:_Evaluation_of_the_Dizzy_Patient-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vertigo can also be classified into objective, subjective, and pseudovertigo. Objective vertigo describes when the person has the sensation that stationary objects in the environment are moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_&amp;_Co_Inc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merck_&amp;_Co_Inc-13">[13]</a></sup> Subjective vertigo refers to when the person feels as if they are moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_&amp;_Co_Inc_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merck_&amp;_Co_Inc-13">[13]</a></sup> The third type is known as pseudovertigo, an intensive sensation of rotation inside the person's head. While this classification appears in textbooks, it has little to do with the <a href="/wiki/Pathophysiology" title="Pathophysiology">pathophysiology</a> or treatment of vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_and_Victor’s_Principles_of_Neurology_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams_and_Victor’s_Principles_of_Neurology-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peripheral">Peripheral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Peripheral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vertigo that is caused by problems with the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">vestibular system</a>, which is composed of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semicircular_canal" title="Semicircular canal">semicircular canals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vestibule_of_the_ear" title="Vestibule of the ear">vestibule</a> (<a href="/wiki/Utricle_(ear)" title="Utricle (ear)">utricle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saccule" title="Saccule">saccule</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_nerve" title="Vestibular nerve">vestibular nerve</a> is called "peripheral", "otologic" or "vestibular" vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medline_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medline-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup> The most common cause is benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BPPV" title="BPPV">BPPV</a>), which accounts for 32% of all peripheral vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup> Other causes include <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9ni%C3%A8re%27s_disease" title="Ménière's disease">Ménière's disease</a> (12%), <a href="/wiki/Superior_canal_dehiscence_syndrome" title="Superior canal dehiscence syndrome">superior canal dehiscence syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labyrinthitis" title="Labyrinthitis">labyrinthitis</a>, and visual vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Any cause of inflammation such as <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">common cold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a>, and bacterial infections may cause transient vertigo if it involves the inner ear, as may chemical insults (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Aminoglycoside" title="Aminoglycoside">aminoglycosides</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aminoglycoside_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-aminoglycoside-18">[18]</a></sup> or physical trauma (e.g., skull fractures).  <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">Motion sickness</a> is sometimes classified as a cause of peripheral vertigo.
</p><p>People with peripheral vertigo typically present with mild to moderate <a href="/wiki/Balance_disorder" title="Balance disorder">imbalance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafness" title="Deafness">hearing loss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tinnitus" title="Tinnitus">tinnitus</a>, fullness, and pain in the ear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition, lesions of the internal auditory canal may be associated with facial weakness on the same side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup> Due to a rapid compensation process, acute vertigo as a result of a peripheral lesion tends to improve in a short period of time (days to weeks).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central">Central</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Central">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vertigo that arises from injury to the balance centers of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> (CNS), often from a lesion in the <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medline_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Medline-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advances_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advances-19">[19]</a></sup> is called "central" vertigo and is generally associated with less prominent movement illusion and <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> than vertigo of peripheral origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dieterich_(2007)_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dieterich_(2007)-20">[20]</a></sup>  Central vertigo may have accompanying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurological_deficit" title="Neurological deficit">neurologic deficits</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slurred_speech" title="Slurred speech">slurred speech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diplopia" title="Diplopia">double vision</a>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathologic_nystagmus" title="Pathologic nystagmus">pathologic nystagmus</a> (which is pure vertical/torsional).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dieterich_(2007)_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dieterich_(2007)-20">[20]</a></sup> Central pathology can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">disequilibrium</a> which is the sensation of being off balance. The <a href="/wiki/Balance_disorder" title="Balance disorder">balance disorder</a> associated with central lesions causing vertigo is often so severe that many patients are unable to stand or walk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of conditions that involve the central nervous system may lead to vertigo including:  lesions caused by <a href="/wiki/Infarction" title="Infarction">infarctions</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhagic_stroke" title="Hemorrhagic stroke">hemorrhage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brain_tumor" title="Brain tumor">tumors</a> present in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebellopontine_angle" title="Cerebellopontine angle">cerebellopontine angle</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_schwannoma" title="Vestibular schwannoma">vestibular schwannoma</a> or cerebellar tumors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hearing_loss_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hearing_loss-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adolescent_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-adolescent-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervical_spine" title="Cervical spine">cervical spine</a> disorders such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervical_spondylosis" title="Cervical spondylosis">cervical spondylosis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hearing_loss_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hearing_loss-11">[11]</a></sup> degenerative ataxia disorders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Migraine" title="Migraine">migraine headaches</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lateral_medullary_syndrome" title="Lateral medullary syndrome">lateral medullary syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiari_malformation" title="Chiari malformation">Chiari malformation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parkinsonism" title="Parkinsonism">parkinsonism</a>, as well as cerebral dysfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup> Central vertigo may not improve or may do so more slowly than vertigo caused by disturbance to peripheral structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karatas2008_16-9"><a href="#cite_note-Karatas2008-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vertigo is a sensation of spinning while stationary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  It is commonly associated with <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adolescent_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-adolescent-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Balance_disorder" title="Balance disorder">unsteadiness</a> (postural instability),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advances_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Advances-19">[19]</a></sup> falls,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> changes to a person's thoughts, and difficulties in walking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rehab_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rehab-24">[24]</a></sup>  Recurrent episodes in those with vertigo are common and frequently impair the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Blurred_vision" title="Blurred vision">Blurred vision</a>, difficulty in speaking, a lowered level of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a>, and hearing loss may also occur. The signs and symptoms of vertigo can present as a persistent (insidious) onset or an episodic (sudden) onset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Persistent onset vertigo is characterized by symptoms lasting for longer than one day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-25">[25]</a></sup> and is caused by degenerative changes that affect balance as people age. Naturally, the nerve conduction slows with aging and a decreased vibratory sensation is common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
Additionally, there is a degeneration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osseous_ampullae" title="Osseous ampullae">ampulla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otolith" title="Otolith">otolith</a> organs with an increase in age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Persistent onset is commonly paired with central vertigo signs and symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-one-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The characteristics of an episodic onset vertigo are indicated by symptoms lasting for a smaller, more memorable amount of time, typically lasting for only seconds to minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-one-25">[25]</a></sup> Typically, episodic vertigo is correlated with peripheral symptoms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The neurochemistry of vertigo includes six primary <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a> that have been identified between the three-neuron arc<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> that drives the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestibulo-ocular_reflex" title="Vestibulo-ocular reflex">vestibulo-ocular reflex</a> (VOR). Glutamate maintains the resting discharge of the central vestibular neurons and may modulate <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synapse" title="Chemical synapse">synaptic transmission</a> in all three neurons of the VOR arc. Acetylcholine appears to function as an excitatory neurotransmitter in both the peripheral and central synapses. <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-Aminobutyric acid">Gamma-Aminobutyric acid</a> (GABA) is thought to be inhibitory for the commissures of the <a href="/wiki/Medial_vestibular_nucleus" title="Medial vestibular nucleus">medial vestibular nucleus</a>, the connections between the cerebellar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purkinje_cells" title="Purkinje cells">Purkinje cells</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_vestibular_nucleus" title="Lateral vestibular nucleus">lateral vestibular nucleus</a>, and the vertical VOR.
</p><p>Three other neurotransmitters work centrally. <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">Dopamine</a> may accelerate vestibular compensation. <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">Norepinephrine</a> modulates the intensity of central reactions to vestibular stimulation and facilitates compensation. <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">Histamine</a> is present only centrally, but its role is unclear. Dopamine, histamine, <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a>, and acetylcholine are neurotransmitters thought to produce vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> It is known that centrally acting antihistamines modulate the symptoms of acute symptomatic vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Australia_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Australia-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_approach">Diagnostic approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diagnostic approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tests for vertigo often attempt to elicit nystagmus and to differentiate vertigo from other causes of dizziness such as <a href="/wiki/Presyncope" title="Presyncope">presyncope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation_syndrome" title="Hyperventilation syndrome">hyperventilation syndrome</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">disequilibrium</a>, or psychiatric causes of lightheadedness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> Tests of <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">vestibular system</a> (balance) function include <a href="/wiki/Electronystagmography" title="Electronystagmography">electronystagmography</a> (ENG),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> Dix-Hallpike maneuver,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> rotation tests, head-thrust test,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Caloric_reflex_test" title="Caloric reflex test">caloric reflex test</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Posturography" title="Posturography">computerized dynamic posturography</a> (CDP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tests_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-tests-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The HINTS test, which is a combination of three physical exam tests that may be performed by physicians at the bedside has been deemed helpful in differentiating between central and peripheral causes of vertigo. The HINTS test involves the horizontal head impulse test, observation of nystagmus on primary gaze, and the test of skew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computerized_tomography" title="Computerized tomography">CT scans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRIs</a> are sometimes used by physicians when diagnosing vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adolescent_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-adolescent-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tests of <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory system</a> (hearing) function include <a href="/wiki/Pure_tone_audiometry" title="Pure tone audiometry">pure tone audiometry</a>, speech audiometry, <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_reflex" title="Acoustic reflex">acoustic reflex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrocochleography" title="Electrocochleography">electrocochleography</a> (ECoG), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otoacoustic_emissions" title="Otoacoustic emissions">otoacoustic emissions</a> (OAE), and the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_brainstem_response" title="Auditory brainstem response">auditory brainstem response test</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tests_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-tests-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of specific conditions can cause vertigo.  In the elderly, however, the condition is often multifactorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent history of <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a> can indicate a possibility of barotrauma or decompression sickness involvement but does not exclude all other possibilities. The dive profile (which is frequently recorded by <a href="/wiki/Dive_computer" title="Dive computer">dive computer</a>) can be useful to assess a probability for decompression sickness, which can be confirmed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therapeutic_recompression" title="Therapeutic recompression">therapeutic recompression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nachum_et_al_2001_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nachum_et_al_2001-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benign_paroxysmal_positional_vertigo">Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Benign_paroxysmal_positional_vertigo" title="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo">Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo</a> (BPPV) is the most common vestibular disorder<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bre2011_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2011-3">[3]</a></sup> and occurs when loose <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> debris has broken off of the otoconial membrane and enters a semicircular canal thereby creating the sensation of motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> Patients with BPPV may experience brief periods of vertigo, usually under a minute,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-14"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> which occur with change in the position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benign_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benign-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>This is the most common cause of vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> It occurs in 0.6% of the population yearly with 10% having an attack during their lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup>  It is believed to be due to a mechanical malfunction of the inner ear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup>  BPPV may be diagnosed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dix-Hallpike_test" title="Dix-Hallpike test">Dix-Hallpike test</a> and can be effectively treated with repositioning movements such as the <a href="/wiki/Epley_maneuver" title="Epley maneuver">Epley maneuver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benign_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benign-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brazil_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="M.C3.A9ni.C3.A8re.27s_disease"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ménière's_disease">Ménière's disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ménière's disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9ni%C3%A8re%27s_disease" title="Ménière's disease">Ménière's disease</a> is an inner ear disorder of unknown origin, but is thought to be caused by an increase in the amount of <a href="/wiki/Endolymph" title="Endolymph">endolymphatic fluid</a> present in the inner ear (endolymphatic hydrops).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> However, this idea has not been directly confirmed with <a href="/wiki/Histopathology" title="Histopathology">histopathologic</a> studies but <a href="/wiki/Electrophysiology_study" title="Electrophysiology study">electrophysiologic studies</a> have been suggestive of this mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relentless_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-relentless-37">[37]</a></sup> Ménière's disease frequently presents with recurrent, spontaneous attacks of severe vertigo in combination with ringing in the ears (<a href="/wiki/Tinnitus" title="Tinnitus">tinnitus</a>), a feeling of pressure or fullness in the ear (aural fullness), severe nausea or vomiting, imbalance, and hearing loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-15"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-one-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relentless_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-relentless-37">[37]</a></sup> As the disease worsens, hearing loss will progress.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labyrinthitis">Labyrinthitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Labyrinthitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Labyrinthitis" title="Labyrinthitis">Labyrinthitis</a> presents with severe vertigo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> with associated nausea, vomiting, and generalized imbalance and is believed to be caused by a viral infection of the inner ear though several theories have been put forward and the cause remains uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-16"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neuritis_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neuritis-38">[38]</a></sup> Individuals with vestibular neuritis do not typically have auditory symptoms but may experience a sensation of aural fullness or tinnitus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neuritis_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neuritis-38">[38]</a></sup> Persisting balance problems may remain in 30% of people affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vestibular_migraine">Vestibular migraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vestibular migraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestibular_migraine" title="Vestibular migraine">Vestibular migraine</a> is the association of vertigo and <a href="/wiki/Migraine" title="Migraine">migraines</a> and is one of the most common causes of recurrent, spontaneous episodes of vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bre2011_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2011-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-11"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> The cause of vestibular migraines is currently unclear;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bre2011_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2011-3">[3]</a></sup> however, one hypothesized cause is that the stimulation of the <a href="/wiki/Trigeminal_nerve" title="Trigeminal nerve">trigeminal nerve</a> leads to <a href="/wiki/Nystagmus" title="Nystagmus">nystagmus</a> in individuals suffering from migraines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other suggested causes of vestibular migraines include the following: unilateral neuronal instability of the vestibular nerve, idiopathic asymmetric activation of the vestibular nuclei in the brainstem, and <a href="/wiki/Vasospasm" title="Vasospasm">vasospasm</a> of the blood vessels supplying the labyrinth or central vestibular pathways resulting in <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemia</a> to these structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adolescent_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-adolescent-21">[21]</a></sup> Vestibular migraines are estimated to affect 1-3% of the general population<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epi09_10-12"><a href="#cite_note-Epi09-10">[10]</a></sup> and may affect 10% of migraine patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP2010_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, vestibular migraines tend to occur more often in women and rarely affect individuals after the sixth decade of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bre2011_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2011-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motion_sickness">Motion sickness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Motion sickness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">Motion sickness</a> is common and is related to vestibular migraine. It is nausea and vomiting in response to motion and is typically worse if the journey is on a winding road or involves lots of stops and starts, or if the person is reading in a moving car. It is caused by a mismatch between visual input and vestibular sensation. For example, the person is reading a book which is stationary in relation to the body but the vestibular system senses that the car, and thus the body, is moving.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternobaric_vertigo">Alternobaric vertigo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alternobaric vertigo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alternobaric_vertigo" title="Alternobaric vertigo">Alternobaric vertigo</a></div>
<p>Alternobaric vertigo is caused by a pressure difference between the middle ear cavities, usually due to blockage or partial blockage of one eustachian tube, usually when flying or diving underwater. It is most pronounced when the diver is in the vertical position; the spinning is towards the ear with the higher pressure and tends to develop when the pressures differ by 60 cm of water or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_2008_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_2008-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_1976_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ross_1976-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decompression_sickness">Decompression sickness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Decompression sickness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">Decompression sickness</a></div>
<p>Vertigo is recorded as a symptom of decompression sickness in 5.3% of cases by the US Navy as reported by Powell, 2008 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_2008_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_2008-39">[39]</a></sup> It including isobaric decompression sickness.
</p><p>Decompression sickness can also be caused at a constant ambient pressure when switching between gas mixtures containing different proportions of inert gas. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Isobaric_counterdiffusion" title="Isobaric counterdiffusion">isobaric counterdiffusion</a>, and presents a problem for very deep dives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamilton_&amp;_Thalmann_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton_&amp;_Thalmann-41">[41]</a></sup> For example, after using a very helium-rich <a href="/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)" title="Trimix (breathing gas)">trimix</a> at the deepest part of the dive, a diver will switch to mixtures containing progressively less helium and more oxygen and nitrogen during the ascent. Nitrogen diffuses into tissues 2.65 times slower than helium, but is about 4.5 times more soluble. Switching between gas mixtures that have very different fractions of nitrogen and helium can result in "fast" tissues (those tissues that have a good blood supply) actually increasing their total inert gas loading. This is often found to provoke inner ear decompression sickness, as the ear seems particularly sensitive to this effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burton2004_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-burton2004-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stroke">Stroke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Stroke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A stroke (either ischemic or hemorrhagic) involving the <a href="/wiki/Posterior_cranial_fossa" title="Posterior cranial fossa">posterior fossa</a> is a cause of central vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> Risk factors for a stroke as a cause of vertigo include increasing age and known vascular risk factors. Presentation may more often involve headache or neck pain, additionally, those who have had multiple episodes of dizziness in the months leading up to presentation are suggestive of stroke with prodromal <a href="/wiki/Transient_ischemic_attack" title="Transient ischemic attack">TIAs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup> The HINTS exam as well as imaging studies of the brain (<a href="/wiki/CT_scan" title="CT scan">CT</a>, CT <a href="/wiki/Computed_tomography_angiography" title="Computed tomography angiography">angiogram</a>, and/or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a>) are helpful in diagnosis of posterior fossa stroke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Definitive treatment depends on the underlying cause of  vertigo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerg2009_9-17"><a href="#cite_note-Emerg2009-9">[9]</a></sup> Ménière's disease patients have a variety of treatment options to consider when receiving treatment for vertigo and tinnitus including: a low-salt diet and intratympanic injections of the antibiotic <a href="/wiki/Gentamicin" title="Gentamicin">gentamicin</a> or surgical measures such as a shunt or ablation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labyrinth_(inner_ear)" title="Labyrinth (inner ear)">labyrinth</a> in refractory cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
Common drug treatment options for vertigo may include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medication_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-medication-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticholinergics" title="Anticholinergics">Anticholinergics</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyoscine_hydrobromide" title="Hyoscine hydrobromide">hyoscine hydrobromide</a> (scopolamine)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrisonvertigo_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrisonvertigo-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticonvulsants" title="Anticonvulsants">Anticonvulsants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Topiramate" title="Topiramate">topiramate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valproic_acid" title="Valproic acid">valproic acid</a> for vestibular migraines</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihistamines" title="Antihistamines">Antihistamines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Betahistine" title="Betahistine">betahistine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimenhydrinate" title="Dimenhydrinate">dimenhydrinate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Meclizine" title="Meclizine">meclizine</a>, which may have <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetic</a> properties<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_blockers" title="Beta blockers">Beta blockers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Metoprolol" title="Metoprolol">metoprolol</a> for vestibular migraine</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">Corticosteroids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methylprednisolone" title="Methylprednisolone">methylprednisolone</a> for inflammatory conditions such as vestibular neuritis or <a href="/wiki/Dexamethasone" title="Dexamethasone">dexamethasone</a> as a second-line agent for Ménière's disease</li></ul>
<p>All cases of decompression sickness should be treated initially with 100% oxygen until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygen_therapy" title="Hyperbaric oxygen therapy">hyperbaric oxygen therapy</a> (100% oxygen delivered in a high-pressure chamber) can be provided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marx_2010_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marx_2010-47">[47]</a></sup> Several treatments may be necessary, and treatment will generally be repeated until either all symptoms resolve, or no further improvement is apparent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vertigo is from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:verto#Latin" title="wikt:en:verto">vertō</a></i> which means "a whirling or spinning movement".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
