<p><b>Normal human body temperature</b>, also known as <b>normothermia</b> or <b>euthermia</b>, is the typical temperature range found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">humans</a>. The normal human body temperature range is typically stated as 36.5–37.5 °C (97.7–99.5 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kar2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kar2008-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Individual body temperature depends upon the age, exertion, infection, sex, and reproductive status of the subject, the time of day, the place in the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body</a> at which the measurement is made, and the subject's state of consciousness (waking, sleeping or sedated), activity level, and emotional state. It is typically maintained within this range by <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_measurement">Methods of measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods of measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1927" data-file-width="3112" decoding="async" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG/220px-Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG/330px-Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG/440px-Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clinical_thermometer_38.7.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Medical_thermometer" title="Medical thermometer">medical thermometer</a> showing a temperature reading of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002311849999999999♠</span>38.7 °C</span></div></div></div>
<p>Taking a person's temperature is an initial part of a full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinical_examination" title="Clinical examination">clinical examination</a>. There are various types of <a href="/wiki/Medical_thermometer" title="Medical thermometer">medical thermometers</a>, as well as sites used for measurement, including:
</p>
<ul><li>In the rectum (rectal temperature)</li>
<li>In the mouth (oral temperature)</li>
<li>Under the arm (axillary temperature)</li>
<li>In the ear (tympanic temperature)</li>
<li>in the nose</li>
<li>In the vagina (vaginal temperature)</li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bladder" title="Bladder">bladder</a></li>
<li>On the skin of the forehead over the <a href="/wiki/Superficial_temporal_artery" title="Superficial temporal artery">temporal artery</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Body_Temp_Variation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="357" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Body_Temp_Variation.png/260px-Body_Temp_Variation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Body_Temp_Variation.png/390px-Body_Temp_Variation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Body_Temp_Variation.png/520px-Body_Temp_Variation.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Body_Temp_Variation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diurnal variation in body temperature, ranging from about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002310650000000000♠</span>37.5 °C</span> from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m., and falling to about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002309549999999999♠</span>36.4 °C</span> from 2 a.m. to 6 a.m.</div></div></div>
<p>Temperature control (<a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a>) is part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeostatic" title="Homeostatic">homeostatic</a> mechanism that keeps the organism at optimum <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>, as the temperature affects the rate of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, the average internal temperature is 37.0 °C (98.6 °F), though it varies among individuals.  However, no person always has exactly the same temperature at every moment of the day.  Temperatures cycle regularly up and down through the day, as controlled by the person's <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythm</a>.  The  lowest temperature occurs about two hours before the person normally wakes up.  Additionally, temperatures change according to activities and external factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag may rely on an unreliable or less reliable medical source. (November 2017)">unreliable medical source?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In addition to varying throughout the day, normal body temperature may also differ as much as 0.5 °C (0.9 °F) from one day to the next, so that the highest or lowest temperatures on one day will not always exactly match the highest or lowest temperatures on the next day.
</p><p>Normal human body temperature varies slightly from person to person and by the time of day. Consequently, each type of measurement has a range of normal temperatures.  The range for normal human body temperatures, taken orally, is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002309950000000000♠</span>36.8<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.5 °C</span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002309927777777777♠</span>98.2<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.9 °F</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mac-10">[10]</a></sup>  This means that any oral temperature between 36.3 and 37.3 °C (97.3 and 99.1 °F) is likely to be normal.
</p><p>The normal human body temperature is often stated as 36.5–37.5 °C (97.7–99.5 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kar2008_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kar2008-8">[8]</a></sup> In adults a review of the literature has found a wider range of 33.2–38.2 °C (91.8–100.8 °F) for normal temperatures, depending on the gender and location measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reported values vary depending on how it is measured: oral (under the tongue): <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002309950000000000♠</span>36.8<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.4 °C</span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002309927777777777♠</span>98.2<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.72 °F</span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> internal (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectal" title="Rectal">rectal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vaginal</a>): 37.0 °C (98.6 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> A rectal or vaginal measurement taken directly inside the body cavity is typically slightly higher than oral measurement, and oral measurement is somewhat higher than skin measurement. Other places, such as under the arm or in the ear, produce different typical temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> While some people think of these averages as representing normal or ideal measurements, a wide range of temperatures has been found in healthy people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CC09_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CC09-5">[5]</a></sup> The body temperature of a healthy person varies during the day by about 0.5 °C (0.9 °F) with lower temperatures in the morning and higher temperatures in the late afternoon and evening, as the body's needs and activities change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> Other circumstances also affect the body's temperature. The core body temperature of an individual tends to have the lowest value in the second half of the sleep cycle; the lowest point, called the <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>, is one of the primary markers for <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a>.  The body temperature also changes when a person is hungry, sleepy, sick, or cold.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_rhythms">Natural rhythms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural rhythms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Body temperature normally fluctuates over the day following <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">Circadian rhythms</a>, with the lowest levels around 4 a.m. and the highest in the late afternoon, between 4:00 and 6:00 p.m. (assuming the person sleeps at night and stays awake during the day).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mac-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> Therefore, an oral temperature of 37.3 °C (99.1 °F) would, strictly speaking, be a normal, healthy temperature in the afternoon but not in the early morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup> An individual's body temperature typically changes by about 0.5 °C (0.9 °F) between its highest and lowest points each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mac-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Body temperature is sensitive to many hormones, so women have a temperature rhythm that varies with the <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">menstrual cycle</a>, called a <i>circamensal</i> rhythm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>  A woman's <a href="/wiki/Basal_body_temperature" title="Basal body temperature">basal body temperature</a> rises sharply after <a href="/wiki/Ovulation" title="Ovulation">ovulation</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a> production decreases and <a href="/wiki/Progesterone" title="Progesterone">progesterone</a> increases.  <a href="/wiki/Fertility_awareness" title="Fertility awareness">Fertility awareness</a> programs use this change to identify when a woman has ovulated in order to achieve or avoid <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Luteal_phase" title="Luteal phase">luteal phase</a> of the menstrual cycle, both the lowest and the average temperatures are slightly higher than during other parts of the cycle.  However, the amount that the temperature rises during each day is slightly lower than typical, so the highest temperature of the day is not very much higher than usual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag may rely on an unreliable or less reliable medical source. (November 2017)">unreliable medical source?</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormonal_contraceptive" title="Hormonal contraceptive">Hormonal contraceptives</a> both suppress the circamensal rhythm and raise the typical body temperature by about 0.6 °C (1.1 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Temperature also varies with the change of <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> during each year.  This pattern is called a <i>circannual</i> rhythm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly1-13">[13]</a></sup>  Studies of seasonal variations have produced inconsistent results.  People living in different climates may have different seasonal patterns.
</p><p>Increased physical fitness increases the amount of daily variation in temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly1-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>With increased age, both average body temperature and the amount of daily variability in the body temperature tend to decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly1_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly1-13">[13]</a></sup> Elderly patients may have a decreased ability to generate body heat during a fever, so even a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-grade_fever" title="Low-grade fever">somewhat elevated temperature</a> can indicate a serious underlying cause in <a href="/wiki/Geriatrics" title="Geriatrics">geriatrics</a>.
</p>

<p>Different methods used for measuring temperature produce different results.  The temperature reading depends on which part of the body is being measured.  The typical daytime temperatures among healthy adults are as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Temperature in the anus (rectum/rectal), vagina, or in the ear (otic) is about 37.5 °C (99.5 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elert2005_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elert2005-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. <li>Temperature under the arm (axillary) is about 36.5 °C (97.7 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elert2005_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elert2005-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (November 2017)">medical citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<p>Generally, oral, rectal, gut, and core body temperatures, although slightly different, are well-correlated.  /&gt;
</p><p>Oral temperatures are influenced by drinking, chewing, smoking, and breathing with the mouth open. Mouth breathing, cold drinks or food reduce oral temperatures; hot drinks, hot food, chewing, and smoking raise oral temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Each measurement method also has different normal ranges depending on sex.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_due_to_outside_factors">Variations due to outside factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variations due to outside factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many outside factors affect the measured temperature as well. "Normal" values are generally given for an otherwise healthy, non-fasting adult, dressed comfortably, indoors, in a room that is kept at a normal room temperature, 22.7 to 24.4 °C (73 to 76 °F), during the morning, but not shortly after arising from sleep.  Furthermore, for oral temperatures, the subject must not have eaten, drunk, or smoked anything in at least the previous fifteen to twenty minutes, as the temperature of the food, drink, or smoke can dramatically affect the reading.
</p><p>Temperature is increased after eating or drinking anything with calories.  Caloric restriction, as for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weight-loss_diet" title="Weight-loss diet">weight-loss diet</a>, decreases overall body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>  Drinking <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> decreases the amount of daily change, slightly lowering daytime temperatures and noticeably raising nighttime temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Exercise raises body temperatures.  In adults, a noticeable increase usually requires strenuous exercise or exercise sustained over a significant time.  Children develop higher temperatures with milder activities, like playing.
</p><p>Psychological factors also influence body temperature: a very excited person often has an elevated temperature.
</p><p>Wearing more clothing slows daily temperature change and raises body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>  Similarly, sleeping with an <a href="/wiki/Electric_blanket" title="Electric blanket">electric blanket</a> raises the body temperature at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sleep disturbances also affect temperatures.  Normally, body temperature drops significantly at a person's normal bedtime and throughout the night.  Short-term <a href="/wiki/Sleep_deprivation" title="Sleep deprivation">sleep deprivation</a> produces a higher temperature at night than normal, but long-term sleep deprivation appears to reduce temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">Insomnia</a> and poor sleep quality are associated with smaller and later drops in body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>  Similarly, waking up unusually early, sleeping in, <a href="/wiki/Jet_lag" title="Jet lag">jet lag</a> and changes to <a href="/wiki/Shift_work" title="Shift work">shift work</a> schedules may affect body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concepts">Concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fever">Fever</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fever">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">Fever</a></div>
<p>A temperature setpoint is the level at which the body attempts to maintain its temperature.  When the setpoint is raised, the result is a fever.  Most fevers are caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious disease</a> and can be lowered, if desired, with <a href="/wiki/Antipyretic" title="Antipyretic">antipyretic</a> medications.
</p><p>An early morning temperature higher than 37.2 °C (99.0 °F) or a late afternoon temperature higher than 37.7 °C (99.9 °F) is normally considered a fever, assuming that the temperature is elevated due to a change in the hypothalamus's setpoint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup>  Lower thresholds are sometimes appropriate for elderly people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup>  The normal daily temperature variation is typically 0.5 °C (0.90 °F), but can be greater among people recovering from a fever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>An organism at optimum temperature is considered <i>afebrile</i> or <a href="/wiki/Apyrexy" title="Apyrexy"><i>apyrexic</i></a>, meaning "without fever".  If temperature is raised, but the setpoint is not raised, then the result is <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">hyperthermia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hyperthermia">Hyperthermia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hyperthermia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">Hyperthermia</a></div>
<p>Hyperthermia occurs when the body produces or absorbs more heat than it can dissipate. It is usually caused by prolonged exposure to high temperatures. The heat-regulating mechanisms of the body eventually become overwhelmed and unable to deal effectively with the heat, causing the body temperature to climb uncontrollably.  Hyperthermia at or above about 40 °C (104 °F) is a life-threatening medical emergency that requires immediate treatment.  Common symptoms include headache, confusion, and fatigue.  If sweating has resulted in dehydration, then the affected person may have dry, red skin.
</p><p>In a medical setting, mild hyperthermia is commonly called <i>heat exhaustion</i> or <i>heat prostration</i>; severe hyperthermia is called <i>heat stroke</i>.  Heat stroke may come on suddenly, but it usually follows the untreated milder stages.  Treatment involves cooling and rehydrating the body; fever-reducing drugs are useless for this condition.  This may be done through moving out of direct sunlight to a cooler and shaded environment, drinking water, removing clothing that might keep heat close to the body, or sitting in front of a fan.  Bathing in tepid or cool water, or even just washing the face and other exposed areas of the skin, can be helpful.
</p><p>With fever, the body's core temperature rises to a higher temperature through the action of the part of the brain that controls the body temperature; with hyperthermia, the body temperature is raised without the influence of the heat control centers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothermia">Hypothermia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hypothermia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">Hypothermia</a></div>
<p>In hypothermia,  body temperature drops below that required for normal metabolism and bodily functions.  In humans, this is usually due to excessive exposure to cold air or water, but it can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therapeutic_hypothermia" title="Therapeutic hypothermia">deliberately induced as a medical treatment</a>.  Symptoms usually appear when the body's core temperature drops by 1–2 °C (1.8–3.6 °F) below normal temperature.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basal_body_temperature">Basal body temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Basal body temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Basal_body_temperature" title="Basal body temperature">Basal body temperature</a></div>
<p>Basal body temperature is the lowest temperature attained by the body during rest (usually during sleep). It is generally measured immediately after awakening and before any physical activity has been undertaken, although the temperature measured at that time is somewhat higher than the true basal body temperature.  In women, temperature differs at various points in the <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">menstrual cycle</a>, and this can be used in the long-term to track ovulation both for the purpose of aiding conception or avoiding pregnancy.  This process is called <a href="/wiki/Fertility_awareness" title="Fertility awareness">fertility awareness</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Core_temperature">Core temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Core temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Core temperature, also called core body temperature, is the operating temperature of an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a>, specifically in deep structures of the body such as the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, in comparison to temperatures of peripheral tissues.  Core temperature is normally maintained within a narrow range so that essential enzymatic reactions can occur. Significant core temperature elevation (<a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">hyperthermia</a>) or depression (<a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>) that is prolonged for more than a brief period of time is incompatible with human life.
</p><p>Temperature examination in the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> is the traditional <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard_(test)" title="Gold standard (test)">gold standard</a> measurement used to estimate core temperature (oral temperature is affected by hot or cold drinks and mouth-breathing). Rectal temperature is expected to be approximately one Fahrenheit degree higher than an oral temperature taken on the same person at the same time. Ear thermometers measure eardrum temperature using <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> sensors. The blood supply to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tympanic_membrane" title="Tympanic membrane">tympanic membrane</a> is shared with the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>. However, this method of measuring body temperature is not as accurate as rectal measurement and has a low sensitivity for fevers, missing three or four out of every ten fevers in children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodd_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodd-16">[16]</a></sup> Ear temperature measurement may be acceptable for observing trends in body temperature but is less useful in consistently identifying fevers.
</p><p>Until recently, direct measurement of core body temperature required surgical insertion of a probe, so a variety of indirect methods have commonly been used.  The <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vaginal</a> temperature is generally considered to give the most accurate assessment of core body temperature, particularly in hypothermia. In the early 2000s, ingestible <a href="/wiki/Thermistor" title="Thermistor">thermistors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Capsule_(pharmacy)" title="Capsule (pharmacy)">capsule</a> form were produced, allowing the temperature inside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_tract" title="Digestive tract">digestive tract</a> to be transmitted to an external receiver; one study found that these were comparable in accuracy to rectal temperature measurement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDirect_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciDirect-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot">Hot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>44 °C (111.2 °F) or more – Almost certainly death will occur; however, people have been known to survive up to 46.5 °C (115.7 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>43 °C (109.4 °F) – Normally death, or there may be serious brain damage, continuous convulsions and shock. Cardio-respiratory collapse will likely occur.</li>
<li>42 °C (107.6 °F) – Subject may turn pale or remain flushed and red. They may become comatose, be in severe delirium, vomiting, and convulsions can occur. Blood pressure may be high or low and heart rate will be very fast.</li>
<li>41 °C (105.8 °F) – (<a href="/wiki/Medical_emergency" title="Medical emergency">Medical emergency</a>) – Fainting, vomiting, severe headache, dizziness, confusion, hallucinations, delirium and drowsiness can occur. There may also be palpitations and breathlessness.</li>
<li>40 °C (104 °F) – Fainting, dehydration, weakness, vomiting, headache, breathlessness and dizziness may occur as well as profuse sweating. Starts to be life-threatening.</li>
<li>39 °C (102.2 °F) – Severe sweating, flushed and red. Fast heart rate and breathlessness. There may be exhaustion accompanying this. Children and people with epilepsy may be very likely to get convulsions at this point.</li>
<li>38 °C (100.4 °F) – (Classed as <a href="/wiki/Hyperthermia" title="Hyperthermia">hyperthermia</a> if not caused by a <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>) – Feeling hot, sweating, feeling thirsty, feeling very uncomfortable, slightly hungry. If this is caused by <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a>, there may also be <a href="/wiki/Chills" title="Chills">chills</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Normal">Normal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Normal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>36.5–37.5 °C (97.7–99.5 °F) is a typically reported range for normal body temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kar2008_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kar2008-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold">Cold</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cold">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>36 °C (96.8 °F) – Feeling cold, mild to moderate shivering. Body temperature may drop this low during sleep. May be a normal body temperature.</li>
<li>35 °C (95 °F) – (<a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">Hypothermia</a> is less than 35 °C (95 °F)) – Intense shivering, numbness and bluish/grayness of the skin. There is the possibility of heart irritability.</li>
<li>34 °C (93.2 °F) – Severe shivering, loss of movement of fingers, blueness and confusion. Some behavioural changes may take place.</li>
<li>33 °C (91.4 °F) – Moderate to severe confusion, sleepiness, depressed reflexes, progressive loss of shivering, slow heart beat, shallow breathing. Shivering may stop. Subject may be unresponsive to certain stimuli.</li>
<li>32 °C (89.6 °F) – (<a href="/wiki/Medical_emergency" title="Medical emergency">Medical emergency</a>) – Hallucinations, delirium, complete confusion, extreme sleepiness that is progressively becoming comatose. Shivering is absent (subject may even think they are hot). Reflex may be absent or very slight.</li>
<li>31 °C (87.8 °F) – Comatose, very rarely conscious. No or slight reflexes. Very shallow breathing and slow heart rate. Possibility of serious heart rhythm problems.</li>
<li>28 °C (82.4 °F) – Severe heart rhythm disturbances are likely and breathing may stop at any time. Patient may appear to be dead.</li>
<li>24–26 °C (75.2–78.8 °F) or less – Death usually occurs due to irregular heart beat or respiratory arrest; however, some patients have been known to survive with body temperatures as low as 14.2 °C (57.6 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Body_Temp_Records_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Body_Temp_Records-20">[20]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_understanding">Historical understanding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_body_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Historical understanding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 19th century, most books quoted "blood heat" as 98 °F, until a study published the mean (but not the variance) of a large sample as 36.88 °C (98.38 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Subsequently that mean was widely quoted as "37 °C or 98.4 °F"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> until editors realised 37 °C is closer to 98.6 °F than 98.4 °F. The 37 °C value was set by German physician <a href="/wiki/Carl_Reinhold_August_Wunderlich" title="Carl Reinhold August Wunderlich">Carl Reinhold August Wunderlich</a> in his 1868 book <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>, which put temperature charts into widespread clinical use <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>. Dictionaries and other sources that quoted these averages did add the word "about" to show that there is some variance, but generally did not state how wide the variance is.
</p>
