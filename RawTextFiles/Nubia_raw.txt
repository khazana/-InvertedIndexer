<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nubia</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the region in Africa. For the airport in Mongolia, see <a href="/wiki/New_Ulaanbaatar_International_Airport" title="New Ulaanbaatar International Airport">New Ulaanbaatar International Airport</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nubia_(disambiguation)" title="Nubia (disambiguation)">Nubia (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NubianPharoahs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="307" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/NubianPharoahs.jpg/220px-NubianPharoahs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/NubianPharoahs.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NubianPharoahs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nubian pharaohs of the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt">25th Dynasty</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3219" data-file-width="1577" decoding="async" height="449" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg/220px-Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg/330px-Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg/440px-Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Egypt_map-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Ancient Egypt, with <a href="/wiki/Nubian_Desert" title="Nubian Desert">Nubian Desert</a> marked</div></div></div>
<p><b>Nubia</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span></span>-/</a></span></span>) is a region along the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> river encompassing the area between <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>. It was the seat of one of the earliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civilizations" title="Civilizations">civilizations</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient" title="Ancient">ancient</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, with a history that can be traced from at least 2500 BC onward with the <a href="/wiki/Kerma_culture" title="Kerma culture">Kerma culture</a>. The latter was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom of Egypt</a> under <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_I" title="Thutmose I">Thutmose I</a> around 1500 BC. Nubia was home to several <a href="/wiki/African_empires" title="African empires">empires</a>, most prominently the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">kingdom of Kush</a>, which conquered Egypt during the 8th century BC during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Piye" title="Piye">Piye</a> and ruled the country as its <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt">Twenty-fifth Dynasty</a> (to be replaced a century later by the native Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Twenty-sixth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-sixth Dynasty of Egypt">Twenty-sixth Dynasty</a>). 
</p><p>The collapse of Kush in the 4th century AD after more than a thousand years of existence was precipitated by an invasion by <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Kingdom of Aksum</a> and saw the rise of three Christian kingdoms, <a href="/wiki/Nobatia" title="Nobatia">Nobatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makuria" title="Makuria">Makuria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alodia" title="Alodia">Alodia</a>, the last two again lasting for roughly a millennium. Their eventual decline initiated not only the partition of Nubia into the northern half conquered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">Ottomans</a> and the southern half by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sennar_(sultanate)" title="Sennar (sultanate)">Sennar sultanate</a> in the 16th century, but also a rapid <a href="/wiki/Islamization" title="Islamization">Islamization</a> and partial <a href="/wiki/Arabization" title="Arabization">Arabization</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_people" title="Nubian people">Nubian people</a>. Nubia was again united with the <a href="/wiki/Khedivate_of_Egypt" title="Khedivate of Egypt">Khedivate of Egypt</a> in the 19th century. Today, the region of Nubia is split between Egypt and Sudan.
</p><p>The primarily <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> science dealing with ancient Nubia is called <a href="/wiki/Nubiology" title="Nubiology">Nubiology</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nubian_languages" title="Nubian languages">Nubian languages</a></div>
<p>The name <i>Nubia</i> is derived from that of the <a href="/wiki/Noba" title="Noba">Noba</a> people, nomads who settled the area in the 4th century CE following the collapse of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Mero%C3%AB" title="Meroë">Meroë</a>. The Noba spoke a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan" title="Nilo-Saharan">Nilo-Saharan</a> language, ancestral to <a href="/wiki/Old_Nubian_language" title="Old Nubian language">Old Nubian</a>. Old Nubian was mostly used in religious texts dating from the 8th and 15th centuries. Before the 4th century, and throughout <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, Nubia was known as <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kush</a></i>, or, in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">Classical Greek</a> usage, included under the name <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Ethiopia" title="Ancient Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a></i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aithiopia" title="Aithiopia">Aithiopia</a></i>).
</p><p>Historically, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_people" title="Nubian people">people of Nubia</a> spoke at least two varieties of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_language" title="Nubian language">Nubian language</a> group, a subfamily that includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobiin" title="Nobiin">Nobiin</a> (the descendant of Old Nubian), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenuzi-Dongola" title="Kenuzi-Dongola">Kenuzi-Dongola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midob_people" title="Midob people">Midob</a> and several related varieties in the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Nuba_Mountains" title="Nuba Mountains">Nuba Mountains</a> in South <a href="/wiki/Kordofan" title="Kordofan">Kordofan</a>. Until at least 1970, the <a href="/wiki/Birgid_language" title="Birgid language">Birgid language</a> was spoken north of <a href="/wiki/Nyala,_Sudan" title="Nyala, Sudan">Nyala</a> in <a href="/wiki/Darfur" title="Darfur">Darfur</a>, but is now extinct. However, linguistic evidence indicates that the languages spoken in the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerma_Culture" title="Kerma Culture">Kerma Culture</a> (present-day southern <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>) in Nubia, belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Cushitic_languages" title="Cushitic languages">Cushitic</a> branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afroasiatic" title="Afroasiatic">Afroasiatic</a> languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bechaus-Gerst_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bechaus-Gerst-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lbant_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lbant-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aethiopia" title="Aethiopia">Aethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_expeditions_to_Nubia" title="Archaeological expeditions to Nubia">Archaeological expeditions to Nubia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nile_boat" title="Nile boat">Nile boat</a></div>
<p>Nubia was divided into three major regions: Upper, Middle, and Lower Nubia, in reference to their locations along the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>. Lower refers to regions downstream and upper refers to regions upstream. <a href="/wiki/Lower_Nubia" title="Lower Nubia">Lower Nubia</a> lies between the First and the Second <a href="/wiki/Cataracts_of_the_Nile" title="Cataracts of the Nile">Cataracts</a>, within the current borders of Egypt. Middle Nubia lies between the Second and the Third Cataracts. <a href="/wiki/Upper_Nubia" title="Upper Nubia">Upper Nubia</a> lies south of the Third Cataract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tools,_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_(6000-5500_BC).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2988" data-file-width="5312" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg/220px-Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg/330px-Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg/440px-Tools%2C_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_%286000-5500_BC%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tools,_Weapons_and_adornments_from_el-Barga_(6000-5500_BC).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tools, Weapons and adornments from el-Barga (6000–5500 BC)</div></div></div>
<p>Early settlements sprouted in both Upper and Lower Nubia. Egyptians referred to Nubia as "Ta-Seti," or "The Land of the Bow," since the Nubians were known to be expert archers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emberling_2011_8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emberling_2011_8-4">[4]</a></sup> Modern scholars typically refer to the people from this area as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_A-Group" title="Nubian A-Group">A-Group</a>" culture. Fertile farmland just south of the Third Cataract is known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Kerma" title="Pre-Kerma">pre-Kerma</a>" culture in Upper Nubia, as they are the ancestors.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> people in the Nile Valley likely came from <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, and there was shared culture with the two areas and with that of Egypt during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keita_1990_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keita_1990-5">[5]</a></sup> By the 5th millennium BC, the people who inhabited what is now called Nubia participated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_revolution" title="Neolithic revolution">Neolithic revolution</a>. Saharan <a href="/wiki/Sabu-Jaddi" title="Sabu-Jaddi">rock reliefs</a> depict scenes that have been thought to be suggestive of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattle_cult" title="Cattle cult">cattle cult</a>, typical of those seen throughout parts of Eastern Africa and the Nile Valley even to this day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Megaliths discovered at <a href="/wiki/Nabta_Playa" title="Nabta Playa">Nabta Playa</a> are early examples of what seems to be one of the world's first <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> devices, predating <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> by almost 2,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This complexity as observed at Nabta Playa, and as expressed by different levels of authority within the society there, likely formed the basis for the structure of both the Neolithic society at Nabta and the <a href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Old Kingdom of Egypt">Old Kingdom of Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Around 3500 BC, the second "Nubian" culture, termed the A-Group, arose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was a contemporary of, and ethnically and culturally very similar to, the polities in predynastic <a href="/wiki/Naqada" title="Naqada">Naqada</a> of Upper Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keita_further_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keita_further-11">[11]</a></sup> The A-Group people were engaged in trade with the Egyptians. This trade is testified archaeologically by large amounts of Egyptian commodities deposited in the graves of the A-Group people. The imports consisted of gold objects, copper tools, faience amulets and beads, seals, slate palettes, stone vessels, and a variety of pots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nubian._Female_Figurine,_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1121" decoding="async" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg/170px-Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg/255px-Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg/340px-Nubian._Female_Figurine%2C_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nubian._Female_Figurine,_ca._3500-3100_B.C.E.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nubian <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> female figurine from the Neolithic period ca. 3500–3100 B.C.E. <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>Around 3300 BC, there is evidence of a unified kingdom, as shown by the finds at <a href="/wiki/Qustul" title="Qustul">Qustul</a>, that maintained substantial interactions (both cultural and genetic) with the culture of Naqadan Upper Egypt. The Nubian culture may have even contributed to the unification of the Nile Valley.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Toby_Wilkinson" title="Toby Wilkinson">Toby Wilkinson</a>, based on work by Bruce Williams in the 1980s, wrote that "The white crown, associated in historic times with Upper Egypt, is first attested later than the red crown, but is directly associated with the ruler somewhat earlier. The earliest known depiction of the white crown is on a ceremonial incense burner from Cemetery at Qustul in Lower Nubia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_1999_8_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson_1999_8-13">[13]</a></sup> Based on a 1998 excavation report, Jane Roy has written that "At the time of Williams' argument, the Qustul cemetery and the 'royal' iconography found there was dated to the Naqada IIIA period, thus antedating royal cemeteries in Egypt of the Naqada IIIB phase. New evidence from Abydos, however, particularly the excavation of Cemetery U and the tome U-j, dating to Naqada IIIA has shown that this iconography appears earlier in Egypt."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roy_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roy-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nubian_A_group.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="976" data-file-width="2381" decoding="async" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Nubian_A_group.jpg/220px-Nubian_A_group.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Nubian_A_group.jpg/330px-Nubian_A_group.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Nubian_A_group.jpg/440px-Nubian_A_group.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nubian_A_group.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pottery of the Nubian A-Group, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musee_du_Louvre" title="Musee du Louvre">Musee du Louvre</a></div></div></div>
<p>Around the turn of the <a href="/wiki/Naqada_III" title="Naqada III">protodynastic period</a>, Naqada, in its bid to conquer and unify the whole Nile Valley, seems to have conquered Ta-Seti (the kingdom where Qustul was located) and harmonized it with the Egyptian state. Thus, Nubia became the first nome of Upper Egypt. At the time of the first dynasty, the A-Group area seems to have been entirely depopulated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emberling_2011_8_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emberling_2011_8-4">[4]</a></sup> most likely due to immigration to areas west and south.
</p><p>This culture began to decline in the early 28th century BC. <a href="/wiki/George_Andrew_Reisner" title="George Andrew Reisner">George Reisner</a> suggested that it was succeeded by a culture that he called the "B-Group", but most archaeologists today believe that this culture never existed and that the area was depopulated from c. 3000 BC to c. 2500, when a-group descendants returned to the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The causes of this are uncertain, but it was perhaps caused by Egyptian invasions and pillaging that began at this time. Nubia is believed to have served as a trade corridor between Egypt and tropical Africa long before 3100 BC. Egyptian craftsmen of the period used <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ebony" title="Ebony">ebony</a> wood from tropical Africa which came through Nubia.
</p><p>In 2300 BC, Nubia was first mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Old_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Old Kingdom of Egypt">Old Kingdom</a> Egyptian accounts of trade missions. From <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a>, right above the First Cataract, the southern limit of Egyptian control at the time, <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> imported gold, incense, ebony, copper, ivory, and exotic animals from tropical Africa through Nubia. As trade between Egypt and Nubia increased, so did wealth and stability. By the Egyptian 6th dynasty, Nubia was divided into a series of small kingdoms. There is debate over whether these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_C-Group" title="Nubian C-Group">C-Group</a> peoples,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> who flourished from c. 2500 BC to c. 1500 BC, were another internal evolution or invaders. There are definite similarities between the pottery of the A-Group and C-Group, so it may be a return of the ousted Group-As, or an internal revival of lost arts. At this time, the Sahara Desert was becoming too arid to support human beings, and it is possible that there was a sudden influx of Saharan nomads. C-Group pottery is characterized by all-over incised geometric lines with white infill and impressed imitations of basketry.
</p><p>During the Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> (c. 2040–1640 BC), Egypt began expanding into Nubia to gain more control over the trade routes in Northern Nubia and direct access to trade with Southern Nubia. They erected a chain of forts down the Nile below the Second Cataract. These garrisons seemed to have peaceful relations with the local Nubian people, but little interaction during the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A contemporaneous but distinct culture from the C-Group was the Pan Grave culture, so called because of their shallow graves. The Pan Graves are associated with the East bank of the Nile, but the Pan Graves and C-Group definitely interacted. Their pottery is characterized by incised lines of a more limited character than those of the C-Group, generally having interspersed undecorated spaces within the geometric schemes.
</p>

<p>One interpretation is that Nubian A-Group rulers and early Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaohs</a> used related royal symbols. Similarities in rock art of A-Group Nubia and Upper Egypt support this position. Ancient Egypt conquered Nubian territory in various eras, and incorporated parts of the area into its provinces. The Nubians in turn were to conquer Egypt under its <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt">25th Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, relations between the two peoples also show peaceful cultural interchange and cooperation, including mixed marriages. The <a href="/wiki/Medjay" title="Medjay">Medjay</a> (<i>mj3</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>) represents the name <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> gave to a region in northern Sudan where an ancient people of Nubia inhabited. They became part of the Egyptian military as scouts and minor workers.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom of Egypt</a>, "Medjay" no longer referred to the district of Medja but to a tribe or clan of people. It is not known what happened to the district, but, after the <a href="/wiki/First_Intermediate_Period_of_Egypt" title="First Intermediate Period of Egypt">First Intermediate Period of Egypt</a>, it and other districts in Nubia were no longer mentioned in the written record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Written accounts detail the Medjay as nomadic desert people. Over time, they were incorporated into the Egyptian army. In the army, the Medjay served as garrison troops in Egyptian fortifications in Nubia and patrolled the deserts as a kind of gendarmerie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This was done in the hope of preventing their fellow Medjay tribespeople from further attacking Egyptian assets in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-25">[25]</a></sup> Later, they were even used during <a href="/wiki/Kamose" title="Kamose">Kamose</a>'s campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Hyksos" title="Hyksos">Hyksos</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and became instrumental in making the Egyptian state into a military power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth Dynasty of Egypt">Eighteenth Dynasty</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom period</a>, the Medjay were an elite paramilitary police force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-25">[25]</a></sup> No longer did the term refer to an ethnic group and, over time, the new meaning became synonymous with the policing occupation in general. Being an elite police force, the Medjay were often used to protect valuable areas, especially royal and religious complexes. Though they are most notable for their protection of the royal palaces and tombs in <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Egypt" title="Thebes, Egypt">Thebes</a> and the surrounding areas, the Medjay were known to have been used throughout Upper and Lower Egypt.
</p>

<p>Various pharaohs of Nubian origin are held by some Egyptologists to have played an important part towards the area in different eras of Egyptian history, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twelfth Dynasty of Egypt">12th Dynasty</a>. These rulers handled matters in typical Egyptian fashion, reflecting the close cultural influences between the two regions.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[T]he XIIth Dynasty (1991–1786 B.C.E.) originated from the <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a> region. As expected, strong Nubian features and dark coloring are seen in their sculpture and relief work. This dynasty ranks as among the greatest, whose fame far outlived its actual tenure on the throne. Especially interesting, it was a member of this dynasty that decreed that no Nehsy (riverine Nubian of the principality of Kush), except such as came for trade or diplomatic reasons, should pass by the Egyptian fortress and cops at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Cataracts_of_the_Nile" title="Cataracts of the Nile">Second Nile Cataract</a>. Why would this royal family of Nubian ancestry ban other Nubians from coming into Egyptian territory? Because the Egyptian rulers of Nubian ancestry had become Egyptians culturally; as pharaohs, they exhibited typical Egyptian attitudes and adopted typical Egyptian policies. (Yurco 1989) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In the New Kingdom, Nubians became indistinguishable in the archaeological record from Egyptians.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is an extremely difficult task to attempt to describe the Nubians during the course of Egypt's New Kingdom, because their presence appears to have virtually evaporated from the archaeological record. The result has been described as a wholesale Nubian assimilation into Egyptian society. This assimilation was so complete that it masked all Nubian ethnic identities insofar as archaeological remains are concerned beneath the impenetrable veneer of Egypt's material culture. In the Kushite Period, when Nubians ruled as Pharaohs in their own right, the material culture of Dynasty XXV (about 750–655 B.C.E.) was decidedly Egyptian in character. Nubia's entire landscape up to the region of the Third Cataract was dotted with temples indistinguishable in style and decoration from contemporary temples erected in Egypt. The same observation obtains for the smaller number of typically Egyptian tombs in which these elite Nubian princes were interred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kerma">Kerma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kerma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerma_Culture" title="Kerma Culture">Kerma Culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kerma_city.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1512" data-file-width="2016" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Kerma_city.JPG/220px-Kerma_city.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Kerma_city.JPG/330px-Kerma_city.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Kerma_city.JPG/440px-Kerma_city.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kerma_city.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kerma city</div></div></div>
<p>From the pre-Kerma culture, the first kingdom to unify much of the region arose. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerma_Culture" title="Kerma Culture">Kerma Culture</a>, named for its presumed capital at <a href="/wiki/Kerma" title="Kerma">Kerma</a>, was one of the earliest urban centers in the Nile region and spoke languages of the <a href="/wiki/Cushitic_languages" title="Cushitic languages">Cushitic</a> branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bechaus-Gerst_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bechaus-Gerst-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lbant_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lbant-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> By 1750 BC, the kings of Kerma were powerful enough to organize the labor for monumental walls and structures of mud brick. They also had rich tombs, with possessions for the afterlife and large <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifices</a>. <a href="/wiki/George_Andrew_Reisner" title="George Andrew Reisner">George Andrew Reisner</a> excavated sites at Kerma and found distinctive <a href="/wiki/Nubian_architecture" title="Nubian architecture">Nubian architecture</a> such as large tombs and palace-like structures. At one point, Kerma came very close to conquering Egypt. Egypt suffered a serious defeat at the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kingdom of Kush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TurinPapyrus1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1121" data-file-width="2846" decoding="async" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/TurinPapyrus1.jpg/220px-TurinPapyrus1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/TurinPapyrus1.jpg/330px-TurinPapyrus1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/TurinPapyrus1.jpg/440px-TurinPapyrus1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TurinPapyrus1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Turin Papyrus Map</div></div></div>
<p>According to Davies, head of the joint <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> and Egyptian archaeological team, the attack was so devastating that, if the Kerma forces had chosen to stay and occupy Egypt, they might have eliminated it for good and brought the nation to extinction. When Egyptian power revived under the <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom of Egypt</a> (c. 1532–1070 BC), Egyptians began to expand further southwards. The Egyptians destroyed Kerma's kingdom and capitol and expanded the Egyptian empire to the Fourth Cataract.
</p><p>By the end of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_I" title="Thutmose I">Thutmose I</a> (1520 BC), all of northern Nubia had been annexed. The Egyptians built a new administrative center at <a href="/wiki/Napata" title="Napata">Napata</a>, and used the area to produce <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and incense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The Nubian gold production made Egypt a prime source of the precious metal in the Middle East. The primitive working conditions for the slaves are recorded by <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>, who saw some of the mines at a later time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> One of the oldest maps known is of a gold mine in Nubia, the <a href="/wiki/Turin_Papyrus_Map" title="Turin Papyrus Map">Turin Papyrus Map</a> dating to about 1160 BC; this map is also one of the earliest characterized road maps in existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kush">Kush</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kush">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kingdom of Kush</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Napatan_period">Napatan period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Napatan period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_(6552243769).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg/170px-Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg/255px-Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg/340px-Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_%286552243769%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Mus%C3%A9e_du_Louvre_Ancient_Egyptian_sculptures_(6552243769).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of King <a href="/wiki/Taharqa" title="Taharqa">Taharqa</a>, 25th Dynasty</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1964" data-file-width="652" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png/110px-Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png/165px-Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png/220px-Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Senkamenisken-Shabti_BrooklynMuseum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figurine of the King <a href="/wiki/Senkamanisken" title="Senkamanisken">Senkamanisken</a>, Napata period ca. 643-623 BC</div></div></div>
<p>When the Egyptians pulled out of the Napata region, they left a lasting legacy that was merged with indigenous customs, forming the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kush" title="Kingdom of Kush">Kush</a>. Archaeologists have found several burials in the area that seem to belong to local leaders. The Kushites were buried there soon after the Egyptians decolonized the Nubian frontier. Kush adopted many Egyptian practices, such as their religion. The Kingdom of Kush survived longer than that of Egypt, invaded Egypt (under the leadership of king <a href="/wiki/Piye" title="Piye">Piye</a>), and controlled Egypt during the 8th century BC as the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt">Twenty-fifth Dynasty of Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kushites held sway over their northern neighbors for nearly 100 years, until they were eventually repelled by the invading <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrians</a>. The Assyrians forced them to move farther south, where they eventually established their capital at <a href="/wiki/Mero%C3%AB" title="Meroë">Meroë</a>. Of the Nubian kings of this era, <a href="/wiki/Taharqa" title="Taharqa">Taharqa</a> is perhaps the best known. A son and the third successor of the founding pharaoh, <a href="/wiki/Piye" title="Piye">Piye</a>, he was crowned in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Egypt" title="Memphis, Egypt">Memphis, Egypt</a> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 690</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Taharqa ruled over both Nubia and Egypt, restored Egyptian temples at <a href="/wiki/Karnak" title="Karnak">Karnak</a>, and built new temples and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramids_of_Nubia" title="Pyramids of Nubia">pyramids in Nubia</a> before being driven from Egypt by the Assyrians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Meroitic_period">Meroitic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Meroitic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mero%C3%AB" title="Meroë">Meroë</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1069" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg/170px-Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg/255px-Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg/340px-Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marble_head_of_Nubian_denizen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Marble portrait of a Nubian denizen ca. 120–100 BC</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="403" data-file-width="591" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG/220px-Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG/330px-Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG/440px-Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sudan_Meroe_Pyramids_2001.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view at <a href="/wiki/Nubian_pyramids" title="Nubian pyramids">Nubian pyramids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meroe" title="Meroe">Meroe</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mero%C3%AB" title="Meroë">Meroë</a> (800 BC – <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> AD 350</span>) in southern Nubia lay on the east bank of the Nile, about 6 km north-east of the Kabushiya station near Shendi, Sudan, ca. 200 km north-east of <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>. The people there preserved many ancient Egyptian customs, but were unique in many respects. They developed their own form of writing, first utilizing Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieroglyphs" title="Hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a>, and later using an alphabetic script with 23 signs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Since king <a href="/wiki/Arakamani" title="Arakamani">Arakamani</a> the kings were buried in Meroe.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Achaemenid_period">Achaemenid period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Achaemenid period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="983" data-file-width="1150" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg/220px-Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg/330px-Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg/440px-Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xerxes_detail_Ethiopian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Kušiya</i> soldier of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achaemenid_army" title="Achaemenid army">Achaemenid army</a>, circa 480 BCE. <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_I" title="Xerxes I">Xerxes I</a> tomb relief.</div></div></div>
<p>The Achaemenids occupied the Kushan kingdom, possibly from the time of <a href="/wiki/Cambyses_II" title="Cambyses II">Cambyses</a> (circa 530 BCE), and more probably from the time of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_I" title="Darius I">Darius I</a> (550-486 BCE), who mentions the conquest on Kush (<i>Kušiya</i>) in his inscriptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> describes a clash with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> in which the Romans defeated Nubians. According to Strabo, following the Kushite advance, <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Petronius" title="Gaius Petronius">Gaius Petronius</a> (a Prefect of Egypt at the time) prepared a large army and marched south. The Roman forces clashed with the Kushite armies near Thebes and forced them to retreat to Pselchis (Maharraqa) in Kushite lands. Petronius then sent deputies to the Kushites in an attempt to reach a peace agreement and make certain demands.
</p><p>Quoting Strabo, the Kushites "desired three days for consideration" in order to make a final decision. However, after the three days, Kush did not respond and Petronius advanced with his armies and took the Kushite city of Premnis (modern <a href="/wiki/Karanog" title="Karanog">Karanog</a>) south of Maharraqa. From there, he advanced all the way south to Napata, the second Capital in Kush after Meroe. Petronius attacked and sacked Napata, causing the son of the Kushite Queen to flee. Strabo describes the defeat of the Kushites at Napata, stating that "He (Petronius) made prisoners of the inhabitants".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>During this time, the different parts of the region divided into smaller groups with individual leaders, or generals, each commanding small armies of mercenaries. They fought for control of what is now Nubia and its surrounding territories, leaving the entire region weak and vulnerable to attack. Meroë would eventually meet defeat by the new rising <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Kingdom of Aksum</a> to their south under <a href="/wiki/Ezana_of_Axum" title="Ezana of Axum">King Ezana</a>.
</p><p>The classification of the <a href="/wiki/Meroitic_language" title="Meroitic language">Meroitic language</a> is uncertain; it was long assumed to have been one of the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic languages</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian language</a>, but is now considered to have likely been one of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Sudanic_languages" title="Eastern Sudanic languages">Eastern Sudanic languages</a>.
</p><p>At some point during the 4th century, the region was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Noba" title="Noba">Noba</a>, from which the name <i>Nubia</i> may derive; another possibility is that it comes from the Egyptian word for <a href="/wiki/Gold_(hieroglyph)" title="Gold (hieroglyph)">gold</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> From then on, the Romans referred to the area as <a href="/wiki/Nobatia" title="Nobatia">Nobatia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_Nubia">Christian Nubia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christian Nubia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Makuria" title="Makuria">Makuria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobadia" title="Nobadia">Nobadia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alodia" title="Alodia">Alodia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cross,_Faras.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2260" data-file-width="1403" decoding="async" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Cross%2C_Faras.jpg/170px-Cross%2C_Faras.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Cross%2C_Faras.jpg/255px-Cross%2C_Faras.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Cross%2C_Faras.jpg/340px-Cross%2C_Faras.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cross,_Faras.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A church mural depicting an elaborate cross, <a href="/wiki/Faras" title="Faras">Faras</a> (12th century)</div></div></div>
<p>Around AD 350, the area was invaded by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum" title="Kingdom of Aksum">Kingdom of Aksum</a> and the Meroitic kingdom collapsed. Eventually, three smaller <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> kingdoms replaced it: northernmost was <a href="/wiki/Nobatia" title="Nobatia">Nobatia</a> between the first and second cataract of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>, with its capital at Pachoras (modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faras,_Egypt" title="Faras, Egypt">Faras</a>); in the middle was <a href="/wiki/Makuria" title="Makuria">Makuria</a>, with its capital at <a href="/wiki/Old_Dongola" title="Old Dongola">Old Dongola</a>; and southernmost was <a href="/wiki/Alodia" title="Alodia">Alodia</a>, with its capital at Soba (near <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>). King Silky of Nobatia crushed the <a href="/wiki/Blemmyes" title="Blemmyes">Blemmyes</a>, and recorded his victory in a <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription carved in the wall of the temple of Talmis (modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalabsha" title="Kalabsha">Kalabsha</a>) around AD 500.
</p><p>While bishop <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> consecrated one Marcus as bishop of <a href="/wiki/Philae" title="Philae">Philae</a> before his death in 373, showing that <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> had penetrated the region by the 4th century, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Ephesus" title="John of Ephesus">John of Ephesus</a> records that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophysite" title="Monophysite">Monophysite</a> priest named Julian converted the king and his nobles of Nobatia around 545. John of Ephesus also writes that the kingdom of Alodia was converted around 569. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Biclarum" title="John of Biclarum">John of Biclarum</a> records that the kingdom of Makuria was converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> the same year, suggesting that John of Ephesus might be mistaken. Further doubt is cast on John's testimony by an entry in the chronicle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Patriarch_of_Alexandria" title="Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Alexandria">Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Alexandria</a> <a href="/wiki/Eutychius_of_Alexandria" title="Eutychius of Alexandria">Eutychius</a>, which states that in 719 the church of Nubia transferred its allegiance from the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Greek</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic Orthodox Church</a>.
</p><p>By the 7th century, Makuria expanded becoming the dominant power in the region. It was strong enough to halt the southern expansion of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> had taken Egypt. After several failed invasions the new rulers agreed to a treaty with Dongola, called <a href="/wiki/Baqt" title="Baqt">Baqt</a>, allowing for peaceful coexistence and trade. This treaty held for six hundred years. Over time the influx of Arab traders introduced Islam to Nubia and it gradually supplanted <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. While there are records of a bishop at <a href="/wiki/Qasr_Ibrim" title="Qasr Ibrim">Qasr Ibrim</a> in 1372, his see had come to include that located at Faras. It is also clear that the cathedral of Dongola had been converted to a mosque in 1317.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The influx of <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a> and Nubians to Egypt and Sudan had contributed to the suppression of the Nubian identity following the collapse of the last Nubian kingdom around 1504. A vast majority of the Nubian population is currently Muslim, and the Arabic language is their main medium of communication in addition to their indigenous old Nubian language. The unique characteristic of Nubian is shown in their culture (dress, dances, traditions, and music).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_Nubia">Islamic Nubia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Islamic Nubia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamization_of_Sudan" title="Islamization of Sudan">Islamization of Sudan</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallery_of_Nubians_(early_19th_centuy).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="506" data-file-width="594" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg/220px-Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg/330px-Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg/440px-Gallery_of_Nubians_%28early_19th_centuy%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallery_of_Nubians_(early_19th_centuy).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nubians from the early 19th century</div></div></div><p>
In the 14th century, the Dongolan government collapsed and the region became divided and dominated by Arabs. The next centuries would see several Arab invasions of the region, as well as the establishment of a number of smaller kingdoms. Northern Nubia was brought under Egyptian control, while the south came under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sennar" title="Kingdom of Sennar">Kingdom of Sennar</a> in the 16th century. The entire region would come under Egyptian control during the rule of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali</a> in the early 19th century, and later became a joint Anglo-Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Condominium_(international_law)" title="Condominium (international law)">condominium</a>. </p><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_invasion_of_Sudan_1820-24#Invasion_of_Nubia" title="Egyptian invasion of Sudan 1820-24">Egyptian invasion of Sudan 1820-24 § Invasion of Nubia</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_issues">Contemporary issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nubia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Contemporary issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nubian_people" title="Nubian people">Nubian people</a></div>
<p>With the end of colonialism and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_Revolution_of_1952" title="Egyptian Revolution of 1952">establishment</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Egypt" title="Republic of Egypt">Republic of Egypt</a> (1953), and the secession of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Sudan" title="Republic of Sudan">Republic of Sudan</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Egyptian_Sudan" title="Anglo-Egyptian Sudan">unity with Egypt</a> (1956), Nubia was divided between Egypt and Sudan.
</p><p>During the early-1970s, many Egyptian and Sudanese Nubians were forcibly resettled to make room for <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nasser" title="Lake Nasser">Lake Nasser</a> after the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">dams</a> at <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">Aswan</a>. Nubian villages can now be found north of Aswan on the west bank of the Nile and on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elephantine_Island" title="Elephantine Island">Elephantine Island</a>; and many Nubians now live in large cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>.
</p>
