<p><b>Frank Frederick Borman II</b> (born March 14, 1928), (<a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Col</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">USAF</a>, Ret.), is a retired <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_engineering" title="Aeronautical engineering">aeronautical</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a>, and the Commander of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a>, the first mission to fly around the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, together with crew mates <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">Bill Anders</a>, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts#Apollo_astronauts_who_flew_to_the_Moon_without_landing" title="List of Apollo astronauts">first of 24 humans to do so</a>. Before flying on Apollo, he set a fourteen-day spaceflight endurance record on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a>, and served on the NASA review board which investigated the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> fire. After leaving NASA, he was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer">Chief Executive Officer</a> (CEO) of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> from 1975 to 1986. Borman is a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Space_Medal_of_Honor" title="Congressional Space Medal of Honor">Congressional Space Medal of Honor</a>. As of  2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> he is the oldest living former American astronaut, eleven days older than fellow astronaut Jim Lovell.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_early_career">Education and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Borman was born on March 14, 1928, in <a href="/wiki/Gary,_Indiana" title="Gary, Indiana">Gary, Indiana</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borman_Expressway" title="Borman Expressway">Frank Borman Expressway</a> is named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is of <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> descent, born as the only child to parents Edwin and Marjorie Borman. Because he suffered from numerous <a href="/wiki/Maxillary_sinus" title="Maxillary sinus">sinus</a> problems in the cold and damp weather, his father packed up the family and moved to the better climate of <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">Tucson, Arizona</a>, which Borman considers his home town. He started to fly at the age of 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucson_High_School" title="Tucson High School">Tucson High School</a> in 1946. He received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military_Academy" title="United States Military Academy">United States Military Academy</a> at <a href="/wiki/West_Point,_New_York" title="West Point, New York">West Point</a>, in 1950, where he served as an Army Football Manager, and along with part of his graduating class, he entered the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> (USAF) and became a <a href="/wiki/Fighter_pilot" title="Fighter pilot">fighter pilot</a>. He received his <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_Engineering" title="Aeronautical Engineering">Aeronautical Engineering</a> from the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> in 1957. Later, Borman was selected for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_Test_Pilot_School" title="U.S. Air Force Test Pilot School">Aerospace Research Pilot School</a> (Class 60C, I) and became a <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>. He attended the six-week <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School#Advanced_Management_Program" title="Harvard Business School">Advanced Management Program</a> in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman married Susan Bugbee in 1950, and they have two sons: Frederick and Edwin, and four grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_experience">Flight experience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flight experience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following graduation, Borman was a career U.S. Air Force officer. Prior to joining the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a>'s space program in 1962, he received his <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_aeronautical_rating" title="U.S. Air Force aeronautical rating">pilot wings</a> in 1951 and served as a <a href="/wiki/Fighter_pilot" title="Fighter pilot">fighter pilot</a> with the <a href="/wiki/44th_Fighter_Squadron" title="44th Fighter Squadron">44th Fighter Bomber Squadron</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippine Islands</a> from 1951 to 1953, and as an operational pilot and <a href="/wiki/Flight_instructor" title="Flight instructor">flight instructor</a> in various squadrons in the United States, from 1953 until 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> Most of his flying was on <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-80_Shooting_Star" title="Lockheed P-80 Shooting Star">F-80s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_F-84_Thunderjet" title="Republic F-84 Thunderjet">F-84s</a>, swept wing <a href="/wiki/Republic_F-84F_Thunderstreak" title="Republic F-84F Thunderstreak">F-84Fs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-33" title="T-33">T-33s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1957, he became an <a href="/wiki/Assistant_professor" title="Assistant professor">assistant professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">fluid mechanics</a> at West Point, where he served until 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a204_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-a204-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1960, Borman began serving as an experimental <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a> engaged in organizing and administering special projects for the USAF Aerospace Research Pilot School, 1960–1962.
</p><p>During his military service, he logged over 6,000 hours of flying time.
</p><p>In 1966 and 1968, Borman served as special presidential ambassador on trips throughout the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> and Europe. In 1970, he undertook another special presidential mission, a worldwide tour to seek support for the release of American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> held by <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_career">NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Gemini">Project Gemini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Project Gemini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:G5C_spacesuit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/G5C_spacesuit.jpg/220px-G5C_spacesuit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/G5C_spacesuit.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:G5C_spacesuit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frank Borman and Jim Lovell walking up the ramp to the elevator before the Gemini 7 mission</div></div></div>
<p>Borman was selected by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Group_2" title="Astronaut Group 2">second NASA astronaut group</a> in 1962. He was backup Command Pilot for Gemini 4 and was chosen as the Command Pilot for <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a204_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-a204-5">[5]</a></sup> He was one of just four of this group chosen to command their first Gemini missions, the others being <a href="/wiki/James_McDivitt" title="James McDivitt">James McDivitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elliot_See" title="Elliot See">Elliot See</a>. (See was killed in a <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">T-38 trainer</a> <a href="/wiki/1966_NASA_T-38_crash" title="1966 NASA T-38 crash">jet crash</a> three months before his mission. Astronauts <a href="/wiki/Gerald_P._Carr" title="Gerald P. Carr">Gerald Carr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Engle" title="Joe Engle">Joe Engle</a>, selected later, also commanded their first space flights.)
</p><p>Borman flew Gemini 7 in December 1965 with Pilot <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">James A. Lovell, Jr.</a> This was a long-endurance flight which set a fourteen-day record, and also acted as the target vehicle in the first <a href="/wiki/Space_rendezvous" title="Space rendezvous">space rendezvous</a> performed by <a href="/wiki/Gemini_6A" title="Gemini 6A">Gemini 6A</a>. The two craft came within one foot (30 centimeters) of each other and they took turns flying around each other, taking both still and motion pictures.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_program">Apollo program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apollo program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a></div>
<p>Borman was selected in late 1966 to command the third manned <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> mission, planned as an elliptical <a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">medium Earth orbit</a> test of the second manned <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> (LM) on the first manned launch of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> lunar rocket sometime in 1967 or early 1968. However, in January 1967, the crew of the first manned Apollo mission <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> (then designated "AS-204"), <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">Virgil I. "Gus" Grissom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ed_White_(astronaut)" title="Ed White (astronaut)">Ed White</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roger_B._Chaffee" title="Roger B. Chaffee">Roger B. Chaffee</a> were killed in a fire aboard their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command_Module" title="Apollo Command Module">Command Module</a>, delaying the Apollo program. Following this deadly accident, the <i>AS-204 Accident Review Board</i> was charged with investigating the root causes of the fire and recommending corrective measures – Borman was chosen as the only astronaut to serve on the review board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-jsc-7">[7]</a></sup> In April 1967, while serving on the board, Borman was one of five astronauts who testified before a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> committee investigating the Apollo 1 fire. His testimony helped convince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> that Apollo would be safe to fly again.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ap8-S68-56531.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="546" data-file-width="750" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ap8-S68-56531.jpg/220px-Ap8-S68-56531.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ap8-S68-56531.jpg/330px-Ap8-S68-56531.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ap8-S68-56531.jpg/440px-Ap8-S68-56531.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ap8-S68-56531.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In-flight footage of Frank Borman (center) during the Apollo 8 mission</div></div></div>
<p>Borman was then reassigned to his LM test mission, now planned to fly as "Apollo 9" in early 1969 after a first, <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> LM flight commanded by McDivitt in December 1968. But the LM was not ready for its first flight, leading NASA management to decide to replace Borman's mission with a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit</a> flight using just the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command/Service Module</a> as <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> in December, making McDivitt's flight <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> in March 1969. Borman's Lunar Module Pilot (and spacecraft systems engineer) was <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>. The Command Module Pilot and navigator, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(astronaut)" title="Michael Collins (astronaut)">Michael Collins</a>, had to have back surgery and was replaced by his backup, James Lovell, reuniting Borman with his Gemini 7 crewmate. Apollo 8 went into lunar orbit on December 24 and made ten orbits of the Moon in 20 hours before returning to Earth.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1244" data-file-width="1796" decoding="async" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg/220px-The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg/330px-The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg/440px-The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_crew_of_Apollo_8_addresses_the_crew_of_the_USS_Yorktown_after_a_successful_splashdown_and_recovery.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Borman (at microphone) and his crewmates Anders and Lovell, on the deck of <a href="/wiki/USS_Yorktown_(CV-10)" title="USS Yorktown (CV-10)">USS <i>Yorktown</i> (CV-10)</a> following Apollo 8 splashdown</div></div></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And, from the crew of Apollo 8, we close with good night, good luck, a Merry Christmas, and God bless all of you—all of you on the good Earth.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Frank Borman, live broadcast from lunar orbit (1968)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[9]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The Apollo 8 mission is also notable for the <i><a href="/wiki/Earthrise" title="Earthrise">Earthrise</a></i> photograph taken by <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a> of the Earth rising above the Lunar horizon as the Command Module orbited the Moon, and for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8_Genesis_reading" title="Apollo 8 Genesis reading">reading from Genesis</a> that was broadcast to Earth from Lunar orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The success of Apollo 8 avoided jeopardizing the goal of making the first manned <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">Moon landing</a> by the end of 1969 by not waiting for the delayed LM, and also provided invaluable experience in navigation to the Moon.
</p><p>Space journalist <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Chaikin" title="Andrew Chaikin">Andrew Chaikin</a> claims that, following the death of Gus Grissom, Borman became astronaut chief <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">Deke Slayton</a>'s choice to command the first Moon landing attempt. In the fall of 1968, Slayton offered command of the first landing to Borman, who turned it down, choosing to retire instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>128</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-NASA_career">Post-NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Air_Lines">Eastern Air Lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eastern Air Lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early 1969, Borman became a special advisor to <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> and after retiring from NASA and the U.S. Air Force in 1970 as a <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a>, he was made Senior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_President" title="Vice President">Vice President</a>-Operations Group at the company. In 1972, Borman received a phone call one evening informing him that <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines_Flight_401" title="Eastern Air Lines Flight 401">Eastern Flight 401</a> had disappeared off the radarscope near Florida's <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a>. Soon, Borman himself was wading through the murky swamps, helping rescue crash victims and loading survivors into rescue helicopters.
</p><p>He was later promoted to Executive Vice President-General Operations Manager and was elected to Eastern's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Directors" title="Board of Directors">Board of Directors</a> in July 1974. In May 1975, Borman was elected <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">President</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Operating_Officer" title="Chief Operating Officer">Chief Operating Officer</a>. He was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer">Chief Executive Officer</a> of Eastern in December 1975 and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Board" title="Chairman of the Board">Chairman of the Board</a> in December 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-jsc-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>After Borman became Eastern's CEO, the company went through the four most profitable years in the company's history. However, Eastern would struggle in the 1980s due to high debt loads caused by Borman’s decision to twice borrow money to purchase new aircraft. A cost savings agreement proposed by Eastern’s unions would bring the airline back to profitability in the early 1980s. However, in 1983, contentious battles with labor unions, particularly the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Machinists_and_Aerospace_Workers" title="International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers">International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers</a> (IAM) led to union calls for Borman’s removal as CEO before the unions would agree to any changes in union contracts. In response, Eastern’s board decided to sell the airline to <a href="/wiki/Texas_Air_Corporation" title="Texas Air Corporation">Texas Air Corporation</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lorenzo" title="Frank Lorenzo">Frank Lorenzo</a>. Borman retired from Eastern in June 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jsc_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-jsc-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="924" data-file-width="2288" decoding="async" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg/220px-FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg/330px-FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg/440px-FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FrankBormanJamesLovellWilliamAndersDec2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Borman with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a> in December 2008</div></div></div>
<p>Upon retirement, Borman and his wife, Susan Borman, moved to <a href="/wiki/Las_Cruces,_New_Mexico" title="Las Cruces, New Mexico">Las Cruces, New Mexico</a>, where he enjoyed rebuilding and flying vintage <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a> from the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> era. For a time, he was the majority owner of a Las Cruces <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> dealership founded by his son, Fred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman purchased a cattle ranch in the <a href="/wiki/Bighorn_Mountains" title="Bighorn Mountains">Bighorn Mountains</a> of southern <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> in 1998, where he and his wife lived for nearly two decades. In addition to tending cattle, Borman continued his hobbies in rebuilding and modeling aircraft during these years.
</p><p>Following <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a>'s death in December 2016, Borman became the oldest living American astronaut. Borman gave the commencement address to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a>'s 2008 graduating class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He is a member of the Society of Antique Modelers (SAM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ama_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ama-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_media">In media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frank_Borman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Frank_Borman.jpg/220px-Frank_Borman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Frank_Borman.jpg/330px-Frank_Borman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dc/Frank_Borman.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frank_Borman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frank Borman</div></div></div>
<p>In the 1998 <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> <a href="/wiki/Miniseries" title="Miniseries">miniseries</a> <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(miniseries)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (miniseries)">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>, Borman was played by <a href="/wiki/David_Andrews_(actor)" title="David Andrews (actor)">David Andrews</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman has since appeared in the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/When_We_Left_Earth:_The_NASA_Missions" title="When We Left Earth: The NASA Missions">When We Left Earth: The NASA Missions</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On November 13, 2008, Borman and his fellow <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> crewmates, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Anders" title="Bill Anders">Bill Anders</a>, appeared on the <a href="/wiki/NASA_TV" title="NASA TV">NASA TV</a> channel to discuss the Apollo 8 mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman also appeared in the 2005 documentary "Race to the Moon", which was shown as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> <i><a href="/wiki/American_Experience" title="American Experience">American Experience</a></i> series. The film, renamed in 2013 as "Earthrise: The First Lunar Voyage," centered on the events that led up to NASA's Apollo 8 mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borman was featured on Episode 655 of <a href="/wiki/This_American_Life" title="This American Life">This American Life</a> radio program titled <i>The Not-So-Great Unknown</i>, airing on August 24, 2018. His interview with <a href="/wiki/David_Kestenbaum" title="David Kestenbaum">David Kestenbaum</a> in Act One of the episode titled <i>So Over The Moon</i> centered on his unconventional outlook towards space travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_Borman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Interstate_80_in_Indiana" title="Interstate 80 in Indiana">I-80</a>/<a href="/wiki/Interstate_94_in_Indiana" title="Interstate 94 in Indiana">I-94</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_County,_Indiana" title="Lake County, Indiana">Lake County, Indiana</a>, which runs through his birth town of <a href="/wiki/Gary,_Indiana" title="Gary, Indiana">Gary, Indiana</a>, is named the Frank Borman Expressway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>A K-8 school on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis-Monthan_Air_Force_Base" title="Davis-Monthan Air Force Base">Davis-Monthan Air Force Base</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucson" title="Tucson">Tucson</a>, Arizona is named in Borman's honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>A school in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a> is named Frank Borman Elementary School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
