<p>A <b>molecular cloud</b>, sometimes called a <b>stellar nursery</b> (if <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> is occurring within), is a type of <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_cloud" title="Interstellar cloud">interstellar cloud</a>, the density and size of which permit the formation of molecules, most commonly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>). This is in contrast to other areas of the interstellar medium that contain predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionized_gas" title="Ionized gas">ionized gas</a>.
</p><p>Molecular hydrogen is difficult to detect by infrared and radio observations, so the molecule most often used to determine the presence of H<sub>2</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO). The ratio between CO <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> and H<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> is thought to be constant, although there are reasons to doubt this assumption in observations of some other <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within molecular clouds are regions with higher density, where lots of dust and gas cores reside, called clumps. These clumps are the beginning of star formation, if gravity can overcome the high density and force the dust and gas to collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barnard_68.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4193" data-file-width="4193" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Barnard_68.jpg/220px-Barnard_68.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Barnard_68.jpg/330px-Barnard_68.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Barnard_68.jpg/440px-Barnard_68.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barnard_68.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molecular cloud <a href="/wiki/Barnard_68" title="Barnard 68">Barnard 68</a>, about 500 ly distant and 0.5 ly in diameter.</div></div></div>
<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, molecular gas clouds account for less than one percent of the volume of the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (ISM), yet it is also the densest part of the medium, comprising roughly half of the total gas mass interior to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s galactic orbit. The bulk of the molecular gas is contained in a ring between 3.5 and 7.5 <a href="/wiki/Parsec#Parsecs_and_kiloparsecs" title="Parsec">kiloparsecs</a> (11,000 and 24,000 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>) from the center of the Milky Way (the Sun is about 8.5 kiloparsecs from the center).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferriere2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferriere2001-3">[3]</a></sup> Large scale CO maps of the galaxy show that the position of this gas correlates with the spiral arms of the galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> That molecular gas occurs predominantly in the spiral arms suggests that molecular clouds must form and dissociate on a timescale shorter than 10 million years—the time it takes for material to pass through the arm region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-williams2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-williams2000-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1820" data-file-width="3951" decoding="async" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg/220px-Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg/330px-Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg/440px-Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Violent_birth_announcement_from_an_infant_star.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Circinus molecular cloud has a mass around 250,000 times that of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Vertically to the plane of the galaxy, the molecular gas inhabits the narrow midplane of the galactic disc with a characteristic <a href="/wiki/Scale_height" title="Scale height">scale height</a>, <i>Z</i>, of approximately 50 to 75 parsecs, much thinner than the warm <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> (<i>Z </i>from 130 to 400 parsecs) and warm <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionized</a> (<i>Z </i>around 1000 parsecs) gaseous <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium#Interstellar_matter" title="Interstellar medium">components of the ISM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The exception to the ionized-gas distribution are <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">H II regions</a>, which are bubbles of hot ionized gas created in molecular clouds by the intense radiation given off by <a href="/wiki/OB_star" title="OB star">young massive stars</a> and as such they have approximately the same vertical distribution as the molecular gas.
</p><p>This distribution of molecular gas is averaged out over large distances; however, the small scale distribution of the gas is highly irregular with most of it concentrated in discrete clouds and cloud complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferriere2001_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferriere2001-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_molecular_cloud">Types of molecular cloud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of molecular cloud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Giant_molecular_clouds">Giant molecular clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Giant molecular clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1449" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg/220px-Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg/330px-Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg/440px-Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Part_of_the_Taurus_Molecular_Cloud.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Part of the Taurus Molecular Cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>A vast assemblage of molecular gas with a mass of approximately 10<sup>3</sup> to 10<sup>7</sup> times the mass of the Sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-murray-9">[9]</a></sup> is called a <b>giant molecular cloud</b> (<b>GMC</b>). GMCs are around 15 to 600 light-years in diameter (5 to 200 parsecs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-murray-9">[9]</a></sup> Whereas the average density in the solar vicinity is one particle per cubic centimetre, the average density of a GMC is a hundred to a thousand times as great. Although the Sun is much more dense than a GMC, the volume of a GMC is so great that it contains much more mass than the Sun. The substructure of a GMC is a complex pattern of filaments, sheets, bubbles, and irregular clumps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-williams2000_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-williams2000-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The densest parts of the filaments and clumps are called "molecular cores", while the densest molecular cores are called "dense molecular cores" and have densities in excess of 10<sup>4</sup> to 10<sup>6</sup> particles per cubic centimeter. Observationally, typical molecular cores are traced with CO and dense molecular cores are traced with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. The concentration of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> within molecular cores is normally sufficient to block light from background stars so that they appear in silhouette as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_nebulae" title="Dark nebulae">dark nebulae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-francesco2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-francesco2006-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>
GMCs are so large that "local" ones can cover a significant fraction of a constellation; thus they are often referred to by the name of that constellation, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Molecular_Cloud_Complex" title="Orion Molecular Cloud Complex">Orion Molecular Cloud</a> (OMC) or the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Molecular_Cloud" title="Taurus Molecular Cloud">Taurus Molecular Cloud</a> (TMC). These local GMCs are arrayed in a ring in the neighborhood of the Sun coinciding with the <a href="/wiki/Gould_Belt" title="Gould Belt">Gould Belt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The most massive collection of molecular clouds in the galaxy forms an asymmetrical ring about the galactic center at a radius of 120 parsecs; the largest component of this ring is the <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_B2" title="Sagittarius B2">Sagittarius B2</a> complex. The Sagittarius region is chemically rich and is often used as an exemplar by astronomers searching for new molecules in interstellar space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3600" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg/220px-Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg/330px-Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg/440px-Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Distribution_of_molecular_gas_in_30_merging_galaxies.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of molecular gas in 30 merging galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_molecular_clouds">Small molecular clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Small molecular clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bok_globule" title="Bok globule">Bok globule</a></div>
<p>Isolated gravitationally-bound small molecular clouds with masses less than a few hundred times that of the Sun are called <a href="/wiki/Bok_globule" title="Bok globule">Bok globules</a>. The densest parts of small molecular clouds are equivalent to the molecular cores found in GMCs and are often included in the same studies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-latitude_diffuse_molecular_clouds">High-latitude diffuse molecular clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: High-latitude diffuse molecular clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infrared_cirrus" title="Infrared cirrus">Infrared cirrus</a></div>
<p>In 1984 <a href="/wiki/IRAS" title="IRAS">IRAS</a> identified a new type of diffuse molecular cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> These were diffuse filamentary clouds that are visible at high <a href="/wiki/Galactic_coordinate_system" title="Galactic coordinate system">galactic latitudes</a>. These clouds have a typical density of 30 particles per cubic centimeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes">Processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cepheus_B.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1074" data-file-width="1432" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cepheus_B.jpg/220px-Cepheus_B.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cepheus_B.jpg/330px-Cepheus_B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cepheus_B.jpg/440px-Cepheus_B.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cepheus_B.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young stars in and around molecular cloud <a href="/wiki/Cepheus_(constellation)" title="Cepheus (constellation)">Cepheus</a> B. Radiation from one bright, massive star is destroying the cloud (from top to bottom in this image) while simultaneously <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Triggered_star_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Triggered star formation (page does not exist)">triggering</a> the formation of new stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_formation">Star formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Star formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">Star formation</a></div>
<p>The formation of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> occurs exclusively within molecular clouds. This is a natural consequence of their low temperatures and high densities, because the gravitational force acting to collapse the cloud must exceed the internal pressures that are acting "outward" to prevent a collapse. There is observed evidence that the large, star-forming clouds are confined to a large degree by their own gravity (like stars, planets, and galaxies) rather than by external pressure. The evidence comes from the fact that the "turbulent" velocities inferred from CO linewidth scale in the same manner as the orbital velocity (a <a href="/wiki/Virial_theorem" title="Virial theorem">virial</a> relation).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">Physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Serpens_south.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2014" data-file-width="1410" decoding="async" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Serpens_south.jpg/220px-Serpens_south.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Serpens_south.jpg/330px-Serpens_south.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Serpens_south.jpg/440px-Serpens_south.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Serpens_south.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Serpens_South" title="Serpens South">Serpens South</a> star cluster is embedded in a filamentary molecular cloud, seen as a dark ribbon passing vertically through the cluster. This cloud has served as a testbed for studies of molecular cloud stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriesenBourke2016_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FriesenBourke2016-17">[17]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The physics of molecular clouds is poorly understood and much debated. Their internal motions are governed by <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> in a cold, <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetized</a> gas, for which the turbulent motions are highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> but comparable to the speeds of magnetic disturbances. This state is thought to lose energy rapidly, requiring either an overall collapse or a steady reinjection of energy. At the same time, the clouds are known to be disrupted by some process—most likely the effects of massive stars—before a significant fraction of their mass has become stars.
</p><p>Molecular clouds, and especially GMCs, are often the home of <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_maser" title="Astrophysical maser">astronomical masers</a>.
</p>
