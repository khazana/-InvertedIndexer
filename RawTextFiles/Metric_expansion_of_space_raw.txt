<p>The <b>expansion of the universe</b> is the increase of the <a href="/wiki/Proper_length" title="Proper length">distance</a> between two <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe#Size" title="Observable universe">distant parts of the universe</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe" title="Chronology of the universe">time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170220_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170220-1">[1]</a></sup> It is an <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_and_extrinsic_properties_(philosophy)" title="Intrinsic and extrinsic properties (philosophy)">intrinsic</a> expansion whereby <i>the scale of space itself changes</i>. The  universe does not expand "into" anything and does not require space to exist "outside" it. Technically, neither space nor objects in space move. Instead it is the <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity)" title="Metric tensor (general relativity)">metric</a> governing the size and geometry of <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> itself that changes in scale. Although light and objects within spacetime cannot travel faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, this limitation does not restrict the metric itself. To an observer it appears that space is expanding and all but <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">the nearest galaxies</a> are receding into the distance.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Inflationary_epoch" title="Inflationary epoch">inflationary epoch</a> about 10<sup>−32</sup> of a second after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, the universe suddenly expanded, and its volume increased by a factor of at least 10<sup>78</sup> (an expansion of distance by a factor of at least 10<sup>26</sup> in each of the three dimensions), equivalent to expanding an object 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometer</a> (10<sup>−9</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">m</a>, about half the width of a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>) in length to one approximately 10.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_years" title="Light years">light years</a> (about 10<sup>17</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">m</a> or 62 trillion miles) long. A much slower and gradual expansion of space continued after this, until at around 9.8 billion years after the Big Bang (4 billion years ago) it began to gradually <a href="/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe" title="Accelerating expansion of the universe">expand more quickly</a>, and is still doing so.
</p><p>The metric expansion of space is of a kind completely different from the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">expansions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosions</a> seen in daily life. It also seems to be a property of the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_of_general_relativity#Local_versus_global_structure" title="Mathematics of general relativity">universe as a whole</a> rather than a phenomenon that applies just to one part of the universe or can be observed from "outside" it.
</p><p>Metric expansion is a key feature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology" title="Big Bang cosmology">Big Bang cosmology</a>, is modeled mathematically with the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric">Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker metric</a> and is a generic property of the universe we inhabit. However, the model is valid only on large scales (roughly the scale of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a> and above), because <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational attraction</a> binds matter together strongly enough that metric expansion cannot be observed at this time, on a smaller scale. As such, the only galaxies receding from one another as a result of metric expansion are those separated by cosmologically relevant scales larger than the <a href="/wiki/Jeans_instability" title="Jeans instability">length scales</a> associated with the gravitational collapse that are possible in the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a> given the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matter_density" title="Matter density">matter density</a> and average expansion rate.
</p><p>Physicists have postulated the existence of <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, appearing as a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> in the simplest gravitational models as a way to explain the acceleration. According to the simplest extrapolation of the currently-favored cosmological model, the <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">Lambda-CDM model</a>, this acceleration becomes more dominant into the future. In June 2016, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> scientists reported that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> was found to be expanding 5% to 9% faster than thought earlier, based on studies using the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20160603_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20160603-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>While <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> prohibits objects from moving faster than light with respect to a <a href="/wiki/Local_reference_frame" title="Local reference frame">local reference frame</a> where spacetime can be treated as <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">flat and unchanging</a>, it does not apply to situations where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_curvature" title="Spacetime curvature">spacetime curvature</a> or evolution in time become important.  These situations are described by <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, which allows the separation between two distant objects to increase faster than the speed of light, although the definition of "separation" is different from that used in an inertial frame.  This can be seen when observing distant galaxies more than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_radius" title="Hubble radius">Hubble radius</a> away from us (approximately 4.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigaparsec" title="Gigaparsec">gigaparsecs</a> or 14.7 billion <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>); these galaxies have a recession speed that is faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>. Light that is emitted today from galaxies beyond the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_horizon#Event_horizon" title="Cosmological horizon">cosmological event horizon</a>, about 5 gigaparsecs or 16 billion light-years, will never reach us, although we can still see the light that these galaxies emitted in the past. Because of the high rate of expansion, it is also possible for a distance between two objects to be greater than the value calculated by multiplying the speed of light by the age of the universe. These details are a frequent source of confusion among amateurs and even professional physicists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astro-ph/0310808_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-astro-ph/0310808-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to the non-intuitive nature of the subject and what has been described by some as "careless" choices of wording, certain descriptions of the metric expansion of space and the misconceptions to which such descriptions can lead are an ongoing subject of discussion within <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and communication of scientific concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiting_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whiting-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baryshev_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baryshev-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peacock_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peacock-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_inflation">Cosmic inflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cosmic inflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">inflation (cosmology)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflaton" title="Inflaton">inflaton</a></div>
<p>In 1929, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> discovered that light from remote galaxies was <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifted</a>; i.e. the more remote galaxies were, the more shifted was the light coming from them. This observation was quickly interpreted as <i>galaxies  receding from earth</i>. If <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> is not in some special, privileged, central position in the universe, then it would mean all galaxies are moving apart, and the further away, the faster they are moving away. It is now understood that the universe is expanding, carrying the galaxies with it, and causing this observation.  Many other observations agree, and also lead to the same conclusion. However, for many years it was not clear why or how the universe might be expanding, or what it might signify.
</p><p>Based on a huge amount of experimental observation and theoretical work, it is now believed that the reason for the observation is that <i>space itself is expanding</i>, and that it expanded very rapidly within the first fraction of a second after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>. This kind of expansion is known as the "metric expansion". In mathematics and physics, a "<a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a>" means a measure of distance, and the term implies that <i>the sense of distance within the universe is itself changing</i>, although at this time it is far too small an effect to see on less than an intergalactic scale.
</p><p>The modern explanation for the metric expansion of space was proposed by physicist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Guth" title="Alan Guth">Alan Guth</a> in 1979, while investigating the problem of why no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_monopoles" title="Magnetic monopoles">magnetic monopoles</a> are seen today. Guth found in his investigation that if the universe contained a <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">field</a> that has a positive-energy <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum" title="False vacuum">false vacuum</a> state, then according to <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> it would generate an <i>exponential expansion of space</i>. It was very quickly realized that such an expansion would resolve many other long-standing problems. These problems arise from the observation that to look as it does today, the universe would have to have started from very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_tuned_universe" title="Fine tuned universe">finely tuned</a>, or "special" initial conditions at the Big Bang. Inflation theory largely resolves these problems as well, thus making a universe like ours much more likely in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Bang_theory" title="Big Bang theory">Big Bang theory</a>.
</p><p>No field responsible for the cosmic inflation has been discovered. However such a field, if found in the future, would be <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar</a>. The first similar <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar field</a> proven to exist was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higgs_field" title="Higgs field">only discovered</a> in 2012 - 2013 and is still being researched. So it is not seen as problematic that a field responsible for cosmic inflation and the metric expansion of space has not yet been discovered.
</p><p>The proposed field and its <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">subatomic particles</a> related to it) have been named <i><a href="/wiki/Inflaton" title="Inflaton">inflaton</a></i>. If this field did not exist, scientists would have to propose a different explanation for all the observations that strongly suggest a metric expansion of space has occurred, and is still occurring much more slowly today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_metrics_and_comoving_coordinates">Overview of metrics and comoving coordinates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview of metrics and comoving coordinates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">Metric (mathematics)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensor</a></div>
<p>To understand the metric expansion of the universe, it is helpful to discuss briefly what a metric is, and how metric expansion works.
</p><p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor" title="Metric tensor">metric</a></b> defines the concept of <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a>, by stating in mathematical terms how distances between two nearby points in space are measured, in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinate system</a>. Coordinate systems locate points in a space (of whatever number of <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensions</a>) by assigning unique positions on a grid, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate" title="Coordinate">coordinates</a>, to each point. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates" title="Cartesian coordinates">x-y graphs</a> are common examples of coordinates. A metric is a <a href="/wiki/Formula" title="Formula">formula</a> which describes how a number known as "distance" is to be measured between two points.
</p><p>It may seem obvious that distance is measured by a straight line, but in many cases it is not. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_haul" title="Long haul">long haul</a> aircraft travel along a curve known as a "<a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>" and not a straight line, because that is a better metric for air travel. (A straight line would go through the earth). Another example is planning a car journey, where one might want the shortest journey in terms of travel time - in that case a straight line is a poor choice of metric because the shortest distance by road is not normally a straight line, and even the path nearest to a straight line will not necessarily be the quickest. A final example is the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>, where even for nearby towns, the quickest route for data can be via major connections that go across the country and back again. In this case the metric used will be the shortest time that data takes to travel between two points on the network.
</p><p>In cosmology, we cannot use a ruler to measure metric expansion, because our ruler will also be expanding (extremely slowly). Also any objects on or near earth that we might measure are being held together or pushed apart by several forces which are far larger in their effects. So even if we could measure the tiny expansion that is still happening, we would not notice the change on a small scale or in everyday life. On a large intergalactic scale, we can use other tests of distance and these <i>do</i> show that space is expanding, even if a ruler on earth could not measure it.
</p><p>The metric expansion of space is described using the mathematics of <a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metric</a> <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensors</a>. The coordinate system we use is called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_coordinates" title="Comoving coordinates">comoving coordinates</a>", a type of coordinate system which takes account of <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">time as well as space</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, and allows us to incorporate the effects of both <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general</a> and <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Example:_.22Great_Circle.22_metric_for_Earth.27s_surface"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Example:_&quot;Great_Circle&quot;_metric_for_Earth's_surface">Example: "Great Circle" metric for Earth's surface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Example: &quot;Great Circle&quot; metric for Earth's surface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For example, consider the measurement of distance between two places on the surface of the Earth. This is a simple, familiar example of <a href="/wiki/Spherical_geometry" title="Spherical geometry">spherical geometry</a>. Because the surface of the Earth is two-dimensional, points on the surface of the Earth can be specified by two coordinates — for example, the latitude and longitude. Specification of a metric requires that one first specify the coordinates used. In our simple example of the surface of the Earth, we could choose any kind of coordinate system we wish, for example <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, or X-Y-Z <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian coordinates</a>. Once we have chosen a specific coordinate system, the numerical values of the coordinates of any two points are uniquely determined, and based upon the properties of the space being discussed, the appropriate metric is mathematically established too. On the curved surface of the Earth, we can see this effect in long-haul <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> flights where the distance between two points is measured based upon a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>, rather than the straight line one might plot on a two-dimensional map of the Earth's surface. In general, such shortest-distance paths are called "<a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">geodesics</a>". In <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a>, the geodesic is a straight line, while in <a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">non-Euclidean geometry</a> such as on the Earth's surface, this is not the case. Indeed, even the shortest-distance great circle path is always longer than the Euclidean straight line path which passes through the interior of the Earth. The difference between the straight line path and the shortest-distance great circle path is due to the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of the Earth's surface. While there is always an effect due to this curvature, at short distances the effect is small enough to be unnoticeable.
</p><p>On plane maps, great circles of the Earth are mostly not shown as straight lines. Indeed, there is a seldom-used <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">map projection</a>, namely the <a href="/wiki/Gnomonic_projection" title="Gnomonic projection">gnomonic projection</a>, where all great circles are shown as straight lines, but in this projection, the distance scale varies very much in different areas. There is no map projection in which the distance between any two points on Earth, measured along the great circle geodesics, is directly proportional to their distance on the map; such accuracy is possible only with a globe.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_tensors">Metric tensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metric tensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, the backbone mathematics for <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor" title="Metric tensor">metric tensor</a> can be defined which precisely characterizes the space being described by explaining the way distances should be measured in every possible direction. General relativity necessarily invokes a metric in four dimensions (one of time, three of space) because, in general, different reference frames will experience different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_interval" title="Spacetime interval">intervals</a> of time and space depending on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_frame" title="Inertial frame">inertial frame</a>. This means that the metric tensor in general relativity relates precisely how two <a href="/wiki/Event_(relativity)" title="Event (relativity)">events</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> are separated. A metric expansion occurs when the metric tensor changes with <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> (and, specifically, whenever the spatial part of the metric gets larger as time goes forward). This kind of expansion is different from all kinds of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">expansions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosions</a> commonly seen in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> in no small part because times and <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distances</a> are not the same in all reference frames, but are instead subject to change. A useful visualization is to approach the subject rather than objects in a fixed "space" moving apart into "emptiness", as space itself growing between objects without any <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> of the objects themselves. The space between objects shrinks or grows as the various <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">geodesics</a> converge or diverge.
</p><p>Because this expansion is caused by relative changes in the distance-defining metric, this expansion (and the resultant movement apart of objects) is not restricted by the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_bound" title="Upper bound">upper bound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>. Two reference frames that are globally separated can be moving apart faster than light without violating special relativity, although whenever two reference frames diverge from each other faster than the speed of light, there will be observable effects associated with such situations including the existence of various <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_horizon" title="Cosmological horizon">cosmological horizons</a>.
</p><p>Theory and observations suggest that very early in the history of the universe, there was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">inflationary</a> phase where the metric changed very rapidly, and that the remaining time-dependence of this metric is what we observe as the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Law" title="Hubble Law">Hubble expansion</a>, the moving apart of all gravitationally unbound objects in the universe. The expanding universe is therefore a fundamental feature of the universe we inhabit — a universe fundamentally different from the <a href="/wiki/Static_universe" title="Static universe">static universe</a> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> first considered when he developed his gravitational theory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comoving_coordinates">Comoving coordinates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comoving coordinates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_coordinates" title="Comoving coordinates">Comoving coordinates</a></div>
<p>In expanding space, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proper_distance" title="Proper distance">proper distances</a> are dynamical quantities which change with time. An easy way to correct for this is to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_coordinates" title="Comoving coordinates">comoving coordinates</a> which remove this feature and allow for a characterization of different locations in the universe without having to characterize the physics associated with metric expansion. In comoving coordinates, the distances between all objects are fixed and the instantaneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamics_(physics)" title="Dynamics (physics)">dynamics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> are determined by the normal <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>. Any time-evolution however must be accounted for by taking into account the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_law" title="Hubble law">Hubble law</a> expansion in the appropriate equations in addition to any other effects that may be operating (<a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a>, for example). Cosmological simulations that run through significant fractions of the universe's history therefore must include such effects in order to make applicable predictions for <a href="/wiki/Observational_cosmology" title="Observational cosmology">observational cosmology</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Understanding_the_expansion_of_the_universe">Understanding the expansion of the universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Understanding the expansion of the universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">Inflation (cosmology)</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_of_expansion_and_change_of_rate_of_expansion">Measurement of expansion and change of rate of expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Measurement of expansion and change of rate of expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redshift_blueshift.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Redshift_blueshift.svg/250px-Redshift_blueshift.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Redshift_blueshift.svg/375px-Redshift_blueshift.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Redshift_blueshift.svg/500px-Redshift_blueshift.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Redshift_blueshift.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>When an object is receding, its light gets stretched (<a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifted</a>). When the object is approaching, its light gets compressed (<a href="/wiki/Blueshift" title="Blueshift">blueshifted</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>In principle, the expansion of the universe could be measured by taking a standard ruler and measuring the distance between two cosmologically distant points, waiting a certain time, and then measuring the distance again, but in practice, standard rulers are not easy to find on cosmological scales and the timescales over which a measurable expansion would be visible are too great to be observable even by multiple generations of humans. The expansion of space is measured indirectly. The <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a> predicts phenomena associated with the expansion, notably the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>-versus-distance relationship known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_Law" title="Hubble's Law">Hubble's Law</a>; functional forms for <a href="/wiki/Distance_measures_(cosmology)" title="Distance measures (cosmology)">cosmological distance measurements</a> that differ from what would be expected if space were not expanding; and an observable change in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Density_parameter" title="Density parameter">matter and energy density</a> of the universe seen at different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lookback_time" title="Lookback time">lookback times</a>.
</p><p>The first measurement of the expansion of space came with Hubble's realization of the velocity vs. redshift relation. Most recently, by comparing the apparent brightness of distant <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">standard candles</a> to the redshift of their host galaxies, the expansion rate of the universe has been measured to be <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">H<sub>0</sub></a> = <span class="nowrap">73.24 ± 1.74 (km/s)/Mpc</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riess2016_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riess2016-8">[8]</a></sup> This means that for every million <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> of distance from the observer, the light received from that distance is <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">cosmologically redshifted</a> by about 73 kilometres per second (160,000 mph). On the other hand, by assuming a cosmological model, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">Lambda-CDM model</a>, one can infer the Hubble constant from the size of the largest fluctuations seen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Microwave_Background" title="Cosmic Microwave Background">Cosmic Microwave Background</a>. A higher Hubble constant would imply a smaller characteristic size of CMB fluctuations, and vice versa. The Planck collaboration measure the expansion rate this way and determine H<sub>0</sub> = <span class="nowrap">67.4 ± 0.5 (km/s)/Mpc</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There is a disagreement between the two measurements, the distance ladder being model-independent and the CMB measurement depending on the fitted model, which hints at new physics beyond our standard cosmological models. 
</p><p>The Hubble parameter is not thought to be constant through time. There are dynamical forces acting on the particles in the universe which affect the expansion rate. It was earlier expected that the Hubble parameter would be decreasing as time went on due to the influence of gravitational interactions in the universe, and thus there is an additional observable quantity in the universe called the <a href="/wiki/Deceleration_parameter" title="Deceleration parameter">deceleration parameter</a> which cosmologists expected to be directly related to the matter density of the universe. Surprisingly, the deceleration parameter was measured by two different groups to be less than zero (actually, consistent with −1) which implied that today the Hubble parameter is converging to a constant value as time goes on. Some cosmologists have whimsically called the effect associated with the "accelerating universe" the "cosmic <a href="/wiki/Jerk_(physics)" title="Jerk (physics)">jerk</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  The 2011 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> was given for the discovery of this phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2018, scientists presented a new third way (two earlier methods, one based on <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifts</a> and another on the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">cosmic distance ladder</a>, gave results that do not agree), using information from <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational wave</a> events (especially those involving the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star_merger" title="Neutron star merger">merger of neutron stars</a>, like <a href="/wiki/GW170817" title="GW170817">GW170817</a>), of determining the <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble Constant</a>, essential in establishing the rate of expansion of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHYS-20181022_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHYS-20181022-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAT-20181017_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20181017-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At cosmological scales the present universe is geometrically flat,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which is to say that the rules of <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Parallel_postulate" title="Parallel postulate">Euclid's fifth postulate</a> hold, though in the past <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> could have been highly curved. In part to accommodate such different geometries, the expansion of the universe is inherently <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativistic</a>; it cannot be modeled with <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> alone, though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milne_universe" title="Milne universe">such models</a> exist, they are at fundamental odds with the observed interaction between matter and spacetime seen in our universe.
</p><p>The images to the right show two views of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_diagram" title="Spacetime diagram">spacetime diagrams</a> that show the large-scale geometry of the universe according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9BCDM" title="ΛCDM">ΛCDM</a> cosmological model. Two of the dimensions of space are omitted, leaving one dimension of space (the dimension that grows as the cone gets larger) and one of time (the dimension that proceeds "up" the cone's surface). The narrow circular end of the diagram corresponds to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_time" title="Cosmological time">cosmological time</a> of 700 million years after the big bang while the wide end is a cosmological time of 18 billion years, where one can see the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerating_universe" title="Accelerating universe">accelerating expansion</a> as a splaying outward of the spacetime, a feature which eventually dominates in this model. The purple grid lines mark off cosmological time at intervals of one billion years from the big bang. The cyan grid lines mark off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">comoving distance</a> at intervals of one billion light years in the present era (less in the past and more in the future). Note that the circular curling of the surface is an artifact of the embedding with no physical significance and is done purely to make the illustration viewable; space does not actually curl around on itself. (A similar effect can be seen in the tubular shape of the <a href="/wiki/Pseudosphere" title="Pseudosphere">pseudosphere</a>.)
</p><p>The brown line on the diagram is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldline" title="Worldline">worldline</a> of the Earth (or, at earlier times, of the matter which condensed to form the Earth). The yellow line is the worldline of the most distant known <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasar</a>. The red line is the path of a light beam emitted by the quasar about 13 billion years ago and reaching the Earth in the present day. The orange line shows the present-day distance between the quasar and the Earth, about 28 billion light years, which is, notably, a larger distance than the age of the universe multiplied by the speed of light: <i>ct</i>.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">equivalence principle</a> of general relativity, the rules of special relativity are <i>locally</i> valid in small regions of spacetime that are approximately flat. In particular, light always travels locally at the speed <i>c</i>; in our diagram, this means, according to the convention of constructing spacetime diagrams, that light beams always make an angle of 45° with the local grid lines. It does not follow, however, that light travels a distance <i>ct</i> in a time <i>t</i>, as the red worldline illustrates. While it always moves locally at <i>c</i>, its time in transit (about 13 billion years) is not related to the distance traveled in any simple way since the universe expands as the light beam traverses space and time. In fact the distance traveled is inherently ambiguous because of the changing scale of the universe. Nevertheless, we can single out two distances which appear to be physically meaningful: the distance between the Earth and the quasar when the light was emitted, and the distance between them in the present era (taking a slice of the cone along the dimension that we've declared to be the spatial dimension). The former distance is about 4 billion light years, much smaller than <i>ct</i> because the universe expanded as the light traveled the distance, the light had to "run against the treadmill" and therefore went farther than the initial separation between the Earth and the quasar. The latter distance (shown by the orange line) is about 28 billion light years, much larger than <i>ct</i>. If expansion could be instantaneously stopped today, it would take 28 billion years for light to travel between the Earth and the quasar while if the expansion had stopped at the earlier time, it would have taken only 4 billion years.
</p><p>The light took much longer than 4 billion years to reach us though it was emitted from only 4 billion light years away, and, in fact, the light emitted towards the Earth was actually moving <i>away</i> from the Earth when it was first emitted, in the sense that the metric distance to the Earth increased with cosmological time for the first few billion years of its travel time, and also indicating that the expansion of space between the Earth and the quasar at the early time was faster than the speed of light. None of this surprising behavior originates from a special property of metric expansion, but simply from local principles of special relativity <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integrated</a> over a curved surface.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topology_of_expanding_space">Topology of expanding space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Topology of expanding space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1980" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg/450px-CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg/675px-CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg/900px-CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CMB_Timeline300_no_WMAP.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A graphical representation of the expansion of the universe with the inflationary epoch represented as the dramatic expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor" title="Metric tensor">metric</a> seen on the left. This diagram can be confusing because the expansion of space looks like it is happening into an empty "nothingness". However, this is a choice made for convenience of visualization: it is not a part of the physical models which describe the expansion.</div></div></div>
<p>Over <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> that makes up the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is expanding. The words '<a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>' and '<a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>', sometimes used interchangeably, have distinct meanings in this context. Here 'space' is a mathematical concept that stands for the three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">manifold</a> into which our respective positions are embedded while 'universe' refers to everything that exists including the matter and energy in space, the extra-dimensions that may be wrapped up in <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">various strings</a>, and the time through which various events take place. The expansion of space is in reference to this 3-D manifold only; that is, the description involves no structures such as extra dimensions or an exterior universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ultimate <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a> of space is <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a posteriori</a></i> — something which in principle must be observed — as there are no constraints that can simply be reasoned out (in other words there can not be any <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> constraints) on how the space in which we live is <a href="/wiki/Simply_connected_space" title="Simply connected space">connected</a> or whether it wraps around on itself as a <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact space</a>. Though certain cosmological models such as <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6del_metric" title="Gödel metric">Gödel's universe</a> even permit bizarre <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldline" title="Worldline">worldlines</a> which intersect with themselves, ultimately the question as to whether we are in something like a "<a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a> universe" where if traveling far enough in one direction would allow one to simply end up back in the same place like going all the way around the surface of a balloon (or a planet like the Earth) is <a href="/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Detection" title="Shape of the universe">an observational question which is constrained as measurable or non-measurable by the universe's global geometry</a>. At present, observations are consistent with the universe being infinite in extent and simply connected, though we are limited in distinguishing between simple and more complicated proposals by <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_horizon" title="Cosmological horizon">cosmological horizons</a>. The universe could be infinite in extent or it could be finite; but the evidence that leads to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">inflationary model</a> of the early universe also implies that the "total universe" is much larger than the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>, and so any edges or exotic geometries or topologies would not be directly observable as light has not reached scales on which such aspects of the universe, if they exist, are still allowed. For all intents and purposes, it is safe to assume that the universe is infinite in spatial extent, without edge or strange connectedness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Regardless of the overall shape of the universe, the question of what the universe is expanding into is one which does not require an answer according to the theories which describe the expansion; the way we define space in our universe in no way requires additional exterior space into which it can expand since an expansion of an infinite expanse can happen without changing the infinite extent of the expanse. All that is certain is that the manifold of space in which we live simply has the property that the distances between objects are getting larger as time goes on. This only implies the simple observational consequences associated with the metric expansion explored below. No "outside" or embedding in hyperspace is required for an expansion to occur. The visualizations often seen of the universe growing as a bubble into nothingness are misleading in that respect. There is no reason to believe there is anything "outside" of the expanding universe into which the universe expands.
</p><p>Even if the overall spatial extent is infinite and thus the universe cannot get any "larger", we still say that space is expanding because, locally, the characteristic distance between objects is increasing. As an infinite space grows, it remains infinite.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density_of_universe_during_expansion">Density of universe during expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Density of universe during expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite being extremely <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">dense</a> when very young and during part of its early expansion - far denser than is usually required to form a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> - the universe did not re-collapse into a black hole. This is because commonly-used calculations for <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">gravitational collapse</a> are usually based upon objects of relatively constant size, such as <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, and do not apply to rapidly expanding space such as the Big Bang.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_expansion_on_small_scales">Effects of expansion on small scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects of expansion on small scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The expansion of space is sometimes described as a force which acts to push objects apart. Though this is an accurate description of the effect of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, it is not an accurate picture of the phenomenon of expansion in general. For much of the universe's history the expansion has been due mainly to <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>. The matter in the very early universe was flying apart for unknown reasons (most likely as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a>) and has simply continued to do so, though at an ever-decreasing<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> rate due to the attractive effect of gravity.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raisinbread.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="735" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Raisinbread.gif/200px-Raisinbread.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Raisinbread.gif/300px-Raisinbread.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Raisinbread.gif/400px-Raisinbread.gif 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raisinbread.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of an expanding raisin bread model. As the bread doubles in width (depth and length), the distances between raisins also double.</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to slowing the overall expansion, gravity causes local clumping of matter into stars and galaxies. Once objects are formed and bound by gravity, they "drop out" of the expansion and do not subsequently expand under the influence of the cosmological metric, there being no force compelling them to do so.
</p><p>There is no difference between the inertial expansion of the universe and the inertial separation of nearby objects in a vacuum; the former is simply a large-scale extrapolation of the latter.
</p><p>Once objects are bound by gravity, they no longer recede from each other. Thus, the Andromeda galaxy, which is bound to the Milky Way galaxy, is actually falling <i>towards</i> us and is not expanding away. Within the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>, the gravitational interactions have changed the inertial patterns of objects such that there is no cosmological expansion taking place. Once one goes beyond the Local Group, the inertial expansion is measurable, though systematic gravitational effects imply that larger and larger parts of space will eventually fall out of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Flow" title="Hubble Flow">Hubble Flow</a>" and end up as bound, non-expanding objects up to the scales of <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a> of galaxies. We can predict such future events by knowing the precise way the Hubble Flow is changing as well as the masses of the objects to which we are being gravitationally pulled. Currently, the Local Group is being gravitationally pulled towards either the <a href="/wiki/Shapley_Supercluster" title="Shapley Supercluster">Shapley Supercluster</a> or the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Attractor" title="Great Attractor">Great Attractor</a>" with which, if dark energy were not acting, we would eventually merge and no longer see expand away from us after such a time.
</p><p>A consequence of metric expansion being due to inertial motion is that a uniform local "explosion" of matter into a vacuum can be locally described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FLRW_metric" title="FLRW metric">FLRW geometry</a>, the same geometry which describes the expansion of the universe as a whole and was also the basis for the simpler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milne_universe" title="Milne universe">Milne universe</a> which ignores the effects of gravity. In particular, general relativity predicts that light will move at the speed <i>c</i> with respect to the local motion of the exploding matter, a phenomenon analogous to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_dragging" title="Frame dragging">frame dragging</a>.
</p><p>The situation changes somewhat with the introduction of dark energy or a cosmological constant. A cosmological constant due to a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_energy" title="Vacuum energy">vacuum energy</a> density has the effect of adding a repulsive force between objects which is proportional (not inversely proportional) to distance. Unlike inertia it actively "pulls" on objects which have clumped together under the influence of gravity, and even on individual atoms. However, this does not cause the objects to grow steadily or to disintegrate; unless they are very weakly bound, they will simply settle into an equilibrium state which is slightly (undetectably) larger than it would otherwise have been. As the universe expands and the matter in it thins, the gravitational attraction decreases (since it is proportional to the density), while the cosmological repulsion increases; thus the ultimate fate of the ΛCDM universe is a near vacuum expanding at an ever-increasing rate under the influence of the cosmological constant. However, the only locally visible effect of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerating_universe" title="Accelerating universe">accelerating expansion</a> is the disappearance (by runaway <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>) of distant galaxies; gravitationally bound objects like the Milky Way do not expand and the Andromeda galaxy is moving fast enough towards us that it will still merge with the Milky Way in 3 billion years time, and it is also likely that the merged supergalaxy that forms will eventually fall in and merge with the nearby <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Cluster" title="Virgo Cluster">Virgo Cluster</a>. However, galaxies lying farther away from this will recede away at ever-increasing speed and be redshifted out of our range of visibility.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_expansion_and_speed_of_light">Metric expansion and speed of light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Metric expansion and speed of light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_universe" title="Early universe">early universe's</a> <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">inflationary period</a>, all the matter and energy in the universe was set on an <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertial trajectory</a> consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">equivalence principle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">general theory of relativity</a> and this is when the <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">precise and regular form of the universe's expansion</a> had its origin (that is, matter in the universe is separating because it was separating in the past due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflaton_field" title="Inflaton field">inflaton field</a>)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="this seems original research, the causes for inflation are still debated, the same for possible pre-inflation non -homogeneity of the universe (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>While <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> prohibits objects from moving faster than light with respect to a <a href="/wiki/Local_reference_frame" title="Local reference frame">local reference frame</a> where spacetime can be treated as <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">flat and unchanging</a>, it does not apply to situations where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_curvature" title="Spacetime curvature">spacetime curvature</a> or evolution in time become important.  These situations are described by <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, which allows the separation between two distant objects to increase faster than the speed of light, although the definition of "distance" here is somewhat different from that used in an inertial frame.  The definition of distance used here is the summation or integration of local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">comoving distances</a>, all done at constant local proper time.  For example, galaxies that are more than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_radius" title="Hubble radius">Hubble radius</a>, approximately 4.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigaparsec" title="Gigaparsec">gigaparsecs</a> or 14.7 billion <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>, away from us have a recession speed that is faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>.  Visibility of these objects depends on the exact expansion history of the universe.  Light that is emitted today from galaxies beyond the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_horizon#Event_horizon" title="Cosmological horizon">cosmological event horizon</a>, about 5 gigaparsecs or 16 billion light-years, will never reach us, although we can still see the light that these galaxies emitted in the past.
</p><p>Because of the high rate of expansion, it is also possible for a distance between two objects to be greater than the value calculated by multiplying the speed of light by the age of the universe. These details are a frequent source of confusion among amateurs and even professional physicists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astro-ph/0310808_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-astro-ph/0310808-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to the non-intuitive nature of the subject and what has been described by some as "careless" choices of wording, certain descriptions of the metric expansion of space and the misconceptions to which such descriptions can lead are an ongoing subject of discussion in the realm of pedagogy and communication of scientific concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whiting_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whiting-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogg_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baryshev_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baryshev-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peacock_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peacock-7">[7]</a></sup> In June 2016, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> scientists reported that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> was found to be expanding 5% to 9% faster than thought earlier, based on studies using the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20160603_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20160603-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_factor">Scale factor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scale factor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At a fundamental level, the expansion of the universe is a property of spatial measurement on the largest measurable scales of our universe. The distances between cosmologically relevant points increases as time passes leading to observable effects outlined below. This feature of the universe can be characterized by a single parameter that is called the <a href="/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology)" title="Scale factor (cosmology)">scale factor</a> which is a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> of time and a single value for all of space at any instant (if the scale factor were a function of space, this would violate the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_principle" title="Cosmological principle">cosmological principle</a>). By convention, the scale factor is set to be unity at the present time and, because the universe is expanding, is smaller in the past and larger in the future. Extrapolating back in time with certain cosmological models will yield a moment when the scale factor was zero; our current understanding of cosmology sets <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">this time at 13.799 ± 0.021 billion years ago</a>. If the universe continues to expand forever, the scale factor will approach infinity in the future. In principle, there is no reason that the expansion of the universe must be <a href="/wiki/Monotonic_function" title="Monotonic function">monotonic</a> and there are models where at some time in the future the scale factor decreases with an attendant contraction of space rather than an expansion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_conceptual_models_of_expansion">Other conceptual models of expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other conceptual models of expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The expansion of space is often illustrated with conceptual models which show only the size of space at a particular time, leaving the dimension of time implicit.
</p><p>In the "<a href="/wiki/Ant_on_a_rubber_rope" title="Ant on a rubber rope">ant on a rubber rope</a> model" one imagines an ant (idealized as pointlike) crawling at a constant speed on a perfectly elastic rope which is constantly stretching. If we stretch the rope in accordance with the ΛCDM scale factor and think of the ant's speed as the speed of light, then this analogy is numerically accurate — the ant's position over time will match the path of the red line on the embedding diagram above.
</p><p>In the "rubber sheet model" one replaces the rope with a flat two-dimensional rubber sheet which expands uniformly in all directions. The addition of a second spatial dimension raises the possibility of showing local perturbations of the spatial geometry by local curvature in the sheet.
</p><p>In the "balloon model" the flat sheet is replaced by a spherical balloon which is inflated from an initial size of zero (representing the big bang). A balloon has positive Gaussian curvature while observations suggest that the real universe is spatially flat, but this inconsistency can be eliminated by making the balloon very large so that it is locally flat to within the limits of observation. This analogy is potentially confusing since it wrongly suggests that the big bang took place at the center of the balloon. In fact points off the surface of the balloon have no meaning, even if they were occupied by the balloon at an earlier time.
</p><p>In the "raisin bread model" one imagines a loaf of raisin bread expanding in the oven. The loaf (space) expands as a whole, but the raisins (gravitationally bound objects) do not expand; they merely grow farther away from each other.
</p>

<h3><span id="Hubble.27s_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hubble's_law">Hubble's law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hubble's law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Technically, the metric expansion of space is a feature of many solutions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. This explains observations which indicate that <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> that are more distant from us are <a href="/wiki/Recessional_velocity" title="Recessional velocity">receding</a> faster than galaxies that are closer to us (see <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmological_constant_and_the_Friedmann_equations">Cosmological constant and the Friedmann equations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cosmological constant and the Friedmann equations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first general relativistic models predicted that a universe which was dynamical and contained ordinary gravitational matter would contract rather than expand. Einstein's first proposal for a solution to this problem involved adding a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> into his theories to balance out the contraction, in order to obtain a static universe solution. But in 1922 <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann" title="Alexander Friedmann">Alexander Friedmann</a> derived a set of equations known as the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann_equations" title="Friedmann equations">Friedmann equations</a>, showing that the universe might expand and presenting the expansion speed in this case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The observations of <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> in 1929 suggested that distant galaxies were all apparently moving away from us, so that many scientists came to accept that the universe was expanding.
</p>
<h3><span id="Hubble.27s_concerns_over_the_rate_of_expansion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hubble's_concerns_over_the_rate_of_expansion">Hubble's concerns over the rate of expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hubble's concerns over the rate of expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the metric expansion of space appeared to be implied by Hubble's 1929 observations, Hubble disagreed with the expanding-universe interpretation of the data:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[...] if redshift are not primarily due to velocity shift [...] the velocity-distance relation is linear, the distribution of the nebula is uniform, there is no evidence of expansion, no trace of curvature, no restriction of the time scale [...] and we find ourselves in the presence of one of the principles of nature that is still unknown to us today [...] whereas, if redshifts are velocity shifts which measure the rate of expansion, the expanding models are definitely inconsistent with the observations that have been made [...] expanding models are a forced interpretation of the observational results.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>E. Hubble, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ap._J." title="Ap. J.">Ap. J.</a></i>, 84, 517, 1936 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[If the redshifts are a Doppler shift ...] the observations as they stand lead to the anomaly of a closed universe, curiously small and dense, and, it may be added, suspiciously young. On the other hand, if redshifts are not Doppler effects, these anomalies disappear and the region observed appears as a small, homogeneous, but insignificant portion of a universe extended indefinitely both in space and time.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>E. Hubble, <i><a href="/wiki/Monthly_Notices_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society">Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society</a></i>, 97, 506, 1937 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Hubble's skepticism about the universe being too small, dense, and young turned out to be based on an observational error. Later investigations appeared to show that Hubble had confused distant <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">H II regions</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variables</a> and the Cepheid variables themselves had been inappropriately lumped together with low-luminosity <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae" title="RR Lyrae">RR Lyrae</a> stars causing calibration errors that led to a value of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Constant" title="Hubble Constant">Hubble Constant</a> of approximately 500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometer" title="Kilometer">km</a>/<a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">s</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaparsec" title="Megaparsec">Mpc</a> instead of the true value of approximately 70 km/s/Mpc. The higher value meant that an expanding universe would have an age of 2 billion years (younger than the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">Age of the Earth</a>) and extrapolating the observed number density of galaxies to a rapidly expanding universe implied a mass density that was too high by a similar factor, enough to force the universe into a peculiar <a href="/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe" title="Shape of the universe">closed</a> geometry which also implied an impending <a href="/wiki/Big_Crunch" title="Big Crunch">Big Crunch</a> that would occur on a similar time-scale. After fixing these errors in the 1950s, the new lower values for the Hubble Constant accorded with the expectations of an older universe and the density parameter was found to be fairly close to a geometrically flat universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, recent measurements of the distances and velocities of faraway galaxies revealed a 9 percent discrepancy in the value of the Hubble constant, implying a universe that seems expanding too fast compared to previous measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2001, Dr. Wendy Freedman determined space to expand at 72 kilometers per second per megaparsec - roughly 3.3 million light years - meaning that for every 3.3 million light years further away from the earth you are, the matter where you are is moving away from earth 72 kilometers a second faster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup> In the summer of 2016, another measurement reported a value of 73 for the constant, thereby contradicting 2013 measurements from the European Planck mission of slower expansion value of 67. The discrepancy opened new questions concerning the nature of dark energy, or of neutrinos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflation_as_an_explanation_for_the_expansion">Inflation as an explanation for the expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Inflation as an explanation for the expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the theoretical developments in the 1980s no one had an explanation for why this seemed to be the case, but with the development of models of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a>, the expansion of the universe became a general feature resulting from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuum_decay" title="Vacuum decay">vacuum decay</a>.  Accordingly, the question "why is the universe expanding?" is now answered by understanding the details of the inflation decay process which occurred in the first <a href="/wiki/Inflationary_epoch" title="Inflationary epoch">10<sup>−32</sup> seconds</a> of the existence of our universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> During inflation, the metric changed <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponentially</a>, causing any volume of space that was smaller than an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> to grow to around 100 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a> across in a time scale similar to the time when inflation occurred (10<sup>−32</sup> seconds).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_distance_in_a_metric_space">Measuring distance in a metric space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Measuring distance in a metric space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Space_Expansion_(no_center).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5316" data-file-width="2902" decoding="async" height="366" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png/200px-Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png/300px-Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png/400px-Space_Expansion_%28no_center%29.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Space_Expansion_(no_center).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The diagram depicts the expansion of the universe and the relative observer phenomenon. The blue galaxies have expanded further apart than the white galaxies. When choosing an arbitrary reference point such as the gold galaxy or the red galaxy, the increased distance to other galaxies the further away they are appear the same. This phenomenon of expansion indicates two factors: there is no centralized point in the universe, and that the Milky Way Galaxy is not the center of the universe. The appearance of centrality is due to an observer bias that is equivalent no matter what location an observer sits.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_coordinates" title="Comoving coordinates">Comoving coordinates</a></div>
<p>In expanding space, distance is a dynamic quantity which changes with time. There are several different ways of defining distance in cosmology, known as <i>distance measures</i>, but a common method used amongst modern astronomers is <b>comoving distance</b>. 
</p><p>The metric only defines the distance between nearby (so-called "local") points. In order to define the distance between arbitrarily distant points, one must specify both the points and a specific curve (known as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_interval" title="Spacetime interval">spacetime interval</a>") connecting them. The distance between the points can then be found by finding the length of this connecting curve through the three dimensions of space. Comoving distance defines this connecting curve to be a curve of constant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_time" title="Cosmological time">cosmological time</a>. Operationally, comoving distances cannot be directly measured by a single Earth-bound observer. To determine the distance of distant objects, astronomers generally measure luminosity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_candles" title="Standard candles">standard candles</a>, or the redshift factor 'z' of distant galaxies, and then convert these measurements into distances based on some particular model of spacetime, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">Lambda-CDM model</a>. It is, indeed, by making such observations that it was determined that there is no evidence for any 'slowing down' of the expansion in the current epoch.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_evidence">Observational evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expansion_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Observational evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theoretical cosmologists developing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Models_of_the_universe" title="Models of the universe">models of the universe</a> have drawn upon a small number of reasonable assumptions in their work. These workings have led to models in which the metric expansion of space is a likely feature of the universe. Chief among the underlying principles that result in models including metric expansion as a feature are:
</p>
<ul><li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_Principle" title="Cosmological Principle">Cosmological Principle</a> which demands that the universe looks the same way in all directions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a>) and has roughly the same smooth mixture of material (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homogeneous" title="wiktionary:homogeneous">homogeneous</a>).</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernican_Principle" title="Copernican Principle">Copernican Principle</a> which demands that no place in the universe is preferred (that is, the universe has no "starting point").</li></ul>
<p>Scientists have tested carefully whether these assumptions are valid and borne out by observation. <a href="/wiki/Observational_cosmology" title="Observational cosmology">Observational cosmologists</a> have discovered evidence — very strong in some cases — that supports these assumptions, and as a result, metric expansion of space is considered by cosmologists to be an observed feature on the basis that although we cannot see it directly, scientists have tested the properties of the universe and observation provides compelling confirmation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Sources of this confidence and confirmation include:
</p>
<ul><li>Hubble demonstrated that all galaxies and distant astronomical objects were moving away from us, as predicted by a universal expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Using the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectra</a> to determine the distance and speed of remote objects in space, he showed that all objects are moving away from us, and that their speed is proportional to their distance, a feature of metric expansion. Further studies have since shown the expansion to be highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homogeneous" title="wiktionary:homogeneous">homogeneous</a>, that is, it does not seem to have a special point as a "center", but appears universal and independent of any fixed central point.</li>
<li>In studies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos" title="Large-scale structure of the cosmos">large-scale structure of the cosmos</a> taken from <a href="/wiki/Redshift_survey" title="Redshift survey">redshift surveys</a> a so-called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_of_Greatness" title="End of Greatness">End of Greatness</a>" was discovered at the largest scales of the universe. Until these scales were surveyed, the universe appeared "lumpy" with clumps of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_filament" title="Galaxy filament">filaments</a> which were anything but isotropic and homogeneous. This lumpiness disappears into a smooth distribution of galaxies at the largest scales.</li>
<li>The isotropic distribution across the sky of distant <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> is another confirmation of the Cosmological Principle.</li>
<li>The Copernican Principle was not truly tested on a cosmological scale until measurements of the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> radiation on the dynamics of distant astrophysical systems were made. A group of astronomers at the <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">European Southern Observatory</a> noticed, by measuring the temperature of a distant intergalactic cloud in thermal equilibrium with the cosmic microwave background, that the radiation from the Big Bang was demonstrably warmer at earlier times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Uniform cooling of the cosmic microwave background over billions of years is strong and direct observational evidence for metric expansion.</li></ul>
<p>Taken together, these phenomena overwhelmingly support models that rely on space expanding through a change in metric. It was not until the discovery in the year 2000 of direct observational evidence for the changing temperature of the cosmic microwave background that more bizarre constructions could be ruled out. Until that time, it was based purely on an assumption that the universe did not behave as one with the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> sitting at the middle of a fixed-metric with a universal explosion of galaxies in all directions (as seen in, for example, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milne_Model" title="Milne Model">early model proposed by Milne</a>). Yet before this evidence, many rejected the Milne viewpoint based on the <a href="/wiki/Mediocrity_principle" title="Mediocrity principle">mediocrity principle</a>.
</p>
