<p>In physics, a <b>shock wave</b> (also spelled <b>shockwave</b>), or <b>shock</b>, is a type of propagating disturbance that moves faster than the local <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in the medium.  Like an ordinary wave, a shock wave carries energy and can propagate through a medium but is characterized by an abrupt, nearly discontinuous, change in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of the medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the purpose of comparison, in supersonic flows, additional increased expansion may be achieved through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expansion_fan" title="Expansion fan">expansion fan</a>, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Prandtl%E2%80%93Meyer_expansion_fan" title="Prandtl–Meyer expansion fan">Prandtl–Meyer expansion fan</a>. The accompanying expansion wave may approach and eventually collide and recombine with the shock wave, creating a process of destructive interference. The <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a> associated with the passage of a supersonic aircraft is a type of sound wave produced by constructive interference.
</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Soliton" title="Soliton">solitons</a> (another kind of nonlinear wave), the energy and speed of a shock wave alone dissipates relatively quickly with distance. 
When a shock wave passes through matter, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> is preserved but <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> increases. This change in the matter's properties manifests itself as a decrease in the energy which can be extracted as work, and as a <a href="/wiki/Wave_drag" title="Wave drag">drag force on supersonic objects</a>; shock waves are strongly <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">irreversible processes</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shock waves can be:
</p>
<dl><dt>Normal</dt>
<dd>At 90° (perpendicular) to the shock medium's flow direction.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Oblique_shock" title="Oblique shock">Oblique</a></dt>
<dd>At an angle to the direction of flow.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Bow_shock_(aerodynamics)" title="Bow shock (aerodynamics)">Bow</a></dt>
<dd>Occurs upstream of the front (<a href="/wiki/Bow_(ship)" title="Bow (ship)">bow</a>) of a blunt object when the upstream flow velocity exceeds Mach 1.</dd></dl>
<p>Some other terms
</p>
<ul><li>Shock front: The boundary over which the physical conditions undergo an abrupt change because of a shock wave.</li>
<li>Contact front: In a shock wave caused by a driver gas (for example the "impact" of a high explosive on the surrounding air), the boundary between the driver (explosive products) and the driven (air) gases. The Contact Front trails the Shock Front.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_supersonic_flows">In supersonic flows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In supersonic flows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_plot.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="817" data-file-width="1169" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pressure_plot.png/200px-Pressure_plot.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pressure_plot.png/300px-Pressure_plot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Pressure_plot.png/400px-Pressure_plot.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_plot.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pressure-time diagram at an external observation point for the case of a supersonic object propagating past the observer. The leading edge of the object causes a shock (left, in red) and the trailing edge of the object causes an expansion (right, in blue).</div></div></div>
<p>The abruptness of change in the features of the medium, that characterize shock waves, can be viewed as a <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>: the pressure-time diagram of a supersonic object propagating shows how the transition induced by a shock wave is analogous to a <i>dynamic phase transition</i>.
</p><p>When an object (or disturbance) moves faster than the information can propagate into the surrounding fluid, then the fluid near the disturbance cannot react or "get out of the way" before the disturbance arrives. In a shock wave the properties of the fluid (<a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a>)  change almost instantaneously. Measurements of the thickness of shock waves in air have resulted in values around 200 nm (about 10<sup>−5</sup> in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which is on the same order of magnitude as the mean free gas molecule path.  In reference to the continuum, this implies the shock wave can be treated as either a line or a plane if the flow field is two-dimensional or three-dimensional, respectively.
</p><p>Shock waves are formed when a pressure front moves at supersonic speeds and pushes on the surrounding air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> At the region where this occurs, sound waves travelling against the flow reach a point where they cannot travel any further upstream and the pressure progressively builds in that region; a high pressure shock wave rapidly forms.
</p><p>Shock waves are not conventional sound waves; a shock wave takes the form of a very sharp change in the gas properties.  Shock waves in air are heard as a loud "crack" or "snap" noise. Over longer distances, a shock wave can change from a nonlinear wave into a linear wave, degenerating into a conventional sound wave as it heats the air and loses energy. The sound wave is heard as the familiar "thud" or "thump" of a <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a>, commonly created by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> flight of aircraft.
</p><p>The shock wave is one of several different ways in which a gas in a supersonic flow can be compressed. Some other methods are <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process#Isentropic_flow" title="Isentropic process">isentropic</a> compressions, including <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Prandtl" title="Ludwig Prandtl">Prandtl</a>–Meyer compressions. The method of compression of a gas results in different temperatures and densities for a given pressure ratio which can be analytically calculated for a non-reacting gas. A shock wave compression results in a loss of total pressure, meaning that it is a less efficient method of compressing gases for some purposes, for instance in the intake of a <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a>. The appearance of pressure-drag on supersonic aircraft is mostly due to the effect of shock compression on the flow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Normal_shocks">Normal shocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Normal shocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In elementary fluid mechanics utilizing ideal gases, a shock wave is treated as a discontinuity where entropy increases over a nearly infinitesimal region.  Since no fluid flow is discontinuous, a <a href="/wiki/Control_volume" title="Control volume">control volume</a> is established around the shock wave, with the control surfaces that bound this volume parallel to the shock wave (with one surface on the pre-shock side of the fluid medium and one on the post-shock side). The two surfaces are separated by a very small depth such that the shock itself is entirely contained between them. At such control surfaces, momentum, mass flux and energy are constant; within combustion, <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonations</a> can be modelled as heat introduction across a shock wave.  It is assumed the system is adiabatic (no heat exits or enters the system) and no work is being done.  The <a href="/wiki/Rankine%E2%80%93Hugoniot_conditions" title="Rankine–Hugoniot conditions">Rankine–Hugoniot conditions</a> arise from these considerations.
</p><p>Taking into account the established assumptions, in a system where the downstream properties are becoming subsonic: the upstream and downstream flow properties of the fluid are considered <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropic</a>. Since the total amount of energy within the system is constant, the stagnation enthalpy remains constant over both regions. Though, entropy is increasing; this must be accounted for by a drop in stagnation pressure of the downstream fluid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_shocks">Other shocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other shocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oblique_shocks">Oblique shocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oblique shocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When analyzing shock waves in a flow field, which are still attached to the body, the shock wave which is deviating at some arbitrary angle from the flow direction is termed <a href="/wiki/Oblique_shock" title="Oblique shock">oblique shock</a>. These shocks require a component vector analysis of the flow; doing so allows for the treatment of the flow in an orthogonal direction to the oblique shock as a normal shock.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bow_shocks">Bow shocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bow shocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When an oblique shock is likely to form at an angle which cannot remain on the surface, a nonlinear phenomenon arises where the shock wave will form a continuous pattern around the body. These are termed <a href="/wiki/Bow_shock_(aerodynamics)" title="Bow shock (aerodynamics)">bow shocks</a>. In these cases, the 1d flow model is not valid and further analysis is needed to predict the pressure forces which are exerted on the surface.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shock_waves_due_to_nonlinear_steepening">Shock waves due to nonlinear steepening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shock waves due to nonlinear steepening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shock waves can form due to steepening of ordinary waves. The best-known example of this phenomenon is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_wave" title="Ocean wave">ocean waves</a> that form <a href="/wiki/Breaking_wave" title="Breaking wave">breakers</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Shore" title="Shore">shore</a>. In shallow water, the speed of surface waves is dependent on the depth of the water. An incoming ocean wave has a slightly higher wave speed near the crest of each wave than near the troughs between waves, because the wave height is not infinitesimal compared to the depth of the water. The crests overtake the troughs until the leading edge of the wave forms a vertical face and spills over to form a turbulent shock (a breaker) that dissipates the wave's energy as sound and heat.
</p><p>Similar phenomena affect strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound waves</a> in gas or plasma, due to the dependence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_speed" title="Sound speed">sound speed</a> on <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. Strong waves heat the medium near each pressure front, due to adiabatic compression of the air itself, so that high pressure fronts outrun the corresponding pressure troughs.  There is a theory that the sound pressure levels in brass instruments such as the trombone become high enough for steepening to occur, forming an essential part of the bright timbre of the instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  While shock formation by this process does not normally happen to unenclosed sound waves in Earth's atmosphere, it is thought to be one mechanism by which the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> are heated, via waves that propagate up from the solar interior.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogies">Analogies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Analogies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A shock wave may be described as the furthest point upstream of a moving object which "knows" about the approach of the object. In this description, the shock wave position is defined as the boundary between the zone having no information about the shock-driving event and the zone aware of the shock-driving event, analogous with the <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">light cone</a> described in the theory of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>.
</p><p>To produce a shock wave, an object in a given medium (such as air or water) must travel faster than the local speed of sound. In the case of an aircraft travelling at high subsonic speed, regions of air around the aircraft may be travelling at exactly the speed of sound, so that the sound waves leaving the aircraft pile up on one another, similar to a traffic jam on a motorway. When a shock wave forms, the local air pressure increases and then spreads out sideways. Because of this amplification effect, a shock wave can be very intense, more like an explosion when heard at a distance (not coincidentally, since explosions create shock waves).
</p><p>Analogous phenomena are known outside fluid mechanics. For example, particles accelerated beyond the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refractive medium</a> (where the speed of light is less than that in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>) create visible shock effects, a phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation" title="Cherenkov radiation">Cherenkov radiation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomena_types">Phenomena types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phenomena types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Below are a number of examples of shock waves, broadly grouped with similar shock phenomena:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1361" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="468" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg/220px-Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg/330px-Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg/440px-Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trinity_explosion_film_strip.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shock wave propagating into a stationary medium, ahead of the fireball of an explosion. The shock is made visible by the <a href="/wiki/Shadowgraph" title="Shadowgraph">shadow effect</a> (Trinity explosion)</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moving_shock">Moving shock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Moving shock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Usually consists of a shock wave propagating into a stationary medium</li>
<li>In this case, the gas ahead of the shock is stationary (in the laboratory frame) and the gas behind the shock can be supersonic in the laboratory frame. The shock propagates with a wavefront which is normal (at right angles) to the direction of flow. The speed of the shock is a function of the original pressure ratio between the two bodies of gas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moving_shock" title="Moving shock">Moving shocks</a> are usually generated by the interaction of two bodies of gas at different pressure, with a shock wave propagating into the lower pressure gas and an expansion wave propagating into the higher pressure gas.</li>
<li>Examples: Balloon bursting, <a href="/wiki/Shock_tube" title="Shock tube">Shock tube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blast_wave" title="Blast wave">shock wave from explosion</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detonation_wave">Detonation wave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Detonation wave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">Detonation</a></div>
<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonation</a> wave is essentially a shock supported by a trailing <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a>. It involves a wave travelling through a highly combustible or chemically unstable medium, such as an oxygen-methane mixture or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high explosive</a>. The chemical reaction of the medium occurs following the shock wave, and the chemical energy of the reaction drives the wave forward.</li>
<li>A detonation wave follows slightly different rules from an ordinary shock since it is driven by the chemical reaction occurring behind the shock wavefront. In the simplest theory for detonations, an unsupported, self-propagating detonation wave proceeds at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapman-Jouguet_condition" title="Chapman-Jouguet condition">Chapman-Jouguet</a> flow velocity. A detonation will also cause a shock of type 1, above to propagate into the surrounding air due to the overpressure induced by the explosion.</li>
<li>When a shock wave is created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high explosives</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> (which has a <a href="/wiki/Detonation_velocity" title="Detonation velocity">detonation velocity</a> of 6,900 m/s), it will always travel at high, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a>  velocity from its point of origin.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet,_1888.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1884" data-file-width="1884" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg/220px-Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg/330px-Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg/440px-Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet%2C_1888.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photography_of_bow_shock_waves_around_a_brass_bullet,_1888.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Schlieren_photography" title="Schlieren photography">Schlieren photograph</a> of the detached shock on a bullet in supersonic flight, published by Ernst Mach and Peter Salcher in 1887.</div></div></div>

<h3><span id="Bow_shock_.28detached_shock.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bow_shock_(detached_shock)">Bow shock (detached shock)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bow shock (detached shock)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bow_shock_(aerodynamics)" title="Bow shock (aerodynamics)">Bow shock (aerodynamics)</a></div>
<ul><li>These shocks are curved and form a small distance in front of the body. Directly in front of the body, they stand at 90 degrees to the oncoming flow and then curve around the body. Detached shocks allow the same type of analytic calculations as for the attached shock, for the flow near the shock. They are a topic of continuing interest, because the rules governing the shock's distance ahead of the blunt body are complicated and are a function of the body's shape. Additionally, the shock standoff distance varies drastically with the temperature for a non-ideal gas, causing large differences in the heat transfer to the thermal protection system of the vehicle. See the extended discussion on this topic at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">Atmospheric reentry</a>. These follow the "strong-shock" solutions of the analytic equations, meaning that for some oblique shocks very close to the deflection angle limit, the downstream Mach number is subsonic. See also <a href="/wiki/Bow_shock_(aerodynamics)" title="Bow shock (aerodynamics)">bow shock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oblique_shock" title="Oblique shock">oblique shock</a></li>
<li>Such a shock occurs when the maximum deflection angle is exceeded. A detached shock is commonly seen on blunt bodies, but may also be seen on sharp bodies at low Mach numbers.</li>
<li>Examples: Space return vehicles (Apollo, Space shuttle), bullets, the boundary (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">Bow shock</a>) of a <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. The name "bow shock" comes from the example of a <a href="/wiki/Bow_wave" title="Bow wave">bow wave</a>, the detached shock formed at the bow (front) of a ship or boat moving through water, whose slow surface wave speed is easily exceeded (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface wave</a>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attached_shock">Attached shock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Attached shock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>These shocks appear as <i>attached</i> to the tip of sharp bodies moving at supersonic speeds.</li>
<li>Examples: Supersonic wedges and cones with small apex angles.</li>
<li>The attached shock wave is a classic structure in aerodynamics because, for a perfect gas and inviscid flow field, an analytic solution is available, such that the pressure ratio, temperature ratio, angle of the wedge and the downstream Mach number can all be calculated knowing the upstream Mach number and the shock angle. Smaller shock angles are associated with higher upstream Mach numbers, and the special case where the shock wave is at 90° to the oncoming flow (Normal shock), is associated with a Mach number of one. These follow the "weak-shock" solutions of the analytic equations.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_rapid_granular_flows">In rapid granular flows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In rapid granular flows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shock waves can also occur in rapid flows of dense granular materials down inclined channels or slopes. Strong shocks in rapid dense granular flows can be studied theoretically and analyzed to compare with experimental data. Consider a configuration in which the rapidly moving material down the chute impinges on an obstruction wall erected perpendicular at the end of a long and steep channel. Impact leads to a sudden change in the flow regime from a fast moving <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_flow" title="Supercritical flow">supercritical</a> thin layer to a stagnant thick heap. This flow configuration is particularly interesting because it is analogous to some hydraulic and aerodynamic situations associated with flow regime changes from supercritical to subcritical flows.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_astrophysics">In astrophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In astrophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shock_waves_in_astrophysics" title="Shock waves in astrophysics">Shock waves in astrophysics</a></div>
<p>Astrophysical environments feature many different types of shock waves. Some common examples are <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> shock waves or <a href="/wiki/Blast_wave" title="Blast wave">blast waves</a> travelling through the interstellar medium, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a> caused by the Earth's magnetic field colliding with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and shock waves caused by <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> colliding with each other. Another interesting type of shock in astrophysics is the quasi-steady reverse shock or termination shock that terminates the ultra relativistic wind from young <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteor_entering_events">Meteor entering events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Meteor entering events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg/220px-Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg/330px-Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg/440px-Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chelyabinsk_meteor_event_consequences_in_Drama_Theatre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Damage caused by <a href="/wiki/Chelyabinsk_meteor" title="Chelyabinsk meteor">a meteor shock wave</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tunguska_event" title="Tunguska event">Tunguska event</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Russian_meteor_event" title="2013 Russian meteor event">2013 Russian meteor event</a> are the best documented evidence of the shock wave produced by a <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid#Notable_meteors" title="Meteoroid">massive meteoroid</a>.
</p><p>When the 2013 meteor entered into the Earth’s atmosphere with an energy release equivalent to 100 or more kilotons of TNT, dozens of times more powerful than the <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">atomic bomb dropped on Hiroshima</a>, the meteor's shock wave produced damages as in a <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_speed" title="Supersonic speed">supersonic</a> jet's flyby (directly underneath the meteor's path) and as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detonation_wave" title="Detonation wave">detonation wave</a>, with the circular shock wave centred at the meteor explosion, causing multiple instances of broken glass in the city of <a href="/wiki/Chelyabinsk" title="Chelyabinsk">Chelyabinsk</a> and neighbouring areas (pictured).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_applications">Technological applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Technological applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the examples below, the shock wave is controlled, produced by (ex. airfoil) or in the interior of a technological device, like a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recompression_shock">Recompression shock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Recompression shock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transonic_flow_patterns.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="234" data-file-width="286" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Transonic_flow_patterns.svg/220px-Transonic_flow_patterns.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Transonic_flow_patterns.svg/330px-Transonic_flow_patterns.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Transonic_flow_patterns.svg/440px-Transonic_flow_patterns.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Transonic_flow_patterns.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Recompression shock on a transonic flow airfoil, at and above <a href="/wiki/Critical_Mach_number" title="Critical Mach number">critical Mach number</a>.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>These shocks appear when the flow over a transonic body is decelerated to subsonic speeds.</li>
<li>Examples: Transonic wings, turbines</li>
<li>Where the flow over the suction side of a transonic wing is accelerated to a supersonic speed, the resulting re-compression can be by either Prandtl–Meyer compression or by the formation of a normal shock. This shock is of particular interest to makers of transonic devices because it can cause separation of the boundary layer at the point where it touches the transonic profile. This can then lead to full separation and stall on the profile, higher drag, or shock-buffet, a condition where the separation and the shock interact in a resonance condition, causing resonating loads on the underlying structure.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pipe_flow">Pipe flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Pipe flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>This shock appears when supersonic flow in a pipe is decelerated.</li>
<li>Examples:
<ul><li>In supersonic propulsion: <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unstart" title="Unstart">unstart</a>.</li>
<li>In flow control: needle valve, choked venturi.</li></ul></li>
<li>In this case the gas ahead of the shock is supersonic (in the laboratory frame), and the gas behind the shock system is either supersonic (<i>oblique shock</i>s) or subsonic (a <i>normal shock</i>) (Although for some oblique shocks very close to the deflection angle limit, the downstream Mach number is subsonic.) The shock is the result of the deceleration of the gas by a converging duct, or by the growth of the boundary layer on the wall of a parallel duct.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion_engines">Combustion engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Combustion engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wave_disk_engine" title="Wave disk engine">wave disk engine</a> (also named "Radial Internal Combustion Wave Rotor") is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Pistonless_rotary_engine" title="Pistonless rotary engine">pistonless rotary engine</a> that utilizes <i>shock waves</i> to transfer energy between a high-energy fluid to a low-energy fluid, thereby increasing both temperature and pressure of the low-energy fluid.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memristors">Memristors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Memristors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Memristor" title="Memristor">memristors</a>, under externally-applied electric field, shock waves can be launched across the transition-metal oxides, creating fast and non-volatile resistivity changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shock_capturing_and_detection">Shock capturing and detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shock_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Shock capturing and detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="structure2016-01-29"></span>Advanced techniques are needed to capture shock waves and to detect shock waves in both numerical computations and experimental observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">Computational fluid dynamics</a> is commonly used to obtain the flow field with shock waves. Though shock waves are sharp discontinuities, in numerical solutions of fluid flow with discontinuities (shock wave, contact discontinuity or slip line), the shock wave can be smoothed out by low-order numerical method (due to numerical dissipation) or there are spurious oscillations near shock surface by high-order numerical method (due to Gibbs phenomena).
</p><p>There exist some other discontinuities in fluid flow than the shock wave. The slip surface (3D) or slip line (2D) is a plane across which the tangent velocity is discontinuous, while pressure and normal velocity are continuous. Across the contact discontinuity, the pressure and velocity are continuous and the density is discontinuous. A strong  expansion wave or shear layer may also contain high gradient regions which appear to be a discontinuity. Some common features of these flow structures and shock waves  and the insufficient aspects of numerical and experimental tools lead to two important problems in practices:
(1) some shock waves can not be detected or their positions are detected wrong, (2) some flow structures which are not shock waves are wrongly detected to be shock waves.
</p><p>In fact, correct capturing and detection of shock waves are important since shock waves have the following influences:  
(1) causing loss of total pressure, which may be a concern related to scramjet engine performance, 
(2)providing lift for wave-rider configuration, as the oblique shock wave at lower surface of the vehicle can produce high pressure to generate lift, 
(3)leading to wave drag of high-speed vehicle which is harmful to vehicle performance,
(4)inducing severe pressure load and heat flux, e.g. the Type IV shock–shock interference could yield a 17 times heating increase at vehicle surface, (5)interacting with other structures, such as boundary layers, to produce new flow structures such as flow separation, transition, etc.
</p>
