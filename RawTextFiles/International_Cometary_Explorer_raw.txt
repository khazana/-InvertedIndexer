<p>The <b>International Cometary Explorer</b> (<b>ICE</b>) <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> (designed and launched as the <b>International Sun-Earth Explorer-3</b> (<b>ISEE-3</b>) satellite), was launched August 12, 1978, into a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>. It was one of three spacecraft, along with the mother/daughter pair of <a href="/wiki/ISEE-1" title="ISEE-1">ISEE-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISEE-2" title="ISEE-2">ISEE-2</a>, built for the International Sun-Earth Explorer (ISEE) program, a joint effort by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESRO" title="ESRO">ESRO</a>/<a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">ESA</a> to study the interaction between the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.
</p><p>ISEE-3 was the first spacecraft to be placed in a <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">halo orbit</a> at the L<sub>1</sub> Earth-Sun <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_sse_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_sse-2">[2]</a></sup> Renamed ICE, it became the first spacecraft to visit a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>, passing through the plasma tail of comet <a href="/wiki/21P/Giacobini%E2%80%93Zinner" title="21P/Giacobini–Zinner">Giacobini-Zinner</a> within about 7,800 km (4,800 mi) of the nucleus on September 11, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>NASA suspended routine contact with ISEE-3 in 1997, and made brief status checks in 1999 and 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge20140523_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-verge20140523-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-io9-20140415_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-io9-20140415-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 29, 2014, two-way communication with the spacecraft was reestablished by the ISEE-3 Reboot Project, an unofficial group<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> with support from the Skycorp company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars20140529_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars20140529-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140615_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140615-9">[9]</a></sup>  On July 2, 2014, they fired the thrusters for the first time since 1987. However, later firings of the thrusters failed, apparently due to a lack of nitrogen pressurant in the fuel tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISEE3-20140710_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISEE3-20140710-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140718_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140718-11">[11]</a></sup> The project team initiated an alternative plan to use the spacecraft to "collect scientific data and send it back to Earth,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140709_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140709-12">[12]</a></sup> but on September 16, 2014, contact with the probe was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lost-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>ISEE-3 carries no cameras; instead, its instruments measure energetic particles, waves, <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasmas</a>, and fields.
</p><p>ISEE-3 originally operated in a <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">halo orbit</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> Sun-Earth <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a>, 235 Earth radii above the surface (about 1.5 million km, or 924,000 miles).  It was the first artificial object placed at a so-called "libration point", entering orbit there on November 20, 1978,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_sse_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_sse-2">[2]</a></sup> proving that such a suspension between gravitational fields was possible. It rotates at 19.76 rpm about an axis perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, to keep it oriented for its experiments, to generate solar power and to communicate with Earth.
</p><p>The purposes of the mission were:
</p>
<ul><li>to investigate solar-terrestrial relationships at the outermost boundaries of the Earth's magnetosphere;</li>
<li>to examine in detail the structure of the solar wind near the Earth and the shock wave that forms the interface between the solar wind and Earth's magnetosphere;</li>
<li>to investigate motions of and mechanisms operating in the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_sheet" title="Plasma sheet">plasma sheets</a>; and,</li>
<li>to continue the investigation of cosmic rays and solar flare emissions in the interplanetary region near 1 AU.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_mission:_International_Cometary_Explorer">Second mission: International Cometary Explorer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Second mission: International Cometary Explorer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After completing its original mission, ISEE-3 was re-tasked to study the interaction between the solar wind and a cometary atmosphere. On June 10, 1982, the spacecraft performed a maneuver which removed it from its halo orbit around the L<sub>1</sub> point and placed it in a <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">transfer orbit</a>. This involved a series of passages between Earth and the Sun-Earth <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>2</sub></a> Lagrangian point, through the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetotail" title="Magnetotail">magnetotail</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc-isee3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc-isee3-14">[14]</a></sup> Fifteen propulsive maneuvers and five lunar gravity assists resulted in the spacecraft being ejected from the Earth-Moon system and into a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>. Its last and closest pass over the Moon, on December 22, 1983, was only 119.4 km (74 mi) above the lunar surface; following this pass, the spacecraft was re-designated as the International Cometary Explorer (ICE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eoportal-isee3_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-eoportal-isee3-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Giacobini-Zinner_encounter">Giacobini-Zinner encounter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Giacobini-Zinner encounter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its new orbit put it ahead of the Earth on a trajectory to intercept comet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/21P/Giacobini-Zinner" title="21P/Giacobini-Zinner">Giacobini-Zinner</a>. On September 11, 1985, the craft passed through the comet's plasma tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eoportal-isee3_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-eoportal-isee3-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>ICE did a <a href="/wiki/Flyby_(spaceflight)" title="Flyby (spaceflight)">flyby</a> of the comet nucleus at a distance of 7,800 km (4,800 mi) of the nucleus on September 11, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halley_encounter">Halley encounter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Halley encounter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ICE transited between the Sun and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Halley" title="Comet Halley">Comet Halley</a> in late March 1986, when other spacecraft  were near the comet on their early-March comet rendezvous missions. (This "<a href="/wiki/Halley_Armada" title="Halley Armada">Halley Armada</a>" included <a href="/wiki/Giotto_(spacecraft)" title="Giotto (spacecraft)">Giotto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">Vega 1 and 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suisei_(spacecraft)" title="Suisei (spacecraft)">Suisei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sakigake" title="Sakigake">Sakigake</a>.) ICE flew through the tail; its minimum distance to the comet nucleus was 28 million kilometres (17,000,000 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For comparison, Earth's minimum distance to Comet Halley in 1910 was 20.8 million kilometres (12,900,000 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliospheric_mission">Heliospheric mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heliospheric mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An update to the ICE mission was approved by NASA in 1991. It defines a <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">heliospheric</a> mission for ICE consisting of investigations of <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejections</a> in coordination with ground-based observations, continued <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> studies, and the <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">Ulysses</a> probe. By May 1995, ICE was being operated under a low duty cycle, with some data-analysis support from the Ulysses project.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_mission">End of mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: End of mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 5, 1997, NASA ended the ICE mission, leaving only a carrier signal operating. The ISEE-3/ICE downlink bit rate was nominally 2048 bits per second during the early part of the mission, and 1024 bit/s during the Giacobini-Zinner comet encounter. The bit rate then successively dropped to 512 bit/s (on December 9, 1985), 256 bit/s (on January 5, 1987), 128 bit/s (on January 24, 1989) and finally to 64 bit/s (on December 27, 1991). Though still in space, NASA donated the craft to the Smithsonian Museum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Privateers_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Privateers-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>By January 1990, ICE was in a 355-day <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a> with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a> of 1.03 AU, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> of 0.93 AU and an inclination of 0.1 degree.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_contact">Further contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Further contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, NASA made brief contact with ICE to verify its carrier signal.
</p><p>On September 18, 2008, NASA, with the help of <a href="/wiki/KinetX" title="KinetX">KinetX</a>, located ICE using the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">NASA Deep Space Network</a> after discovering that it had not been powered off after the 1999 contact.  A status check revealed that all but one of its 13 experiments were still functioning, and it still had enough propellant for 150 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a> (490 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feet_per_second" title="Feet per second">ft/s</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">Δv</a>.
</p><p>It was determined to be possible to reactivate the spacecraft in 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">[20]</a></sup> when it again made a close approach to Earth, and scientists discussed reusing the probe to observe more comets in 2017 or 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reboot_effort">Reboot effort</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reboot effort">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sometime after NASA's interest in the ICE waned, others realized that the spacecraft might be steered to pass close to another comet. A team of engineers, programmers, and scientists began to study the feasibility and challenges involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140615_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140615-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2014, its members formally announced their intentions to "recapture" the spacecraft for use, calling the effort the ISEE-3 Reboot Project. A team webpage said, "We intend to contact the ISEE-3 (International Sun-Earth Explorer) spacecraft, command it to fire its engine and enter an orbit near Earth, and then resume its original mission... If we are successful we intend to facilitate the sharing and interpretation of all of the new data ISEE-3 sends back via crowd sourcing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 15, the project reached its <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a> goal of US$125,000 on <a href="/wiki/RocketHub" title="RocketHub">RocketHub</a>, which was expected to cover the costs of writing the software to communicate with the probe, searching through the NASA archives for the information needed to control the spacecraft, and buying time on the dish antennas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The project then set a "stretch goal" of $150,000, which it also met with a final total of $159,502 raised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The project members were working on deadline: if they got the spacecraft to change its orbit by late May or early June 2014, or in early July by using more fuel, it could use the Moon's gravity to get back into a useful <a href="/wiki/Halo_orbit" title="Halo orbit">halo orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-here-now_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-here-now-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacing_lost_hardware">Replacing lost hardware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Replacing lost hardware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Earlier in 2014, officials with the Goddard Space Flight Center said the Deep Space Network equipment necessary to transmit signals to the spacecraft had been decommissioned in 1999, and was too expensive to replace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, project members were able to find documentation for the original equipment and were able to simulate the complex modulator/demodulator electronics using modern <a href="/wiki/Software-defined_radio" title="Software-defined radio">software-defined radio</a> (SDR) techniques and open-source programs from the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Radio" title="GNU Radio">GNU Radio</a> project. They obtained the needed hardware, an off-the-shelf SDR transceiver<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and power amplifier,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_2014-05-15_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_2014-05-15-30">[30]</a></sup> and installed it on the 305-meter <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo dish antenna</a> on May 19, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_2014-05-15_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_2014-05-15-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arecibo_antenna_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arecibo_antenna-31">[31]</a></sup> Once they gained control of the spacecraft, the capture team planned to shift the primary ground station to the 21-meter dish located at Kentucky's <a href="/wiki/Morehead_State_University" title="Morehead State University">Morehead State University</a> Space Science Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_2014-05-15_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_2014-05-15-30">[30]</a></sup> The 20-meter dish antenna in <a href="/wiki/Bochum_Observatory" title="Bochum Observatory">Bochum Observatory</a>, Germany, would be a support station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardware_2014-05-15_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hardware_2014-05-15-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although NASA was not funding the project, it made advisors available and gave approval to try to establish contact. On May 21, 2014, NASA announced that it had signed a Non-Reimbursable <a href="/wiki/Space_Act_Agreement" title="Space Act Agreement">Space Act Agreement</a> with the ISEE-3 Reboot Project. "This is the first time NASA has worked such an agreement for use of a spacecraft the agency is no longer using or ever planned to use again," officials said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAagreement_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAagreement-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_reestablished">Contact reestablished</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Contact reestablished">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 29, 2014, the reboot team successfully commanded the probe to switch into Engineering Mode to begin to broadcast telemetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr29may14_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr29may14-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 26, project members using the <a href="/wiki/Goldstone_Deep_Space_Communications_Complex" title="Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex">Goldstone Deep Space Communications Complex</a> DSS-24 antenna achieved synchronous communication and obtained the four ranging points needed to refine the spacecraft's orbital parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
The project team received approval from NASA to continue operations through at least July 16, and made plans to attempt the orbital maneuver in early July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-here-now_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-here-now-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 2, the reboot project fired the thrusters for the first time since 1987.  They spun up the spacecraft to its nominal roll rate, in preparation for the upcoming trajectory correction maneuver in mid-July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 8, a longer sequence of thrusters firings failed, apparently due to loss of the nitrogen gas needed to pressurize the fuel tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISEE3-20140710_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ISEE3-20140710-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140718_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140718-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140709_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140709-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 24, the ISEE-3 Reboot Team announced that all attempts to change orbit using the ISEE-3 propulsion system had failed. Instead, the team said, the <i>ISEE-3 Interplanetary Citizen Science Mission</i> would gather data as the spacecraft flies by the Moon on August 10 and enters a heliocentric orbit similar to Earth's. The team began shutting down propulsion components to maximize the electrical power available for the science experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 30, the team announced that it still planned to acquire data from as much of ISEE-3's 300-day orbit as possible. With five of the 13 instruments on the spacecraft still working, the science possibilities included listening for <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a>, where observations from additional locations in the Solar System can be valuable. The team was also recruiting additional receiving sites around the globe to improve diurnal coverage, in order to upload additional commands while the spacecraft is close to Earth and later to receive data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 10 at 18:16 UTC, the spacecraft passed about 15,600 km (9,700 mi) from the surface of the Moon. It will continue in its heliocentric orbit, and will return to the vicinity of Earth in 2031.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140808_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140808-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_lost">Contact lost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Contact lost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 25, 2014, the Reboot team announced that contact with the probe was lost on September 16. It is unknown whether contact can be reestablished because the probe's exact orbit is uncertain. The spacecraft's post-lunar flyby orbit takes it further from the Sun, causing electrical power available from its solar arrays to drop, and its battery failed in 1981. Reduced power could have caused the craft to enter a <a href="/wiki/Safe_mode_(spacecraft)" title="Safe mode (spacecraft)">safe mode</a>, from which it may be impossible to awaken without the precise orbital location information needed to point transmissions at the craft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lost_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-lost-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_design">Spacecraft design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spacecraft design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ISEE-C_(ISEE_3)_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2817" data-file-width="2218" decoding="async" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg/170px-ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg/255px-ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg/340px-ISEE-C_%28ISEE_3%29_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ISEE-C_(ISEE_3)_in_dynamic_test_chamber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ISEE-3 (later ICE), undergoing testing and evaluation.</div></div></div>
<p>The ICE spacecraft is a barrel-like cylindrical shape covered by solar panels. Four long antennas protrude equidistant around the circumference of the spacecraft, spanning 91 metres (299 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It has a dry mass of 390 kg (860 lb) and can generate nominal power of 173 watts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Payload">Payload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Payload">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ICE carries 13 scientific instruments to measure plasmas, energetic particles, waves, and fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_sse_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_sse-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eoportal-isee3_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-eoportal-isee3-15">[15]</a></sup> As of  July 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, five were known to be functional. It does not carry a camera or imaging system. Its detectors measure high energy particles such as X- and gamma-rays, solar wind, plasma and cosmic particles. A data handling system gathers the scientific and engineering data from all systems in the spacecraft and formats them into a serial stream for transmission. The transmitter output power is five watts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_payload_and_experiments">Scientific payload and experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Cometary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Scientific payload and experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Solar Wind Plasma Experiment, failed after February 26, 1980</li>
<li>Vector Helium <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">Magnetometer</a></li>
<li>Low Energy <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic Ray</a> Experiment, designed to measure solar, interplanetary, and magnetospheric energetic ions</li>
<li>Medium Energy Cosmic Ray Experiment, 1-500 MeV/n, Z = 1-28; Electrons: 2-10 MeV</li>
<li>High Energy Cosmic Ray Experiment, H to Ni, 20-500 MeV/n</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Waves_in_plasmas" title="Waves in plasmas">Plasma Wave</a> Instrument</li>
<li>Low Energy Proton Experiment, also known as the Energetic Particle Anisotropy Spectrometer (EPAS), designed to study low-energy solar proton acceleration and propagation processes in interplanetary space</li>
<li>Cosmic Ray Electrons and Nuclei</li>
<li>X-Rays and Electrons Instrument, to provide continuous coverage of solar-flare X rays and transient cosmic gamma-ray bursts</li>
<li>Radio Mapping Experiment, 30 kHz - 2 MHz, to map the trajectories of type III solar bursts</li>
<li>Plasma Composition Experiment</li>
<li>Heavy <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">Isotope</a> Spectrometer Telescope</li>
<li>Ground Based Solar Studies Experiment</li></ul>
