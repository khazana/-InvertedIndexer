<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Role of chance in scientific discoveries</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>role of chance, or</b> "<b><a href="/wiki/Luck" title="Luck">luck</a></b>", <b>in science</b> comprises all ways in which unexpected discoveries are made.
</p><p>Many domains, especially psychology, are concerned with the way science interacts with chance — particularly "<a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">serendipity</a>" (accidents that, through sagacity, are transformed into opportunity). Psychologist Kevin Dunbar and colleagues estimate that between 30% and 50% of all scientific discoveries are accidental in some sense (see <a href="#Serendipitous_discoveries">examples below</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Psychologist Alan A. Baumeister says a scientist must be "sagacious" (attentive and clever) to benefit from an accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AABaumeister_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AABaumeister-2">[2]</a></sup> Dunbar quotes <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>'s saying that "Chance favors only the prepared mind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The prepared mind, Dunbar suggests, is one trained for observational rigor. Dunbar adds that there is a great deal of writing about the role that serendipity ("happy accidents") plays in the scientific method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research suggests that scientists are taught various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heuristics" title="Heuristics">heuristics</a> and practices that allow their investigations to benefit, and not suffer, from accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oliver,_J.E._1991_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver,_J.E._1991-7">[7]</a></sup> First, careful <a href="/wiki/Scientific_control" title="Scientific control">control conditions</a> allow scientists to properly identify something as "unexpected". Once a finding is recognized as legitimately unexpected and in need of explaining, researchers can attempt to explain it: They work across various disciplines, with various colleagues, trying various analogies in order to understand the first curious finding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparing_to_make_discoveries">Preparing to make discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Role_of_chance_in_scientific_discoveries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparing to make discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png/220px-DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png/330px-DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png/440px-DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DunbarFugelsangLuckInScience.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A model based on the work of Kevin Dunbar and Jonathan Fugelsang. The two say that the first step is to realize a result is unexpected and unexplained.</div></div></div>
<p>Accidental discoveries have been a topic of discussion especially from the 20th century onwards. Kevin Dunbar and Jonathan Fugelsang say that somewhere between 33% and 50% of all scientific discoveries are unexpected. This helps explain why scientists often call their discoveries "lucky", and yet scientists themselves may not be able to detail exactly what role luck played (see also <a href="/wiki/Introspection_illusion" title="Introspection illusion">introspection illusion</a>). Dunbar and Fugelsang believe scientific discoveries are the result of carefully prepared experiments, but also "prepared minds".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The author <a href="/wiki/Nassim_Nicholas_Taleb" title="Nassim Nicholas Taleb">Nassim Nicholas Taleb</a> calls science "anti-fragile". That is, science can actually use — and benefit from — the chaos of the real world. While some methods of investigation are fragile in the face of human error and randomness, the scientific method relies on randomness in many ways. Taleb believes that the more anti-fragile the system, the more it will flourish in the real world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taleb_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taleb-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taleb_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taleb-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalebBook_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalebBook-9">[9]</a></sup> According to  M. K. Stoskopf, it is in this way that serendipity is often the "foundation for important intellectual leaps of understanding" in science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stoskopf_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stoskopf-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word "<a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">Serendipity</a>" is frequently understood as simply "a happy accident", but <a href="/wiki/Horace_Walpole" title="Horace Walpole">Horace Walpole</a> used the word 'serendipity' to refer to a certain kind of happy accident: the kind that can only be exploited by a "sagacious" or clever person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stoskopf_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stoskopf-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MertonBarber_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MertonBarber-11">[11]</a></sup> Thus Dunbar and Fugelsang talk about, not just luck or chance in science, but specifically "serendipity" in science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dunbar and Fugelsang suggest that the process of discovery often starts when a researcher finds bugs in their experiment. These unexpected results lead a researcher to self-doubt, and to try and fix what they <i>think</i> is an error in their own  methodology. The first recourse is to explain the error using local hypotheses (e.g. analogies typical of the discipline). This process is also local in the sense that the scientist is relatively independent or else working with one partner. Eventually, the researcher decides that the error is too persistent and systematic to be a coincidence. Self-doubt is complete, and so the methods shift to become more broad: The researcher begin to think of theoretical explanations for the error, sometimes seeking the help of colleagues across different domains of expertise. The highly controlled, cautious, curious and even social aspects of the scientific method are what make it well suited for identifying persistent systematic errors (anomalies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oliver,_J.E._1991_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver,_J.E._1991-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Hofmann" title="Albert Hofmann">Albert Hofmann</a>, the Swiss chemist who discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">psychedelic</a> properties when he tried ingesting it at his lab, wrote
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is true that my discovery of LSD was a chance discovery, but it was the outcome of planned experiments and these experiments took place in the framework of systematic pharmaceutical, chemical research. It could better be described as serendipity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p> Dunbar and colleagues cite the discoveries of Hofmann and others as having involved serendipity. In contrast, the mind can be "prepared" in ways that obstruct serendipity — making new knowledge difficult or impossible to take in. Psychologist Alan A. Baumeister describes at least one such instance: Researcher Robert Heath failed to recognize evidence of "pleasure brain circuits" (in the <a href="/wiki/Septal_nuclei" title="Septal nuclei">septal nuclei</a>). When Heath stimulated the brains of his schizophrenic patients, some of them reported feeling pleasure — a finding that Heath could have explored. Heath, however, was "prepared" (based on prior beliefs) for patients to report alertness, and when other patients did, it was on the reports of alertness that Heath focused his investigations. Heath failed to realize he had seen something unexpected and unexplained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AABaumeister_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AABaumeister-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_brain">The brain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Role_of_chance_in_scientific_discoveries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The brain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fugelsang and Dunbar observe scientists while they work together in labs or analyze data, but they also use experimental settings and even <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_MRI" title="Functional MRI">fMRI</a> investigation found that unexpected findings were associated with particular brain activity. Unexpected findings were found to activate the <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a> as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_hemisphere" title="Left hemisphere">left hemisphere</a> in general. This suggests that unexpected findings provoke more attention, and the brain applies more linguistic, conscious systems to help explain those findings. This supports the idea that scientists are using particular abilities that exist to some extent in all humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:40%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">Absent sagacity, a chance observation of an important phenomenon will have no impact, and the observer may be denied historical attribution for the discovery.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">Alfred A Baumeister <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AABaumeister_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AABaumeister-2">[2]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>On the other hand, Dunbar and Fugelsang say that an ingenious experimental design (and control conditions) may not be enough for the researcher to properly appreciate when a finding is "unexpected". Serendipitous discoveries often requires certain mental conditions in the investigator beyond rigor. For example, a scientist must know all about what is expected before they can be surprised, and this requires experience in the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DunbarFugelsangLuck_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-DunbarFugelsangLuck-1">[1]</a></sup> Researchers also require the sagacity to know to invest in the most curious findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AABaumeister_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-AABaumeister-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serendipitous_discoveries">Serendipitous discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Role_of_chance_in_scientific_discoveries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Serendipitous discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_discoveries_influenced_by_chance_circumstances" title="List of discoveries influenced by chance circumstances">List of discoveries influenced by chance circumstances</a></div>
<p>Royston Roberts says that various discoveries required a degree of genius, but also some lucky element for that genius to act on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-14">[14]</a></sup> Richard Gaughan writes that accidental discoveries result from the convergence of preparation, opportunity, and desire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaughan_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaughan-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>An example of luck in science is when drugs under investigation become known for different, unexpected uses. This was the case for <a href="/wiki/Minoxidil" title="Minoxidil">minoxidil</a> (an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihypertensive" title="Antihypertensive">antihypertensive</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator">vasodilator</a> that was subsequently found to also slow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alopecia" title="Alopecia">hair loss</a> and promote hair regrowth in some people) and for <a href="/wiki/Sildenafil" title="Sildenafil">sildenafil</a> (a medicine for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_arterial_hypertension" title="Pulmonary arterial hypertension">pulmonary arterial hypertension</a>, now familiar as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viagra" title="Viagra">Viagra</a>", used to treat <a href="/wiki/Erectile_dysfunction" title="Erectile dysfunction">erectile dysfunction</a>). 
</p><p>The hallucinogenic effects of <a href="/wiki/Lysergic_acid_diethylamide" title="Lysergic acid diethylamide">lysergic acid diethylamide</a> (LSD) were discovered by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Hofmann" title="Albert Hofmann">Albert Hofmann</a>, who was originally working with the substance to try and treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migraines" title="Migraines">migraines</a> and bleeding after childbirth. Hofmann experienced mental distortions and suspected it may have been the effects of LSD. He decided to test this hypothesis on himself by taking what he thought was "an extremely small quantity": 250 micrograms. Hofmann's description of what he experienced as a result of taking so much LSD is regarded by Royston Roberts as "one of the most frightening accounts in recorded medical history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
