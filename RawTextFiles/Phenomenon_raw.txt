<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Phenomenon</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">philosophical concept</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Phenomena" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Phenomena_(disambiguation)" title="Phenomena (disambiguation)">Phenomena (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Phenomenal" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Phenomenal_(disambiguation)" title="Phenomenal (disambiguation)">Phenomenal (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the film, see <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon_(film)" title="Phenomenon (film)">Phenomenon (film)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>phenomenon</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a>: φαινόμενον, <i>phainómenon</i>, from the verb <i>phainein</i>, to show, shine, appear, to be manifest or manifest itself, plural <b>phenomena</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> is any thing which manifests itself. Phenomena are often, but not always, understood as "things that appear" or "<a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experiences</a>" for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentient" title="Sentient">sentient</a> being, or in principle may be so.
</p><p>The term came into its modern philosophical usage through <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>, who contrasted it with the <a href="/wiki/Noumenon" title="Noumenon">noumenon</a>. In contrast to a phenomenon, a noumenon cannot be directly observed. Kant was heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> in this part of his philosophy, in which phenomenon and noumenon serve as interrelated technical terms. Far predating this, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Philosophy" title="Ancient Greek Philosophy">ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhonism" title="Pyrrhonism">Pyrrhonist</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Sextus_Empiricus" title="Sextus Empiricus">Sextus Empiricus</a> also used phenomenon and noumenon as interrelated technical terms.
</p>

Scientists use phenomena to refine some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotheses" title="Hypotheses">hypotheses</a> and sometimes to disprove a <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_philosophical_usage">Modern philosophical usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modern philosophical usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In modern philosophical use, the term 'phenomena' has come to mean 'what is experienced is the basis of <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a>'.  In Immanuel Kant's <i>Inaugural Dissertation, On the Form and Principles of the Sensible and Intelligible World</i> (1770), Kant theorizes that the human mind is restricted to the logical world and thus can only interpret and understand occurrences according to their physical appearances.  He wrote that humans could infer only as much as their senses allowed, but not experience the actual object itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Thus, the term phenomenon refers to any incident deserving of inquiry and investigation, especially events that are particularly unusual or of distinctive importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup> According to The Columbia Encyclopedia, "Modern philosophers have used 'phenomenon' to designate what is apprehended before judgment is applied."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-3">[3]</a></sup> This may not be possible if observation is <a href="/wiki/Theory-ladenness" title="Theory-ladenness">theory laden</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific">Scientific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Phenomenalism" title="Phenomenalism">Phenomenalism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Space_Fire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="771" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/220px-Space_Fire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/330px-Space_Fire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Space_Fire.jpg/440px-Space_Fire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Space_Fire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A comparison between a candle flame on Earth (left) and in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">microgravity</a> environment, such as that found on the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (right).<br/> The same <i>burning</i> phenomenon is <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observed</a>, but different flame <i>shape</i> and <i>colouring</i> phenomena are also observed.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Streichholz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3504" data-file-width="2336" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Streichholz.jpg/170px-Streichholz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Streichholz.jpg/255px-Streichholz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Streichholz.jpg/340px-Streichholz.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Streichholz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The combustion of a match is an observable occurrence, or event, and therefore a phenomenon.</div></div></div>
<p>In scientific usage, a phenomenon is any event that is observable, however common it might be, even if it requires the use of instrumentation to observe, record, or compile data concerning it. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, a phenomenon may be described by a system of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> related to <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>'s observations of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>'s orbit and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_gravitation" title="Universal gravitation">gravity</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>'s observations of the motion of a <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another example of scientific phenomena can be found in the experience of <a href="/wiki/Phantom_limb" title="Phantom limb">phantom limb</a> sensations.  This occurrence, the sensation of feeling in amputated limbs, is reported by over 70% of amputees.  Although the limb is no longer present, they report still experiencing sensations. This is an extraordinary event that defies typical logic and has been a source of much curiosity within the medical and physiological fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_natural_science">In natural science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In natural science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical">Mechanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mechanical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>mechanical phenomenon</b> is a physical phenomenon associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a> of objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some examples are <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_cradle" title="Newton's cradle">Newton's cradle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Double_pendulum" title="Double pendulum">double pendulums</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Group_and_social">Group and social</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Group and social">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Social_phenomenon" title="Social phenomenon">Social phenomenon</a></div>
<p>Group phenomena concern the behavior of a particular group of individual entities, usually organisms and most especially people. The behavior of individuals often changes in a group setting in various ways, and a group may have its own behaviors not possible for an individual because of the <a href="/wiki/Herd_mentality" title="Herd mentality">herd mentality</a>.
</p><p>Social phenomena apply especially to organisms and people in that subjective states are implicit in the term. Attitudes and events particular to a group may have effects beyond the group, and either be adapted by the larger society, or seen as aberrant, being punished or shunned.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_usage">Popular usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popular usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In popular usage, a phenomenon often refers to an extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Event_(philosophy)" title="Event (philosophy)">event</a>. The term is most commonly used to refer to occurrences that at first defy explanation or baffle the observer. According to the <i>Dictionary of Visual Discourse</i>, "In ordinary language 'phenomenon/phenomena' refer to any occurrence worthy of note and investigation, typically an untoward or unusual event, person or fact that is of special significance or otherwise notable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dictionary-1">[1]</a></sup>
