<p>An <b>aurora</b> (plural: <b>auroras</b> or <b>aurorae</b>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> sometimes referred to as <b>polar lights</b>, <b>northern lights</b> (aurora borealis) or <b>southern lights</b> (aurora australis), is a natural light display in the Earth's sky, predominantly seen in the <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">high-latitude regions</a> (around the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>).
</p><p>Auroras are produced when the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> is sufficiently disturbed by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> that the trajectories of <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a> in both solar wind and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_belt" title="Van Allen belt">magnetospheric plasma</a>, mainly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, precipitate them into the upper atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>/<a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a>) due to <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>, where their energy is lost.
</p><p>The resulting <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> and excitation of atmospheric constituents emits light of varying color and complexity. The form of the aurora, occurring within bands around both polar regions, is also dependent on the amount of acceleration imparted to the precipitating particles. Precipitating protons generally produce optical emissions as incident <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms after gaining electrons from the atmosphere. Proton auroras are usually observed at lower latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "aurora" is derived from the name of the Roman godess <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(mythology)" title="Aurora (mythology)">Aurora</a> who traveled from east to west announcing the coming of the sun.  Ancient Roman poets used the name metaphorically to refer to dawn, often mentioning its play of colors across the otherwise dark sky (e.g. "rosy fingered dawn").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_of_terrestrial_auroras">Occurrence of terrestrial auroras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence of terrestrial auroras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most auroras occur in a band known as the "auroral zone",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feldstein86_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-feldstein86-5">[4]</a></sup> which is typically 3° to 6° wide in latitude and between 10° and 20° from the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_pole" title="Geomagnetic pole">geomagnetic poles</a> at all local times (or longitudes), most clearly seen at night against a dark sky. A region that currently displays an aurora is called the "auroral oval", a band displaced towards the night side of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> Early evidence for a geomagnetic connection comes from the statistics of auroral observations. <a href="/wiki/Elias_Loomis" title="Elias Loomis">Elias Loomis</a> (1860), and later Hermann Fritz (1881)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> and S. Tromholt (1882)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> in more detail, established that the aurora appeared mainly in the auroral zone. Day-to-day positions of the auroral ovals are posted on the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In northern <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, the effect is known as the aurora borealis or the northern lights. The former term was coined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a> in 1619, from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(mythology)" title="Aurora (mythology)">goddess of the dawn</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> name for the north wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The southern counterpart, the aurora australis or the southern lights, has features  almost identical to the aurora borealis and changes simultaneously with changes in the northern auroral zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The Aurora Australis is visible from high southern latitudes in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storm</a> causes the auroral ovals (north and south) to expand, and bring the aurora to lower latitudes. The instantaneous distribution of auroras ("auroral oval")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feldstein86_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-feldstein86-5">[4]</a></sup> is slightly different, being centered about 3–5° nightward of the magnetic pole, so that auroral arcs reach furthest toward the equator when the <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">magnetic pole</a> in question is in between the observer and the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The aurora can be seen best at this time, which is called <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_midnight" title="Magnetic midnight">magnetic midnight</a>.
</p><p>Auroras seen within the auroral oval may be directly overhead, but from farther away, they illuminate the poleward horizon as a greenish glow, or sometimes a faint red, as if the Sun were rising from an unusual direction. Auroras also occur poleward of the auroral zone as either diffuse patches or arcs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> which can be subvisual.
</p>

<p>Auroras are occasionally seen in latitudes below the auroral zone, when a geomagnetic storm temporarily enlarges the auroral oval. Large geomagnetic storms are most common during the peak of the 11-year <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspot cycle</a> or during the three years after the peak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
An aurora may appear overhead as a "corona" of rays, radiating from a distant and apparent central location, which results from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">perspective</a>.
An electron spirals (gyrates) about a field line at an angle that is determined by its velocity vectors, parallel and perpendicular, respectively, to the local geomagnetic field vector B. This angle is known as the "pitch angle" of the particle. The distance, or radius, of the electron from the field line at any time is known as its Larmor radius. The pitch angle increases as the electron travels to a region of greater field strength nearer to the atmosphere. Thus, it is possible for some particles to return, or mirror, if the angle becomes 90° before entering the atmosphere to collide with the denser molecules there. Other particles that do not mirror enter the atmosphere and contribute to the auroral display over a range of altitudes.
Other types of auroras have been observed from space, e.g."poleward arcs" stretching sunward across the polar cap, the related "theta aurora",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> and "dayside arcs" near noon. These are relatively infrequent and poorly understood. Other interesting effects occur such as flickering aurora, "black aurora" and subvisual red arcs. In addition to all these, a weak glow (often deep red) observed around the two polar cusps, the field lines separating the ones that close through the Earth from those that are swept into the tail and close remotely.

<p>The altitudes where auroral emissions occur were revealed by Carl Størmer and his colleagues, who used cameras to triangulate more than 12,000 auroras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> They discovered that most of the light is produced between 90 and 150 km above the ground, while extending at times to more than 1000 km.
Images of auroras are significantly more common today than in the past due to the increase in the use of <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">digital cameras</a> that have high enough sensitivities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Film and digital exposure to auroral displays is fraught with difficulties. Due to the different color spectra present, and the temporal changes occurring during the exposure, the results are somewhat unpredictable. Different layers of the film emulsion respond differently to lower light levels, and choice of a film can be very important. Longer exposures superimpose rapidly changing features, and often blanket the dynamic attribute of a display. Higher sensitivity creates issues with graininess.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Malin" title="David Malin">David Malin</a> pioneered multiple exposure using multiple filters for astronomical photography, recombining the images in the laboratory to recreate the visual display more accurately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> For scientific research, proxies are often used, such as ultraviolet, and color-correction to simulate the appearance to humans. Predictive techniques are also used, to indicate the extent of the display, a highly useful tool for aurora hunters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Terrestrial features often find their way into aurora images, making them more accessible and more likely to be published by major websites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Excellent images are possible with standard film (using <a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">ISO ratings</a> between 100 and 400) and a <a href="/wiki/Single-lens_reflex_camera" title="Single-lens reflex camera">single-lens reflex camera</a> with full <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>, a fast lens (f1.4 50 mm, for example), and exposures between 10 and 30 seconds, depending on the aurora's brightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early work on the imaging of the auroras was done in 1949 by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Saskatchewan" title="University of Saskatchewan">University of Saskatchewan</a> using the <a href="/wiki/SCR-270" title="SCR-270">SCR-270</a> radar.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_forms_and_colors">Visual forms and colors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visual forms and colors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Auroras frequently appear either as a diffuse glow or as "curtains" that extend approximately in the east–west direction. At some times, they form "quiet arcs"; at others, they evolve and change constantly. These are called "active aurora".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_shapes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4904" data-file-width="10606" decoding="async" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Aurora_shapes.jpg/220px-Aurora_shapes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Aurora_shapes.jpg/330px-Aurora_shapes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Aurora_shapes.jpg/440px-Aurora_shapes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_shapes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Different forms</div></div></div>
<p>The most distinctive and brightest are the curtain-like auroral arcs. Each curtain consists of many parallel rays, each lined up with the local direction of the magnetic field, consistent with auroras being shaped by Earth's magnetic field. <i>In situ</i> particle measurements confirm that auroral electrons are guided by the geomagnetic field, and spiral around them while moving toward Earth. The similarity of an auroral display to curtains is often enhanced by folds within the arcs. Arcs can fragment or break up into separate, at times rapidly changing, often rayed features that may fill the whole sky. These are the discrete auroras, which are at times bright enough to read a newspaper by at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> and can display rapid subsecond variations in intensity.
The diffuse aurora, though, is a relatively featureless glow sometimes close to the limit of visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> It can be distinguished from moonlit clouds because stars can be seen undiminished through the glow. Diffuse auroras are often composed of patches whose brightness exhibits regular or near-regular pulsations. The pulsation period can be typically many seconds, so is not always obvious. Often there black aurora i.e. narrow regions in diffuse aurora with reduced luminosity. A typical auroral display consists of these forms appearing in the above order throughout the night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Red</b>: At the highest altitudes, excited atomic oxygen emits at 630 nm (red); low concentration of atoms and lower sensitivity of eyes at this wavelength make this color visible only under more intense solar activity. The low number of oxygen atoms and their gradually diminishing concentration is responsible for the faint appearance of the top parts of the "curtains". Scarlet, crimson, and carmine are the most often-seen hues of red for the auroras.</li>
<li><b>Green</b>: At lower altitudes, the more frequent collisions suppress the 630-nm (red) mode: rather the 557.7 nm emission (green) dominates. Fairly high concentration of atomic oxygen and higher eye sensitivity in green make green auroras the most common. The excited molecular nitrogen (atomic nitrogen being rare due to high stability of the N<sub>2</sub> molecule) plays a role here, as it can transfer energy by collision to an oxygen atom, which then radiates it away at the green wavelength. (Red and green can also mix together to produce pink or yellow hues.) The rapid decrease of concentration of atomic oxygen below about 100 km is responsible for the abrupt-looking end of the lower edges of the curtains. Both the 557.7 and 630.0 nm wavelengths correspond to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_transition" title="Forbidden transition">forbidden transitions</a> of atomic oxygen, slow mechanism that is responsible for the graduality (0.7 s and 107 s respectively) of flaring and fading.</li>
<li><b>Blue</b>: At yet lower altitudes, atomic oxygen is uncommon, and molecular nitrogen and ionized molecular nitrogen take over in producing visible light emission, radiating at a large number of wavelengths in both red and blue parts of the spectrum, with 428 nm (blue) being dominant. Blue and purple emissions, typically at the lower edges of the "curtains", show up at the highest levels of solar activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> The molecular nitrogen transitions are much faster than the atomic oxygen ones.</li>
<li><b>Ultraviolet</b>: Ultraviolet radiation from auroras (within the optical window but not visible to virtually all humans) has been observed with the requisite equipment. Ultraviolet auroras have also been seen on Mars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci-news.com_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-sci-news.com-26">[25]</a></sup> Jupiter and Saturn.</li>
<li><b>Infrared</b>: Infrared radiation, in wavelengths that are within the optical window, is also part of many auroras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci-news.com_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-sci-news.com-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dapep_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-dapep-27">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Yellow</b> and <b>pink</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Additive_colour" title="Additive colour">a mix</a> of red and green or blue. Other shades of red, as well as orange, may be seen on rare occasions; yellow-green is moderately common. As red, green, and blue are the primary colours of additive synthesis of colours, in theory, practically any colour might be possible, but the ones mentioned in this article comprise a virtually exhaustive list.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_auroral_radiation">Other auroral radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other auroral radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition, the aurora and associated currents produce a strong radio emission around 150 kHz known as <a href="/wiki/Auroral_kilometric_radiation" title="Auroral kilometric radiation">auroral kilometric radiation</a> (AKR), discovered in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Ionospheric absorption makes AKR only observable from space. X-ray emissions, originating from the particles associated with auroras, have also been detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aurora_noise">Aurora noise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aurora noise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aurora <a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">noise</a>, similar to a hissing, or crackling noise, begins about 70 m (230 ft) above the Earth's surface and is caused by charged particles in an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> layer of the atmosphere formed during a cold night. The charged particles discharge when particles from the Sun hit the inversion layer, creating the noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_auroras">Causes of auroras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Causes of auroras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A full understanding of the physical processes which lead to different types of auroras is still incomplete, but the basic cause involves the interaction of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetosphere" title="Earth's magnetosphere">Earth's magnetosphere</a>. The varying intensity of the solar wind produces effects of different magnitudes, but includes one or more of the following physical scenarios.
</p>
<ol><li>A quiescent solar wind flowing past the Earth's magnetosphere steadily interacts with it and can both inject solar wind particles directly onto the geomagnetic field lines that are 'open', as opposed to being 'closed' in the opposite hemisphere, and provide diffusion through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a>. It can also cause particles already trapped in the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">radiation belts</a> to precipitate into the atmosphere. Once particles are lost to the atmosphere from the radiation belts, under quiet conditions, new ones replace them only slowly, and the loss-cone becomes depleted. In the magnetotail, however, particle trajectories seem constantly to reshuffle, probably when the particles cross the very weak magnetic field near the equator. As a result, the flow of electrons in that region is nearly the same in all directions ("isotropic"), and assures a steady supply of leaking electrons. The leakage of electrons does not leave the tail positively charged, because each leaked electron lost to the atmosphere is replaced by a low energy electron drawn upward from the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. Such replacement of "hot" electrons by "cold" ones is in complete accord with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_law_of_thermodynamics" title="2nd law of thermodynamics">2nd law of thermodynamics</a>. The complete process, which also generates an electric ring current around the Earth, is uncertain.</li>
<li>Geomagnetic disturbance from an enhanced <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> causes distortions of the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetotail</a> ("magnetic substorms"). These 'substorms' tend to occur after prolonged spells(hours) during which the interplanetary magnetic field has had an appreciable southward component. This leads to a higher rate of interconnection between its field lines and those of Earth. As a result, the solar wind moves <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a> (tubes of magnetic field lines, 'locked' together with their resident plasma) from the day side of Earth to the magnetotail, widening the obstacle it presents to the solar wind flow and constricting the tail on the night-side. Ultimately some tail plasma can separate ("<a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a>"); some blobs ("<a href="/wiki/Plasmoid" title="Plasmoid">plasmoids</a>") are squeezed downstream and are carried away with the solar wind; others are squeezed toward Earth where their motion feeds strong outbursts of auroras, mainly around midnight ("unloading process"). A geomagnetic storm resulting from greater interaction adds many more particles to the plasma trapped around Earth, also producing enhancement of the "ring current". Occasionally the resulting modification of the Earth's magnetic field can be so strong that it produces auroras visible at middle latitudes, on field lines much closer to the equator than those of the auroral zone.
<dl><dd><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_and_Aurora.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1110" data-file-width="1700" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Moon_and_Aurora.jpg/220px-Moon_and_Aurora.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Moon_and_Aurora.jpg/330px-Moon_and_Aurora.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Moon_and_Aurora.jpg/440px-Moon_and_Aurora.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_and_Aurora.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moon and Aurora</div></div></div></dd></dl></li>
<li>Acceleration of auroral charged particles invariably accompanies a magnetospheric disturbance that causes an aurora. This mechanism, which is believed to predominantly arise from strong electric fields along the magnetic field or wave-particle interactions, raises the velocity of a particle in the direction of the guiding magnetic field. The pitch angle is thereby decreased, and increases the chance of it being precipitated into the atmosphere. Both electromagnetic and electrostatic waves, produced at the time of greater geomagnetic disturbances, make a significant contribution to the energising processes that sustain an aurora. Particle acceleration provides a complex intermediate process for transferring energy from the solar wind indirectly into the atmosphere.</li></ol>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="626" data-file-width="631" decoding="async" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg/220px-Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg/330px-Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg/440px-Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_australis_20050911.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aurora australis (11 September 2005) as captured by NASA's <a href="/wiki/IMAGE" title="IMAGE">IMAGE</a> satellite, digitally overlaid onto <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Marble" title="The Blue Marble">The Blue Marble</a></i> composite image. <a href="/wiki/File:Aurora_Australis.gif" title="File:Aurora Australis.gif">An animation</a> created using the same satellite data is also available</div></div></div>
<p>The details of these phenomena are not fully understood. However it is clear that the prime source of auroral particles is the solar wind feeding the magnetosphere, the reservoir containing the radiation zones, and temporarily magnetically trapped, particles confined by the geomagnetic field, coupled with particle acceleration processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Furthermore, in 2016 more than fifty <a href="/wiki/Citizen_science" title="Citizen science">citizen science</a> observations (under the aegis of Aurorasaurus) observed a heretofore unknown type of aurora which was dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Steve_(atmospheric_phenomenon)" title="Steve (atmospheric phenomenon)">STEVE</a>" (a reference to the animated film <a href="/wiki/Over_the_Hedge_(film)" title="Over the Hedge (film)">Over the Hedge</a> in which one of the characters randomly names a previously unknown creature "Steve"); subsequent reportage went <a href="/wiki/Viral_phenomenon" title="Viral phenomenon">viral</a>, and STEVE remains under active scientific investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STEVE_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-STEVE-33">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auroral_particles">Auroral particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Auroral particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The immediate cause of the ionization and excitation of atmospheric constituents leading to auroral emissions was discovered in 1960, when a pioneering rocket flight from Fort Churchill in Canada revealed a flux of electrons entering the atmosphere from above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> Since then an extensive collection of measurements has been acquired painstakingly and with steadily improving resolution since the 1960s by many research teams using rockets and satellites to traverse the auroral zone. The main findings have been that auroral arcs and other bright forms are due to electrons that have been accelerated during the final few 10,000 km or so of their plunge into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> These electrons often, but not always, exhibit a peak in their energy distribution, and are preferentially aligned along the local direction of the magnetic field.
Electrons mainly responsible for diffuse and pulsating auroras have, in contrast, a smoothly falling energy distribution, and an angular (pitch-angle) distribution favouring directions perpendicular to the local magnetic field. Pulsations were discovered to originate at or close to the equatorial crossing point of auroral zone magnetic field lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Protons are also associated with auroras, both discrete and diffuse.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auroras_and_the_atmosphere">Auroras and the atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Auroras and the atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Auroras result from emissions of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> in the Earth's upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, above 80 km (50 mi), from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionized" title="Ionized">ionized</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> atoms regaining an electron, and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> based molecules returning from an <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited state</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>  They are ionized or excited by the collision of particles precipitated into the atmosphere. Both incoming electrons and protons may be involved. Excitation energy is lost within the atmosphere by the emission of a photon, or by collision with another atom or molecule:
</p>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> emissions</dt>
<dd>green or orange-red, depending on the amount of energy absorbed.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> emissions</dt>
<dd>blue or red; blue if the atom regains an electron after it has been ionized, red if returning to ground state from an excited state.</dd></dl>
<p>Oxygen is unusual in terms of its return to ground state: it can take three quarters of a second to emit green light and up to two minutes to emit red. Collisions with other atoms or molecules absorb the excitation energy and prevent emission. Because the highest atmosphere has a higher percentage of oxygen and is sparsely distributed such collisions are rare enough to allow time for oxygen to emit red. Collisions become more frequent progressing down into the atmosphere, so that red emissions do not have time to happen, and eventually even green light emissions are prevented.
This is why there is a color differential with altitude; at high altitudes oxygen red dominates, then oxygen green and nitrogen blue/red, then finally nitrogen blue/red when collisions prevent oxygen from emitting anything. Green is the most common color. Then comes pink, a mixture of light green and red, followed by pure red, then yellow (a mixture of red and green), and finally, pure blue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auroras_and_the_ionosphere">Auroras and the ionosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Auroras and the ionosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bright auroras are generally associated with <a href="/wiki/Birkeland_current" title="Birkeland current">Birkeland currents</a> (Schield et al., 1969;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> Zmuda and Armstrong, 1973<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup>), which flow down into the ionosphere on one side of the pole and out on the other. In between, some of the current connects directly through the ionospheric E layer (125 km); the rest ("region 2") detours, leaving again through field lines closer to the equator and closing through the "partial ring current" carried by magnetically trapped plasma. The ionosphere is an <a href="/wiki/Ohm%27s_law" title="Ohm's law">ohmic conductor</a>, so some consider that such currents require a driving voltage, which an, as yet unspecified, dynamo mechanism can supply. Electric field probes in orbit above the polar cap suggest voltages of the order of 40,000 volts, rising up to more than 200,000 volts during intense magnetic storms. In another interpretation the currents are the direct result of electron acceleration into the atmosphere by wave/particle interactions.
</p><p>Ionospheric resistance has a complex nature, and leads to a secondary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall_current" title="Hall current">Hall current</a> flow. By a strange twist of physics, the magnetic disturbance on the ground due to the main current almost cancels out, so most of the observed effect of auroras is due to a secondary current, the auroral <a href="/wiki/Electrojet" title="Electrojet">electrojet</a>. An auroral electrojet index (measured in nanotesla) is regularly derived from ground data and serves as a general measure of auroral activity. <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAPE_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAPE-40">[39]</a></sup> deduced that the currents flowed in the east–west directions along the auroral arc, and such currents, flowing from the dayside toward (approximately) midnight were later named "auroral electrojets" (see also <a href="/wiki/Birkeland_current" title="Birkeland current">Birkeland currents</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_of_the_solar_wind_with_Earth">Interaction of the solar wind with Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interaction of the solar wind with Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Earth is constantly immersed in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, a rarefied flow of hot plasma (a gas of free electrons and positive ions) emitted by the Sun in all directions, a result of the two-million-degree temperature of the Sun's outermost layer, the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>. The solar wind reaches Earth with a velocity typically around 400 km/s, a density of around 5 ions/cm<sup>3</sup> and a magnetic field intensity of around 2–5 nT (for comparison, Earth's surface field is typically 30,000–50,000 nT). During <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">magnetic storms</a>, in particular, flows can be several times faster; the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a> (IMF) may also be much stronger. <a href="/wiki/Joan_Feynman" title="Joan Feynman">Joan Feynman</a> deduced in the 1970s that the long-term averages of solar wind speed correlated with geomagnetic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>  Her work resulted from data collected by the <a href="/wiki/Explorer_33" title="Explorer 33">Explorer 33</a> spacecraft.
The solar wind and magnetosphere consist of <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> (ionized gas), which conducts electricity. It is well known (since <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>'s work around 1830) that when an electrical conductor is placed within a magnetic field while relative motion occurs in a direction that the conductor cuts <i>across</i> (or is cut <i>by</i>), rather than <i>along</i>, the lines of the magnetic field, an electric current is induced within the conductor. The strength of the current depends on a) the rate of relative motion, b) the strength of the magnetic field, c) the number of conductors ganged together and d) the distance between the conductor and the magnetic field, while the <i>direction</i> of flow is dependent upon the direction of relative motion. <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">Dynamos</a> make use of this basic process ("the <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo effect</a>"), any and all conductors, solid or otherwise are so affected, including plasmas and other fluids.
The IMF originates on the Sun, linked to the <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">field lines (lines of force)</a> are dragged out by the solar wind. That alone would tend to line them up in the Sun-Earth direction, but the rotation of the Sun angles them at Earth by about 45 degrees forming a spiral in the ecliptic plane), known as the <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">Parker spiral</a>. The field lines passing Earth are therefore usually linked to those near the western edge ("limb") of the visible Sun at any time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
The solar wind and the magnetosphere, being two electrically conducting fluids in relative motion, should be able in principle to generate electric currents by dynamo action and impart energy from the flow of the solar wind. However, this process is hampered by the fact that plasmas conduct readily along magnetic field lines, but less readily perpendicular to them. Energy is more effectively transferred by temporary magnetic connection between the field lines of the solar wind and those of the magnetosphere. Unsurprisingly this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a>. As already mentioned, it happens most readily when the interplanetary field is directed southward, in a similar direction to the geomagnetic field in the inner regions of both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_magnetic_pole" title="North magnetic pole">north magnetic pole</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_magnetic_pole" title="South magnetic pole">south magnetic pole</a>.
</p>

<p>Auroras are more frequent and brighter during the intense phase of the solar cycle when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">coronal mass ejections</a> increase the intensity of the solar wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetosphere">Magnetosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Magnetosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Earth's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> is shaped by the impact of the solar wind on the Earth's magnetic field. This forms an obstacle to the flow, diverting it, at an average distance of about 70,000 km (11 Earth radii or Re),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> producing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a> 12,000 km to 15,000 km (1.9 to 2.4 Re) further upstream. The width of the magnetosphere abreast of Earth, is typically 190,000 km (30 Re), and on the night side a long "magnetotail" of stretched field lines extends to great distances (&gt; 200 Re).
The high latitude magnetosphere is filled with plasma as the solar wind passes the Earth. The flow of plasma into the magnetosphere increases with additional turbulence, density and speed in the solar wind. This flow is favoured by a southward component of the IMF, which can then directly connect to the high latitude geomagnetic field lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> The flow pattern of magnetospheric plasma is mainly from the magnetotail toward the Earth, around the Earth and back into the solar wind through the <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">magnetopause</a> on the day-side. In addition to moving perpendicular to the Earth's magnetic field, some magnetospheric plasma travels down along the Earth's magnetic field lines, gains additional energy and loses it to the atmosphere in the auroral zones. The cusps of the magnetosphere, separating geomagnetic field lines that close through the Earth from those that close remotely allow a small amount of solar wind to directly reach the top of the atmosphere, producing an auroral glow.
On 26 February 2008, <a href="/wiki/THEMIS" title="THEMIS">THEMIS</a> probes were able to determine, for the first time, the triggering event for the onset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetospheric_substorm" title="Magnetospheric substorm">magnetospheric substorms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Two of the five probes, positioned approximately one third the distance to the moon, measured events suggesting a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a> event 96 seconds prior to auroral intensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">Geomagnetic storms</a> that ignite auroras may occur more often during the months around the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a>. It is not well understood, but geomagnetic storms may vary with Earth's seasons. Two factors to consider are the tilt of both the solar and Earth's axis to the ecliptic plane. As the Earth orbits throughout a year, it experiences an interplanetary magnetic field (IMF) from different latitudes of the Sun, which is tilted at 8 degrees. Similarly, the 23 degree tilt of the Earth's axis about which the geomagnetic pole rotates with a diurnal variation, changes the daily average angle that the geomagnetic field presents to the incident IMF throughout a year. These factors combined can lead to minor cyclical changes in the detailed way that the IMF links to the magnetosphere. In turn, this affects the average probability of opening a door through which energy from the solar wind can reach the Earth's inner magnetosphere and thereby enhance auroras.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Auroral_particle_acceleration">Auroral particle acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Auroral particle acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The electrons responsible for the brightest forms of aurora are well accounted for by their acceleration in the dynamic electric fields of plasma turbulence encountered during precipitation from the magnetosphere into the auroral atmosphere. In contrast, static electric fields are unable to transfer energy to the electrons due to their conservative nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> The electrons and ions that cause the diffuse aurora appear not to be accelerated during precipitation.
The increase in strength of magnetic field lines towards the Earth creates a 'magnetic mirror' that turns back many of the downward flowing electrons. The bright forms of auroras are produced when downward acceleration not only increases the energy of precipitating electrons but also reduces their pitch angles (angle between electron velocity and the local magnetic field vector). This greatly increases the rate of deposition of energy into the atmosphere, and thereby the rates of ionisation, excitation and consequent emission of auroral light. Acceleration also increases the electron current flowing between the atmosphere and magnetosphere.
</p><p>One early theory proposed for the acceleration of auroral electrons is based on an assumed static, or quasi-static, electric field creating a uni-directional potential drop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evidence_for_the_low_altitude_acceleration_of_auroral_particles_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evidence_for_the_low_altitude_acceleration_of_auroral_particles-49">[48]</a></sup> No mention is provided of either the necessary space-charge or equi-potential distribution, and these remain to be specified for the notion of acceleration by double layers to be credible. Fundamentally, <a href="/wiki/Poisson%27s_equation" title="Poisson's equation">Poisson's equation</a> indicates that there can be no configuration of charge resulting in a net potential drop.  Inexplicably though, some authors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> still invoke quasi-static parallel electric fields as net accelerators of auroral electrons, citing interpretations of transient observations of fields and particles as supporting this theory as firm fact.  In another example,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> there is little justification given for saying 'FAST observations demonstrate detailed quantitative agreement between the measured electric potentials and the <b>ion beam</b> energies...., leaving no doubt that parallel potential drops are a dominant source of <b>auroral particle</b> acceleration'.
</p><p>Another theory is based on acceleration by Landau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> resonance in the turbulent electric fields of the acceleration region. This process is essentially the same as that employed in plasma fusion laboratories throughout the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio-frequency_Plasma_Heating_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radio-frequency_Plasma_Heating-54">[53]</a></sup> and appears well able to account in principle for most – if not all – detailed properties of the electrons responsible for the brightest forms of auroras, above, below and within the acceleration region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_Borealis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1312" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_Borealis.jpg/220px-Aurora_Borealis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_Borealis.jpg/330px-Aurora_Borealis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_Borealis.jpg/440px-Aurora_Borealis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_Borealis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ISS <a href="/wiki/Expedition_6" title="Expedition 6">Expedition 6</a> team, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Manicouagan" title="Lake Manicouagan">Lake Manicouagan</a> is visible to the bottom left</div></div></div>
<p>Other mechanisms have also been proposed, in particular, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_waves" title="Alfvén waves">Alfvén waves</a>, wave modes involving the magnetic field first noted by <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">Hannes Alfvén</a> (1942), which have been observed in the laboratory and in space. The question is whether these waves might just be a different way of looking at the above process, however, because this approach does not point out a different energy source, and many plasma bulk phenomena can also be described in terms of Alfvén waves.
</p>

<p>Other processes are also involved in the aurora, and much remains to be learned. Auroral electrons created by large geomagnetic storms often seem to have energies below 1 keV, and are stopped higher up, near 200 km. Such low energies excite mainly the red line of oxygen, so that often such auroras are red. On the other hand, positive ions also reach the ionosphere at such time, with energies of 20–30 keV, suggesting they might be an "overflow" along magnetic field lines of the copious "ring current" ions accelerated at such times, by processes different from the ones described above.
Some O+ ions ("conics") also seem accelerated in different ways by plasma processes associated with the aurora. These ions are accelerated by plasma waves in directions mainly perpendicular to the field lines. They therefore start at their "mirror points" and can travel only upward. As they do so, the "mirror effect" transforms their directions of motion, from perpendicular to the field line to a cone around it, which gradually narrows down, becoming increasingly parallel at large distances where the field is much weaker.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Auroral_events_of_historical_significance">Auroral events of historical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Auroral events of historical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The discovery of a 1770 Japanese <a href="/wiki/Diary" title="Diary">diary</a> in 2017 depicting auroras above the ancient Japanese capital of <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> suggested that the storm may have been 7% larger than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrington_event" title="Carrington event">Carrington event</a>, which affected telegraph networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1770_Japanese_diary_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-1770_Japanese_diary-56">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inclined_zenith_aurora_over_Kyoto_on_17_September_1770:_Graphical_evidence_of_extreme_magnetic_storm_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inclined_zenith_aurora_over_Kyoto_on_17_September_1770:_Graphical_evidence_of_extreme_magnetic_storm-57">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The auroras that resulted from the "<a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">great geomagnetic storm</a>" on both 28 August and 2 September 1859, however, are thought to be the most spectacular in recent recorded history. In a paper to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> on 21 November 1861, Balfour Stewart described both auroral events as documented by a self-recording <a href="/wiki/Magnetograph" title="Magnetograph">magnetograph</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kew_Observatory" title="Kew Observatory">Kew Observatory</a> and established the connection between the 2 September 1859 auroral storm and the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">Carrington</a>-Hodgson flare event when he observed that, "It is not impossible to suppose that in this case our luminary was taken <i>in the act</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSp1_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSp1-58">[57]</a></sup> The second auroral event, which occurred on 2 September 1859 as a result of the exceptionally intense Carrington-Hodgson white light <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flare</a> on 1 September 1859, produced auroras, so widespread and extraordinarily bright, that they were seen and reported in published scientific measurements, ship logs, and newspapers throughout the United States, Europe, Japan, and Australia. It was reported by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> that in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> on Friday 2 September 1859 the aurora was "so brilliant that at about one o'clock ordinary print could be read by the <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADVSR1_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADVSR1-59">[58]</a></sup> One o'clock EST time on Friday 2 September, would have been 6:00 GMT and the self-recording <a href="/wiki/Magnetograph" title="Magnetograph">magnetograph</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kew_Observatory" title="Kew Observatory">Kew Observatory</a> was recording the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storm</a>, which was then one hour old, at its full intensity. Between 1859 and 1862, <a href="/wiki/Elias_Loomis" title="Elias Loomis">Elias Loomis</a> published a series of nine papers on the <a href="/wiki/Elias_Loomis#Great_Auroral_Exhibition_of_1859" title="Elias Loomis">Great Auroral Exhibition of 1859</a> in the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Journal_of_Science" title="American Journal of Science">American Journal of Science</a></i> where he collected worldwide reports of the auroral event.
</p><p>That aurora is thought to have been produced by one of the most intense <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejections</a> in history. It is also notable for the fact that it is the first time where the phenomena of auroral activity and electricity were unambiguously linked. This insight was made possible not only due to scientific <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> measurements of the era, but also as a result of a significant portion of the 125,000 miles (201,000 km) of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">telegraph</a> lines then in service being significantly disrupted for many hours throughout the storm. Some telegraph lines, however, seem to have been of the appropriate length and orientation to produce a sufficient <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current" title="Geomagnetically induced current">geomagnetically induced current</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic field</a> to allow for continued communication with the telegraph operator power supplies switched off. The following conversation occurred between two operators of the American Telegraph Line between <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>, on the night of 2 September 1859 and reported in the <i>Boston Traveler</i>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><b>Boston operator (to Portland operator):</b> "Please cut off your battery [power source] entirely for fifteen minutes."<br/>
</p><p><b>Portland operator:</b> "Will do so. It is now disconnected."<br/>
<b>Boston:</b> "Mine is disconnected, and we are working with the auroral current. How do you receive my writing?"<br/>
<b>Portland:</b> "Better than with our batteries on. – Current comes and goes gradually."<br/>
<b>Boston:</b> "My current is very strong at times, and we can work better without the batteries, as the aurora seems to neutralize and augment our batteries alternately, making current too strong at times for our relay magnets. Suppose we work without batteries while we are affected by this trouble."<br/>
<b>Portland:</b> "Very well. Shall I go ahead with business?"<br/>
<b>Boston:</b> "Yes. Go ahead."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The conversation was carried on for around two hours using no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> power at all and working solely with the current induced by the aurora, and it was said that this was the first time on record that more than a word or two was transmitted in such manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADVSR1_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADVSR1-59">[58]</a></sup> Such events led to the general conclusion that
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The effect of the aurorae on the electric telegraph is generally to increase or diminish the electric current generated in working the wires. Sometimes it entirely neutralizes them, so that, in effect, no fluid is discoverable in them. The aurora borealis seems to be composed of a mass of electric matter, resembling in every respect, that generated by the electric galvanic battery. The currents from it change coming on the wires, and then disappear: the mass of the aurora rolls from the horizon to the zenith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span id="Historical_theories.2C_superstition_and_mythology"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_theories,_superstition_and_mythology">Historical theories, superstition and mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Historical theories, superstition and mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An aurora was described by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> in the 4th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca</a> wrote about auroras in the first book of his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturales_Quaestiones" title="Naturales Quaestiones">Naturales Quaestiones</a></i>, classifying them, for instance as <i>pithaei</i> ('barrel-like'); <i>chasmata</i> ('chasm'); <i>pogoniae</i> ('bearded'); <i>cyparissae</i> ('like <a href="/wiki/Cypress" title="Cypress">cypress</a> trees'), and describing their manifold colors. He wrote about whether they were above or below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>, and recalled that under <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a>, an aurora formed above the port city of <a href="/wiki/Ostia_Antica" title="Ostia Antica">Ostia</a> that was so intense and red that a cohort of the army, stationed nearby for fire duty, galloped to the rescue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> It has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> depicted the aurora borealis in his <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i>, when he refers to <i>trabes</i>, <i>chasma</i>, 'falling red flames' and 'daylight in the night'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_australis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Aurora_australis.jpg/220px-Aurora_australis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Aurora_australis.jpg/330px-Aurora_australis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Aurora_australis.jpg/440px-Aurora_australis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_australis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Aboriginal Australians associated auroras (which are mainly low on the horizon and predominantly red) with fire.</div></div></div>
<p>In the traditions of <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal Australians</a>, the Aurora Australis is commonly associated with fire. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunditjmara_people" title="Gunditjmara people">Gunditjmara people</a> of western <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> called auroras <i>puae buae</i> ('ashes'), while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunai_people" title="Gunai people">Gunai people</a> of eastern Victoria perceived auroras as <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">bushfires</a> in the spirit world. The <a href="/wiki/Diyari" title="Diyari">Dieri</a> people of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> say that an auroral display is <i>kootchee</i>, an evil spirit creating a large fire. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Ngarrindjeri" title="Ngarrindjeri">Ngarrindjeri people</a> of South Australia refer to auroras seen over <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a> as the campfires of spirits in the 'Land of the Dead'. Aboriginal people in southwest <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> believe the auroras to be the fires of the <i>Oola Pikka</i>, ghostly spirits who spoke to the people through auroras. Sacred law forbade anyone except male elders from watching or interpreting the messages of ancestors they believed were transmitted through an aurora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Bulfinch%27s_Mythology" title="Bulfinch's Mythology">Bulfinch's Mythology</a></i> relates that in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, the armour of the <a href="/wiki/Valkyrie" title="Valkyrie">Valkyrior</a> "sheds a strange flickering light, which flashes up over the northern skies, making what Men call the 'aurora borealis', or 'Northern Lights' ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> There appears to be no evidence in <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_literature" title="Old Norse literature">Old Norse literature</a> to substantiate this assertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> The first Old Norse account of <i>norðrljós</i> is found in the Norwegian chronicle <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konungs_Skuggsj%C3%A1" title="Konungs Skuggsjá">Konungs Skuggsjá</a></i> from AD 1230. The chronicler has heard about this phenomenon from compatriots returning from <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and he gives three possible explanations: that the ocean was surrounded by vast fires; that the sun flares could reach around the world to its night side; or that <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> could store energy so that they eventually became <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Walter William Bryant wrote in his book <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Kepler" title="q:Kepler"><i>Kepler</i></a> (1920) that <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> "seems to have been something of a <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy" title="Homeopathy">homœopathist</a>, for he recommends <a href="/wiki/Sulfur#History" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> to cure infectious diseases "brought on by the sulphurous vapours of the Aurora Borealis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup></li></ul><p>
In the 1740s [Benjamin Franklin] theorized in his paper <i>Aurora Borealis, Suppositions and Conjectures towards forming an Hypothesis for its Explanation</i> that an aurora was caused by a concentration of electrical charge in the polar regions intensified by the snow and moisture in the air:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>May not then the great quantity of electricity brought into the polar regions by the clouds, which are condens'd there, and fall in snow, which electricity would enter the earth, but cannot penetrate the ice; may it not, I say (as a bottle overcharged) break thro' that low atmosphere and run along in the vacuum over the air towards the equator, diverging as the degrees of longitude enlarge, strongly visible where densest, and becoming less visible as it more diverges; till it finds a passage to the earth in more temperate climates, or is mingled with the upper air?</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Benjamin Franklin</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Observations of the rhythmic movement of compass needles due to the influence of an aurora were confirmed in the Swedish city of <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a> by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Anders Celsius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olof_Hiorter" title="Olof Hiorter">Olof Hiorter</a>. In 1741, Hiorter was able to link large magnetic fluctuations with an aurora being observed overhead. This evidence helped to support their theory that 'magnetic storms' are responsible for such compass fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Church_1911.4.1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2704" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Church_1911.4.1.jpg/220px-Church_1911.4.1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Church_1911.4.1.jpg/330px-Church_1911.4.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Church_1911.4.1.jpg/440px-Church_1911.4.1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Church_1911.4.1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Frederic_Edwin_Church" title="Frederic Edwin Church">Church's</a> 1865 painting <i><a href="/wiki/Aurora_Borealis_(painting)" title="Aurora Borealis (painting)">Aurora Borealis</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>A variety of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> myths surround the spectacle. The European explorer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hearne" title="Samuel Hearne">Samuel Hearne</a> traveled with <a href="/wiki/Chipewyan" title="Chipewyan">Chipewyan Dene</a> in 1771 and recorded their views on the <i>ed-thin</i> ('caribou'). According to Hearne, the Dene people saw the resemblance between an aurora and the sparks produced when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribou" title="Caribou">caribou</a> fur is stroked. They believed that the lights were the spirits of their departed friends dancing in the sky, and when they shone brightly it meant that their deceased friends were very happy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the night after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fredericksburg" title="Battle of Fredericksburg">Battle of Fredericksburg</a>, an aurora was seen from the battlefield. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confederate_Army" title="Confederate Army">Confederate Army</a> took this as a sign that God was on their side, as the lights were rarely seen so far south. The painting <i><a href="/wiki/Aurora_Borealis_(painting)" title="Aurora Borealis (painting)">Aurora Borealis</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Edwin_Church" title="Frederic Edwin Church">Frederic Edwin Church</a> is widely interpreted to represent the conflict of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>A mid 19th-century British source says auroras were a rare occurrence before the 18th-century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup> It quotes <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">Halley</a> as saying that before the aurora of 1716, no such phenomenon had been recorded for more than 80 years, and none of any consequence since 1574. It says no appearance is recorded in the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences"><i>Transactions of the French Academy of Sciences</i></a> between 1666 and 1716. And that one aurora recorded in <i>Berlin Miscellany</i> for 1797 was called a very rare event. One observed in 1723 at <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> was stated to be the first ever seen there. <a href="/wiki/Anders_Celsius" title="Anders Celsius">Celsius</a> (1733) states the oldest residents of <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a> thought the phenomenon a great rarity before 1716. The period between approximately 1645 to 1715 corresponds to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maunder_minimum" title="Maunder minimum">Maunder minimum</a> in sunspot activity.
</p><p>It was the Norwegian scientist <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a> who, in the early 1900s, laid the foundation for our current understanding of geomagnetism and polar auroras.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-terrestrial_auroras">Non-terrestrial auroras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aurora&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Non-terrestrial auroras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1617" data-file-width="2842" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg/220px-Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg/330px-Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg/440px-Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter.Aurora.HST.UV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> aurora; the far left bright spot connects magnetically to <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>; the spots at the bottom of the image lead to <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="516" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg/220px-Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg/330px-Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg/440px-Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saturns_Northern_Aurora_still.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An aurora high above the northern part of Saturn; image taken by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_spacecraft" title="Cassini spacecraft">Cassini spacecraft</a>. <a href="/wiki/File:Saturns_Northern_Aurora_in_Motion.gif" title="File:Saturns Northern Aurora in Motion.gif">A movie</a> shows images from 81 hours of observations of Saturn's aurora</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Jupiter#Aurorae" title="Magnetosphere of Jupiter">Magnetosphere of Jupiter § Aurorae</a></div>
<p>Both <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> have magnetic fields that are stronger than Earth's (Jupiter's equatorial field strength is 4.3 gauss, compared to 0.3 gauss for Earth), and both have extensive radiation belts. Auroras have been observed on both gas planets, most clearly using the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, as well as on <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-esa-74">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The auroras on the gas giants seem, like Earth's, to be powered by the solar wind. In addition, however, Jupiter's moons, especially <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, are powerful sources of auroras on Jupiter. These arise from electric currents along field lines ("field aligned currents"), generated by a dynamo mechanism due to the relative motion between the rotating planet and the moving moon. Io, which has active <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> and an ionosphere, is a particularly strong source, and its currents also generate radio emissions, which have been studied since 1955. Using the Hubble Space Telescope, auroras over Io, Europa and Ganymede have all been observed.
</p><p>Auroras have also been observed on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. Venus has no magnetic field and so Venusian auroras appear as bright and diffuse patches of varying shape and intensity, sometimes distributed over the full disc of the planet. A Venusian aurora originates when electrons from the solar wind collide with the night-side atmosphere.
</p><p>An aurora was detected on Mars,on 14 August 2004, by the SPICAM instrument aboard <a href="/wiki/Mars_Express" title="Mars Express">Mars Express</a>. The aurora was located at <a href="/wiki/Terra_Cimmeria" title="Terra Cimmeria">Terra Cimmeria</a>, in the region of 177° East, 52° South. The total size of the emission region was about 30 km across, and possibly about 8 km high. By analyzing a map of crustal magnetic anomalies compiled with data from <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">Mars Global Surveyor</a>, scientists observed that the region of the emissions corresponded to an area where the strongest magnetic field is localized. This correlation indicated that the origin of the light emission was a flux of electrons moving along the crust magnetic lines and exciting the upper atmosphere of Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-esa-74">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first ever <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-solar_object" title="Extra-solar object">extra-solar</a> auroras were discovered in July 2015 over the <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> star <a href="/wiki/LSR_J1835%2B3259" title="LSR J1835+3259">LSR J1835+3259</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup> The mainly red aurora was found to be a million times brighter than the Northern Lights, a result of the charged particles interacting with hydrogen in the atmosphere. It has been speculated that stellar winds may be stripping off material from the surface of the brown dwarf to produce its own electrons. Another possible explanation for the auroras is that an as-yet-undetected body around the dwarf star is throwing off material, as is the case with Jupiter and its moon Io.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
