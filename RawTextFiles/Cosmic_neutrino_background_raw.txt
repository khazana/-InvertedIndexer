<p>The <b>cosmic neutrino background</b> (<b>CNB</b>, <b>CνB</b><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_nu"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#endnote_nu">[1]</a></sup>) is the universe's background particle radiation composed of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>. They are sometimes known as <b>relic neutrinos</b>.
</p><p>The CνB is a relic of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_bang" title="Big bang">big bang</a>; while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> (CMB) dates from when the universe was 379,000 years old, the <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_decoupling" title="Neutrino decoupling">CνB decoupled</a> (separated) from matter when the universe was just one second old. It is estimated that today, the CνB has a temperature of roughly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000195000000000000♠</span>1.95 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span>.
</p><p>As neutrinos rarely interact with matter, these neutrinos still exist today. They have a very low energy, around 10<sup>−4</sup> to 10<sup>−6</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">eV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_neutrino_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_neutrino-1">[1]</a></sup> Even high energy neutrinos are <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_detector" title="Neutrino detector">notoriously difficult to detect</a>, and the CνB has energies around 10<sup>−10</sup> times smaller, so the CνB may not be directly observed in detail for many years, if at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_neutrino_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_neutrino-1">[1]</a></sup> However, Big Bang cosmology makes many predictions about the CνB, and there is very strong indirect evidence that the CνB exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_neutrino_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_neutrino-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Derivation_of_the_C.CE.BDB_temperature"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivation_of_the_CνB_temperature">Derivation of the CνB temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Derivation of the CνB temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Given the temperature of the CMB, the temperature of the CνB can be estimated. Before <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_decoupling" title="Neutrino decoupling">neutrinos decoupled</a> from the rest of matter, the universe primarily consisted of neutrinos, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, all in <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">thermal equilibrium</a> with each other. Once the temperature dropped to approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6987400544121750000♠</span>2.5 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a></span>, the neutrinos decoupled from the rest of matter. Despite this decoupling, neutrinos and photons remained at the same temperature as the universe expanded. However, when the temperature dropped below the mass of the electron, most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron-positron_annihilation" title="Electron-positron annihilation">electrons and positrons annihilated</a>, transferring their heat and entropy to photons, and thus increasing the temperature of the photons. So the ratio of the temperature of the photons before and after the electron-positron annihilation is the same as the ratio of the temperature of the neutrinos and the photons today. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis">Big Bang nucleosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Big Bang nucleosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its effect on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expansion rate</a> of the universe during <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">Big Bang nucleosynthesis</a> (BBN), the theoretical expectations for the primordial abundances of light elements depend on <i>N<sub>ν</sub></i>. Astrophysical measurements of the primordial <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>He<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></a></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>D<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></a></span> abundances lead to a value of <i>N<sub>ν</sub></i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000314000000000000♠</span>3.14<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">+0.70<br/>−0.65</span></span></span> at 68% <a href="/wiki/Confidence_interval" title="Confidence interval">c.l.</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in very good agreement with the Standard Model expectation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CMB_anisotropies_and_structure_formation">CMB anisotropies and structure formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: CMB anisotropies and structure formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The presence of the CνB affects the evolution of CMB anisotropies as well as the growth of matter perturbations in two ways: due to its contribution to the radiation density of the universe (which determines for instance the time of matter-radiation equality), and due to the neutrinos' anisotropic stress which dampens the acoustic oscillations of the spectra. Additionally, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-streaming" title="Free-streaming">free-streaming</a> massive neutrinos suppress the growth of structure on small scales. The <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">WMAP</a> spacecraft's five-year data combined with type Ia <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> data and information about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation" title="Baryon acoustic oscillation">baryon acoustic oscillation</a> scale yielded <i>N<sub>ν</sub></i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000434000000000000♠</span>4.34<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">+0.88<br/>−0.86</span></span></span> at 68% c.l.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> providing an independent confirmation of the BBN constraints. The <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck spacecraft</a> collaboration has published the tightest bound to date on the effective number of neutrino species, at <i>N<sub>ν</sub></i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000315000000000000♠</span>3.15<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.23</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Indirect_evidence_from_phase_changes_to_the_Cosmic_Microwave_Background_.28CMB.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_evidence_from_phase_changes_to_the_Cosmic_Microwave_Background_(CMB)">Indirect evidence from phase changes to the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indirect evidence from phase changes to the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Big Bang cosmology makes many predictions about the CνB, and there is very strong indirect evidence that the cosmic neutrino background exists, both from <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">Big Bang nucleosynthesis</a> predictions of the helium abundance, and from anisotropies in the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a>. One of these predictions is that neutrinos will have left a subtle imprint on the cosmic microwave background (CMB). It is well known that the CMB has irregularities. Some of the CMB fluctuations were roughly regularly spaced, because of the effect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation" title="Baryon acoustic oscillation">baryon acoustic oscillation</a>. In theory, the decoupled neutrinos should have had a very slight effect on the <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> of the various CMB fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_neutrino_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_neutrino-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, it was reported that such shifts had been detected in the CMB. Moreover the fluctuations corresponded to neutrinos of almost exactly the temperature predicted by Big Bang theory (<span class="nowrap">1.96 ± 0.02K</span> compared to a prediction of 1.95K), and exactly three types of neutrino, the same number of neutrino flavours currently predicted by the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes_neutrino_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_neutrino-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Prospects_for_the_direct_detection_of_the_C.CE.BDB"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prospects_for_the_direct_detection_of_the_CνB">Prospects for the direct detection of the CνB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prospects for the direct detection of the CνB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
