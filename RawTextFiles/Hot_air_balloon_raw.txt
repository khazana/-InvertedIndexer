<p>A <b>hot air balloon</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lighter-than-air" title="Lighter-than-air">lighter-than-air</a> aircraft consisting of a bag, called an envelope, which contains heated air. Suspended beneath is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondola_(balloon)" title="Gondola (balloon)">gondola</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wicker" title="Wicker">wicker basket</a> (in some long-distance or high-altitude balloons, a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/capsule" title="wikt:capsule">capsule</a>), which carries passengers and a source of heat, in most cases an open flame caused by burning liquid propane. The heated air inside the envelope makes it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoyant" title="Buoyant">buoyant</a> since it has a <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">lower density</a> than the colder air outside the envelope. As with all <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, hot air balloons cannot fly beyond the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Gas_balloon" title="Gas balloon">gas balloons</a>, the envelope does not have to be sealed at the bottom, since the air near the bottom of the envelope is at the same pressure as the surrounding air. In modern sport balloons the envelope is generally made from <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> fabric and the inlet of the balloon (closest to the burner flame) is made from a fire resistant material such as <a href="/wiki/Nomex" title="Nomex">Nomex</a>. Modern balloons have been made in all kinds of shapes, such as rocket ships and the shapes of various commercial products, though the traditional shape is used for most non-commercial, and many commercial, applications.
</p><p>The hot air balloon is the first successful human-carrying <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> technology. The first untethered manned hot air balloon flight was performed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Pil%C3%A2tre_de_Rozier" title="Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier">Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Laurent_d%27Arlandes" title="François Laurent d'Arlandes">François Laurent d'Arlandes</a> on November 21, 1783, in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in a balloon created by the <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier brothers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first hot-air balloon flown in the Americas was launched from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walnut_Street_Jail" title="Walnut Street Jail">Walnut Street Jail</a> in Philadelphia on January 9, 1793 by the French aeronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Pierre_Blanchard" title="Jean Pierre Blanchard">Jean Pierre Blanchard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-3">[3]</a></sup> Hot air balloons that can be propelled through the air rather than simply drifting with the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> are known as <a href="/wiki/Thermal_airship" title="Thermal airship">thermal airships</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ballooning" title="History of ballooning">History of ballooning</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premodern_and_unmanned_balloons">Premodern and unmanned balloons</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sky_lantern" title="Sky lantern">Sky lantern</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SkyLanternRichy01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/SkyLanternRichy01.jpg/170px-SkyLanternRichy01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/SkyLanternRichy01.jpg/255px-SkyLanternRichy01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/SkyLanternRichy01.jpg/340px-SkyLanternRichy01.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SkyLanternRichy01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Sky_lantern" title="Sky lantern">Chinese lantern</a> (sky lantern), the oldest type of hot air balloon.</div></div></div>
<p>Early unmanned hot air balloons were used in China. <a href="/wiki/Zhuge_Liang" title="Zhuge Liang">Zhuge Liang</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Shu_Han" title="Shu Han">Shu Han</a> kingdom, during the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">Three Kingdoms</a> era (220–280 AD), used airborne lanterns for military signaling. These lanterns are known as <a href="/wiki/Sky_lantern" title="Sky lantern">Chinese lanterns</a> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">孔明灯</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">孔明燈</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 18th century the Portuguese Jesuit priest <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_de_Gusm%C3%A3o" title="Bartolomeu de Gusmão">Bartolomeu de Gusmão</a> envisioned an aerial apparatus called <i>Passarola</i> which was the predecessor of the hot air balloon. The purpose of <i>Passarola</i> was to serve as air vessel in order to facilitate communication and as a strategical device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1709 <a href="/wiki/John_V_of_Portugal" title="John V of Portugal">John V of Portugal</a> decided to fund Bartolomeu de Gusmão's project following a petition made by the jesuit priest <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and an unmanned demonstration was performed at <a href="/wiki/Casa_da_%C3%8Dndia" title="Casa da Índia">Casa da India</a> in presence of John V, the queen <a href="/wiki/Maria_Anna_of_Austria" title="Maria Anna of Austria">Maria Anna of Austria</a>, having as witnesses the Italian cardinal <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_XIII" title="Pope Innocent XIII">Michelangelo Conti</a>, two members of the Portuguese Royal Academy of History, one Portuguese diplomat and one chronicler. This event would bring some European attention to this event and this project. A later article dated on October 20, 1786 by the London <i>Daily Universal Register</i> would state that the inventor was able to raise himself by the use of his prototype. Also in 1709, the Portuguese jesuit wrote <i>Manifesto summário para os que ignoram poderse navegar pelo elemento do ar</i> (<i>Short Manifesto for those who are unaware that is possible to sail through the element air</i>). Persecuted by the Inquisition, he was prevented from continuing his research; however, he left designs for a manned air vessel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_manned_flight">First manned flight</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG/170px-Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG/255px-Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG/340px-Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montgolfier_Balloon.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A model of the Montgolfier brothers' balloon at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Science_Museum" title="London Science Museum">London Science Museum</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The French brothers Joseph-Michel and Jacques-Étienne <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier</a> developed a hot air balloon in <a href="/wiki/Annonay" title="Annonay">Annonay</a>, Ardeche, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and demonstrated it publicly on September 19, 1783, making an unmanned flight lasting 10 minutes. After experimenting with unmanned balloons and flights with animals, the first balloon flight with humans aboard, a tethered flight, performed on or around October 15, 1783, by Jean-Francois Pilatre de Rozier who made at least one tethered flight from the yard of the Reveillon workshop in the <a href="/wiki/Faubourg_Saint-Antoine" title="Faubourg Saint-Antoine">Faubourg Saint-Antoine</a>. Later that same day, Pilatre de Rozier became the second human to ascend into the air, reaching an altitude of 26 m (85 ft), the length of the tether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  The first free flight with human passengers was made a few weeks later, on November 21, 1783.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">King Louis XVI</a> had originally decreed that condemned criminals would be the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilots</a>, but de Rozier, along with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Laurent_d%27Arlandes" title="François Laurent d'Arlandes">Marquis François d'Arlandes</a>, petitioned successfully for the honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/History_of_military_ballooning" title="History of military ballooning">first military use</a> of a hot air balloon happened in 1794 during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fleurus_(1794)" title="Battle of Fleurus (1794)">battle of Fleurus</a>, when the French used the balloon <i>l'Entreprenant</i> for observation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cloudhoppers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cloudhoppers.jpg/220px-Cloudhoppers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cloudhoppers.jpg/330px-Cloudhoppers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Cloudhoppers.jpg/440px-Cloudhoppers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cloudhoppers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloudhopper" title="Cloudhopper">Hopper balloons</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG/220px-Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG/330px-Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG/440px-Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bristol_Balloon_Fiesta_2009-20.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bristol_International_Balloon_Fiesta" title="Bristol International Balloon Fiesta">Bristol International Balloon Fiesta</a></div></div></div>
<p>Modern hot air balloons, with an onboard heat source, were developed by <a href="/wiki/Ed_Yost" title="Ed Yost">Ed Yost</a>, beginning during the 1950s; his work resulted in his first successful flight, on October 22, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The first modern hot air balloon to be made in the United Kingdom (UK) was the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Belle" title="Bristol Belle">Bristol Belle</a>, built in 1967. Presently, hot air balloons are used primarily for recreation. Hot air balloons are able to fly to extremely high altitudes. On November 26, 2005 <a href="/wiki/Vijaypat_Singhania" title="Vijaypat Singhania">Vijaypat Singhania</a> set the world altitude record for highest hot air balloon flight, reaching 21,027 m (68,986 ft). He took off from downtown <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and landed 240 km (150 mi) south in Panchale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The previous record of 19,811 m (64,997 ft) had been set by <a href="/wiki/Per_Lindstrand" title="Per Lindstrand">Per Lindstrand</a> on June 6, 1988, in <a href="/wiki/Plano,_Texas" title="Plano, Texas">Plano, Texas</a>.
</p><p>On January 15, 1991, the 'Virgin Pacific Flyer' balloon completed the longest flight in a hot air balloon when <a href="/wiki/Per_Lindstrand" title="Per Lindstrand">Per Lindstrand</a> (born in Sweden, but resident in the UK) and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Branson" title="Richard Branson">Richard Branson</a> of the UK flew 7,671.91 km (4,767.10 mi) from Japan to Northern Canada. With a volume of 74,000 cubic meters (2.6 million cubic feet), the balloon envelope was the largest ever built for a hot air craft. Designed to fly in the trans-oceanic <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet streams</a>, the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_Flyer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pacific Flyer (page does not exist)">Pacific Flyer</a></i> recorded the fastest ground speed for a manned balloon at 245 mph (394 km/h). The longest duration record was set by Swiss psychiatrist <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Piccard" title="Bertrand Piccard">Bertrand Piccard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Piccard" title="Auguste Piccard">Auguste Piccard</a>'s grandson; and Briton Brian Jones, flying in the Breitling Orbiter 3. It was the first nonstop trip around the world by balloon. The balloon left Château-d'Oex, Switzerland, on March 1, 1999, and landed at 1:02 a.m. on March 21 in the Egyptian desert 300 miles (480 km) south of Cairo. The two men exceeded distance, endurance, and time records, traveling 19 days, 21 hours, and 55 minutes. <a href="/wiki/Steve_Fossett" title="Steve Fossett">Steve Fossett</a>, flying solo, exceeded the record for briefest time traveling around the world on 3 July 2002 on his sixth attempt,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> in 320 h 33 min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-record-ballooning_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-record-ballooning-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fedor_Konyukhov#Ballooning" title="Fedor Konyukhov">Fedor Konyukhov</a> flew solo round the world on his first attempt in a hybrid hot-air/helium balloon from 11 to 23 July 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> for a round-the world time of 272h 11m, as of  17 September 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hot_air_balloon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> awaiting official confirmation as the new record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-record-ballooning_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-record-ballooning-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span></h2>
<p>A hot air balloon for manned flight uses a single-layered, fabric gas bag (lifting "envelope"), with an opening at the bottom called the mouth or throat. Attached to the envelope is a basket, or gondola, for carrying the passengers. Mounted above the basket and centered in the mouth is the "burner", which injects a flame into the envelope, heating the air within. The heater or burner is fueled by <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied gas</a> stored in pressure vessels, similar to high pressure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forklift_truck" title="Forklift truck">forklift</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">cylinders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Envelope">Envelope</span></h3>
<p>Modern hot air balloons are usually made of materials such as <a href="/wiki/Ripstop#Ripstop_nylon" title="Ripstop">ripstop nylon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">dacron</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eballoon_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-eballoon-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1347" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg/220px-Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg/330px-Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg/440px-Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yellow.balloon.inflation.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A hot air balloon is inflated partially with cold air from a gasoline-powered fan, before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propane_burner" title="Propane burner">propane burners</a> are used for final inflation.</div></div></div>
<p>During the manufacturing process, the material is cut into panels and sewn together, along with structural <a href="/wiki/Webbing" title="Webbing">load tapes</a> that carry the weight of the gondola or basket. The individual sections, which extend from the throat to the crown (top) of the envelope, are known as <a href="/wiki/Gore_(segment)" title="Gore (segment)">gores</a> or gore sections. Envelopes can have as few as 4 gores or as many as 24 or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Head_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Head-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Envelopes often have a crown ring at their very top. This is a hoop of smooth metal, usually aluminium, and approximately 1 ft (0.30 m) in diameter. Vertical load tapes from the envelope are attached to the crown ring.
</p><p>At the bottom of the envelope the vertical load tapes are sewn into loops that are connected to cables (one cable per load tape). These cables, often referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_wires" title="Flying wires">flying wires</a>, are connected to the basket by <a href="/wiki/Carabiner" title="Carabiner">carabiners</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seams">Seams</span></h4>
<p>The most common technique for sewing panels together is called the <i>French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glossary_of_textile_terminology" title="Glossary of textile terminology">felled</a></i>, <i>French fell</i>, or <i>double lap</i> seam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The two pieces of fabric are folded over on each other at their common edge, possibly with a load tape as well, and sewn together with two rows of parallel stitching. Other methods include a <i>flat lap</i> seam, in which the two pieces of fabric are held together simply with two rows of parallel stitching, and a <i>zigzag</i>, where parallel zigzag stitching holds a double lap of fabric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coatings">Coatings</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg/220px-Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg/330px-Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg/440px-Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ballooning_Away_in_Maasai_Mara.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hot air balloon safari in <a href="/wiki/Maasai_Mara" title="Maasai Mara">Maasai Mara</a>.</div></div></div><p>The fabric (or at least part of it, the top 1/3 for example) may be coated with a sealer, such as <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>, to make it impermeable to air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron-27">[27]</a></sup> It is often the degradation of this coating and the corresponding loss of impermeability that ends the effective life of an envelope, not weakening of the fabric itself. Heat, moisture, and mechanical wear-and-tear during set-up and pack-up are the primary causes of degradation. Once an envelope becomes too <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porous</a> to fly, it may be retired and discarded or perhaps used as a 'rag bag': cold inflated and opened for children to run through. Products for recoating the fabric are becoming available commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MidAtlantic_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-MidAtlantic-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sizes_and_capacity">Sizes and capacity</span></h4>
<p>A range of envelope sizes is available. The smallest, one-person, basket-less balloons (called "<a href="/wiki/Hopper_balloon" title="Hopper balloon">Hoppers</a>" or "Cloudhoppers") have as little as  600 m<sup>3</sup> (21,000 cu ft) of envelope volume;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LindstrandUK_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-LindstrandUK-29">[29]</a></sup> for a perfect sphere the radius would be around 5 m (16 ft). At the other end of the scale, balloons used by commercial sightseeing operations may be able to carry well over two dozen people, with envelope volumes of up to 17,000 m<sup>3</sup> (600,000 cu ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LindstrandUK_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-LindstrandUK-29">[29]</a></sup> The most-used size is about 2,800 m<sup>3</sup> (99,000 cu ft), and can carry 3 to 5 people.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vents">Vents</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="917" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg/220px-Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg/330px-Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg/440px-Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hot_air_balloon_interior.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The parachute vent at the top of an envelope, as seen from below through the mouth.</div></div></div>
<p>The top of the balloon usually has a vent of some sort, enabling the pilot to release hot air to slow an ascent, start a descent, or increase the rate of descent, usually for landing. Some hot air balloons have <i>turning vents</i>, which are side vents that, when opened, cause the balloon to rotate. Such vents are particularly useful for balloons with rectangular baskets, to facilitate aligning the wider side of the basket for landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common type of top vent is a disk-shaped flap of fabric called a <i>parachute vent</i>, invented by Tracy Barnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The fabric is connected around its edge to a set of "vent lines" that converge in the center. (The arrangement of fabric and lines roughly resembles a <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a>—thus the name.) These "vent lines" are themselves connected to a control line that runs to the basket. A parachute vent is opened by pulling on the control line. Once the control line is released, the pressure of the remaining hot air pushes the vent fabric back into place. A parachute vent can be opened briefly while in flight to initiate a rapid descent. (Slower descents are initiated by allowing the air in the balloon to cool naturally.) The vent is pulled open completely to collapse the balloon after landing.
</p><p>An older, and presently less commonly used, style of top vent is called a "<a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">Velcro</a>-style" vent. This too is a disk of fabric at the top of the balloon. However, rather than having a set of "vent lines" that can repeatedly open and close the vent, the vent is secured by "hook and loop" fasteners (such as Velcro) and is only opened at the end of the flight. Balloons equipped with a Velcro-style vent typically have a second "maneuvering vent" built into the side (as opposed to the top) of the balloon.  Another common type of top design is the "Smart Vent," which, rather than lowering a fabric disc into the envelope as in the "parachute" type, gathers the fabric together in the center of the opening.  This system can theoretically be used for in-flight maneuvering, but is more commonly used only as a rapid-deflation device for use after landing, of particular value in high winds. Other designs, such as the "pop top" and "MultiVent" systems, have also attempted to address the need for rapid deflation on landing, but the parachute top remains popular as an all-around maneuvering and deflation system.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">Shape</span></h4>
<p>Besides special shapes, possibly for marketing purposes, there are several variations on the traditional "inverted tear drop" shape. The simplest, often used by home builders, is a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">hemisphere</a> on top of a truncated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(geometry)" title="Cone (geometry)">cone</a>. More-sophisticated designs attempt to minimize the circumferential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> on the fabric, with different degrees of success depending on whether they take fabric weight and varying air density into account. This shape may be referred to as "natural".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Finally, some specialized balloons are designed to minimize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a> (in the vertical direction) to improve flight performance in competitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basket">Basket</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="479" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg/220px-Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg/330px-Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg/440px-Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hot_Air_Balloon_Basket_in_Flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hot air balloon basket in flight</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1355" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg/220px-Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg/330px-Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg/440px-Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wicker.balloon.basket.bath.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Wicker" title="Wicker">wicker basket</a> capable of holding 16 passengers.</div></div></div>
<p>Baskets are commonly made of <a href="/wiki/Basket_weaving" title="Basket weaving">woven</a> <a href="/wiki/Wicker" title="Wicker">wicker</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rattan" title="Rattan">rattan</a>. These materials have proven to be sufficiently light, strong, and durable for balloon flight. Such baskets are usually rectangular or triangular in shape. They vary in size from just big enough for two people to large enough to carry thirty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Larger baskets often have internal partitions for structural bracing and to compartmentalize the passengers. Small holes may be woven into the side of the basket to act as foot holds for passengers climbing in or out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Baskets may also be made of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, especially a collapsible aluminium frame with a fabric skin, to reduce weight or increase portability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> These may be used by pilots without a ground crew or who are attempting to set altitude, duration, or distance records. Other specialty baskets include the fully enclosed gondolas used for around-the-world attempts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and baskets that consist of little more than a seat for the pilot and perhaps one passenger.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burner">Burner</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balloon_fuel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3504" data-file-width="2336" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balloon_fuel.jpg/220px-Balloon_fuel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balloon_fuel.jpg/330px-Balloon_fuel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Balloon_fuel.jpg/440px-Balloon_fuel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balloon_fuel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A burner directing a flame into the envelope.</div></div></div>
<p>The burner unit gasifies liquid <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> mixes it with air, ignites the mixture, and directs the flame and exhaust into the mouth of the envelope. Burners vary in power output; each will generally produce 2 to 3 MW of heat (7 to 10 million <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTUs</a> per hour), with double, triple, or quadruple burner configurations installed where more power is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kubicek_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kubicek-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RafficaOy_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-RafficaOy-40">[40]</a></sup> The pilot actuates a burner by opening a propane valve, known as a <b>blast valve</b>. The valve may be spring-loaded so that it closes automatically, or it may stay open until closed by the pilot. The burner has a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_light" title="Pilot light">pilot light</a> to ignite the propane and air mixture. The pilot light may be lit by the pilot with an external device, such as a <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> striker or a <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">lighter</a>, or with a built-in <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezo electric</a> spark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LindstrandFuel_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-LindstrandFuel-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Where more than one burner is present, the pilot can use one or more at a time depending on the desired heat output. Each burner is characterized by a metal coil of propane tubing the flame shoots through to preheat the incoming liquid propane. The burner unit may be suspended from the mouth of the envelope, or supported rigidly over the basket. The burner unit may be mounted on a <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">gimbal</a> to enable the pilot to aim the flame and avoid overheating the envelope fabric. A burner may have a secondary propane valve that releases propane more slowly and thereby generates a different sound. This is called a <b>whisper burner</b> and is used for flight over livestock to lessen the chance of spooking them. It also generates a more yellow flame and is used for night glows because it lights up the inside of the envelope better than the primary valve.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_tanks">Fuel tanks</span></h3>
<p>Propane fuel tanks are usually cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessels</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> with a valve at one end to feed the burner and to refuel. They may have a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gauge" title="Fuel gauge">fuel gauge</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement" title="Pressure measurement">pressure gauge</a>. Common tank sizes are 10 (38), 15 (57), and 20 (76) US <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">liters</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron-27">[27]</a></sup> They may be intended for upright or horizontal use, and may be mounted inside or outside the basket.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg/220px-Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg/330px-Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg/440px-Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hot_air_balloon_fuel_tanks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">Stainless steel</a> <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tanks</a>, wrapped in red insulating covers, mounted vertically, and with fuel gauges, during refueling.</div></div></div>
<p>The pressure necessary to force the fuel through the line to the burner may be supplied by the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> of the propane itself, if warm enough, or by the introduction of an inert gas such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LindstrandFuel_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-LindstrandFuel-41">[41]</a></sup> Tanks may be preheated with electrical <a href="/wiki/Trace_heating" title="Trace heating">heat tapes</a> to produce sufficient vapor pressure for cold weather flying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BalloonLife_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-BalloonLife-42">[42]</a></sup> Warmed tanks will usually also be wrapped in an insulating blanket to preserve heat during the setup and flight.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumentation">Instrumentation</span></h3>
<p>A balloon may be outfitted with a variety of instruments to aid the pilot. These commonly include an <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Variometer" title="Variometer">rate of climb (vertical speed) indicator</a> known as a variometer, envelope (air) temperature, and ambient (air) temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindstrand_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-lindstrand-43">[43]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> receiver can be useful to indicate ground speed (traditional aircraft air speed indicators would be useless) and direction.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_of_operation">Theory of operation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Generating_lift">Generating lift</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Infarot_9.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Infarot_9.jpg/220px-Infarot_9.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Infarot_9.jpg/330px-Infarot_9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Infarot_9.jpg/440px-Infarot_9.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Infarot_9.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thermal image showing temperature variation in a hot air balloon.</div></div></div>
<p>Increasing the air temperature inside the envelope makes it less dense than the surrounding (ambient) air. The balloon floats because of the buoyant force exerted on it. This force is the same force that acts on objects when they are in water and is described by <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy#Archimedes'_principle" title="Buoyancy">Archimedes' principle</a>. The amount of lift (or <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>) provided by a hot air balloon depends primarily upon the difference between the temperature of the air inside the envelope and the temperature of the air outside the envelope. For most envelopes made of nylon fabric, the maximum internal temperature is limited to approximately 120 °C (250 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>It should be noted that the melting point of nylon is significantly greater than this maximum <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a> — about 230 °C (450 °F) — but higher temperatures cause the strength of the nylon fabric to degrade more quickly over time. With a maximum operating temperature of 120 °C (250 °F), balloon envelopes can generally be flown for between 400 and 500 hours before the fabric needs to be replaced. Many balloon pilots operate their envelopes at temperatures significantly less than the maximum to extend envelope fabric life.
</p><p>The lift generated by 100,000 ft³ (2831.7 m³) of dry air heated to various temperatures may be calculated as follows:

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Density_of_air" title="Density of air">density of air</a> at 20 °C, 68 °F is about 1.2 kg/m³. The total lift for a balloon of 100,000 ft³ heated to  (99 °C, 210 °F) would be 1595 lb, 723.5 kg. This is just enough to generate neutral buoyancy for the total system mass (not including the heated air trapped in the envelope, of course) stated in the previous section. Liftoff would require a slightly greater temperature, depending on the desired rate of climb. In reality, the air contained in the envelope is not all the same temperature, as the accompanying thermal image shows, and so these calculations are based on averages.
</p><p>For typical atmospheric conditions (20 °C, 68 °F), a hot air balloon heated to (99 °C, 210 °F) requires about 3.91 m³ of envelope volume to lift 1 kilogram (62.5 ft³/lb). The precise amount of lift provided depends not only upon the internal temperature mentioned above, but the external temperature, altitude above sea level, and humidity of the air surrounding. On a warm day, a balloon cannot lift as much as on a cool day, because the temperature required for launch will exceed the maximum sustainable for nylon envelope fabric. Also, in the lower atmosphere, the lift provided by a hot air balloon decreases about 3% for each 1,000 meters (1% per 1,000 ft) of altitude gained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Montgolfier">Montgolfier</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2047" data-file-width="1539" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg/220px-Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg/330px-Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg/440px-Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Virgin_Hot_Air_Balloon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Balloon_Flights" title="Virgin Balloon Flights">Virgin hot air balloon</a> flying over <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Standard hot air balloons are known as <b>Montgolfier balloons</b> and rely solely on the buoyancy of hot air provided by the burner and contained by the envelope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> This style of balloon was developed by the <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier brothers</a>, and had its first public demonstration on 4 June 1783 with an unmanned flight lasting 10 minutes, followed later that year with manned flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid">Hybrid</span></h4>
<p>The 1785 <a href="/wiki/Rozi%C3%A8re_balloon" title="Rozière balloon">Rozière balloon</a>, a type of <b>hybrid balloon</b>, named after its creator, Jean-François Pilâtre de Rozier, has a separate cell for a lighter than air gas (typically <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>,) as well as a cone below for hot air (as is used in a hot air balloon) to heat the helium at night. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> gas was used in the very early stages of development but was quickly abandoned due to the obvious danger of introducing an open flame near the gas.  All modern Roziere balloons now use helium as a <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lifting gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amsbaugh_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amsbaugh-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="858" data-file-width="1150" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg/220px-Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg/330px-Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg/440px-Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solarballon_Hot_18_Wikipedia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 4 meters high solar balloon floating over a meadow.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solar_balloon" title="Solar balloon">Solar balloons</a> are hot air balloons that use just <a href="/wiki/Solar_power#Solar_thermal" title="Solar power">solar energy</a> captured by a dark envelope to heat the air inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steering">Steering</span></h3>
<p>Hot air balloon can be steered to a limited degree by changing the altitude of the flight. Wind in the northern hemisphere tends to turn right due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">coriolis effect</a> as the altitude increases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_equipment">Safety equipment</span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hot_air_balloon&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2009</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>To help ensure the safety of pilot and passengers, a hot air balloon may carry several pieces of safety equipment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_basket">In the basket</span></h3>
<p>To relight the burner if the pilot light goes out and the optional piezo ignition fails, the pilot should have ready access to a means of backup ignition, such as a flint spark lighter. Many systems, especially those that carry passengers, have completely duplicate fuel and burner systems: two fuel tanks, connected to two separate hoses, which feed two distinct burners. This enables a safe landing in the case of a blockage somewhere in one system or if a system must be disabled because of a fuel leak.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher" title="Fire extinguisher">fire extinguisher</a> suitable for extinguishing propane fires is useful. Most balloons carry a 1 or 2  kg <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_classes" title="Fire classes">AB:E</a> type fire extinguisher.
</p><p>A handling or drop line is mandatory safety equipment in many countries. This is a rope or webbing of 20–30 meters in length attached to the balloon basket with a quick release connection at one end. In very calm winds the balloon pilot can throw the handling line from the balloon so that the ground crew can guide the balloon safely away from obstructions on the ground.
</p><p>For commercial passenger balloons, a pilot restraint harness is mandatory in some countries. This consists of a hip belt and a webbing line that together allow for some movement while preventing the pilot from being ejected from the basket during a hard landing.
</p><p>Further safety equipment may include a first-aid kit, a fire blanket and a strong rescue knife.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_occupants">On the occupants</span></h3>
<p>At a minimum, the pilot should wear leather or flame-retardant fiber (such as <a href="/wiki/Nomex" title="Nomex">nomex</a>) gloves, so that they may shut off a gas valve in the case of a leak, even if there is a flame present; quick action in this regard can turn a potential catastrophe into a mere inconvenience. The pilot should additionally wear flame-resistant clothing covering their arms and legs; either natural fiber, such as <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, or engineered flame-retardant fiber, such as nomex, is acceptable in this capacity.  Most engineered fibers (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a>, which is also safe to wear) are <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastic</a>; many are also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>.  This makes such fabrics very much unsuitable to wear near high temperatures, since non-flame-retardant thermoplastics will melt onto the wearer, and most hydrocarbons, whether fibrous or not, are suitable to use as fuels.  Natural fiber will singe rather than melt or burn readily, and flame-retardant fiber generally has a very high melting point and is intrinsically non-flammable.  Many pilots also advise their passengers to wear similar protective clothing that covers their arms and legs, as well as strong shoes or boots that offer good ankle support. Finally, some balloon systems, especially those that hang the burner from the envelope instead of supporting it rigidly from the basket, require the use of helmets by the pilot and passengers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_the_ground_crew">On the ground crew</span></h3>
<p>The ground crew should wear gloves whenever there is a possibility of handling ropes or lines. The mass and exposed surface to air movement of a medium-sized balloon is sufficient to cause rope friction burns to the hands of anyone trying to stop or prevent movement. The ground crew should also wear sturdy shoes and at least long pants in case of the need to access a landing or landed balloon in rough or overgrown terrain.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance_and_repair">Maintenance and repair</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balloonreflection.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Balloonreflection.jpg/250px-Balloonreflection.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Balloonreflection.jpg/375px-Balloonreflection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Balloonreflection.jpg/500px-Balloonreflection.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Balloonreflection.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Taken from the basket, the reflection of the balloon can be seen in the lake below. Obstacles in the landscape can inhibit smooth retrieval of the balloon upon landing.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg/250px-2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg/375px-2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg/500px-2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2015_Birds_In_Hand_Balloons_02_image_FRD.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A commercial balloon ride approaching its landing site at <a href="/wiki/Bird-in-Hand,_Pennsylvania" title="Bird-in-Hand, Pennsylvania">Bird-in-Hand, Pennsylvania</a></div></div></div>
<p>As with aircraft, hot air balloons require regular maintenance to remain airworthy. As aircraft made of fabric and that lack direct horizontal control, hot air balloons may occasionally require repairs to rips or snags. While some operations, such as cleaning and drying, may be performed by the owner or pilot, other operations, such as sewing, must be performed by a qualified repair technician and recorded in the balloon's maintenance log book.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span></h3>
<p>To ensure long life and safe operation, the envelope should be kept clean and dry. This prevents <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a> and mildew from forming on the fabric and abrasion from occurring during packing, transport, and unpacking due to contact with foreign particles. In the event of a landing in a wet (because of precipitation or early morning or late evening dew) or muddy location (farmer's field), the envelope should be cleaned and laid out or hung to dry.
</p><p>The burner and fuel system must also be kept clean to ensure safe operation on demand. Damaged fuel hoses need to be replaced. Stuck or leaky valves must be repaired or replaced. The wicker basket may require occasional refinishing or repair. The skids on its bottom may require occasional replacement.
</p><p>Balloons in most parts of the world are maintained in accordance with a fixed manufacturer's maintenance schedule that includes regular (100 flight hours or 12 month) inspections, in addition to maintenance work to correct any damage. In Australia, balloons used for carrying commercial passengers must be inspected and maintained by approved workshops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repair">Repair</span></h3>
<p>In the case of a snag, burn, or rip in the envelope fabric, a patch may be applied or the affected panel completely replaced. Patches may be held in place with glue, tape, stitching, or a combination of these techniques. Replacing an entire panel requires the stitching around the old panel to be removed, and a new panel to be sewn in with the appropriate technique, thread, and stitch pattern.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Licensing">Licensing</span></h2>
<p>Depending on the size of the balloon, location, and intended use, hot air balloons and their pilots need to comply with a variety of regulations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Balloons">Balloons</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Setting_up_hot_air_balloon,_Poughkeepsie,_NY_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG/220px-Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG/330px-Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG/440px-Setting_up_hot_air_balloon%2C_Poughkeepsie%2C_NY_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Setting_up_hot_air_balloon,_Poughkeepsie,_NY_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Top of balloon during inflation. <a href="/wiki/Crew" title="Crew">Crew</a> is securing parachute vent.</div></div></div>
<p>As with other aircraft in the USA, balloons must be registered (have an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration" title="Aircraft registration">N-number</a>), have an <a href="/wiki/Airworthiness" title="Airworthiness">airworthiness</a> certificate, and pass annual inspections. Balloons below a certain size (empty weight of less than 155 pounds or 70 kg including envelope, basket, burners and empty fuel tanks) can be used as an <a href="/wiki/Ultralight_aircraft_(United_States)" title="Ultralight aircraft (United States)">ultralight aircraft</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilots">Pilots</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Australia">In Australia</span></h4>
<p>In Australia, private balloon pilots are managed by the Australian Ballooning Federation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
and typically become members of regional hot air ballooning clubs. Commercial operations carrying fare paying passengers or charging for promotional flights must operate under an Air Operators Certificate from the Australian Civil Aviation and Safety Authority (CASA) with a nominated Chief Pilot. Pilots must have different degrees of experience before they are allowed to progress to larger balloons. Hot air balloons must be registered aircraft with CASA and are subject to regular airworthiness checks by authorised personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_UK">In the UK</span></h4>
<p>In the UK, the person in command must hold a valid Private Pilot's Licence issued by the Civil Aviation Authority specifically for ballooning; this is known as the PPL(B). There are two types of commercial balloon licences: CPL(B) Restricted and CPL(B) (Full). The CPL(B) Restricted is required if the pilot is undertaking work for a sponsor or being paid by an external agent to operate a balloon. The pilot can fly a sponsored balloon with everything paid for with a PPL unless asked to attend any event. Then a CPL(B) Restricted is required. The CPL(B) is required if the pilot is flying passengers for money. The balloon then needs a transport category C of A (certificate of air worthiness). If the pilot is only flying sponsor's guests, and not charging money for flying other passengers, then the pilot is exempted from holding an AOC (air operator's certificate) though a copy of it is required.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="how can pilot have copy of license not held? (February 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For passenger flying the balloon also requires a maintenance log.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">In the United States</span></h4>
<p>In the United States, a pilot of a hot air balloon must have a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_certification_in_the_United_States" title="Pilot certification in the United States">pilot certificate</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> (FAA) and it must carry the rating of "Lighter-than-air free balloon", and unless the pilot is also qualified to fly gas balloons, will also carry this limitation: "Limited to hot air balloons with airborne heater". A pilot does not need a license to fly an <a href="/wiki/Ultralight_aviation" title="Ultralight aviation">ultralight</a> aircraft, but training is highly advised, and some hot air balloons meet the criteria.
</p><p>To carry paying passengers for hire (and attend some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon_festivals" title="Hot air balloon festivals">balloon festivals</a>), a pilot must have a <a href="/wiki/Pilot_certification_in_the_United_States#Commercial" title="Pilot certification in the United States">commercial pilot certificate</a>. Commercial hot air balloon pilots may also act as hot air balloon <a href="/wiki/Flight_instructor" title="Flight instructor">flight instructors</a>. While most balloon pilots fly for the pure joy of floating through the air, many are able to make a living as a professional balloon pilot. Some professional pilots fly commercial passenger sightseeing flights, while others fly corporate advertising balloons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Professional_Pilots_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Professional_Pilots-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ballooning#Notable_accidents" title="History of ballooning">History of ballooning § Notable accidents</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/1989_Alice_Springs_hot_air_balloon_crash" title="1989 Alice Springs hot air balloon crash">1989 Alice Springs hot air balloon crash</a>: On 4 13 August 1989, two hot air balloons collided at <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, causing one to fall, killing all 13 people on board.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=2011_Somerset_hot_air_balloon_crash&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="2011 Somerset hot air balloon crash (page does not exist)">2011 Somerset hot air balloon crash</a>: On 1 January 2011, a hot air balloon attempting a high-altitude flight crashed at Pratten's Bowls Club in <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Somerset" title="Westfield, Somerset">Westfield, Somerset</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, killing both people on board.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2012_Carterton_hot_air_balloon_crash" title="2012 Carterton hot air balloon crash">2012 Carterton hot air balloon crash</a>: On 7 January 2012, a hot air balloon collided with a power line, caught fire and crashed at <a href="/wiki/Carterton,_New_Zealand" title="Carterton, New Zealand">Carterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Island" title="North Island">North Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, killing all 11 people on board.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2012_Ljubljana_Marshes_hot_air_balloon_crash" title="2012 Ljubljana Marshes hot air balloon crash">2012 Ljubljana Marshes hot air balloon crash</a>: On 23 August 2012, a storm blew a hot air balloon to the ground, causing it to catch fire on impact near <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>. The crash killed 6 of the 32 people on board, and injured the other 26.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2013_Luxor_hot_air_balloon_crash" title="2013 Luxor hot air balloon crash">2013 Luxor hot air balloon crash</a>: On 26 February 2013, a hot air balloon carrying foreign tourists ignited and crashed near the ancient city of <a href="/wiki/Luxor" title="Luxor">Luxor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, killing 19 of the 21 people on board, making it the deadliest balloon accident in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMH_Egypt_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMH_Egypt-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2016_Lockhart_hot_air_balloon_crash" title="2016 Lockhart hot air balloon crash">2016 Lockhart hot air balloon crash</a>: On 30 July 2016, a hot air balloon carrying 16 people caught fire and crashed near <a href="/wiki/Lockhart,_Texas" title="Lockhart, Texas">Lockhart, Texas</a>. There were no survivors.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturers">Manufacturers</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1365" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg/220px-2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg/330px-2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg/440px-2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2017_Cameron_Balloon_designed_by_Seattle_Ballooning_.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New 2017 Cameron Hot Air Balloon in flight <a class="external autonumber" href="https://seattleballooning.com" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></div></div></div>
<p>The largest manufacturer of hot air balloons in the world is <a href="/wiki/Cameron_Balloons" title="Cameron Balloons">Cameron Balloons</a> company of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, England, which also owns <a href="/wiki/Lindstrand_Balloons" title="Lindstrand Balloons">Lindstrand Balloons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oswestry" title="Oswestry">Oswestry</a>, England. Cameron Balloons, Lindstrand Balloons and another English balloon manufacturing company, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder_and_Colt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thunder and Colt (page does not exist)">Thunder and Colt</a> (since acquired by Cameron), have been innovators and developers of special shaped balloons. These hot air balloons use the same principle of lift as conventional inverted teardrop shaped balloons but often sections of the special balloon envelope shape do not contribute to the balloon's ability to stay aloft.
</p><p>The second largest manufacturer of hot air balloons in the world is <a href="/wiki/Ultramagic" title="Ultramagic">Ultramagic</a> company, based in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, which produces from 80 to 120 balloons per year. Ultramagic can produce very large balloons, such as the N-500 that accommodates as many as 27 persons in the basket, and has also produced many balloons with special shapes, as well as cold-air inflatables.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guru_Balloon_guruballoon.com_407.758.7007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/90/Guru_Balloon_guruballoon.com_407.758.7007.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Guru_Balloon_guruballoon.com_407.758.7007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the last Aerostar International, Inc. RX8 balloons.</div></div></div>
<p>In the USA <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aerostar_International,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aerostar International, Inc. (page does not exist)">Aerostar International, Inc.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sioux_Falls,_South_Dakota" title="Sioux Falls, South Dakota">Sioux Falls, South Dakota</a> was North America's largest balloon manufacturer and a close second in world manufacturing before ceasing to build balloons in January 2007. The oldest U.S. certified manufacture is now Adams Balloons out of Albuquerque, New Mexico. <a href="/wiki/Firefly_Balloons" title="Firefly Balloons">Firefly Balloons</a>, formerly The Balloon Works, is a manufacturer of hot-air balloons in <a href="/wiki/Statesville,_North_Carolina" title="Statesville, North Carolina">Statesville, North Carolina</a>. Another manufacturer is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Head_Balloons,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Head Balloons, Inc. (page does not exist)">Head Balloons, Inc.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Helen,_Georgia" title="Helen, Georgia">Helen, Georgia</a>.
</p><p>The major manufacturers in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sundance_Balloons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sundance Balloons (page does not exist)">Sundance Balloons</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fantasy_Sky_Promotions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fantasy Sky Promotions (page does not exist)">Fantasy Sky Promotions</a>. Other manufacturers include Kavanagh Balloons of Australia, Schroeder Fire Balloons of Germany, <a href="/wiki/Kubicek_Balloons" title="Kubicek Balloons">Kubicek Balloons</a> of the Czech Republic, and LLopis Balloons of France.
</p>
