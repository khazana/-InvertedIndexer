<p>The <b>Constellation Program</b> (abbreviated <b>CxP</b>) is a cancelled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_spaceflight" title="Manned spaceflight">manned spaceflight</a> program developed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> agency of the United States, from 2005 to 2009. The major goals of the program were "completion of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>" and a "return to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> no later than 2020" with a crewed flight to the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> as the ultimate goal. The program's <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> reflected the three stages of the program: the Earth (ISS), the Moon, and finally Mars—while the Mars goal also found expression in the name given to the program's booster rockets: <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares</a> (the Greek equivalent of the Roman god <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-2">[2]</a></sup> The technological aims of the program included the regaining of significant <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> experience beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> and the development of technologies necessary to enable sustained human presence on other planetary bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Constellation began in response to the goals laid out in the <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">Vision for Space Exploration</a> under NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Keefe" title="Sean O'Keefe">Sean O'Keefe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> O'Keefe's successor, <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">Michael D. Griffin</a>, ordered a complete review, termed the <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Systems_Architecture_Study" title="Exploration Systems Architecture Study">Exploration Systems Architecture Study</a>, which reshaped how NASA would pursue the goals laid out in the Vision for Space Exploration, and its findings were formalized by the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Authorization_Act_of_2005" title="NASA Authorization Act of 2005">NASA Authorization Act of 2005</a>. The Act directed NASA to "develop a sustained human presence on the Moon, including a robust precursor program to promote exploration, science, commerce and US preeminence in space, and as a stepping stone to future exploration of Mars and other destinations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-1">[1]</a></sup> Work began on this revised Constellation Program, to send astronauts first to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, then to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and then to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and beyond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Subsequent to the findings of the <a href="/wiki/Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee" title="Review of United States Human Space Flight Plans Committee">Augustine Committee</a> in 2009 that the Constellation Program could not be executed without substantial increases in funding, on February 1, 2010, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> announced a proposal to cancel the program, effective with the passage of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_federal_budget" title="2010 United States federal budget">U.S. 2011 fiscal year budget</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_cancels_Moon_return_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_cancels_Moon_return-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FY2011_budget_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FY2011_budget-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_eliminates_funds_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost_eliminates_funds-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He later announced changes to the proposal in a <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Barack_Obama_administration#Space_policy_speech_at_Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Space policy of the Barack Obama administration">major space policy speech</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> on April 15, 2010. Obama signed the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Authorization_Act_of_2010" title="NASA Authorization Act of 2010">NASA Authorization Act of 2010</a> on October 11, which shelved the program,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with Constellation contracts remaining in place until Congress would act to overturn the previous mandate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-space1-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2011, NASA announced that it had adopted the design of its new <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Heavy-lift_Rocket_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Heavy-lift_Rocket-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designs">Designs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Designs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the main goals of Constellation was the development of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">booster vehicles</a> to replace the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>. NASA had already begun designing two boosters, the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a>, when the program was created. Ares I was designed for the sole purpose of launching mission crews into orbit, while Ares V would have been used to launch other hardware which required a heavier lift capacity than the Ares I booster provided. In addition to these two boosters, NASA designed other spacecraft for use during Constellation, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion</a> crew capsule, the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">Earth Departure Stage</a> secondary booster, and the <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a> lunar lander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles">Vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Orion">Orion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion (spacecraft)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="4267" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg/220px-Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg/330px-Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg/440px-Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orion_with_ATV_SM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>2013 version shown</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion Crew Exploration Vehicle</a> was designed for the Constellation program as a crew compartment for use in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> was selected as the prime contractor for the Orion project on August 31, 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> was selected to build its primary heat shield on September 15, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> NASA initially planned to develop different Orion capsules tailored for specific missions. The Block I Orion was to be used for <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> missions and other Earth orbit missions, while the Block II and III variants were designed for deep-space exploration.
</p><p>Orion's design consists of three main parts: a Crew Module (CM) similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command_Module" title="Apollo Command Module">Apollo Command Module</a>, but capable of sustaining four to six crew members; a cylindrical Service Module (SM) containing the primary propulsion systems and consumable supplies; and the Launch Abort System (LAS), which provides capability for the astronauts and Crew Module to escape from the launch vehicle should problems arise during launch ascent. The Orion Crew Module is designed to be reusable for up to ten flights, allowing NASA to construct a fleet of Orion crew modules.
</p><p>Despite the cancellation of the Constellation program, development of a variant of the Orion spacecraft, the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion Multipurpose Crew Vehicle (MPCV)</a>, continues, with a test launch performed on 5 December 2014. A second one is scheduled for six years later.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Altair">Altair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Altair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair (spacecraft)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altair-Lander_(latest).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1183" data-file-width="1368" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg/220px-Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg/330px-Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg/440px-Altair-Lander_%28latest%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Altair-Lander_(latest).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Design for Altair</div></div></div>
<p>Altair (formerly known as the Lunar Surface Access Module, LSAM) was designed to be the main transport vehicle for astronauts on lunar missions. The Altair design was much larger than its predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a>, at almost five times the volume, occupying a total of 1,120 cubic feet (32 m<sup>3</sup>) compared with the Apollo lander's 235 cubic feet (6.7 m<sup>3</sup>). It was to stand 32 feet (9.8 m) tall and span 49 feet (15 m) wide from tip to tip of the landing gear.
</p><p>Like its predecessor, the Altair design consists of two parts: an ascent stage which houses the four-person crew; and a descent stage consisting of the landing gear, and storage for the majority of the crew's consumables (oxygen and water) and for scientific equipment. Unlike the Lunar Module, Altair was designed to land in the lunar polar regions favored by NASA for future lunar base construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Altair, like the Lunar Module, was not designed to be reusable, and the ascent stage would be discarded after use.
</p><p>The Altair descent stage was to be powered by four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RL-10" title="RL-10">RL-10</a> rocket engines, which are also those used in the <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur upper stage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket. Unlike the current RL-10 engines in use, these newer RL-10s were to have the ability to throttle down to as low as 10% rated thrust (the older specifications allow for 20%), thus allowing the use of Altair for both the lunar orbit insertion (LOI) and landing stages of lunar missions. The ascent stage was designed to be powered by a single engine, likely a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> engine similar or identical to the main engine of the Orion CSM, which would use the descent stage as both a launchpad and a platform for future base construction. Alternately, there was a small possibility that the original plan of using LOX/CH4–fueled engines on board the Block II (lunar) Orion CSM and Altair ascent stage would have been adopted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">Propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maximum_payload.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="1050" decoding="async" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/50/Maximum_payload.PNG/420px-Maximum_payload.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/50/Maximum_payload.PNG/630px-Maximum_payload.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/50/Maximum_payload.PNG/840px-Maximum_payload.PNG 2x" width="420"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maximum_payload.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of maximum payload to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>.<br/>1. <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> payload includes crew and cargo. 2. <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> payload includes only crew and inherent craft. 3. <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> payload includes crew, inherent craft and cargo. 4. <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a> payload includes only cargo and inherent craft.</div></div></div>
<p>NASA planned to use two separate boosters for the Constellation Program missions – the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> for crew and the <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a> for cargo. This would have allowed the two launch vehicles to be optimized for their respective missions, and allowed a much higher total lift for the Ares V without being cost-prohibitive. The Constellation Program thus combined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbit_Rendezvous" title="Lunar Orbit Rendezvous">Lunar Orbit Rendezvous</a> method adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>'s lunar missions with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Orbit_Rendezvous" title="Earth Orbit Rendezvous">Earth Orbit Rendezvous</a> method which had also been considered.
</p><p>The name <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a> (the Greek god called Mars in Roman mythology) was chosen for the boosters as a reference to the project's goal of landing on Mars. The numbers I and V were chosen to pay homage to the Saturn rockets of the 1960s.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ares_I">Ares I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ares I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="1996" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/220px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/330px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/440px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The launch of Ares I prototype, <a href="/wiki/Ares_I-X" title="Ares I-X">Ares I-X</a> on October 28, 2009</div></div></div>
<p>The Orion spacecraft would have been launched into a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> by the Ares I rocket (the "Stick"), developed by <a href="/wiki/Alliant_Techsystems" title="Alliant Techsystems">Alliant Techsystems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocketdyne" title="Rocketdyne">Rocketdyne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ares1stStage_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ares1stStage-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rocketdyne_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rocketdyne-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Formerly referred to as the Crew Launch Vehicle (CLV), the Ares I consisted of a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_Rocket_Booster" title="Solid Rocket Booster">Solid Rocket Booster</a> (SRB) derived in part from the primary boosters used in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> system, connected at its upper end by an interstage support assembly to a new liquid-fueled second stage powered by a <a href="/wiki/J-2X" title="J-2X">J-2X</a> rocket engine. NASA selected the Ares designs for their anticipated overall safety, reliability and cost-efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SafetyDesign_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SafetyDesign-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>NASA began developing the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> low Earth orbit launch vehicle (analogous to Apollo's <a href="/wiki/Saturn_IB" title="Saturn IB">Saturn IB</a>), returning to a development philosophy used for the original <a href="/wiki/Saturn_I" title="Saturn I">Saturn I</a>, test-launching one stage at a time, which <a href="/wiki/George_Mueller_(NASA)" title="George Mueller (NASA)">George Mueller</a> abandoned in favor of "all-up" testing for the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>. As of May 2010, the program got as far as launching the first <a href="/wiki/Ares_I#Ares_I-X_test_launch" title="Ares I">Ares I-X</a> first-stage flight on October 28, 2009 and testing the Orion launch abort system before its cancellation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ares_V">Ares V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ares V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:106px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ares_V_View.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="812" data-file-width="104" decoding="async" height="812" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ea/Ares_V_View.png" width="104"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ares_V_View.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Exploded view of Ares V showing where Altair would be</div></div></div>
<p>Ares V would have had a maximum lift capacity of about 188 metric tons (414,000 lb) to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO), compared to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>'s capacity of 24.4 metric tons (54,000 lb), and the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>'s 118 metric tons (260,000 lb). The Ares V would have carried about 71 metric tons (157,000 lb) to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, versus the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>'s 45 metric tons (99,000 lb) lunar payload.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AresV_overview_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-AresV_overview-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ares V design consisted of six <a href="/wiki/RS-68" title="RS-68">RS-68</a> engines with assistance from a pair of 5.5-segment SRBs. Five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Main_Engine" title="Space Shuttle Main Engine">Space Shuttle Main Engines</a> (SSME) were originally planned for the Ares V, but the RS-68 engines are more powerful and less complex and therefore less expensive than the SSMEs. The Ares V would have flown for the first eight minutes of powered flight, then the Earth Departure Stage would have placed itself and the Altair spacecraft into low Earth orbit while awaiting the arrival of the Orion.  Toward the end of the program, it became apparent that the ablatively-cooled RS-68B engines would not withstand the heat from the solid rocket boosters at launch, and NASA began again to consider using Space Shuttle Main Engines instead of upgrading the RS-68 to be <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_cooling" title="Regenerative cooling">regeneratively-cooled</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_Departure_Stage">Earth Departure Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Earth Departure Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="971" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg/220px-EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg/330px-EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg/440px-EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EDP_and_CEV_leaving_orbit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">Earth Departure Stage</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">Earth Departure Stage</a></div>
<p>The Earth Departure Stage (EDS) was the propulsion system designed to put the Altair upper stage on a lunar trajectory from within low Earth orbit. It was designed as the second liquid-fueled stage of the Ares V rocket. The Orion spacecraft would have been launched separately by Ares I, and then met and docked with the Ares V-launched EDS/Altair combination, delivering the crew and configuring the spacecraft for its journey to the Moon in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Earth_orbit_rendezvous" title="Earth orbit rendezvous">Earth orbit rendezvous</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_Apollo_and_Space_Shuttle_designs">Comparison to Apollo and Space Shuttle designs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparison to Apollo and Space Shuttle designs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle program</a></div>
<p>NASA planned to use the first vehicles developed in the Constellation Program for Earth-orbit tasks formerly undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAhq_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAhq-25">[25]</a></sup>  But unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-33" title="X-33">X-33</a> and other programs intended to replace the Shuttle, Constellation reused concepts from the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle programs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAhq_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAhq-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The shape of the Orion command module closely resembles the aerodynamic shape of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a>. However, in other areas Orion employs updated technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  The design of the launch vehicle taking Orion into orbit, the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a>, employs many concepts from the Apollo program.
</p><p>The design of the <a href="/wiki/J-2X" title="J-2X">J-2X engine</a> intended for use on the Ares V booster rocket was originally to be similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J-2_(rocket_engine)" title="J-2 (rocket engine)">J-2</a> engine of the Apollo-era Saturn V and Saturn IB rockets. In designing the J-2X, NASA engineers visited museums, searched for Apollo-era documentation and consulted with engineers who worked on the Apollo program. "The mechanics of landing on the Moon and getting off the Moon to a large extent have been solved," said Constellation program manager Jeff Hanley. "That is the legacy that Apollo gave us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  However, as the J-2X program progressed, it became apparent that, because of revised safety requirements and the growing mass of the upper stage, it was necessary to scrap the original J-2 design completely and use a completely new design for the J-2X.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like Apollo, Constellation would have flown a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit_rendezvous" title="Lunar orbit rendezvous">lunar orbit rendezvous</a> mission profile, but unlike Apollo, Constellation would have also employed <a href="/wiki/Earth_orbit_rendezvous" title="Earth orbit rendezvous">Earth orbit rendezvous</a>, conveying the crew to the vehicle. The lander, known as <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a>, would have been launched separately on the <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a> rocket, a rocket based on both Space Shuttle and Apollo technologies. Orion would have been launched separately and would have linked up with Altair in low Earth orbit. Also, unlike Apollo, Orion would have remained unmanned in lunar orbit while the entire crew landed on the lunar surface. Toward the end of the mission, the Altair spacecraft would have launched into lunar orbit to link up with the Orion spacecraft in lunar orbit rendezvous.  Like Apollo, the Orion capsule would then have returned to Earth, re-entering the atmosphere and landing in water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Constellation_missions" title="List of Constellation missions">List of Constellation missions</a></div>
<p>Like those of the Apollo Program, Constellation program missions would involve its main vehicle, the Orion spacecraft, flying missions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Earth_Orbit" title="Low Earth Orbit">low Earth orbit</a> to service the International Space Station, and in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">Earth Departure Stage</a> vehicles, on manned flights to the polar regions of the Moon. There were no well-defined plans at the time of cancelation for a manned flight to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, the ultimate goal of the project, but a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_Asteroid_Mission" title="Orion Asteroid Mission">mission to a Near-Earth asteroid</a> was in the initial planning phase as of 2008.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Space_Station_and_low-Earth_orbit_flights">International Space Station and low-Earth orbit flights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: International Space Station and low-Earth orbit flights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a></div>
<p>After being manufactured at private plants, the parts of the Ares I/Orion stack would be tested and assembled at the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">Vehicle Assembly Building</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>. Once assembly was completed and a launch date was set, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crawler-Transporter" title="Crawler-Transporter">Crawler-Transporter</a> would transport the completed stack, along with the launch support tower and the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Launcher_Platform" title="Mobile Launcher Platform">Mobile Launcher Platform</a>, out to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_Complex_39" title="Launch Complex 39">Launch Pad39B</a>. Once the Crawler-Transporter reached the pad, the stack and the Launcher Platform would be left in place and the Crawler-Transporter removed to a safe distance.
</p><p>After final safety checks, the ground crew would fill up the second stage with liquid hydrogen (LH<sub>2</sub>) and liquid oxygen (LOX) fuel, and the crew, wearing <a href="/wiki/Constellation_Space_Suit" title="Constellation Space Suit">all-purpose spacesuits</a>, would enter the spacecraft three hours before liftoff. Once they were locked in, and after all systems were cleared by controllers at both the Cape and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_Control" title="Mission Control">Mission Control</a> in Houston, the Ares I would then launch.
</p><p>After a two-day orbital chase, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_spacecraft" title="Orion spacecraft">Orion spacecraft</a>, having jettisoned much of the initial stack during takeoff, would meet with the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. After getting the go ahead from Houston, Orion would then dock with the ISS. The six-man crew (at a maximum) would then enter the station in order to perform numerous tasks and activities for the duration of their flight, usually lasting six months, but possibly shortened to four or lengthened to eight, depending upon NASA's goals for that particular mission. Once completed, the crew would then reenter the Orion, seal itself off from the ISS, and then undock from the station.
</p><p>Once the Orion reached a safe distance from the ISS, the Command Module (after having jettisoned the disposable service module) would re-enter in the same manner as all NASA spacecraft prior to the Shuttle, using the ablative heat shield to both deflect heat from the spacecraft and to slow it down from a speed of 28,000 km/h (17,500 mph or Mach 23) to 480 km/h (300 mph or Mach 0.5). After reentry was completed, the forward assembly would be jettisoned, and two drogue parachutes released, followed at 20,000 feet (6,100 m) by three main parachutes and airbags filled with nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>), which does not combust when exposed to heat, allowing the spacecraft to splashdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Command Module would then be returned to Kennedy Space Center for refurbishment for a later flight. Unlike the Apollo CM, which was used only for one flight, an Orion CM could theoretically be used up to ten times under normal operating conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_sortie_flights">Lunar sortie flights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lunar sortie flights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orion_lunar_orbit_(Sept_2006).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg/220px-Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg/330px-Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg/440px-Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orion_lunar_orbit_(Sept_2006).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion Spacecraft</a> in lunar orbit</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lunar_sortie" title="Lunar sortie">Lunar sortie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lunar_outpost_(NASA)" title="Lunar outpost (NASA)">Lunar outpost (NASA)</a></div>
<p>Unlike the Apollo missions, where both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a> were launched together on the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket, the manned Orion craft would be launched separately from the unmanned <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">EDS</a> and lunar lander. The Ares V/EDS/Altair stack would be assembled at the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">Vehicle Assembly Building</a> and then transported to Pad A of <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center_Launch_Complex_39" title="Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39">Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39</a>, and the Ares I/Orion stack would be transported to the adjacent Pad 39B. The Ares V/EDS/Altair stack would be launched first, into a 360 kilometers (220 mi) high circular orbit. Approximately 90 minutes later, the Ares I/Orion would then launch with the crew into a nearly identical orbit.
</p><p>The Orion would then <a href="/wiki/Earth_orbit_rendezvous" title="Earth orbit rendezvous">rendezvous</a> and dock with the Altair/EDS combination already in low-Earth orbit. After the necessary preparations for lunar flight, the EDS would fire for 390 seconds in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translunar_injection" title="Translunar injection">translunar injection</a> (TLI) maneuver, accelerating the spacecraft to 40,200 kilometers per hour (25,000 mph). After this burn, the EDS would be jettisoned and sent either into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_orbit" title="Solar orbit">orbit around the Sun</a> or to crash into the lunar surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>During the three-day trans-lunar coast, the four-man crew would monitor the Orion's systems, inspect their Altair spacecraft and its support equipment, and correct their flight path as necessary to allow the Altair to land at a near-polar landing site suitable for a future lunar base. Approaching the lunar far side, the Orion/Altair combination would orient the Altair's engines forward and make the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit_insertion" title="Lunar orbit insertion">lunar orbit insertion</a> (LOI) burn.
</p><p>Once in lunar orbit, the crew would refine the trajectory and configure the Orion CSM for unmanned flight, allowing all four crew members to transfer to the Altair vehicle and land on the Moon, while the Orion waits for their return. Upon receiving clearance from Mission Control, the Altair would undock from the Orion and perform an inspection maneuver, allowing ground controllers to inspect the spacecraft via live TV mounted on Orion for any visible problems that would prevent landing (on Apollo this was done by the Command Module Pilot). After receiving approval from ground controllers, the two craft would separate to a safe distance and the Altair's descent engines would fire again for powered descent to a pre-determined landing spot previously selected by unmanned spacecraft.
</p><p>Upon landing, the crew would put on their <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">extravehicular activity</a> (EVA) spacesuits and commence the first of five to seven lunar EVAs, collecting samples and deploying experiments. After completing their Lunar Sortie operations, the crew would then enter the Altair and fire the ascent stage engine to lift off from the surface, using the descent stage as a launchpad (and leaving it as a platform for future base construction). Upon entering orbit, the Altair would rendezvous and dock with the waiting Orion spacecraft, and the crew would then transfer, along with samples collected on the Moon, back to the Orion. After jettisoning the Altair, the crew would perform the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans_Earth_Injection" title="Trans Earth Injection">Trans Earth Injection</a> (TEI) burn for the return trip to Earth.
</p><p>After a two-and-a-half-day coast, the crew would jettison the Service Module (allowing it to burn up in the atmosphere) and the CM would reenter the Earth's atmosphere using a special reentry trajectory designed to slow the vehicle from its speed of 40,200 kilometers per hour (25,000 mph) to 480 kilometers per hour (300 mph) and thus allow a Pacific Ocean splashdown. The Crew Module would then be flown back to Kennedy Space Center for refurbishment, while lunar samples would be routed to the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a>'s (JSC) <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Receiving_Laboratory" title="Lunar Receiving Laboratory">Lunar Receiving Laboratory</a> for analysis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orion_asteroid_mission">Orion asteroid mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Orion asteroid mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the asteroid visit mission superseding the corresponding Constellation program mission, see <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_Redirect_Mission" title="Asteroid Redirect Mission">Asteroid Redirect Mission</a>.</div>
<p>The <i>Orion Asteroid Mission</i> was a proposed NASA mission to a <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">near-Earth asteroid</a> (NEA) which would use the standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_spacecraft" title="Orion spacecraft">Orion spacecraft</a>, and a landing module based on a modified <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a> lunar lander. Most of its specific details are now deprecated by the cancellation of the Constellation Program and related designs. Such a mission could assess the potential value of water, iron, nickel, platinum and other resources on the asteroid; test possible ways to extract them; and possibly examine or develop techniques which could be used to protect the Earth from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_impact" title="Asteroid impact">asteroid impacts</a>. This would be the first manned mission to any extraterrestrial body besides the Moon, and would represent a step towards a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">manned mission to Mars</a>.
</p><p>The mission would start in a similar fashion to the <a href="#Lunar_sortie_flights">lunar landing mission</a> described above, using an Ares V to launch the landing module into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a>, followed by the launch of an Orion spacecraft, with a two- or three-person crew (as opposed to a four-person crew for lunar missions) on an Ares I rocket. Once the Orion spacecraft docked with the landing module and the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Departure_Stage" title="Earth Departure Stage">Earth Departure Stage</a> (EDS), the EDS would then fire again and propel the Orion spacecraft to a nearby near-Earth asteroid where the crew would then land and explore its surface.
</p><p>Once the task was completed, the Orion spacecraft would then depart from the asteroid and, upon reaching the vicinity of Earth, would jettison both the <a href="/wiki/Service_module" title="Service module">service module</a> and the landing module in a manner similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> before entering the atmosphere for a Pacific Ocean splashdown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orion_Mars_mission">Orion Mars mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Orion Mars mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ultimate goal of NASA's Constellation program was a manned mission landing humans on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in the 2030s as a spiritual successor to the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Applications_Program" title="Apollo Applications Program">Apollo Applications Program</a> in the 1960s. The mission would utilize the hardware of the Constellation Project, primarily the Orion spacecraft (or a variation based on the Orion), and the Ares V cargo-launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAMoonMars_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAMoonMars-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>A design study utilizing Constellation launch vehicles, known as <a href="/wiki/Mars_Design_Reference_Mission" title="Mars Design Reference Mission">Design Reference Architecture</a> 5.0, was completed in 2009. In DRA 5.0, a Mars mission would have involved multiple launches of an Ares V rocket, as well as an Ares I to launch the crew. In the first Mars <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch window</a>, two cargo payloads would be launched into Earth orbit, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_thermal_rocket" title="Nuclear thermal rocket">nuclear thermal rocket</a> stage for each payload, in order to boost them to Mars. Alternatively, chemical (specifically liquid hydrogen/liquid oxygen) propellant stages could have been used, although this would have required more launches. One cargo payload would include a Mars Ascent Vehicle (MAV), as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">In-situ resource utilization</a> equipment to generate propellant for the MAV. The second cargo payload would be a habitat that the astronauts would live in during the stay on the surface. In the next launch window, 26 months after the first, the crew would go to Mars in an interplanetary transfer vehicle with nuclear thermal rocket and propellant modules assembled in Earth orbit. Once at Mars, the crew would rendezvous with the Mars habitat in orbit, land on Mars, and explore for 500 days. The crew would use the MAV to return to their interplanetary vehicle in Mars orbit, which would then be used to return to Earth. The mission would conclude with the re-entry and landing of the Orion capsule. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRA5_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-DRA5-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justification_for_a_return_to_the_Moon">Justification for a return to the Moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Justification for a return to the Moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NASA lists a number of reasons for a human return to the Moon on its website:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>to extend human <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">colonization</a>,</li>
<li>to further pursue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_science" title="Moon science">scientific activities intrinsic to the Moon</a>,</li>
<li>to test new technologies, systems, flight operations and techniques to serve future <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> missions,</li>
<li>to provide a challenging, shared and peaceful activity to unite nations in pursuit of common objectives,</li>
<li>to expand the economic sphere while conducting research activities that benefit our home planet,</li>
<li>to engage the public and students to help develop the high-technology workforce that will be required to address the challenges of tomorrow.</li></ol>
<p>In the words of former NASA Administrator, <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">Michael D. Griffin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> "The goal isn't just scientific exploration.... It's also about extending the range of human habitat out from Earth into the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> as we go forward in time.... In the long run a single-planet species will not survive.... If we humans want to survive for hundreds of thousands or millions of years, we must ultimately populate other planets ... colonize the Solar System and one day go beyond."
</p><p>A report published in June 2014 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="US National Academy of Sciences">US National Academy of Sciences</a> called for clear long-term space goals at NASA. The report said that the agency's current course invited “failure, disillusionment, and [loss of] the longstanding international perception that human space-flight is something that the United States does best.” The report recommended that Mars be the next major goal of human space flight. Several possible paths for reaching the planet by 2037 were explored in the report, which noted that returning to the Moon would offer “significant advantages” as an intermediate step in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">National Space Society</a> (NSS), a private nonprofit, regards a return to the Moon as a high priority for the US space program, in order to develop the body of scientific knowledge of the Moon, particularly in regards to its potential for the creation of new industries, in order to provide further funding for further space exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_and_cancellation">Budget and cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Budget and cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bush_administration">Bush administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bush administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 14, 2004 President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> requested that NASA develop a proposal for continued manned space exploration after the completion of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> and the planned retirement of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program in 2010. This proposal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAaddress_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAaddress-37">[37]</a></sup> was to be a way to "establish an extended human presence on the Moon" to "vastly reduce the costs of further space exploration." Included in this would be the "harvesting and processing of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">lunar soil</a> into rocket fuel or breathable air." According to Bush, experience gained could help "develop and test new approaches and technologies and systems"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAaddress_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAaddress-37">[37]</a></sup> to begin a "sustainable course of long-term exploration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BushView_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-BushView-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>NASA estimated that the original policy would cost $230 billion (in 2004 dollars) through 2025, including the Commercial Crew and Cargo program, which is separate from the Constellation program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accountability1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-accountability1-39">[39]</a></sup>  However, unsolved technical and design challenges made it impossible for NASA to provide a conclusive estimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accountability1_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-accountability1-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Obama_administration">Obama administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Obama administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon taking office, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Obama</a> declared Constellation to be "over budget, behind schedule, and lacking in innovation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_cancels_Moon_return_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_cancels_Moon_return-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FY2011_budget_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FY2011_budget-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_eliminates_funds_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost_eliminates_funds-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  A review concluded that it would cost on the order of $150 billion for Constellation to reach its objective if adhering to the original schedule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Another review in 2009, ordered by President Obama, indicated that neither a return to the Moon nor a manned flight to Mars was within NASA's current budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustine_panel" title="Augustine panel">Augustine panel</a> proposed various options, that included two primary destination points (the Moon and deep space), three different types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparison_of_super_heavy_lift_launch_systems" title="Comparison of super heavy lift launch systems">Super Heavy Launch vehicles</a>, and a robust research and development program that would include work on <a href="/wiki/Propellant_depot" title="Propellant depot">propellant depots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Augustine_Report_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Augustine_Report-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>After reviewing the report, and following congressional testimony,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBorenstein_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBorenstein-44">[44]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_administration" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a> decided to exclude Constellation from the <a href="/wiki/2011_United_States_federal_budget" title="2011 United States federal budget">2011 United States federal budget</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> On February 1, 2010, the President's proposed budget was released, which included no funding for the project, and it became law on April 15, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_cancels_Moon_return_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_cancels_Moon_return-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FY2011_budget_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FY2011_budget-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_eliminates_funds_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost_eliminates_funds-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>President Obama hosted a Space Conference on April 15, 2010, in Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> This came at a time when the president's administration was being criticized considerably for leaving the Constellation Program out of the 2011 budget. At the conference, President Obama and top officials, as well as leaders in the field of spaceflight, discussed the future of U.S. efforts in human spaceflight and unveiled a plan for NASA that followed the Augustine Panel's "Flexible Path to Mars" option,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> modifying President Obama's prior proposal to include the continuing development of the Orion capsule as an auxiliary system to the ISS and setting the year 2015 as the deadline for the design of a new Super Heavy Launch Vehicle. In October 2010, the NASA authorization bill for 2010 was signed into law which canceled Constellation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>  But previous legislation kept Constellation contracts in force until passage of a new funding bill for 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-space1-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacements_and_alternatives">Replacements and alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Replacements and alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NASA continues development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_spacecraft" title="Orion spacecraft">Orion spacecraft</a> for deep space travel. In an effort to reduce costs, it has contracted for private development of vehicles for use in low Earth orbit. The <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Crew_Development" title="Commercial Crew Development">Commercial Crew Development</a> program seeks one or more vehicles to bring people to and from the International Space Station, and for the <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> involves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-rating" title="Human-rating">human-rating</a> the United States Air Force's <a href="/wiki/Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle" title="Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle">Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicles</a>.  Private spacecraft are already operating under the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Orbital_Transportation_Services" title="Commercial Orbital Transportation Services">Commercial Orbital Transportation Services</a> program bringing cargo to ISS.
</p>
