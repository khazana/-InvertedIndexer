<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Westphalian sovereignty</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=National_sovereignty&amp;redirect=no" title="National sovereignty">National sovereignty</a>)</span></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Westphalian sovereignty</b>, or <b>state sovereignty</b>, is the principle in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> that each <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> has exclusive <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> over its territory. The principle underlies the modern <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a> and is enshrined in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a>, which states that "nothing should authorise intervention in matters essentially within the domestic jurisdiction of any state."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist20080515_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist20080515-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the idea, every state, no matter how large or small, has an equal right to sovereignty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpson2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpson2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Political scientists have traced the concept to the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (1648), which ended the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. The principle of non-interference was further developed in the 18th century. The Westphalian system reached its peak in the 19th and 20th centuries, but it has faced recent challenges from advocates of <a href="/wiki/Humanitarian_intervention" title="Humanitarian intervention">humanitarian intervention</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> is considered by political scientists to be the beginning of the modern international system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osiander_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osiander-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gross-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croxton_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Croxton-6">[6]</a></sup> in which external powers should avoid interfering in another country's domestic affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kissinger_world_order_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kissinger_world_order-7">[7]</a></sup> However, recent scholarship has argued that the Westphalian treaties actually had little to do with the principles with which they are often associated: sovereignty, non-intervention, and the legal equality of states. For example, Osiander<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2018)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> writes that "[t]he treaties confirm neither [France's or Sweden's] 'sovereignty' nor anybody else's; least of all do they contain anything about sovereignty as a principle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Nonetheless, "Westphalian sovereignty" continues to be used as a shorthand for the basic legal principles underlying the modern state system. The applicability and relevance of these principles have been questioned from the mid-20th century onward from a variety of viewpoints. Much of the debate has turned on the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)" title="Internationalism (politics)">internationalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a>, which conflict<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Please clarify the preceding statement or statements with a good explanation from a reliable source. (October 2018)">how?</span></a></i>]</sup> with Westphalian sovereignty.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_(Gerard_Terborch_1648).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4649" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg/220px-Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg/330px-Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg/440px-Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_%28Gerard_Terborch_1648%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Westfaelischer_Friede_in_Muenster_(Gerard_Terborch_1648).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ratification of the Treaty of Münster, part of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> that ended the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The origins of Westphalian sovereignty have been traced in the scholarly literature to the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (1648). The peace treaties put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, a <a href="/wiki/European_wars_of_religion" title="European wars of religion">war of religion</a> that devastated Germany and killed 30% of its population. Since neither the Catholics nor the Protestants had won a clear victory, the peace settlement established a <i>status quo</i> order in which states would refrain from interfering in each other's religious practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kissinger_world_order_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kissinger_world_order-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> wrote:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Westphalian peace reflected a practical accommodation to reality, not a unique moral insight. It relied on a system of independent states refraining from interference in each other's domestic affairs and checking each other's ambitions through a general equilibrium of power. No single claim to truth or universal rule had prevailed in Europe's contests. Instead, each state was assigned the attribute of sovereign power over its territory. Each would acknowledge the domestic structures and religious vocations of its fellow states and refrain from challenging their existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kissinger_world_order_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-kissinger_world_order-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The principle of non-interference in other countries' domestic affairs was laid out in the mid-18th century by Swiss jurist <a href="/wiki/Emer_de_Vattel" title="Emer de Vattel">Emer de Vattel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krasner2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-krasner2010-9">[9]</a></sup> States became the primary institutional agents in an interstate system of relations. The Peace of Westphalia is said to have ended attempts to impose supranational authority on European states. The "Westphalian" doctrine of states as independent agents was bolstered by the rise in 19th century thought of <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>, under which legitimate <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">states</a> were assumed to correspond to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nations" title="Nations">nations</a></i>—groups of people united by language and culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The Westphalian system reached its peak in the late 19th century. Although practical considerations still led powerful states to seek to influence the affairs of others, forcible intervention by one country in the domestic affairs of another was less frequent between 1850 and 1900 than in most previous and subsequent periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Westphalian_sovereignty#Dubious" title="Talk:Westphalian sovereignty">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>
</p><p>After the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the United States and Western Europe began talking of a post-Westphalian order in which countries could intervene against <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> abuses in other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist20080515_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-economist20080515-1">[1]</a></sup> Critics have pointed out such intervention would be (and has been) used to continue processes similar to standard Euro-American colonialism, and that the colonial powers always used ideas similar to "humanitarian intervention" to justify colonialism, slavery, and similar practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> China and Russia have thus used their <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_veto_power" title="United Nations Security Council veto power">United Nations Security Council veto power</a> to block what they see as American actions to violate the sovereignty of other nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters20120208_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters20120208-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_to_Westphalia">Challenges to Westphalia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Challenges to Westphalia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> saw increased international integration and, arguably, the erosion of Westphalian sovereignty. Much of the literature was primarily concerned with criticizing <a href="/wiki/Realism_(international_relations)" title="Realism (international relations)">realist</a> models of international politics in which the  notion of the state as a unitary agent is taken as axiomatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, at a Symposium on the Continuing Political Relevance of the Peace of Westphalia, <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Javier_Solana" title="Javier Solana">Javier Solana</a> said that "humanity and democracy [were] two principles essentially irrelevant to the original Westphalian order" and levied a criticism that "the Westphalian system had its limits. For one, the principle of sovereignty it relied on also produced the basis for rivalry, not community of states; exclusion, not integration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> gave a speech in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> where he "set out a new, post-Westphalian, 'doctrine of the international community<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>". Blair argued that <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> had made the Westphalian approach anachronistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Blair was later referred to by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> as "the man who ushered in the post-Westphalian era".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Others have also asserted that globalization has superseded the Westphalian system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_Minister_of_Germany" title="Foreign Minister of Germany">Foreign Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Joschka_Fischer" title="Joschka Fischer">Joschka Fischer</a> referred to the Peace of Westphalia in his <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Humboldt</a> Speech, which argued that the system of European politics set up by Westphalia was obsolete: "The core of the concept of Europe after 1945 was and still is a rejection of the European balance-of-power principle and the hegemonic ambitions of individual states that had emerged following the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, a rejection which took the form of closer meshing of vital interests and the transfer of nation-state sovereign rights to supranational European institutions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s concept of shared sovereignty is also somewhat contrary to historical views of Westphalian sovereignty, as it provides for external agents to influence and interfere in the internal affairs of its member countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In a 2008 article Phil Williams links the rise of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a> and violent non-state actors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VNSA" title="VNSA">VNSAs</a>), which pose a threat to the Westphalian sovereignty of the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_intervention">Military intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interventions such as in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> by <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Cambodian%E2%80%93Vietnamese_War" title="Cambodian–Vietnamese War">Cambodian–Vietnamese War</a>) or in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> (then a part of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>) by <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh_Liberation_War" title="Bangladesh Liberation War">Bangladesh Liberation War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1971">Indo-Pakistani War of 1971</a>) were seen by some as examples of humanitarian intervention, although their basis in international law is debatable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other more recent interventions, and their attendant infringements of state sovereignty, also have prompted debates about their legality and motivations.
</p><p>A new notion of <a href="/wiki/Contingent_sovereignty" title="Contingent sovereignty">contingent sovereignty</a> seems to be emerging, but it has not yet reached the point of international legitimacy. <a href="/wiki/Neoconservatism" title="Neoconservatism">Neoconservatism</a> in particular has developed this line of thinking further, asserting that a lack of democracy may foreshadow future humanitarian crises, or that democracy itself constitutes a human right, and therefore states not respecting democratic principles open themselves up to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Just_war" title="Just war">just war</a> by other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> However, proponents of this theory have been accused of being concerned about democracy, human rights and humanitarian crises only in countries where American global dominance is challenged, while hypocritically ignoring the same issues in other countries friendlier to the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Further criticism of Westphalian sovereignty arises regarding allegedly <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">failed states</a>, of which <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> (before <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">the 2001 US-led invasion</a>) is often considered an example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-22">[22]</a></sup> In this case, it is argued that no sovereignty exists and that international intervention is justified on humanitarian grounds and by the threats posed by failed states to neighboring countries and the world as a whole.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defenders_of_Westphalia">Defenders of Westphalia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Westphalian_sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Defenders of Westphalia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Westphalian system developed in <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_Europe" title="Early modern Europe">early modern Europe</a>, its staunchest defenders can now be found in the non-Western world. The presidents of China and Russia issued a joint statement in 2001 vowing to "counter attempts to undermine the fundamental norms of the international law with the help of concepts such as 'humanitarian intervention' and 'limited sovereignty<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> China and Russia have used their <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council_veto_power" title="United Nations Security Council veto power">United Nations Security Council veto power</a> to block what they see as American violations of state sovereignty in Syria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters20120208_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters20120208-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Russia was left out of the original Westphalian system in 1648,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kissinger_world_order_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-kissinger_world_order-7">[7]</a></sup> but post-Soviet Russia has seen Westphalian sovereignty as a means to balance American power by encouraging a <a href="/wiki/Polarity_(international_relations)" title="Polarity (international relations)">multipolar world order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some in the West also speak favorably of the Westphalian state. American political scientist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Walt" title="Stephen Walt">Stephen Walt</a> urged U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> to return to Westphalian principles, calling it a "sensible course" for American foreign policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> American political commentator <a href="/wiki/Pat_Buchanan" title="Pat Buchanan">Pat Buchanan</a> has also spoken in favor of the traditional nation-state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
