<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Diffuse sky radiation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Red sky" redirects here. </p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="2531" decoding="async" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg/220px-Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg/330px-Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg/440px-Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spectrum_of_blue_sky.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A spectrum taken of blue sky clearly shows solar <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">Fraunhofer lines</a> and the atmospheric water absorption band.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Diffuse sky radiation</b> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> reaching the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface after having been <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattered</a> from the direct solar beam by <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Also called <b>sky radiation</b>, <b>diffuse skylight</b>, or just <b>skylight</b>, it is the reason for the color changes of the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a>. Of the total <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> removed from the direct solar beam by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">scattering</a> in the atmosphere (approximately 25% of the incident radiation when and where the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is high in the sky, depending on the amount of dust, <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>, and other particulates in the atmosphere), about two-thirds ultimately reaches the earth as <a href="/wiki/Photon_diffusion" title="Photon diffusion">diffuse</a> sky radiation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When the Sun is at the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> in a cloudless sky, with 1361 W/m<sup>2</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> above the atmosphere, direct sunlight is about 1050 W/m<sup>2</sup>, and total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> about 1120 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_constant_at_ground_level_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_constant_at_ground_level-2">[2]</a></sup> This implies that under these conditions the diffuse radiation is only about 70 W/m<sup>2</sup> out of the original 1361 W/m<sup>2</sup>.
</p><p>The dominant radiative scattering processes in the atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mie_scattering" title="Mie scattering">Mie scattering</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Elastic_scattering" title="Elastic scattering">elastic</a> in nature, by which light can be deviated from its path without being absorbed and with no change in wavelength.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_sky_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trees-sky.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="1003" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Trees-sky.jpg/220px-Trees-sky.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Trees-sky.jpg/330px-Trees-sky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Trees-sky.jpg/440px-Trees-sky.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trees-sky.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clear blue sky</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="491" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png/220px-Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png/330px-Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png/440px-Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of the scattering efficiency of blue light in the atmosphere compared to red light.</div></div></div>
<p>The sunlit sky is blue because <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">air</a> scatters short-<a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> light more than longer wavelengths. Since blue light is at the short-wavelength end of the visible spectrum, it is more strongly scattered in the atmosphere than long-wavelength red light. The result is that when looking toward parts of the sky other than the sun, the human eye perceives them to be blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The color perceived is similar to that obtained by a monochromatic blue of a wavelength of <span class="nowrap">474–476 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a></span> mixed with white light, <i>i.e.</i>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_(color_theory)" title="Saturation (color theory)">unsaturated</a> blue light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The explanation of the blue color by Rayleigh in 1871 is one of the most famous examples of the application of <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a> in solving a problem in physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Near <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>, most of the sunlight arrives nearly tangentially to the Earth's surface; thus, the light's path through the atmosphere is so long that much of the blue and even green light is scattered out along the way, leaving the sun rays and the clouds it illuminates red. Therefore, when looking at the sunset and sunrise, we see the color red more than the other colors.
</p><p>Scattering and absorption are major causes of the <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a> of radiation by the atmosphere. Scattering varies as a function of the ratio of the particle diameter to the wavelength of the radiation. When this ratio is less than about one-tenth, <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> occurs. In this case, the scattering coefficient varies inversely with the fourth power of the wavelength. At larger ratios, scattering varies in a complex fashion, described for spherical particles by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mie_theory" title="Mie theory">Mie theory</a>; the laws of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric_optics" title="Geometric optics">geometric optics</a> begin to apply at a ratio of the order of 10.
</p><p>In the example of the sky at the zenith, it is blue during broad daylight due to Rayleigh scattering involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> gases (<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen"><span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen"><span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a>). Near sunset and especially during twilight, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) absorption significantly contributes to maintaining the sky's blue color.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutral_points">Neutral points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_sky_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neutral points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four commonly detectable points of zero <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a> of diffuse sky radiation (known as neutral points) lying along the <a href="/wiki/Vertical_circle" title="Vertical circle">vertical circle</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>The <b>Arago point</b>, named after <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago" title="François Arago">its discoverer</a>, is customarily located at about 20° above the <a href="/wiki/Antisolar_point" title="Antisolar point">antisolar point</a>; but it lies at higher altitudes in turbid air. The latter property makes the Arago distance a useful measure of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>.</li>
<li>The <b>Babinet point</b>, discovered by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Babinet" title="Jacques Babinet">Jacques Babinet</a> in 1840, is located about 15° to 20° above the sun, hence it is difficult to observe because of solar glare.</li>
<li>The <b>Brewster point</b>, discovered by <a href="/wiki/David_Brewster" title="David Brewster">David Brewster</a> in 1840, is located about 15° to 20° below the sun; hence it is difficult to observe because of solar glare.</li>
<li>The fourth point, located at about 20° below the <a href="/wiki/Antisolar_point" title="Antisolar point">antisolar point</a>, visible only at higher altitudes in the air or in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_an_overcast_sky">Under an overcast sky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_sky_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Under an overcast sky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is essentially no direct sunlight under an overcast sky, so all light is then diffuse sky radiation. The flux of light is not very wavelength-dependent because the cloud droplets are larger than the light's wavelength and scatter all colors approximately equally. The light passes through the translucent clouds in a manner similar to frosted glass. The intensity ranges (roughly) from ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>6</sub></span> of direct sunlight for relatively thin clouds down to ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>1000</sub></span> of direct sunlight under the extreme of thickest storm clouds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eruption_of_Mt._Pinatubo_and_agriculture">Eruption of Mt. Pinatubo and agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_sky_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eruption of Mt. Pinatubo and agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="299" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg/300px-Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg/450px-Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinatubo_dust_layer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Space Shuttle (Mission <a href="/wiki/STS-43" title="STS-43">STS-43</a>) photograph of the Earth over <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> taken on August 8, 1991, which captures the double layer of Pinatubo aerosol clouds (dark streaks) above lower cloud tops.</div></div></div><p>
The eruption of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> - <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in June 1991 ejected roughly 10 km<sup>3</sup> (2.4 cu mi) of magma and "17,000,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">metric tons</a>"(17 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram#SI_multiples" title="Kilogram">teragrams</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> SO<sub>2</sub> into the air, introducing ten times as much total SO<sub>2</sub> as the 1991 Kuwaiti fires,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> mostly during the explosive <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">Plinian/Ultra-Plinian</a> event of June 15, 1991, creating a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">global stratospheric SO<sub>2</sub> haze layer</a> which persisted for years. This resulted in the global average temperature dropping by about 0.5 °C (0.9 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_News_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science_News-9">[9]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> falls out of the atmosphere rapidly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the negative agricultural effects of the eruption were largely immediate and localized to a relatively small area in close proximity to the eruption, as they were caused by the resulting thick ash cover that resulted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Globally however, despite a several-month 5% drop in overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_irradiation" title="Solar irradiation">solar irradiation</a>, and a reduction in direct sunlight by 30%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> there was no negative impact to global agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup> Surprisingly, a 3-4 year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> increase in global <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_productivity" title="Agricultural productivity">Agricultural productivity</a> and forestry growth was observed, excepting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal</a> forest regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-17">[17]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZeaMays.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1356" decoding="async" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/ZeaMays.jpg/170px-ZeaMays.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/ZeaMays.jpg/255px-ZeaMays.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/ZeaMays.jpg/340px-ZeaMays.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZeaMays.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Under more-or-less direct sunlight, dark <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">shadows</a> that limit photosynthesis are <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">cast</a> onto understorey <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaves" title="Leaves">leaves</a>. Within the <a href="/wiki/Thicket" title="Thicket">thicket</a>, very little direct sunlight can enter.</div></div></div><p> The means by which this was discovered, is that initially at the time, a mysterious drop in the rate at which <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) was filling the atmosphere was observed, which is charted in what is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This led numerous scientists to assume that this reduction was due to the lowering of the Earth's temperature, and with that, a slow down in plant and soil <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_respiration" title="Ecosystem respiration">respiration</a>, indicating a <a href="/wiki/Deleterious" title="Deleterious">deleterious</a> impact to global agriculture from the volcanic haze layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup> However upon actual investigation, the reduction in the rate at which carbon dioxide filled the atmosphere did not match up with the hypothesis that plant respiration rates had declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Instead the advantageous anomaly was relatively firmly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> linked to an unprecedented increase in the growth/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Net_primary_production" title="Net primary production">net primary production</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> of global plant life, resulting in the increase of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a> effect of global photosynthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup> The mechanism by which the increase in plant growth was possible, was that the 30% reduction of direct sunlight can also be expressed as an increase or "enhancement" in the amount of <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffuse</a> sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canopy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Canopy.jpg/220px-Canopy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Canopy.jpg/330px-Canopy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Canopy.jpg/440px-Canopy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canopy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Well lit understorey areas due to <a href="/wiki/Overcast" title="Overcast">overcast</a> clouds creating diffuse/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_light" title="Soft light">soft sunlight</a> conditions, that permits photosynthesis on leaves under the canopy.</div></div></div>
<p>This diffuse light, owing to its intrinsic nature, can illuminate under-<a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopy</a> leaves permitting more efficient total whole-plant <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> than would otherwise be the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup> In stark contrast to the effect of totally clear skies and the direct sunlight that results from it, which casts shadows onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Understorey" title="Understorey">understorey</a> leaves, strictly limiting plant photosynthesis to the top canopy layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-15">[15]</a></sup> This increase in global agriculture from the volcanic haze layer also naturally results as a product of other aerosols that are not emitted by volcanoes, such as man-made "moderately thick smoke loading" pollution, as the same mechanism, the "aerosol direct radiative effect" is behind both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
