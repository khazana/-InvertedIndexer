<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cold front</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">Cold wave</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>cold front</b> is the leading edge of a cooler mass of air, replacing at ground level a warmer mass of air, which lies within a fairly sharp surface <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure</a>.  It forms in the wake of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, at the leading edge of its cold air <a href="/wiki/Advection#Meteorology" title="Advection">advection</a> pattern, which is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>'s dry conveyor belt circulation.  Temperature changes across the boundary can exceed 30 °C (54 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  When enough moisture is present, rain can occur along the boundary.  If there is significant instability along the boundary, a narrow line of thunderstorms can form along the frontal zone.  If instability is less, a broad shield of rain can move in behind the <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">front</a>, which increases the temperature difference across the boundary.  Cold fronts are stronger in the fall and spring transition seasons and weakest during the summer.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_cold_fronts">Development of cold fronts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development of cold fronts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cooler and denser air wedges under the less-dense warmer air, lifting it. This upward motion causes lowered <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> along the cold front and can cause the formation of a narrow line of <a href="/wiki/Shower" title="Shower">showers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> when enough <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> is present. On <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a>, the surface position of the cold front is marked with the symbol of a blue line of triangles/spikes (pips) pointing in the direction of travel. A cold front's location is at the leading edge of the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> drop off, which in an <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Temperature_and_related_subjects" title="Contour line">isotherm</a> analysis would show up as the leading edge of the isotherm gradient, and it normally lies within a sharp surface <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DR-2">[2]</a></sup> Cold fronts move faster than <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a> and can produce sharper changes in weather.  Since cold air is denser than warm air, it rapidly replaces the warm air preceding the boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the northern hemisphere, a cold front usually causes a shift of wind from southwest to northwest clockwise, also known as veering, and in the southern hemisphere a shift from northwest to southwest (anticlockwise, backing). Normally, cold fronts can be marked by these characteristics:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clouds">Clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the cold front is highly unstable, <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus clouds</a> producing thunderstorms commonly form along the front. <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_incus" title="Cumulonimbus incus">Anvil</a> <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a> may spread a considerable distance downwind from the thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;N:207-212_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;N:207-212-5">[5]</a></sup> The other cloud types associated with a cold front depend on atmospheric conditions such as air mass stability and wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holt-6">[6]</a></sup>  As the front approaches, middle-étage gives way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altostratus" title="Altostratus">altostratus</a> and low-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a> with intermittent light precipitation if the warm airmass being displaced by the cold front is mostly stable. With significant airmass instability, vertically developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus" title="Cumulus">cumulus</a> or cumulonimbus with showers and thunderstorms will form along the front.
</p><p>After the passage of the cold front, the sky usually clears as high pressure builds in behind the system, although significant amounts of cumulus or stratocumulus, often in the form of long bands called <i>cloud streets</i> may persist if the air mass behind the front remains humid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weston437-438_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weston437-438-7">[7]</a></sup> Small and unchanging amounts of cumulus or cirrus clouds in an otherwise clear sky are usually indications of continuing fair weather as long as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> remains comparatively high.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation">Precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County,_Kansas.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="678" data-file-width="799" decoding="async" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png/275px-Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png/413px-Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png/550px-Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County%2C_Kansas.png 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radar_image_of_severe_thunderstorms_and_cold_front_over_Marion_County,_Kansas.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cold front as it appeared on the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service_Wichita,_Kansas" title="National Weather Service Wichita, Kansas">National Weather Service Wichita, Kansas</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WSR-88D" title="WSR-88D">WSR-88D</a> on April 3, 2011. The thin blue line labeled "cold front" is the front, with severe thunderstorms seen developing behind the front, which is moving towards the bottom right.</div></div></div>
<p>A cold front commonly brings a narrow band of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> that follows along the leading edge of the cold front. These bands of precipitation are often very strong,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and can bring severe thunderstorms, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hailstorms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> snow squalls,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> and/or <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. In the spring, these cold fronts can be very strong, and can bring strong winds when the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> is higher than normal. During the winter months, cold fronts sometimes come through an area with little or no precipitation.  Wider rain bands can occur behind cold fronts which tend to have more stratiform, and less convective, precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  These rainstorms sometimes bring <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, and can move very slowly when the storm steering it is strong and embedded within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional_flow" title="Meridional flow">meridional flow</a> pattern (with more pole to equator motion rather than west to east motion). In the winter, cold fronts can bring cold spells, and occasionally snow. In the spring or summer in temperate latitudes, hail may occasionally fall along with the rain.  If moisture is not sufficient, such as when a system has previously moved across a mountain barrier, cold fronts can pass without cloudiness.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frontogenetical_circulation">Frontogenetical circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Frontogenetical circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frontogenesis" title="Frontogenesis">Frontogenesis</a> is the process of creating or steepening the temperature gradient of a front. During this process the atmosphere reacts in an attempt to restore balance, the consequence is a circular motion along the front where air is being lifted up, along the cold front and dropping downward, behind the frontal boundary. This is the actual force of upward motion along a front that is responsible for clouds and precipitation.
</p><p>As the temperature gradient steepens during frontogenesis, the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_wind" title="Thermal wind">thermal wind</a> becomes imbalanced. To maintain balance, the <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a> aloft and below adjust, such that regions of divergence/convergence form. <a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation" title="Continuity equation">Mass continuity</a> would require a vertical transport of air along the cold front where there is divergence (lowered <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>). Although this circulation is described by a series of processes, they are actually occurring at the same time, observable along the front as a thermally direct circulation. There are several factors that influence the final shape and tilt of the circulation around the front, ultimately determining the kind and location of clouds and precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holt_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holt-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carl_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carl-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_changes">Temperature changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Temperature changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cold fronts are the leading edge of cooler air masses, hence the name "cold front". They have stronger temperature changes during the <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">fall</a> (autumn) and spring and during the middle of winter.  Temperature changes associated with cold fronts can be as much as 50 °F (30 °C).  When cold fronts come through, there is usually a quick, yet strong gust of wind, that shows that the cold front is passing.  In <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observations</a>, a remark known as FROPA is coded when this occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  The effects from a cold front can last from hours to days. The air behind the front is cooler than the air it is replacing and the warm air is forced to rise, so it cools.  As the cooler air cannot hold as much moisture as warm air, clouds form and rain occurs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_boundaries_around_an_extratropical_cyclone">Characteristics of boundaries around an extratropical cyclone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cold_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics of boundaries around an extratropical cyclone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Occluded_cyclone.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="710" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Occluded_cyclone.svg/220px-Occluded_cyclone.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Occluded_cyclone.svg/330px-Occluded_cyclone.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Occluded_cyclone.svg/440px-Occluded_cyclone.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Occluded_cyclone.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">Occluded cyclone</a> example. The triple point is the intersection of the cold, warm, and occluded fronts.</div></div></div>
<p>Cold fronts form when a cooler air mass moves into an area of warmer air in the wake of a developing extratropical cyclone. The warmer air interacts with the cooler air mass along the boundary, and usually produces precipitation.  Cold fronts often follow a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> or squall line. Very commonly, cold fronts have a warm front ahead but with a perpendicular orientation.  In areas where cold fronts catch up to the warm front, the <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded front</a> develops.  Occluded fronts have an area of warm air aloft.  When such a feature forms poleward of an extratropical cyclone, it is known as a <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trowal" title="Trowal">trowal</a></b>, which is short for <b>TR</b>ough <b>O</b>f <b>W</b>arm <b>A</b>ir a<b>L</b>oft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TROW_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TROW-13">[13]</a></sup>  A cold front is considered a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> if it begins to retreat ahead of the next extratropical cyclone along the frontal boundary, and called a <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a> if it stalls.
</p>
