<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Active galactic nucleus</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Compact region at the center of a galaxy that has a much higher than normal luminosity</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg&amp;lang=en"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="343" data-file-width="393" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/210px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/315px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/420px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inner structure of a galaxy with an active galactic nucleus</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>active galactic nucleus</b> (<b>AGN</b>) is a compact region at the center of a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> that has a much higher than normal <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> over at least some portion of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> with characteristics indicating that the luminosity is not produced by <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Such excess non-stellar emission has been observed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">optical</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-violet" title="Ultra-violet">ultra-violet</a>, <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> wavebands. A galaxy hosting an AGN is called an "active galaxy". The radiation from an AGN is believed to result from the <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accretion</a> of matter by a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> at the center of its host galaxy.
</p><p>Active galactic nuclei are the most luminous persistent sources of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> in the universe, and as such can be used as a means of discovering distant objects; their evolution as a function of cosmic time also puts constraints on <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">models of the cosmos</a>.
</p><p>The observed characteristics of an AGN depend on several properties such as the mass of the central black hole, the rate of gas accretion onto the black hole, the orientation of the <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disk</a>, the degree of <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">obscuration</a> of the nucleus by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a>, and presence or absence of <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jet" title="Astrophysical jet">jets</a>.
</p><p>Numerous subclasses of AGN have been defined based on their observed characteristics; the most powerful AGN are classified as <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">blazar</a> is an AGN with a jet pointed toward the Earth, in which radiation from the jet is enhanced by <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_beaming" title="Relativistic beaming">relativistic beaming</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early photographic observations of nearby galaxies detected some characteristic signatures of AGN emission, although there was not yet a physical understanding of the nature of the AGN phenomenon. Some early observations included the first spectroscopic detection of <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">emission lines</a> from the nuclei of <a href="/wiki/Messier_77" title="Messier 77">NGC 1068</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messier_81" title="Messier 81">Messier 81</a> by Edward Fath (published in 1909),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jet" title="Astrophysical jet">jet</a> in <a href="/wiki/Messier_87" title="Messier 87">Messier 87</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heber_Curtis" title="Heber Curtis">Heber Curtis</a> (published in 1918).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Further spectroscopic studies by astronomers including <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milton_Humason" title="Milton Humason">Milton Humason</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Mayall" title="Nicholas Mayall">Nicholas Mayall</a> noted the presence of unusual emission lines in some galaxy nuclei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1943, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Keenan_Seyfert" title="Carl Keenan Seyfert">Carl Seyfert</a> published a paper in which he described observations of nearby galaxies having bright nuclei that were sources of unusually broad emission lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Galaxies observed as part of this study included <a href="/wiki/Messier_77" title="Messier 77">NGC 1068</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGC_4151" title="NGC 4151">NGC 4151</a>, NGC 3516, and NGC 7469. Active galaxies such as these are known as <a href="/wiki/Seyfert_galaxy" title="Seyfert galaxy">Seyfert galaxies</a> in honor of Seyfert's pioneering work.
</p><p>The development of <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> was a major catalyst to understanding AGN. Some of the earliest detected radio sources are nearby active <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a>  such as <a href="/wiki/Messier_87" title="Messier 87">Messier 87</a> and <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Another radio source, <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_A" title="Cygnus A">Cygnus A</a>, was identified by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Baade" title="Walter Baade">Walter Baade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_Minkowski" title="Rudolph Minkowski">Rudolph Minkowski</a> as a tidally distorted galaxy with an unusual <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">emission-line</a> spectrum, having a <a href="/wiki/Recessional_velocity" title="Recessional velocity">recessional velocity</a> of 16,700 kilometers per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Third_Cambridge_Catalogue_of_Radio_Sources" title="Third Cambridge Catalogue of Radio Sources">3C radio survey</a> led to further progress in discovery of new radio sources as well as identifying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible-light</a> sources associated with the radio emission. In photographic images, some of these objects were nearly point-like or quasi-stellar in appearance, and were classified as <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasi-stellar radio sources</a> (later abbreviated as "quasars").
</p><p>A major breakthrough was the measurement of the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of the quasar <a href="/wiki/3C_273" title="3C 273">3C 273</a> by <a href="/wiki/Maarten_Schmidt" title="Maarten Schmidt">Maarten Schmidt</a>, published in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Schmidt noted that if this object was <a href="/wiki/Extragalactic_astronomy" title="Extragalactic astronomy">extragalactic</a> (outside the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, at a cosmological distance) then its large redshift of 0.158 implied that it was the nuclear region of a galaxy about 100 times more powerful than other radio galaxies that had been identified. Shortly afterward, optical spectra were used to measured the redshifts of a growing number of quasars including <a href="/wiki/3C_48" title="3C 48">3C 48</a>, even more distant at redshift 0.37.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The enormous luminosities of these quasars as well as their unusual spectral properties indicated that their power source could not be ordinary stars. Accretion of gas onto a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> was suggested as the source of quasars' power in papers by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Salpeter" title="Edwin Salpeter">Edwin Salpeter</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakov_Zel%27Dovich" title="Yakov Zel'Dovich">Yakov Zel'Dovich</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1969 <a href="/wiki/Donald_Lynden-Bell" title="Donald Lynden-Bell">Donald Lynden-Bell</a> proposed that nearby galaxies contain supermassive black holes at their centers as relics of "dead" quasars, and that black hole accretion was the power source for the non-stellar emission in nearby Seyfert galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  In the 1960s and 1970s, early <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">X-ray astronomy</a> observations demonstrated that Seyfert galaxies and quasars are powerful sources of X-ray emission, which originates from the inner regions of black hole accretion disks.
</p><p>Today, AGN are a major topic of astrophysical research, both <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">observational</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_astrophysics" title="Theoretical astrophysics">theoretical</a>.  AGN research encompasses observational surveys to find AGN over broad ranges of luminosity and redshift, examination of the cosmic evolution and growth of black holes, studies of the physics of black hole accretion and the emission of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> from AGN, examination of the properties of jets and outflows of matter from AGN, and the impact of black hole accretion and quasar activity on <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">galaxy evolution</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2052" data-file-width="3918" decoding="async" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg/220px-Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg/330px-Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg/440px-Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lasers_and_supermassive_black_holes_UGC_6093.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/UGC_6093" title="UGC 6093">UGC 6093</a> is classified as an active galaxy, which means that it hosts an active galactic nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>For a long time it has been argued<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> that an AGN must be powered by <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accretion</a> of mass onto massive black holes (10<sup>6</sup> to 10<sup>10</sup> times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">Solar mass</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> AGN are both compact and persistently extremely luminous. Accretion can potentially give very efficient conversion of potential and kinetic energy to radiation, and a massive black hole has a high <a href="/wiki/Eddington_luminosity" title="Eddington luminosity">Eddington luminosity</a>, and as a result, it can provide the observed high persistent luminosity. Supermassive black holes are now believed to exist in the centres of most if not all massive galaxies since the mass of the black hole correlates well with the <a href="/wiki/Velocity_dispersion" title="Velocity dispersion">velocity dispersion</a> of the galactic bulge (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M-sigma_relation" title="M-sigma relation">M-sigma relation</a>) or with bulge luminosity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Thus AGN-like characteristics are expected whenever a supply of material for accretion comes within the <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(black_hole)" title="Sphere of influence (black hole)">sphere of influence</a> of the central black hole.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accretion_disc">Accretion disc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accretion disc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the standard model of AGN, cold material close to a black hole forms an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">accretion disc</a>. Dissipative processes in the accretion disc transport matter inwards and angular momentum outwards, while causing the accretion disc to heat up. The expected spectrum of an accretion disc peaks in the optical-ultraviolet waveband; in addition, a <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> of hot material forms above the accretion disc and can <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">inverse-Compton scatter</a> <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> up to X-ray energies. The radiation from the accretion disc excites cold atomic material close to the black hole and this in turn radiates at particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a>. A large fraction of the AGN's radiation may be obscured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_gas" title="Interstellar gas">interstellar gas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_dust" title="Interstellar dust">dust</a> close to the accretion disc, but (in a steady-state situation) this will be re-radiated at some other waveband, most likely the infrared.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_jets">Relativistic jets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relativistic jets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">Relativistic jet</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M87_jet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1276" data-file-width="1222" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/200px-M87_jet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/300px-M87_jet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/400px-M87_jet.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M87_jet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image taken by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> of a 5000-<a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-year</a>-long jet ejected from the active <a href="/wiki/Messier_87" title="Messier 87">galaxy M87</a>. The blue <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron radiation</a> contrasts with the yellow starlight from the host galaxy.</div></div></div>
<p>Some accretion discs produce jets of twin, highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collimated" title="Collimated">collimated</a>, and fast outflows that emerge in opposite directions from close to the disc. The direction of the jet ejection is determined either by the angular momentum axis of the accretion disc or the spin axis of the black hole. The jet production mechanism and indeed the jet composition on very small scales are not understood at present due to the resolution of astronomical instruments being too low. The jets have their most obvious observational effects in the radio waveband, where <a href="/wiki/Very-long-baseline_interferometry" title="Very-long-baseline interferometry">very-long-baseline interferometry</a> can be used to study the synchrotron radiation they emit at resolutions of sub-<a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a> scales. However, they radiate in all wavebands from the radio through to the gamma-ray range via the <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">synchrotron</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">inverse-Compton scattering</a> process, and so AGN jets are a second potential source of any observed continuum radiation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiatively_inefficient_AGN">Radiatively inefficient AGN</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radiatively inefficient AGN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There exists a class of 'radiatively inefficient' solutions to the equations that govern accretion. The most widely known of these is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advection_Dominated_Accretion_Flow" title="Advection Dominated Accretion Flow">Advection Dominated Accretion Flow</a> (ADAF),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> but other theories exist. In this type of accretion, which is important for accretion rates well below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddington_limit" title="Eddington limit">Eddington limit</a>, the accreting matter does not form a thin disc and consequently does not efficiently radiate away the energy that it acquired as it moved close to the black hole. Radiatively inefficient accretion has been used to explain the lack of strong AGN-type radiation from massive black holes at the centres of elliptical galaxies in clusters, where otherwise we might expect high accretion rates and correspondingly high luminosities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Radiatively inefficient AGN would be expected to lack many of the characteristic features of standard AGN with an accretion disc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Particle_acceleration">Particle acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Particle acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AGN are a candidate source of high and ultra-high energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> <i>(see also <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_mechanism_of_acceleration" title="Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration">Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration</a>)</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_characteristics">Observational characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Observational characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is no single observational signature of an AGN. The list below covers some of the features that have allowed systems to be identified as AGN.
</p>
<ul><li>Nuclear optical continuum emission. This is visible whenever there is a direct view of the accretion disc. Jets can also contribute to this component of the AGN emission. The optical emission has a roughly power-law dependence on wavelength.</li>
<li>Nuclear infra-red emission. This is visible whenever the accretion disc and its environment are obscured by gas and dust close to the nucleus and then re-emitted ('reprocessing'). As it is thermal emission, it can be distinguished from any jet or disc-related emission.</li>
<li>Broad optical emission lines. These come from cold material close to the central black hole. The lines are broad because the emitting material is revolving around the black hole with high speeds causing a range of Doppler shifts of the emitted photons.</li>
<li>Narrow optical emission lines. These come from more distant cold material, and so are narrower than the broad lines.</li>
<li>Radio continuum emission. This is always due to a jet. It shows a spectrum characteristic of synchrotron radiation.</li>
<li>X-ray continuum emission. This can arise both from a jet and from the hot corona of the accretion disc via a scattering process: in both cases it shows a power-law spectrum. In some radio-quiet AGN there is an excess of soft X-ray emission in addition to the power-law component. The origin of the soft X-rays is not clear at present.</li>
<li>X-ray line emission. This is a result of illumination of cold heavy elements by the X-ray continuum that causes <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> of X-ray emission lines, the best-known of which is the iron feature around 6.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KeV" title="KeV">keV</a>. This line may be narrow or broad: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broad_iron_K_line" title="Broad iron K line">relativistically broadened iron lines</a> can be used to study the dynamics of the accretion disc very close to the nucleus and therefore the nature of the central black hole.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_active_galaxy">Types of active galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types of active galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is convenient to divide AGN into two classes, conventionally called radio-quiet and radio-loud. Radio-loud objects have emission contributions from both the jet(s) and the lobes that the jets inflate. These emission contributions dominate the luminosity of the AGN at radio wavelengths and possibly at some or all other wavelengths. Radio-quiet objects are simpler since jet and any jet-related emission can be neglected at all wavelengths.
</p><p>AGN terminology is often confusing, since the distinctions between different types of AGN sometimes reflect historical differences in how the objects were discovered or initially classified, rather than real physical differences.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio-quiet_AGN">Radio-quiet AGN</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Radio-quiet AGN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Low-ionization_nuclear_emission-line_region" title="Low-ionization nuclear emission-line region">Low-ionization nuclear emission-line regions</a> (LINERs). As the name suggests, these systems show only weak nuclear emission-line regions, and no other signatures of AGN emission. It is debatable<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> whether all such systems are true AGN (powered by accretion on to a supermassive black hole). If they are, they constitute the lowest-luminosity class of radio-quiet AGN. Some may be radio-quiet analogues of the low-excitation radio galaxies (see below).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Seyfert_galaxy" title="Seyfert galaxy">Seyfert galaxies</a>. Seyferts were the earliest distinct class of AGN to be identified. They show optical range nuclear continuum emission, narrow and occasionally broad emission lines, occasionally strong nuclear X-ray emission and sometimes a weak small-scale radio jet. Originally they were divided into two types known as Seyfert 1 and 2: Seyfert 1s show strong broad emission lines while Seyfert 2s do not, and Seyfert 1s are more likely to show strong low-energy X-ray emission. Various forms of elaboration on this scheme exist: for example, Seyfert 1s with relatively narrow broad lines are sometimes referred to as narrow-line Seyfert 1s. The host galaxies of Seyferts are usually spiral or irregular galaxies.</li>
<li>Radio-quiet <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>/QSOs. These are essentially more luminous versions of Seyfert 1s: the distinction is arbitrary and is usually expressed in terms of a limiting optical magnitude. Quasars were originally 'quasi-stellar' in optical images as they had optical luminosities that were greater than that of their host galaxy. They always show strong optical continuum emission, X-ray continuum emission, and broad and narrow optical emission lines. Some astronomers use the term QSO (Quasi-Stellar Object) for this class of AGN, reserving 'quasar' for radio-loud objects, while others talk about radio-quiet and radio-loud quasars. The host galaxies of quasars can be spirals, irregulars or ellipticals. There is a correlation between the quasar's luminosity and the mass of its host galaxy, in that the most luminous quasars inhabit the most massive galaxies (ellipticals).</li>
<li>'Quasar 2s'. By analogy with Seyfert 2s, these are objects with quasar-like luminosities but without strong optical nuclear continuum emission or broad line emission. They are scarce in surveys, though a number of possible candidate quasar 2s have been identified.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio-loud_AGN">Radio-loud AGN</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Radio-loud AGN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See main article <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">Radio galaxy</a> for a discussion of the large-scale behaviour of the jets. Here, only the active nuclei are discussed.
</p>
<ul><li>Radio-loud quasars behave exactly like radio-quiet quasars with the addition of emission from a jet. Thus they show strong optical continuum emission, broad and narrow emission lines, and strong X-ray emission, together with nuclear and often extended radio emission.</li>
<li>“<a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">Blazars</a>” (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BL_Lac_object" title="BL Lac object">BL Lac objects</a> and <a href="/wiki/OVV_quasar" title="OVV quasar">OVV quasars</a>) classes are distinguished by rapidly variable, polarized optical, radio and X-ray emission. BL Lac objects show no optical emission lines, broad or narrow, so that their redshifts can only be determined from features in the spectra of their host galaxies. The emission-line features may be intrinsically absent or simply swamped by the additional variable component. In the latter case, emission lines may become visible when the variable component is at a low level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> OVV quasars behave more like standard radio-loud quasars with the addition of a rapidly variable component. In both classes of source, the variable emission is believed to originate in a relativistic jet oriented close to the line of sight. Relativistic effects amplify both the luminosity of the jet and the amplitude of variability.</li>
<li>Radio galaxies. These objects show nuclear and extended radio emission. Their other AGN properties are heterogeneous. They can broadly be divided into low-excitation and high-excitation classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Low-excitation objects show no strong narrow or broad emission lines, and the emission lines they do have may be excited by a different mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Their optical and X-ray nuclear emission is consistent with originating purely in a jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chiaberge_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-chiaberge-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardcastle_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-hardcastle-25">[25]</a></sup> They may be the best current candidates for AGN with radiatively inefficient accretion. By contrast, high-excitation objects (narrow-line radio galaxies) have emission-line spectra similar to those of Seyfert 2s. The small class of broad-line radio galaxies, which show relatively strong nuclear optical continuum emission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> probably includes some objects that are simply low-luminosity radio-loud quasars. The host galaxies of radio galaxies, whatever their emission-line type, are essentially always ellipticals.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unification_of_AGN_species">Unification of AGN species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Unification of AGN species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unified models propose that different observational classes of AGN are a single type of physical object observed under different conditions. The currently favoured unified models are 'orientation-based unified models' meaning that they propose that the apparent differences between different types of objects arise simply because of their different orientations to the observer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, they are debated (see below).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio-quiet_unification">Radio-quiet unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radio-quiet unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At low luminosities, the objects to be unified are Seyfert galaxies. The unification models propose that in Seyfert 1s the observer has a direct view of the active nucleus. In Seyfert 2s the nucleus is observed through an obscuring structure which prevents a direct view of the optical continuum, broad-line region or (soft) X-ray emission. The key insight of orientation-dependent accretion models is that the two types of object can be the same if only certain angles to the line of sight are observed. The standard picture is of a <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a> of obscuring material surrounding the accretion disc. It must be large enough to obscure the broad-line region but not large enough to obscure the narrow-line region, which is seen in both classes of object. Seyfert 2s are seen through the torus. Outside the torus there is material that can scatter some of the nuclear emission into our line of sight, allowing us to see some optical and X-ray continuum and, in some cases, broad emission lines—which are strongly polarized, showing that they have been scattered and proving that some Seyfert 2s really do contain hidden Seyfert 1s. Infrared observations of the nuclei of Seyfert 2s also support this picture.
</p><p>At higher luminosities, quasars take the place of Seyfert 1s, but, as already mentioned, the corresponding 'quasar 2s' are elusive at present. If they do not have the scattering component of Seyfert 2s they would be hard to detect except through their luminous narrow-line and hard X-ray emission.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio-loud_unification">Radio-loud unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Radio-loud unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, work on radio-loud unification has concentrated on high-luminosity radio-loud quasars. These can be unified with narrow-line radio galaxies in a manner directly analogous to the Seyfert 1/2 unification (but without the complication of much in the way of a reflection component: narrow-line radio galaxies show no nuclear optical continuum or reflected X-ray component, although they do occasionally show polarized broad-line emission). The large-scale radio structures of these objects provide compelling evidence that the orientation-based unified models really are true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> X-ray evidence, where available, supports the unified picture: radio galaxies show evidence of obscuration from a torus, while quasars do not, although care must be taken since radio-loud objects also have a soft unabsorbed jet-related component, and high resolution is necessary to separate out thermal emission from the sources' large-scale hot-gas environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> At very small angles to the line of sight, relativistic beaming dominates, and we see a blazar of some variety.
</p><p>However, the population of radio galaxies is completely dominated by low-luminosity, low-excitation objects. These do not show strong nuclear emission lines — broad or narrow — they have optical continua which appear to be entirely jet-related,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chiaberge_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-chiaberge-24">[24]</a></sup> and their X-ray emission is also consistent with coming purely from a jet, with no heavily absorbed nuclear component in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hardcastle_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-hardcastle-25">[25]</a></sup> These objects cannot be unified with quasars, even though they include some high-luminosity objects when looking at radio emission, since the torus can never hide the narrow-line region to the required extent, and since infrared studies show that they have no hidden nuclear component:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> in fact there is no evidence for a torus in these objects at all. Most likely, they form a separate class in which only jet-related emission is important. At small angles to the line of sight, they will appear as BL Lac objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_radio-quiet_unification">Criticism of the radio-quiet unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism of the radio-quiet unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the recent literature on AGN, being subject to an intense debate, an increasing set of observations appear to be in conflict with some of the key predictions of the Unified Model, e.g. that each Seyfert 2 has an obscured Seyfert 1 nucleus (a hidden broad-line region).
</p><p>Therefore, one cannot know whether the gas in all Seyfert 2 galaxies is ionized due to photoionization from a single, non-stellar continuum source in the center or due to shock-ionization from e.g. intense, nuclear starbursts. Spectropolarimetric studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> reveal that only 50% of Seyfert 2s show a hidden broad-line region and thus split Seyfert 2 galaxies into two populations. The two classes of populations appear to differ by their luminosity, where the Seyfert 2s without a hidden broad-line region are generally less luminous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This suggests absence of broad-line region is connected to low Eddington ratio, and not to obscuration.
</p><p>The covering factor of the torus might play an important role. Some torus models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> predict how Seyfert 1s and Seyfert 2s can obtain different covering factors from a luminosity- and accretion rate- dependence of the torus covering factor, something supported by studies in the x-ray of AGN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The models also suggest an accretion-rate dependence of the broad-line region and provide a natural evolution from more active engines in Seyfert 1s to more “dead” Seyfert 2s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and can explain the observed break-down of the unified model at low luminosities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and the evolution of the broad-line region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>While studies of single AGN show important deviations from the expectations of the unified model, results from statistical tests have been contradictory. The most important short-coming of statistical tests by direct comparisons of statistical samples of Seyfert 1s and Seyfert 2s is the introduction of selection biases due to anisotropic selection criteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Studying neighbour galaxies rather than the AGN themselves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> first suggested the numbers of neighbours were larger for Seyfert 2s than for Seyfert 1s, in contradiction with the Unified Model. Today, having overcome the previous limitations of small sample sizes and anisotropic selection, studies of neighbours of hundreds to thousands of AGN<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> have shown that the neighbours of Seyfert 2s are intrinsically dustier and more star-forming than Seyfert 1s and a connection between AGN type, host galaxy morphology and collision history. Moreover, angular clustering studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> of the two AGN types confirm that they reside in different environments and show that they reside within dark matter halos of different masses. The AGN environment studies are in line with evolution-based unification models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> where Seyfert 2s transform into Seyfert 1s during merger, supporting earlier models of merger-driven activation of Seyfert 1 nuclei.
</p><p>While controversy about the soundness of each individual study still prevails, they all agree on that the simplest viewing-angle based models of AGN Unification are incomplete. Seyfert-1 and Seyfert-2 seem to differ in star formation and AGN engine power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>While it still might be valid that an obscured Seyfert 1 can appear as a Seyfert 2, not all Seyfert 2s must host an obscured Seyfert 1. Understanding whether it is the same engine driving all Seyfert 2s, the connection to radio-loud AGN, the mechanisms of the variability of some AGN that vary between the two types at very short time scales, and the connection of the AGN type to small- and large-scale environment remain important issues to incorporate into any unified model of active galactic nuclei.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmological_uses_and_evolution">Cosmological uses and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_galactic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cosmological uses and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For a long time, active galaxies held all the records for the highest-<a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> objects known either in the optical or the radio spectrum, because of their high luminosity. They still have a role to play in studies of the early universe, but it is now recognised that an AGN gives a highly biased picture of the "typical" high-redshift galaxy.
</p><p>Most luminous classes of AGN (radio-loud and radio-quiet) seem to have been much more numerous in the early universe. This suggests  that massive black holes formed early on and that the conditions for the formation of luminous AGN were more common in the early universe, such as a much higher availability of cold gas near the centre of galaxies than at present. It also implies that many objects that were once luminous quasars are now much less luminous, or entirely quiescent. The evolution of the low-luminosity AGN population is much less well understood due to the difficulty of observing these objects at high redshifts.
</p>
