<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mariner program</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2400" decoding="async" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg/220px-Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg/330px-Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg/440px-Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlas_Agena_with_Mariner_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch of <a href="/wiki/Mariner_1" title="Mariner 1">Mariner 1</a> in 1962</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Mariner program</b> was a 10-mission program conducted by the American space agency <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> (JPL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M2_final_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-M2_final-1">[1]</a></sup> The program launched a series of <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">interplanetary probes</a>, from 1962 to 1973, designed to investigate <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M_program_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-M_program-2">[2]</a></sup> The program included a number of firsts, including the first <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">planetary flyby</a>, the first planetary <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a>, and the first <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a> maneuver.
</p><p>Of the ten vehicles in the Mariner series, seven were successful, forming the starting point for many subsequent NASA/JPL space probe programs. The planned Mariner Jupiter-Saturn vehicles were adapted into the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager program</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butrica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-butrica-3">[3]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking program</a> orbiters were enlarged versions of the <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a> spacecraft.  Later Mariner-based spacecraft include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magellan_spacecraft" title="Magellan spacecraft">Magellan</a> probe and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">Galileo</a> probe, while the second-generation <a href="/wiki/Mariner_Mark_II" title="Mariner Mark II">Mariner Mark II</a> series evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> probe.
</p><p>The total cost of the Mariner program was approximately $554 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The name of the Mariner program was decided in "May 1960-at the suggestion of Edgar M. Cortright" to have the "planetary mission probes ... patterned after nautical terms, to convey "the impression of travel to great distances and remote lands."" That "decision was the basis for naming Mariner, Ranger, Surveyor, and Viking probes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_layout">Basic layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2206" data-file-width="2832" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg/300px-Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg/450px-Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg/600px-Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Receives_Mariner_2_Model.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center> This 1963 photo shows Dr. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Hayward_Pickering" title="William Hayward Pickering">William H. NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James Webb</a> is standing directly behind the Mariner model</center></div></div></div>
<p>All Mariner spacecraft were based on a hexagonal or octagonal "<a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a>", which housed all of the electronics, and to which all components were attached, such as antennae, cameras, propulsion, and power sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M_program_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-M_program-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-M2-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mariner_2" title="Mariner 2">Mariner 2</a> was based on the <a href="/wiki/Ranger_program" title="Ranger program">Ranger</a> Lunar probe. All of the Mariners launched after Mariner 2 had four solar panels for power, except for <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a>, which had two.  Additionally, all except <a href="/wiki/Mariner_1" title="Mariner 1">Mariner 1</a>, Mariner 2 and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_5" title="Mariner 5">Mariner 5</a> had TV cameras.
</p><p>The first five Mariners were launched on <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">Atlas-Agena</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>, while the last five used the <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Centaur" title="Atlas-Centaur">Atlas-Centaur</a>.  All Mariner-based probes after <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a> used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_III" title="Titan III">Titan IIIE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a> unmanned rockets or the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> with a solid-fueled <a href="/wiki/Inertial_Upper_Stage" title="Inertial Upper Stage">Inertial Upper Stage</a> and multiple planetary flybys.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariners_1_and_2">Mariners 1 and 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mariners 1 and 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_1" title="Mariner 1">Mariner 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_2" title="Mariner 2">Mariner 2</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2.jpg"><img alt="Mariner 2.jpg" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="3029" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Mariner_2.jpg/150px-Mariner_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Mariner_2.jpg/225px-Mariner_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Mariner_2.jpg/300px-Mariner_2.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mariner_1" title="Mariner 1">Mariner 1</a> (P-37) and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_2" title="Mariner 2">Mariner 2</a> (P-38) were two deep-space probes making up NASA's Mariner-R project. The primary goal of the project was to develop and launch two spacecraft sequentially to the near vicinity of Venus, receive communications from the spacecraft and to perform radiometric temperature measurements of the planet. A secondary objective was to make <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a> and/or particle measurements on the way to, and in the vicinity of, Venus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MR_tracking_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MR_tracking-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MR_craft_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MR_craft-8">[8]</a></sup>  Mariner 1 (designated Mariner R-1) was launched on July 22, 1962, but was destroyed approximately 5 minutes after liftoff by the Air Force <a href="/wiki/Range_safety#Range_Safety_Officer" title="Range safety">Range Safety Officer</a> when its malfunctioning <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">Atlas-Agena</a> rocket went off course. Mariner 2 (designated Mariner R-2) was launched on August 27, 1962, sending it on a 3½-month flight to Venus. The mission was a success, and Mariner 2 became the first spacecraft to have flown by another planet.
</p>
<ul><li>Mission: Venus flyby</li>
<li>weight: 203 kg (446 lb)</li>
<li>Sensors: <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiometers, cosmic dust, solar <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> and high-energy <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a></li></ul>
<p>Status:
</p>
<ul><li>Mariner 1 – Destroyed shortly after liftoff.</li>
<li>Mariner 2 – Defunct after successful mission, occupies a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariners_3_and_4">Mariners 3 and 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mariners 3 and 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_3" title="Mariner 3">Mariner 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_4" title="Mariner 4">Mariner 4</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_3_and_4.jpg"><img alt="Mariner 3 and 4.jpg" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mariner_3_and_4.jpg/150px-Mariner_3_and_4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mariner_3_and_4.jpg/225px-Mariner_3_and_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mariner_3_and_4.jpg/300px-Mariner_3_and_4.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div><p> Sisterships <a href="/wiki/Mariner_3" title="Mariner 3">Mariner 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_4" title="Mariner 4">Mariner 4</a> were Mars flyby missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M3-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mariner 3 was lost when the launch vehicle's nose fairing failed to jettison.
</p><p>Mariner 4, launched on November 28, 1964, was the first successful flyby of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and gave the first glimpse of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> at close range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M3-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Mission: Mars flyby<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M3-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mass: 261 kg (575 lb)</li>
<li>Sensors: <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a> with digital tape recorder (about 20 pictures), <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a>, solar plasma, trapped radiation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_fields" title="Magnetic fields">magnetic fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_occultation" title="Radio occultation">radio occultation</a> and celestial mechanics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M4-10">[10]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Status:
</p>
<ul><li>Mariner 3 – Malfunctioned. Derelict in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M3_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M3-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mariner 4 – Communications lost after bombardment by micrometeoroids.  Derelict in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M4_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M4-10">[10]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariner_5">Mariner 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mariner 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_5" title="Mariner 5">Mariner 5</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_5.jpg"><img alt="Mariner 5.jpg" data-file-height="445" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Mariner_5.jpg/150px-Mariner_5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Mariner_5.jpg/225px-Mariner_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Mariner_5.jpg/300px-Mariner_5.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mariner_5" title="Mariner 5">Mariner 5</a> spacecraft was launched to <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> on June 14, 1967 and arrived in the vicinity of the planet in October 1967. It carried a complement of experiments to probe <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>' <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a>, scan its brightness in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>, and sample the solar particles and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> fluctuations above the planet.
</p><ul><li>Mission: <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> flyby</li>
<li>Mass: 245 kg (540 lb)</li>
<li>Sensors: ultraviolet photometer, cosmic dust, solar plasma, trapped radiation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_fields" title="Magnetic fields">magnetic fields</a>, radio occultation and celestial mechanics</li></ul>
<p>Status:
Mariner 5 – Defunct. Now in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">Heliocentric orbit</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariners_6_and_7">Mariners 6 and 7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mariners 6 and 7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_6_and_7" title="Mariner 6 and 7">Mariner 6 and 7</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_6and7.gif"><img alt="Mariner 6and7.gif" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="445" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mariner_6and7.gif/150px-Mariner_6and7.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mariner_6and7.gif/225px-Mariner_6and7.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Mariner_6and7.gif/300px-Mariner_6and7.gif 2x" width="150"/></a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Mariner_6_and_7" title="Mariner 6 and 7">Mariners 6 and 7</a> were identical teammates in a two-spacecraft mission to Mars. Mariner 6 was launched on February 24, 1969, followed by Mariner 7 on March 21, 1969. They flew over the equator and southern hemisphere of the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><ul><li>Mission: <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> flybys</li>
<li>Mass 413 kg (908 lb)</li>
<li>Sensors: wide- and narrow-angle cameras with digital tape recorder, infrared spectrometer and radiometer, ultraviolet spectrometer, radio occultation and celestial mechanics.</li></ul>
<p>Status:
</p>
<ul><li>Mariner 6 – Defunct. Now in a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">Heliocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mariner 7 – Defunct. Now in a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">Heliocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M6&amp;7-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariners_8_and_9">Mariners 8 and 9</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mariners 8 and 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_8" title="Mariner 8">Mariner 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner09.jpg"><img alt="Mariner09.jpg" data-file-height="2242" data-file-width="2995" decoding="async" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Mariner09.jpg/150px-Mariner09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Mariner09.jpg/225px-Mariner09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Mariner09.jpg/300px-Mariner09.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Mariner_8" title="Mariner 8">Mariner 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a> were identical sister craft designed to map the Martian surface simultaneously, but Mariner 8 was lost in a launch vehicle failure. Its identical sister craft, Mariner 9, was launched in May 1971 and became the first artificial satellite of Mars. It entered Martian orbit in November 1971 and began photographing the surface and analyzing the atmosphere with its infrared and ultraviolet instruments. 
</p><ul><li>Mission: orbit Mars</li>
<li>Mass 998 kg (2,200 lb)</li>
<li>Sensors: wide- and narrow-angle cameras with digital tape recorder, infrared spectrometer and radiometer, ultraviolet spectrometer, radio occultation and celestial mechanics</li></ul>
<p>Status:
</p>
<ul><li>Mariner 8 – Destroyed in a launch vehicle failure.</li>
<li>Mariner 9 – Shut off. In Areocentric (Mars) orbit until at least 2022 when it is projected to fall out of orbit and into the Martian atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariner_10">Mariner 10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mariner 10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a></div>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_10.jpg"><img alt="Mariner 10.jpg" data-file-height="2208" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Mariner_10.jpg/150px-Mariner_10.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Mariner_10.jpg/225px-Mariner_10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Mariner_10.jpg/300px-Mariner_10.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a> spacecraft launched on November 3, 1973 and was the first to use a gravity assist trajectory, accelerating as it entered the gravitational influence of Venus, then being flung by the planet's gravity onto a slightly different course to reach Mercury. It was also the first spacecraft to encounter two planets at close range, and for 33 years the only spacecraft to photograph Mercury in closeup.
</p><ul><li>Mission: plasma, charged particles, magnetic fields, radio occultation and celestial mechanics</li></ul>
<p>Status: Mariner 10 – Defunct. Now in a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">Heliocentric orbit</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mariner_Jupiter-Saturn">Mariner Jupiter-Saturn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mariner Jupiter-Saturn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager program</a></div>
<p>Mariner Jupiter-Saturn was approved in 1972 after the cancellation of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour_program" title="Grand Tour program">Grand Tour program</a>, which proposed visiting all the outer planets with multiple spacecraft. The Mariner Jupiter-Saturn program proposed two Mariner-derived probes that would perform a scaled back mission involving flybys of only the two gas giants, though designers at JPL built the craft with the intention that further encounters past Saturn would be an option. Trajectories were chosen to allow one probe to visit Jupiter and Saturn first, and perform a flyby of Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> to gather information about the moon's substantial atmosphere. The other probe would arrive at Jupiter and Saturn later, and its trajectory would enable it to continue on to <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> assuming the first probe accomplished all its objectives, or be redirected to perform a Titan flyby if necessary. The program's name was changed to <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager</a> just before launch in 1977, and after <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a> successfully completed its Titan encounter, <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> went on to visit the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_giants" title="Ice giants">ice giants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butrica_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-butrica-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
