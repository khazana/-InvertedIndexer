<td>Selection of astronomical bodies and objects
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>An <b>astronomical object</b> or <b>celestial object</b> is a naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">physical entity</a>, association, or structure that exists in the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the terms <i>object</i> and <i>body</i> are often used interchangeably. However, an <b>astronomical body</b> or <b>celestial body</b> is a single, tightly bound, contiguous entity, while an astronomical or celestial <i>object</i> is a complex, less cohesively bound structure, which may consist of multiple bodies or even other objects with substructures.
</p><p>Examples of astronomical objects include <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> are astronomical bodies. A <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> may be identified as both body and object: It is a <i>body</i> when referring to the frozen <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">nucleus</a> of ice and dust, and an <i>object</i> when describing the entire comet with its diffuse <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comet_tail" title="Comet tail">tail</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxy_and_larger">Galaxy and larger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_object&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Galaxy and larger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> can be viewed as having a hierarchical structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At the largest scales, the fundamental component of assembly is the <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>. Galaxies are organized into <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters" title="Galaxy groups and clusters">groups and clusters</a>, often within larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superclusters" title="Superclusters">superclusters</a>, that are strung along great <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_filament" title="Galaxy filament">filaments</a> between nearly empty <a href="/wiki/Void_(astronomy)" title="Void (astronomy)">voids</a>, forming a web that spans the observable universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smolin1998_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-smolin1998-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The universe has a variety of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification" title="Galaxy morphological classification">morphologies</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Irregular_galaxy" title="Irregular galaxy">irregular</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disc_galaxy" title="Disc galaxy">disk-like</a> shapes, depending on their formation and evolutionary histories, including <a href="/wiki/Interacting_galaxy" title="Interacting galaxy">interaction</a> with other galaxies, which may lead to a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_merger" title="Galaxy merger">merger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Disc galaxies encompass <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy" title="Lenticular galaxy">lenticular</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxies" title="Spiral galaxies">spiral galaxies</a> with features, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_arms" title="Spiral arms">spiral arms</a> and a distinct <a href="/wiki/Galactic_halo" title="Galactic halo">halo</a>. At the core, most galaxies have a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>, which may result in an <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nucleus</a>. Galaxies can also have satellites in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxies" title="Dwarf galaxies">dwarf galaxies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular clusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Within_a_galaxy">Within a galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_object&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Within a galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The constituents of a galaxy are formed out of gaseous matter that assembles through gravitational self-attraction in a hierarchical manner. At this level, the resulting fundamental components are the stars, which are typically assembled in clusters from the various condensing nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The great variety of stellar forms are determined almost entirely by the mass, composition and evolutionary state of these stars. Stars may be found in multi-star systems that orbit about each other in a hierarchical organization. A planetary system and various minor objects such as asteroids, comets and debris, can form in a hierarchical process of accretion from the <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disks</a> that surrounds newly formed stars.
</p><p>The various distinctive types of stars are shown by the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a> (H–R diagram)—a plot of absolute stellar luminosity versus surface temperature. Each star follows an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolutionary_track" title="Evolutionary track">evolutionary track</a> across this diagram. If this track takes the star through a region containing an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrinsic_variable" title="Intrinsic variable">intrinsic variable</a> type, then its physical properties can cause it to become a <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable star</a>. An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Instability_strip" title="Instability strip">instability strip</a>, a region of the H-R diagram that includes <a href="/wiki/Delta_Scuti_variable" title="Delta Scuti variable">Delta Scuti</a>, <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">RR Lyrae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variables</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_et_al2004_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_et_al2004-7">[7]</a></sup> Depending on the initial mass of the star and the presence or absence of a companion, a star may spend the last part of its life as a <a href="/wiki/Compact_star" title="Compact star">compact object</a>; either a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>.
</p>
