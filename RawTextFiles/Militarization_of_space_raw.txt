<p>The <b>militarisation of space</b> is the placement and development of weaponry and military technology in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The early exploration of space in the mid-20th century had, in part, a military motivation, as the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> used it as an opportunity to demonstrate <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missile</a> technology and other technologies having the potential for military application. Outer space has since been used as an operating location for <a href="/wiki/Military_satellite" title="Military satellite">military spacecraft</a> such as imaging and communications satellites, and some ballistic missiles pass through outer space during their flight. As yet, however, weapons are not known to have been stationed in space, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Almaz#Defense_measures" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> space station and the <a href="/wiki/TP-82_Cosmonaut_survival_pistol" title="TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol">TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol</a> (for post-landing, pre-recovery use).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cold_War">The Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a></div>
<p>During the Cold War, the world's two great superpowers — the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> — spent large proportions of their <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> on developing military technologies.  The drive to place objects in orbit stimulated space research and started the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>.  In 1957, the USSR launched the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellite</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a></i>.
</p><p>By the end of the 1960s, both countries regularly deployed satellites. <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">Reconnaissance satellites</a> were used by militaries to take accurate pictures of their rivals' military installations.  As time passed the resolution and accuracy of orbital reconnaissance alarmed both sides of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_curtain" title="Iron curtain">iron curtain</a>.  Both the United States and the Soviet Union began to develop <a href="/wiki/Anti-satellite_weapon" title="Anti-satellite weapon">anti-satellite weapons</a> to blind or destroy each other's satellites.  <a href="/wiki/Directed-energy_weapon" title="Directed-energy weapon">Directed-energy weapons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze" title="Kamikaze">kamikaze</a>-style satellites, as well as orbital nuclear explosion were researched with varying levels of success.  Spy satellites were, and continue to be, used to monitor the dismantling of military assets in accordance with arms control treaties signed between the two superpowers. To use spy satellites in such a manner is often referred to in treaties as "national technical means of verification".
</p><p>The superpowers developed <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a> to enable them to use <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weaponry</a> across great distances. As rocket science developed, the range of missiles increased and <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a> (ICBM) were created, which could strike virtually any target on Earth in a timeframe measured in minutes rather than hours or days.  In order to cover large distances ballistic missiles are usually launched into <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg/200px-Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg/300px-Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg/400px-Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peacekeeper-missile-testing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Test of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LG-118A_Peacekeeper" title="LG-118A Peacekeeper">LG-118A Peacekeeper</a> missile, each one of which could carry 10 independently targeted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_warhead" title="Nuclear warhead">nuclear warheads</a> along trajectories outside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>As soon as intercontinental missiles were developed, military planners began programmes and strategies to counter their effectiveness.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="USA">USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Nike#Nike_Zeus" title="Project Nike">Project Nike § Nike Zeus</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Project_Defender&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Project Defender (page does not exist)">Project Defender</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sentinel_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sentinel Program (page does not exist)">Sentinel Program</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safeguard_Program" title="Safeguard Program">Safeguard Program</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_A119" title="Project A119">Project A119</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Horizon" title="Project Horizon">Project Horizon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lunex_Project" title="Lunex Project">Lunex Project</a></div>
<p>Early American efforts included the <a href="/wiki/Project_Nike#Nike_Zeus" title="Project Nike">Nike-Zeus</a> Program, <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_missile_defense#Project_Defender" title="United States national missile defense">Project Defender</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sentinel_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sentinel Program (page does not exist)">Sentinel Program</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Safeguard_Program" title="Safeguard Program">Safeguard Program</a>.  The late 1950s Nike-Zeus programme involved firing Nike nuclear missiles against oncoming ICBMs, thus exploding nuclear warheads over the North Pole.  This idea was soon scrapped and work began on Project Defender in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-1">[1]</a></sup>  Project Defender attempted to destroy Soviet ICBMs at launch with satellite weapon systems, which orbited over Russia.  This programme proved infeasible with the technology from that era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-1">[1]</a></sup>  Work then began on the Sentinel Program which used <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">anti-ballistic missiles</a> (ABM) to shoot down incoming ICBMs.
</p><p>In the late 1950s <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> considered detonating an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> to display U.S. superiority to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the rest of the world (<a href="/wiki/Project_A119" title="Project A119">Project A119</a>).  In 1959, a feasibility study of a possible military base on the Moon (<a href="/wiki/Project_Horizon" title="Project Horizon">Project Horizon</a>) was conducted.  In 1958, a plan for a 21-airman underground Air Force base on the Moon by 1968 was developed (<a href="/wiki/Lunex_Project" title="Lunex Project">Lunex Project</a>).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Safeguard_Program" title="Safeguard Program">Safeguard Program</a> was deployed in the mid-1970s and was based on the Sentinel Program.  Since the ABM treaty only allowed for construction of a single ABM facility to protect either the nation's capital city or an ICBM field, the <a href="/wiki/Stanley_R._Mickelsen_Safeguard_Complex" title="Stanley R. Mickelsen Safeguard Complex">Stanley R. Mickelsen Safeguard Complex</a> was constructed near <a href="/wiki/Nekoma,_North_Dakota" title="Nekoma, North Dakota">Nekoma, North Dakota</a> to protect the <a href="/wiki/321st_Missile_Wing_LGM-30_Minuteman_Missile_Launch_Sites" title="321st Missile Wing LGM-30 Minuteman Missile Launch Sites">Grand Forks ICBM facility</a>.  Though it was only operational as an ABM facility for less than a year, the Perimeter Acquisition Radar (PAR), one of Safeguard's components, was still operational as of 2005.  One major problem with the Safeguard Program, and past ABM systems, was that the interceptor missiles, though state-of-the-art, required nuclear warheads to destroy incoming ICBMs.  Future ABMs will likely be more accurate and use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hit-to-kill" title="Hit-to-kill">hit-to-kill</a> or conventional warheads to knock down incoming warheads.  The technology involved in such systems was shaky at best, and deployment was limited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABM_treaty" title="ABM treaty">ABM treaty</a> of 1972.
</p><p>In 1983, American president <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> proposed the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a> (SDI), a space-based system to protect the United States from attack by strategic nuclear missiles. The plan was ridiculed by some as unrealistic and expensive, and Dr Carol Rosin nicknamed the policy "Star Wars", after <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">the popular science-fiction movie franchise</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Astronomer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> pointed out that in order to defeat SDI, the Soviet Union had only to build more missiles, allowing them to overcome the defence by sheer force of numbers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Proponents of SDI said the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategy_of_technology" title="Strategy of technology">strategy of technology</a> would hasten the Soviet Union's downfall.  According to this doctrine, Communist leaders were forced to either shift large portions of their GDP to counter SDI, or else watch as their expensive nuclear stockpiles were rendered obsolete.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Space_Command" title="United States Space Command">United States Space Command</a> (USSPACECOM), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Combatant_Command" title="Unified Combatant Command">unified command</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_military" title="United States military">United States military</a> was created in 1985 to help institutionalise the use of <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> by the United States Armed Forces.  The Commander in Chief of U.S. Space Command (CINCUSSPACECOM), with headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Peterson_Air_Force_Base" title="Peterson Air Force Base">Peterson Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> was also the Commander in Chief of the bi-national U.S.-Canadian <i><a href="/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="North American Aerospace Defense Command">North American Aerospace Defense Command</a></i> (CINCNORAD) and for the majority of time during USSPACECOM’s existence also the Commander of the U.S. Air Force major command <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Space_Command" title="Air Force Space Command">Air Force Space Command</a>.  Military space operations coordinated by USSPACECOM proved to be very valuable for the U.S.-led coalition in the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a>.
</p><p>The U.S. military has relied on communications, intelligence, navigation, missile warning and weather satellite systems in areas of conflict since the early 1990s, including the Balkans, Southwest Asia and Afghanistan.  Space systems are considered indispensable providers of tactical information to U.S. war-fighters.
</p><p>As part of the ongoing initiative to transform the U.S. military, on 26 June 2002, Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a> announced that U.S. Space Command would merge with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSTRATCOM" title="USSTRATCOM">USSTRATCOM</a>.  The UCP directed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Combatant_Command" title="Unified Combatant Command">Unified Combatant Commands</a> be capped at ten, and with the formation of the new <a href="/wiki/United_States_Northern_Command" title="United States Northern Command">United States Northern Command</a>, one would have to be deactivated in order to maintain that level.  Thus the USSPACECOM merger into USSTRATCOM.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="USSR">USSR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: USSR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fractional_Orbital_Bombardment_System" title="Fractional Orbital Bombardment System">Fractional Orbital Bombardment System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:317px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polo_(Polyus).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="852" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polo_%28Polyus%29.png/315px-Polo_%28Polyus%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polo_%28Polyus%29.png/472px-Polo_%28Polyus%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polo_%28Polyus%29.png/629px-Polo_%28Polyus%29.png 2x" width="315"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polo_(Polyus).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> (1987).</center></div></div></div>
<p>The Soviet Union was also researching innovative ways of gaining space supremacy.  Two of their most notable efforts were the <a href="/wiki/R-36_(missile)#R-36ORB" title="R-36 (missile)">R-36ORB</a> <a href="/wiki/Fractional_Orbital_Bombardment_System" title="Fractional Orbital Bombardment System">Fractional Orbital Bombardment System</a> (FOBS) and <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> orbital weapons system.
</p><p>The R-36ORB was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBM</a> in the 1960s that, once launched, would go into a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> whereupon it would de-orbit for an attack. This system would approach North America over the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, thereby striking targets from the opposite direction from that to which <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command" title="North American Aerospace Defense Command">NORAD</a> early warning systems are oriented.  The missile was phased out in January 1983 in compliance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SALT_II" title="SALT II">SALT II</a> treaty.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SALT_II" title="SALT II">SALT II</a> treaty (1979) prohibited the deployment of FOBS systems:
</p>
<dl><dd><i> Each Party undertakes not to develop, test, or deploy: </i></dd>
<dd><i>(...)</i></dd>
<dd><i>(c) systems for placing into Earth orbit <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> or any other kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>, including fractional orbital missiles;</i></dd></dl>
<p>On May 15, 1987, an <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">Energia</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> flew for the first time. The payload was a prototype orbital weapons platform <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> (also known as Polus, Skif-DM or 17F19DM), the final version of which according to some reports could be armed with nuclear space mines and defensive cannon. The Polyus weapons platform was designed to defend itself against anti-satellite weapons with recoilless cannon.  It was also equipped with a sensor blinding laser to confuse approaching weapons and could launch test targets to validate the fire control system. The attempt to place the satellite into orbit failed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Cold_War">Post-Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Navy_Theater_Ballistic_Missile_Defense.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Navy_Theater_Ballistic_Missile_Defense.JPG/230px-Navy_Theater_Ballistic_Missile_Defense.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/Navy_Theater_Ballistic_Missile_Defense.JPG 1.5x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Navy_Theater_Ballistic_Missile_Defense.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Lightweight Exo-Atmospheric Projectile (LEAP), which attaches to a modified SM-2 Block IV missile used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a></div></div></div>
<p>As the Cold War ended with the implosion of the Soviet Union the space race between the two superpowers ended.  The United States of America was left as the only superpower on Earth with a large concentration of the world's wealth and technological advancement.  Despite the United States' new status in the world, the monopoly of space militarisation is in no way certain.  Countries such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> have begun their own space programmes, while the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> collectively works to create satellite systems to rival those of the United States.
</p><p>The USSR Space Forces were established as the Ministry of Defense Space Units in 1982. In 1991 the Soviet Union disintegrated. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Armed Forces of the Russian Federation">Russian Armed Forces</a> were established on 7 May 1992, enabling the creation of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Forces" title="Russian Space Forces">Russian Space Forces</a> later that year on 10 August. In July 1997 the Space Force was dissolved as a separate service arm and incorporated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Rocket_Forces" title="Strategic Rocket Forces">Strategic Rocket Forces</a> along with the Space Missile Defense Forces, which previously were part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Defense_Forces" title="Soviet Air Defense Forces">Troops of Air Defense</a>. The Russian Space Forces were officially reborn on June 1, 2001 as an independent section of the Russian military.
</p><p>Post Cold War space militarisation seems to revolve around three types of applications. (The word "seems" is used because much of this subject matter is inconclusively verifiable, due to the high level of secrecy that exists among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_powers" title="Great powers">great powers</a> with regard to the details of space sensing systems.)  The first application is the continuing development of "spy" or reconnaissance satellites which began in the Cold War era, but has progressed significantly since that time.  Spy satellites perform a variety of missions such as high resolution photography (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMINT" title="IMINT">IMINT</a>), communications eavesdropping (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIGINT" title="SIGINT">SIGINT</a>), and covert communications (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HUMINT" title="HUMINT">HUMINT</a>).  These tasks are performed on a regular basis both during peacetime and war operations. Satellites are also used by the nuclear states to provide early warning of missile launches, locate nuclear detonations, and detect preparations for otherwise clandestine or surprise nuclear tests (at least those tests or preparations carried out above-ground); this was the case when, in 1998, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> both conducted a series of nuclear tests; in addition, a nuclear-detection satellite of the <a href="/wiki/Vela_(satellite)" title="Vela (satellite)">Vela</a> type was also reported to have detected a nuclear detonation in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> in 1978 that was believed to be a <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> nuclear test in what was famously called the <a href="/wiki/Vela_Incident" title="Vela Incident">Vela Incident</a>. Early-warning satellites can also be used to detect tactical missile launches; this capability was used during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desert_Storm" title="Desert Storm">Desert Storm</a> when America was able to provide advance warning to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> of Iraqi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-1_SCUD" title="SS-1 SCUD">SS-1 SCUD</a> missile launches.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Positioning_Systems">Global Positioning Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global Positioning Systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a>, <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a>, <a href="/wiki/BeiDou_Navigation_Satellite_System" title="BeiDou Navigation Satellite System">BeiDou Navigation Satellite System</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Regional_Navigational_Satellite_System" title="Indian Regional Navigational Satellite System">Indian Regional Navigational Satellite System</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="806" data-file-width="1006" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg/220px-GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg/330px-GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg/440px-GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GPS_Satellite_NASA_art-iif.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of a <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> satellite in Earth orbit.</div></div></div>
<p>The second application of space militarisation currently in use is GPS or <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a>.   This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation_system" title="Satellite navigation system">satellite navigation system</a> is used for determining one's precise location and providing a highly accurate <a href="/wiki/Time_transfer" title="Time transfer">time reference</a> almost anywhere on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> or in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_orbit" title="Earth orbit">Earth orbit</a>. It uses an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermediate_circular_orbit" title="Intermediate circular orbit">intermediate circular orbit</a> (ICO) <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">satellite constellation</a> of at least 24 satellites.  The GPS system was designed by and is controlled by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> and can be used by anyone, free of charge. The cost of maintaining the system is approximately US$400 million per year, including the replacement of ageing satellites. The first of 24 satellites that form the current GPS constellation (Block II) was placed into orbit on February 14, 1989. The 52nd GPS satellite since the beginning in 1978 was launched November 6, 2004 aboard a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> rocket.
The primary military purposes are to allow improved command and control of forces through improved location awareness, and to facilitate accurate targeting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_bombs" title="Smart bombs">smart bombs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_missiles" title="Cruise missiles">cruise missiles</a>, or other munitions. The satellites also carry nuclear detonation detectors, which form a major portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Nuclear_Detonation_Detection_System" title="United States Nuclear Detonation Detection System">United States Nuclear Detonation Detection System</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> concern about the level of control over the GPS network and commercial issues has resulted in the planned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_positioning_system" title="Galileo positioning system">Galileo positioning system</a>. Russia already operates an independent system called <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a> (global navigation system), the system operates with 24 satellites that are deployed in 3 orbital planes as opposed to the 4 GPS is deployed in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
The Chinese "Beidou" system provides China a similar regional (not global) navigation capability. India is currently developing its own regional satellite navigation system, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Regional_Navigational_Satellite_System" title="Indian Regional Navigational Satellite System">Indian Regional Navigational Satellite System</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_communication_systems">Military communication systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Military communication systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The third current application of militarisation of space can be demonstrated by the emerging military doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Network-centric_warfare" title="Network-centric warfare">network-centric warfare</a>.  Network-centric warfare relies heavily on the use of high speed communications which allows all soldiers and branches of the military to view the battlefield in real-time.  Real-time technology improves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situational_awareness" title="Situational awareness">situational awareness</a> of all of the military’s assets and commanders in a given theatre. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldier</a> in the battle zone can access satellite imagery of enemy positions two blocks away, and if necessary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-mail" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> the coordinates to a bomber or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapon_platform" title="Weapon platform">weapon platform</a> hovering overhead while the commander, hundreds of miles away, watches as the events unfold on a monitor.  This high-speed communication is facilitated by a separate <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> created by the military for the military.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Communication satellites hold this system together by creating an informational grid over the given theatre of operations.  The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> is currently working to establish a <a href="/wiki/Global_Information_Grid" title="Global Information Grid">Global Information Grid</a> to connect all military units and branches into a computerised network in order to share information and create a more efficient military.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons_in_space">Weapons in space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Weapons in space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_weapon" title="Space weapon">Space weapon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TP-82.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/TP-82.jpg/220px-TP-82.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/TP-82.jpg/330px-TP-82.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/TP-82.jpg/440px-TP-82.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TP-82.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Triple-barreled <a href="/wiki/TP-82_Cosmonaut_survival_pistol" title="TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol">TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol</a> pistol in Saint-Petersburg Artillery museum</div></div></div>
<p>Space weapons are <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Space_warfare" title="Space warfare">space warfare</a>. They include weapons that can attack space systems in orbit (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Anti-satellite_weapon" title="Anti-satellite weapon">anti-satellite weapons</a>), attack targets on the earth from space or disable missiles travelling through space. In the course of the militarisation of space, such weapons were developed mainly by the contesting <a href="/wiki/Superpower" title="Superpower">superpowers</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and some remain under development today. Space weapons are also a central theme in <a href="/wiki/Military_science_fiction" title="Military science fiction">military science fiction</a> and sci-fi <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial-type_weapons_in_space">Terrestrial-type weapons in space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Terrestrial-type weapons in space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Soviet space station <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a> was fitted with a 23mm cannon, which was successfully test fired at target satellites, at ranges from 500 to 3,000 metres (1,600 to 9,800 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olberg_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olberg-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2008, it was reported that Russian cosmonauts have regularly carried the <a href="/wiki/TP-82_Cosmonaut_survival_pistol" title="TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol">TP-82 Cosmonaut survival pistol</a> on Soyuz spacecraft, as part of the emergency landing survival kit. The intent of the weapon is to protect cosmonauts from wild animals in the event of an off-course wilderness landing. The specially designed gun is capable of firing bullets, shotgun shells, or flares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_warfare">Space warfare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Space warfare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_warfare" title="Space warfare">Space warfare</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ASAT_missile_launch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="793" decoding="async" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ASAT_missile_launch.jpg/220px-ASAT_missile_launch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ASAT_missile_launch.jpg/330px-ASAT_missile_launch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ASAT_missile_launch.jpg/440px-ASAT_missile_launch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ASAT_missile_launch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAF" title="USAF">USAF</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15 Eagle</a> launching an <a href="/wiki/ASM-135_ASAT" title="ASM-135 ASAT">ASM-135 ASAT</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-satellite_missile" title="Anti-satellite missile">anti-satellite missile</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Space warfare is combat that takes place in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>, i.e. outside the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Technically, as a distinct classification<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, it refers to <a href="/wiki/Battle" title="Battle">battles</a> where the targets themselves are in space. Space warfare therefore includes <i>ground-to-space warfare</i>, such as attacking <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> from the Earth, as well as <i>space-to-space warfare</i>, such as satellites attacking satellites.
</p><p>It does not include the use of satellites for <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">surveillance</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Military_communications" title="Military communications">military communications</a>, however useful those activities might be. It does not technically include <i>space-to-ground warfare</i>, where orbital objects attack ground, sea or air targets directly, but the public and media frequently use the term to include any conflict which includes space as a theatre of operations, regardless of the intended target. For example, a rapid delivery system in which troops are deployed from orbit might be described as "space warfare," even though the military uses the term as described above.
</p><p>A film was produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Military" title="U.S. Military">U.S. Military</a> in the early 1960s called <i>Space and National Security</i> which depicted space warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1985 to 2002 there was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Space_Command" title="United States Space Command">United States Space Command</a>, which in 2002 merged with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Strategic_Command" title="United States Strategic Command">United States Strategic Command</a>. There is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Forces_(VKS)" title="Russian Space Forces (VKS)">Russian Space Force</a>, which was established on August 10, 1992, and which became an independent section of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_military" title="Russian military">Russian military</a> on December 1, 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Only a few incidents of space warfare have occurred in world history, and all were training missions, as opposed to actions against real opposing forces. In the mid-1980s a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAF" title="USAF">USAF</a> pilot in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-15_Eagle" title="F-15 Eagle">F-15</a> successfully shot down the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P78-1" title="P78-1">P78-1</a>, a communications satellite in a 345-mile (555 km) orbit.
</p><p>In 2007 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> used a missile system to destroy one of its obsolete satellites (see <a href="/wiki/2007_Chinese_anti-satellite_missile_test" title="2007 Chinese anti-satellite missile test">2007 Chinese anti-satellite missile test</a>), and in 2008 the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> similarly destroyed its malfunctioning satellite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USA_193" title="USA 193">USA 193</a>. To date, there have been no human casualties resulting from conflict in space, nor has any ground target been successfully neutralised from orbit.
</p><p>International treaties governing space limit or regulate conflicts in space and limit the installation of weapon systems, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_treaties">Space treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Space treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Treaties are agreed to when all parties perceive a benefit from becoming a signatory participant in the treaty.  As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_assured_destruction" title="Mutually assured destruction">mutually assured destruction</a> (MAD) became the deterrent strategy between the two superpowers in the Cold War, many countries worked together to avoid extending the threat of nuclear weapons to space based launchers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_Space_Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Outer Space Treaty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a></div>
<p>The Outer Space Treaty was considered by the Legal Subcommittee in 1966.  Later that year, agreement was reached in the General Assembly.  The treaty included the following principles:
</p>
<ul><li>the exploration and use of outer space shall be carried out for the benefit and in the interests of all countries and shall be the province of all mankind;</li>
<li>outer space shall be free for exploration and use by all States;</li>
<li>outer space is not subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means;</li>
<li>States shall not place nuclear weapons or other weapons of mass destruction in orbit or on celestial bodies or station them in outer space in any other manner;</li>
<li>the Moon and other celestial bodies shall be used exclusively for peaceful purposes;</li>
<li>Astronauts shall be regarded as the envoys of mankind;</li>
<li>States shall be responsible for national space activities whether carried out by governmental or non-governmental activities;</li>
<li>States shall be liable for damage caused by their space objects; and</li>
<li>States shall avoid harmful contamination of space and celestial bodies.</li></ul>
<p>In summary, the treaty initiated the banning of signatories' placing of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> or any other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a> in orbit of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, installing them on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a> or any other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a>, or to otherwise station them in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The United States, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the Soviet Union signed the treaty and it entered into effect on October 10, 1967. As of January 1, 2005, 98 States have ratified, and an additional 27 have signed the Outer Space Treaty.
</p><p>Note that this treaty does not ban the placement of weapons in space in general, only nuclear weapons and WMD.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_Preservation_Treaty">Space Preservation Treaty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Space Preservation Treaty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Preservation_Treaty" title="Space Preservation Treaty">Space Preservation Treaty</a></div>
<p>The Space Preservation Treaty was a proposed 2006 UN General Assembly resolution against all space weapons. Only the United States of America voted against the treaty, with Israel abstaining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2008, China and Russia together submitted a draft to the UN known as the <i>Treaty on Prevention of the Placement of Weapons in Outer Space and of the Threat or Use of Force against Outer Space Objects</i> (PPWT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The US opposed the draft treaty due to security concerns over its space assets despite the treaty explicitly affirming a State's inherent right of self-defence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On December 4, 2014, the General Assembly of the UN passed two resolutions on preventing an arms race in outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first resolution, <i>Prevention of an arms race in outer space</i>, "call[s] on all States, in particular those with major space capabilities, to contribute actively to the peaceful use of outer space, prevent an arms race there, and refrain from actions contrary to that objective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup> There were 178 countries that voted in favour to none against, with 2 abstentions (Israel, United States).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The second resolution, <i>No first placement of weapons in outer space</i>, which emphasises the prevention of an arms race in space and that "other measures could contribute to ensuring that weapons were not placed in outer space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup> 126 countries voted in favour to 4 against (Georgia, Israel, Ukraine, United States), with 46 abstentions (EU member States abstained on the resolution).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="National_Missile_Defense_.28NMD.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Missile_Defense_(NMD)">National Missile Defense (NMD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Militarisation_of_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: National Missile Defense (NMD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Missile_Defense" title="National Missile Defense">National Missile Defense</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mdalogo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="189" data-file-width="200" decoding="async" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Mdalogo.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">The logo of the <a href="/wiki/Missile_Defense_Agency" title="Missile Defense Agency">Missile Defense Agency</a></div></div></div>
<p>With the fall of the Soviet Union and the end of the Cold War defence spending was reduced and space research was chiefly focused on peaceful research. American military research is focused on a more modest goal of preventing the United States from being subject to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_blackmail" title="Nuclear blackmail">nuclear blackmail</a> or nuclear terrorism by a <a href="/wiki/Rogue_state" title="Rogue state">rogue state</a>.
</p><p>On 16 December 2002, US President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> signed National Security Presidential Directive which outlined a plan to begin deployment of operational ballistic missile defence systems by 2004. The following day the US formally requested from the UK and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> use of facilities in <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fylingdales" title="RAF Fylingdales">RAF Fylingdales</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, respectively, as a part of the NMD Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The administration continued to push the programme, despite highly publicised but not unexpected trial-and-error technical failures during development and over the objections of some scientists who opposed it. The projected cost of the programme for the years 2004 to 2009 was 53 billion US dollars, making it the largest single line in <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a>'s budget.
</p><p>Missile defence does not station weapons in space, but is designed to intercept incoming warheads at a very high altitude which requires the interceptor to travel into space to achieve the intercept. These missiles are both land-based and sea-based.
</p>
