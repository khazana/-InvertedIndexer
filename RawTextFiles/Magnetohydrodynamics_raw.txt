<p><b>Magnetohydrodynamics</b> (<b>MHD</b>; <b>also magneto-fluid dynamics</b> or <b>hydro­magnetics</b>) is the study of the magnetic properties and behaviour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conduction" title="Electrical conduction">electrically conducting</a> <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a>. Examples of such magneto­fluids include <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasmas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal" title="Liquid metal">liquid metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">salt water</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>. The word "magneto­hydro­dynamics" is derived from <i>magneto-</i> meaning <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, <i>hydro-</i> meaning <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, and <i>dynamics</i> meaning movement. The field of MHD was initiated by <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">Hannes Alfvén</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> for which he received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in Physics in 1970.
</p><p>The fundamental concept behind MHD is that magnetic fields can <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">induce</a> currents in a moving conductive fluid, which in turn polarizes the fluid and reciprocally changes the magnetic field itself. The set of equations that describe MHD are a combination of the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%99s_equations" title="Maxwell’s equations">Maxwell’s equations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electro­magnetism</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> must be solved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equation" title="Simultaneous equation">simultaneously</a>, either analytically or <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerically</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1437" data-file-width="1110" decoding="async" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg/180px-M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg/270px-M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg/360px-M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M_Faraday_Th_Phillips_oil_1842.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a></div></div></div>
<p>The first recorded use of the word <i>magnetohydrodynamics</i> is by <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">Hannes Alfvén</a> in 1942:
</p>
<dl><dd>"At last some remarks are made about the transfer of momentum from the Sun to the planets, which is fundamental to the theory (§11). The importance of the magnetohydrodynamic waves in this respect are pointed out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The ebbing salty water flowing past London's <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Bridge" title="Waterloo Bridge">Waterloo Bridge</a> interacts with the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> to produce a potential difference between the two river-banks.  <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> called this effect "magneto-electric induction" and tried this experiment in 1832 but the current was too small to measure with the equipment at the time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the river bed contributed to short-circuit the signal. However, by a similar process the voltage induced by the tide in the English Channel was measured in 1851.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideal_and_resistive_MHD">Ideal and resistive MHD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ideal and resistive MHD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:T3e_troy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="360" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/T3e_troy.jpg" width="360"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">MHD Simulation of the Solar Wind</div></div></div>
<p>The simplest form of MHD, Ideal MHD, assumes that the fluid has so little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> that it can be treated as a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_conductor" title="Perfect conductor">perfect conductor</a>.  This is the limit of infinite <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_Reynolds_number" title="Magnetic Reynolds number">magnetic Reynolds number</a>. In ideal MHD, <a href="/wiki/Lenz%27s_law" title="Lenz's law">Lenz's law</a> dictates that the fluid is in a sense <i>tied</i> to the magnetic field lines. To explain, in ideal MHD a small rope-like volume of fluid surrounding a field line will continue to lie along a magnetic field line,
even as it is twisted and distorted by fluid flows in the system. This is sometimes referred to as the magnetic field lines being "frozen" in the fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
The connection between magnetic field lines and fluid in ideal MHD fixes the <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a> of the magnetic field in the fluid—for example, if a set of magnetic field lines are tied into a knot, then they will remain so as long as the fluid/plasma has negligible resistivity.  This difficulty in reconnecting magnetic field lines makes it possible to store energy by moving the fluid or the source of the magnetic field.  The energy can then become available if the conditions for ideal MHD break down, allowing <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a> that releases the stored energy from the magnetic field.
</p>

<p>The ideal MHD equations consist of the <a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation" title="Continuity equation">continuity equation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cauchy_momentum_equation" title="Cauchy momentum equation">Cauchy momentum equation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ampere%27s_Law" title="Ampere's Law">Ampere's Law</a> neglecting displacement current, and a <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">temperature evolution equation</a>.  As with any fluid description to a kinetic system, a closure approximation must be applied to highest moment of the particle distribution equation.  This is often accomplished with approximations to the heat flux through a condition of <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabaticity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Isothermal_process" title="Isothermal process">isothermality</a>.
</p><p>The main quantities which characterize the electrically conducting fluid are the bulk plasma <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocity_field" title="Velocity field">velocity field</a> <b>v</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Current_density" title="Current density">current density</a> <b>J</b>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_density" title="Mass density">mass density</a> <i>ρ</i>, and the plasma <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> <i>p</i>. The flowing electric charge in the plasma is the source of a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> <b>B</b> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> <b>E</b>. All quantities generally vary with time <i>t</i>. <a href="/wiki/Vector_operator" title="Vector operator">Vector operator</a> notation will be used, in particular ∇ is <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a>, ∇⋅ is <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">divergence</a>, and ∇× is <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a>.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability_of_ideal_MHD_to_plasmas">Applicability of ideal MHD to plasmas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applicability of ideal MHD to plasmas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ideal MHD is only strictly applicable when:
</p>
<ol><li>The plasma is strongly collisional, so that the time scale of collisions is shorter than the other characteristic times in the system, and the particle distributions are therefore close to <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">Maxwellian</a>.</li>
<li>The resistivity due to these collisions is small. In particular, the typical magnetic diffusion times over any scale length present in the system must be longer than any time scale of interest.</li>
<li>Interest in length scales much longer than the ion <a href="/wiki/Plasma_parameters#Lengths" title="Plasma parameters">skin depth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larmor_radius" title="Larmor radius">Larmor radius</a> perpendicular to the field, long enough along the field to ignore <a href="/wiki/Landau_damping" title="Landau damping">Landau damping</a>, and time scales much longer than the ion gyration time (system is smooth and slowly evolving).</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_resistivity">Importance of resistivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Importance of resistivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an imperfectly conducting fluid the magnetic field can generally move through the fluid following a <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_equation" title="Diffusion equation">diffusion law</a> with the resistivity of the plasma serving as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffusion_constant" title="Diffusion constant">diffusion constant</a>. This means that solutions to the ideal MHD equations are only applicable for a limited time for a region of a given size before diffusion becomes too important to ignore.  One can estimate the diffusion time across a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_active_region" title="Solar active region">solar active region</a> (from collisional resistivity) to be  hundreds to thousands of years, much longer than the actual lifetime of a sunspot—so it would seem reasonable to ignore the resistivity. By contrast, a meter-sized volume of seawater has a magnetic diffusion time measured in milliseconds.
</p><p>Even in physical systems – which are large and conductive enough that simple estimates of the <a href="/wiki/Lundquist_number" title="Lundquist number">Lundquist number</a> suggest that the resistivity can be ignored – resistivity may still be important: many <a href="/wiki/Instability" title="Instability">instabilities</a> exist that can increase the effective resistivity of the plasma by factors of more than a billion. The enhanced resistivity is usually the result of the formation of small scale structure like current sheets or fine scale magnetic <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_turbulence" title="Magnetohydrodynamic turbulence">turbulence</a>, introducing small spatial scales into the system over which ideal MHD is broken and magnetic diffusion can occur quickly. When this happens, magnetic reconnection may occur in the plasma to release stored magnetic energy as waves, bulk mechanical acceleration of material, <a href="/wiki/Particle_acceleration" title="Particle acceleration">particle acceleration</a>, and heat.
</p><p>Magnetic reconnection in highly conductive systems is important because it concentrates energy in time and space, so that gentle forces applied to a plasma for long periods of time can cause violent explosions and bursts of radiation.
</p><p>When the fluid cannot be considered as completely conductive, but the other conditions for ideal MHD are satisfied, it is possible to use an extended model called resistive MHD. This includes an extra term in Ohm's Law which models the collisional resistivity. Generally MHD computer simulations are at least somewhat resistive because their computational grid introduces a <a href="/wiki/Numerical_resistivity" title="Numerical resistivity">numerical resistivity</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_kinetic_effects">Importance of kinetic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Importance of kinetic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another limitation of MHD (and fluid theories in general) is that they depend on the assumption that the plasma is strongly collisional (this is the first criterion listed above), so that the time scale of collisions is shorter than the other characteristic times in the system, and the particle distributions are <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">Maxwellian</a>. This is usually not the case in fusion, space and astrophysical plasmas. When this is not the case, or the interest is in smaller spatial scales, it may be necessary to use a kinetic model which properly accounts for the non-Maxwellian shape of the distribution function.  However, because MHD is relatively simple and captures many of the important properties of plasma dynamics it is often qualitatively accurate and is therefore often the first model tried.
</p><p>Effects which are essentially kinetic and not captured by fluid models include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_layer_(plasma)" title="Double layer (plasma)">double layers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landau_damping" title="Landau damping">Landau damping</a>, a wide range of instabilities, chemical separation in space plasmas and electron runaway. In the case of ultra-high intensity laser interactions, the incredibly short timescales of energy deposition mean that hydrodynamic codes fail to capture the essential physics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structures_in_MHD_systems">Structures in MHD systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structures in MHD systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_particle_motion" title="Magnetosphere particle motion">Magnetosphere particle motion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Currents.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="732" data-file-width="898" decoding="async" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Currents.jpg/250px-Currents.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Currents.jpg/375px-Currents.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Currents.jpg/500px-Currents.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Currents.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic view of the different current systems which shape the Earth's magnetosphere</div></div></div>
<p>In many MHD systems most of the electric current is compressed into thin nearly-two-dimensional ribbons termed <a href="/wiki/Current_sheet" title="Current sheet">current sheets</a>.  These can divide the fluid into magnetic domains, inside of which the currents are relatively weak. Current sheets in
the solar corona are thought to be between a few meters and a few kilometers in thickness, which is quite thin compared to the magnetic domains (which are thousands to hundreds of thousands of kilometers across). Another example is in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>, where current sheets separate topologically distinct domains, isolating most of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions">Extensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Extensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Resistive</dt>
<dd>Resistive MHD describes magnetized fluids with finite electron diffusivity (<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \eta \neq 0}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>η<!-- η --></mi>
<mo>≠<!-- ≠ --></mo>
<mn>0</mn>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \eta \neq 0}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\eta \neq 0" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6edf61bf39a2f3346c24d6087aff2a8b0c5e6db2" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:5.43ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span>).  This diffusivity leads to a breaking in the magnetic topology; magnetic field lines can 'reconnect' when they collide.  Usually this term is small and reconnections can be handled by thinking of them as not dissimilar to <a href="/wiki/Shocks_and_discontinuities_(magnetohydrodynamics)" title="Shocks and discontinuities (magnetohydrodynamics)">shocks</a>; this process has been shown to be important in the Earth-Solar magnetic interactions.</dd>
<dt>Extended</dt>
<dd>Extended MHD describes a class of phenomena in plasmas that are higher order than resistive MHD, but which can adequately be treated with a single fluid description.  These include the effects of Hall physics, electron pressure gradients, finite Larmor Radii in the particle gyromotion, and electron inertia.</dd>
<dt>Two-fluid</dt>
<dd>Two-fluid MHD describes plasmas that include a non-negligible Hall <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a>.  As a result, the electron and ion momenta must be treated separately.  This description is more closely tied to Maxwell's equations as an evolution equation for the electric field exists.</dd>
<dt>Hall</dt>
<dd>In 1960, M. J. Lighthill criticized the applicability of ideal or resistive MHD theory for plasmas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It concerned the neglect of the "Hall current term", a frequent simplification  made in magnetic fusion theory.  Hall-magnetohydrodynamics (HMHD) takes into account this electric field description of magnetohydrodynamics.  The most important difference is that in the absence of field line breaking, the magnetic field is tied to the electrons and not to the bulk fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Electron MHD</dt>
<dd>Electron Magnetohydrodynamics (EMHD) describes small scales plasmas when electron motion is much faster than the ion one. The main effects are changes in conservation laws, additional resistivity, importance of electron inertia. Many effects of Electron MHD are similar to effects of the Two fluid MHD and the Hall MHD. EMHD is especially important for <a href="/wiki/Z-pinch" title="Z-pinch">z-pinch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_thrusters" title="Ion thrusters">ion thrusters</a>, and plasma switches.</dd>
<dt>Collisionless</dt>
<dd>MHD is also often used for collisionless plasmas. In that case the MHD equations are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Vlasov_equation" title="Vlasov equation">Vlasov equation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-space-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Reduced</dt>
<dd>By using a <a href="/wiki/Multiple-scale_analysis" title="Multiple-scale analysis">multiscale analysis</a> the (resistive) MHD equations can be reduced to a set of four closed scalar equations. This allows e.g. for more efficient numerical calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paper:hegna_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-paper:hegna-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysics">Geophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beneath the Earth's mantle lies the core, which is made up of two parts: the solid inner core and liquid outer core. Both have significant quantities of iron. The liquid outer core moves in the presence of the magnetic field and eddies are set up into the same due to the Coriolis effect. These eddies develop a magnetic field which boosts Earth's original magnetic field—a process which is self-sustaining and is called the geomagnetic dynamo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="448" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif/350px-NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif 1.5x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reversals of <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a></div></div></div></div>
<p>Based on the MHD equations, Glatzmaier and Paul Roberts have made a supercomputer model of the Earth's interior. After running the simulations for thousands of years in virtual time, the changes in Earth's magnetic field can be studied. The simulation results are in good agreement with the observations as the simulations have correctly predicted that the Earth's magnetic field flips every few hundred thousand years. During the flips, the magnetic field does not vanish altogether—it just gets more complex.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glatz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-glatz-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some monitoring stations have reported that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a> are sometimes preceded by a spike in <a href="/wiki/Ultra_low_frequency" title="Ultra low frequency">ultra low frequency</a> (ULF) activity.  A remarkable example of this occurred before the <a href="/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake" title="1989 Loma Prieta earthquake">1989 Loma Prieta earthquake</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> although a subsequent study indicates that this was little more than a sensor malfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On December 9, 2010, geoscientists announced that the <a href="/wiki/Demeter_(satellite)" title="Demeter (satellite)">DEMETER</a> satellite observed a dramatic increase in ULF radio waves over <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> in the month before the magnitude 7.0 M<sub>w</sub> <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">2010 earthquake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Researchers are attempting to learn more about this correlation to find out whether this method can be used as part of an early warning system for earthquakes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrophysics">Astrophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Astrophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MHD applies to <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, including stars, the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_medium" title="Interplanetary medium">interplanetary medium</a> (space between the planets), and possibly within the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (space between the stars) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">jets</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Most astrophysical systems are not in local thermal equilibrium, and therefore require an additional kinematic treatment to describe all the phenomena within the system (see <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_plasma" title="Astrophysical plasma">Astrophysical plasma</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">Sunspots</a> are caused by the Sun's magnetic fields, as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Larmor" title="Joseph Larmor">Joseph Larmor</a> theorized in 1919. The <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> is also governed by MHD. The differential <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">solar rotation</a> may be the long-term effect of magnetic drag at the poles of the Sun, an MHD phenomenon due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parker_spiral" title="Parker spiral">Parker spiral</a> shape assumed by the extended magnetic field of the Sun.
</p><p>Previously, theories describing the formation of the Sun and planets could not explain how the Sun has 99.87% of the mass, yet only 0.54% of the <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed system</a> such as the cloud of gas and dust from which the Sun was formed, mass and angular momentum are both <a href="/wiki/Conservation_law" title="Conservation law">conserved</a>. That conservation would imply that as the mass concentrated in the center of the cloud to form the Sun, it would spin faster, much like a skater pulling their arms in. The high speed of rotation predicted by early theories would have flung the proto-Sun apart before it could have formed. However, magnetohydrodynamic effects transfer the Sun's angular momentum into the outer solar system, slowing its rotation.
</p><p>Breakdown of ideal MHD (in the form of magnetic reconnection) is known to be the likely cause of <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The magnetic field in a solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_region" title="Active region">active region</a> over a sunspot can store energy that is released suddenly as a burst of motion, <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> when the main current sheet collapses, reconnecting the field.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensors">Sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnetohydrodynamic sensors are used for precision measurements of <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocities</a> in <a href="/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system" title="Inertial navigation system">inertial navigation systems</a> such as in <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>. Accuracy improves with the size of the sensor. The sensor is capable of surviving in harsh environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering">Engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MHD is related to engineering problems such as <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">plasma confinement</a>, liquid-metal cooling of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetic</a> casting (among others).
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_drive" title="Magnetohydrodynamic drive">magnetohydrodynamic drive</a> or MHD propulsor is a method for propelling seagoing vessels using only electric and magnetic fields with no moving parts, using magnetohydrodynamics. The working principle involves electrification of the propellant (gas or water) which can then be directed by a magnetic field, pushing the vehicle in the opposite direction. Although some working prototypes exist, MHD drives remain impractical.
</p><p>The first prototype of this kind of propulsion was built and tested in 1965 by Steward Way, a professor of mechanical engineering at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>.  Way, on leave from his job at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">Westinghouse Electric</a>, assigned his senior-year undergraduate students to develop a submarine with this new propulsion system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  In the early 1990s, a foundation in Japan (Ship &amp; Ocean Foundation (Minato-ku, Tokyo)) built an experimental boat, the <i><a href="/wiki/Yamato_1" title="Yamato 1">Yamato-1</a></i>, which used a <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_drive" title="Magnetohydrodynamic drive">magnetohydrodynamic drive</a> incorporating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a> cooled by <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">liquid helium</a>, and could travel at 15 km/h.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yamato_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-yamato-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHD_generator" title="MHD generator">MHD power generation</a> fueled by potassium-seeded coal combustion gas showed potential for more efficient energy conversion (the absence of solid moving parts allows operation at higher temperatures), but failed due to cost-prohibitive technical difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> One major engineering problem was the failure of the wall of the primary-coal combustion chamber due to abrasion.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Microfluidics" title="Microfluidics">microfluidics</a>, MHD is studied as a fluid pump for producing a continuous, nonpulsating flow in a complex microchannel design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>MHD can be implemented in the <a href="/wiki/Continuous_casting" title="Continuous casting">continuous casting</a> process of metals to suppress instabilities and control the flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Industrial MHD problems can be modeled using the open-source software <a class="external text" href="https://eof-library.com/" rel="nofollow">EOF-Library</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Two simulation examples are 3D MHD with a free surface for <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_levitation" title="Magnetic levitation">electromagnetic levitation</a> melting,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and liquid metal stirring by rotating permanent magnets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_drug_targeting">Magnetic drug targeting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Magnetic drug targeting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important task in cancer research is developing more precise methods for delivery of medicine to affected areas. One method involves the binding of medicine to biologically compatible magnetic particles (e.g. ferrofluids), which are guided to the target via careful placement of permanent magnets on the external body. Magnetohydrodynamic equations and finite element analysis are used to study the interaction between the magnetic fluid particles in the bloodstream and the external magnetic field.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
