<p><b>René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</b> (November 22, 1643 – March 19, 1687) was a 17th century <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_explorers" title="List of explorers">explorer</a> and fur trader in North America. He explored the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. He is best known for an early 1682 expedition in which he canoed the lower Mississippi River from the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_River" title="Illinois River">Illinois River</a> to the Gulf of Mexico and claimed the entire Mississippi River basin for France.
</p><p>La Salle is often credited with being the first European to traverse the Ohio River, and sometimes the Mississippi as well.  It has now been established that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Joliet" title="Louis Joliet">Joliet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Marquette" title="Jacques Marquette">Marquette</a> preceded him on the Mississippi in their journey of 1673-74, and the existing historical evidence does not indicate that La Salle ever reached the Ohio/Allegheny Valley.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sieur_de_La_Salle"><i>Sieur de La Salle</i></span></h2>
<p><i>Sieur de La Salle</i> is a French title roughly translating to "Lord of the manor", from the old French <i>sal(e)</i> (modern <i>salle</i>), "hall", a manor house. <i>Sieur</i> is a French title of nobility, similar to the English "Sir", but under the French Signeurial system, the title is purchased rather than earned, and does not imply military duty. It refers to Robert Cavelier's Signeurial purchase of Lachine 
from the Sulpician order at Ville Marie around 1667. The phrase <i>La Salle</i> has become iconic, and associated with the person as if it were his name, in expressions such as Robert La Salle, or simply "La Salle".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="431" data-file-width="395" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg/175px-Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg/263px-Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg/350px-Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier%2C_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg.png 2x" width="175"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_Arms_of_Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coat of Arms of René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</div></div></div>
<p>Robert Cavelier was born on November 22, 1643, into a comfortably well-off family in <a href="/wiki/Rouen" title="Rouen">Rouen</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, in the parish Saint-Herbland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When he was younger, he enjoyed science and nature. As a man, he studied with the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> religious order and became a member after taking initial vows in 1660.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> At his request on March 27, 1667, after he was in Canada, he was released from the Society of Jesus after citing "moral weaknesses."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup> Although he left the order, never took final vows in it, and later became hostile to it, historians sometimes described him incorrectly as a priest or a leader.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span></h3>
<p>La Salle never married,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> but has been linked to <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_de_Roybon_d%27Allonne" title="Madeleine de Roybon d'Allonne">Madeleine de Roybon d'Allonne</a>, an early settler of <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dupre_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dupre-6">[5]</a></sup> His older brother, Jean Cavelier, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulpician" title="Sulpician">Sulpician</a> priest. His parents were Jean Cavelier and Catherine Geest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lachine">Lachine</span></h3>
<p>Required to reject his father's legacy when he joined the Jesuits, La Salle was nearly destitute when he traveled as a prospective colonist to North America. He sailed for <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> in the spring of 1666.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> His brother Jean, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulpician_Order" title="Sulpician Order">Sulpician</a> priest, had moved there the year before. He was granted a <i><a href="/wiki/Seigneurial_system_of_New_France" title="Seigneurial system of New France">seigneurie</a></i> on land at the western end of the <a href="/wiki/Island_of_Montreal" title="Island of Montreal">Island of Montreal</a>, which became known as <i><a href="/wiki/Lachine,_Quebec" title="Lachine, Quebec">Lachine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[b]</a></sup> La Salle immediately began to issue land grants, set up a village and learn the languages of the native people, several tribes of Iroquois in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expeditions">Expeditions</span></h2>
<h3><span id=".22Ohio.22_expedition.5Bc.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Ohio"_expedition[c]'>"Ohio" expedition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[c]</a></sup></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span></h4>
<p>The Seneca told him of a great river, called the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a>, which flowed into the sea, the "Vermilion Sea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[d]</a></sup>. He began to plan for expeditions to find a western passage to China. He sought and received permission from Governor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_de_R%C3%A9my_de_Courcelle" title="Daniel de Rémy de Courcelle">Daniel Courcelle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intendant" title="Intendant">Intendant</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Talon" title="Jean Talon">Jean Talon</a> to embark on the enterprise. He sold his interests in Lachine to finance the venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Journey">Journey</span></h4>
<p>La Salle left Lachine by the St. Lawrence on July 6, 1669 with a flotilla of nine canoes and 24 men, plus their Seneca Indian guides: himself and 14 hired men in 4 canoes, the two Sulpicians Dollier de Casson and Abbé René de Bréhan de Galinée with 7 new recruits in three canoes, and two canoes of Indians.  There they went up the St. Lawrence and across Lake Superior.  After 35 days, they arrived at what we call today, Irondequoit Bay on the southern shore of Lake Superior at the mouth of Irondequoit Creek, a place now commemorated as "La Salle's Landing". 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_villages">Indian villages</span></h4>
<p>There they were greeted by a party of Indians, who escorted them starting the next day to a village some leagues distant, a journey of a few days.  At the village, the Seneca vehemently attempted to dissuade the party from proceeding into the lands of their enemies, the Algonquins, telling of the dire fate awaiting them.  The necessity of securing guides for the further part of the journey, and the obstinacy of the Seneca to provide them, delayed the expedition a month. A fortuitous capture by the Indians in the lands to the south of a Dutchman who spoke Iroquois well but French ill, and was to be burned at the stake for transgressions unknown, provided an opportunity to obtain a guide. The Dutchman's freedom was purchased by the party in exchange for wampum. 
</p><p>While at the Indian village in Sept. 1669, La Salle was seized with a violent fever<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[e]</a></sup> and expressed the intention of returning to Ville Marie.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Niagara_and_Lake_Erie">Niagara and Lake Erie</span></h4>
<p>At this juncture, he parted from his company and the narrative of the Jesuits, who continued on to upper Lake Erie.The missionaries continued on to the upper lakes, to the land of the Potawatomies.
Other accounts have it that some of La Salle's men soon returned to New Holland or Ville Marie.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_evidence">Further evidence</span></h4>
<p>Beyond that, the factual record of La Salle's first expedition ends, and what prevails is obscurity and fabrication. It is likely that he spent the winter in Ville Marie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[12]</a></sup>  The next confirmed sighting of La Salle was by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Perrot" title="Nicolas Perrot">Nicolas Perrot</a> on the Ottawa River near the <a href="/wiki/Lac_des_Chats" title="Lac des Chats">Rapide des chats</a> in early summer, 1670, hunting with a party of Iroquois. That would be 700 miles as the crow flies from the Falls of the Ohio, the point supposed by some that he reached on the Ohio River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>La Salle's own journal of the expedition was lost in 1756.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[14]</a></sup> Two indirect historical accounts exist.  The one, <i>Récit d’un ami de l’abbé de Galliné</i>, purported to be a recitation by La Salle himself to an unknown writer during his visit to Paris in 1678<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[15]</a></sup>, and the other <i>Mémoire sur le projet du sieur de la Salle pour la descouverte de la partie occidentale de l’Amérique septentrionale entre la Nouvelle-France, la Floride et le Mexique</i>. A letter from Madeleine Cavelier, his now elderly niece, written in 1746, commenting on the journal of La Salle in her possession may also shed some light on the issue. 
</p><p>La Salle himself never claimed to have discovered the Ohio River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[16]</a></sup> In a letter to the intendent Talon in 1677, he claimed discovery of a river, the Baudrane, flowing southwesterly with its mouth on Lake Erie and emptying into the Saint Louis (i.e. the Mississippi), a hydrography which was non-existent. In those days, maps as well as descriptions were based part on observation and part on hearsay, of necessity.  This confounded courses, mouths and confluences among the rivers. At various times, La Salle invented such rivers as the Chucagoa, Baudrane, Louisiane (Anglicized "Saint Louis"), and Ouabanchi-Aramoni.  These included segments of those he'd actually traversed, which were earlier the Illinois and Kankakee, St. Joseph's of Lake Michigan, probably the <i>Ouabache</i> (Wabash) and possibly the upper Allegheny and later, the Chicago and lower Mississippi.  He also correctly described the Missouri, though it was hearsay - he'd never been on it.
</p><p>Confounding fact with fiction started with publication in 1876 of <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Margry_Pierre" title="fr:Pierre Margry Pierre">Margry</a>'s <i>Découvertes et Etablissements des Français</i>. Margry was a French archivist and partisan who had private access to the French archives. He came to be the agent of the American historian Francis Parkman. Margry's work, a massive 9 volumes, encompassed an assemblage of documents some previously published, but many not.  In it, he sometimes published a reproduction of the whole document, and sometimes only an extract, or summary, not distinguishing the one from the other. He also used in some cases one or another copies of original documents previously edited, extracted or altered by others, without specifying which transcriptions were original, and which were copies, or whether the copy was dated earlier or later.  Reproductions were scattered in fragments across chapters, so that it was impossible to ascertain the integrity of the document from its fragments. Chapter headings were oblique and sensational, so as to obfuscate the content therein. English and American scholars were immediately skeptical of the work, since full and faithful publication of some of the original documents had previously existed.  The situation was so fraught with doubt, that the United States Congress appropriated $10,000 in 1873, which Margry wanted as an advance, to have the original documents photostated, witnessed by uninvolved parties as to veracity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_the_Ohio_and_Mississippi">Discovery of the Ohio and Mississippi</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River#History" title="Ohio River">Ohio River § History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River#History" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River § History</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batts_and_Fallam_expedition" title="Batts and Fallam expedition">Batts and Fallam expedition</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Needham_and_Arthur_expedition" title="Needham and Arthur expedition">Needham and Arthur expedition</a></div>
<p>If La Salle is excused from discovering the two great rivers of the midwest, history does not leave a void.  On May 8, 1541, south of present-day Memphis, Tennessee, Spanish conquistador <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> reached the Mississippi River, which the Spanish called the <i>Rio Grande</i> for its immense size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[17]</a></sup> He was the first European to document and cross the river, though not traverse it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is uncontested that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Joliet" title="Louis Joliet">Louis Joliet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Marquette" title="Jacques Marquette">Jacques Marquette</a> were the first Europeans to traverse the upper Mississippi in 1673,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[18]</a></sup> and that Father <a href="/wiki/Louis_Hennepin" title="Louis Hennepin">Louis Hennepin</a> and Antonine Augalle visited the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falls_of_St._Anthony" title="Falls of St. Anthony">Falls of St. Anthony</a> on the upper Mississippi in spring, 1680,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[19]</a></sup> in advance of La Salle's own excursion in early 1682.
</p><p>Credit for discovery of the Ohio River is provisionally given to two obscure early English explorers, Thomas Batts and Robert Fallam from Virginia who visited Wood's River (today called the New River), a tributary of the Ohio via the Kanawha, in what is today West Virginia in Sept. 1671.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[20]</a></sup> Other scholars declaim that this short (one month) expedition did not penetrate to the Ohio to the west, but elect instead Virginia Englishmen James Needham and Gabriel Arthur who in 1673-74 circumnavigated the southeast finally traversing Shawnee villages along the Ohio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[21]</a></sup> The lower Ohio River first began appearing on French maps about 1674<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[22]</a></sup> in approximately its correct hydrography, and in its relation to the Mississippi, though diagrammed more northerly, approaching Lake Erie from the west and may have been confounded with the Maumee portage route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[23]</a></sup>  A memoir by <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Ren%C3%A9_de_Brisay_de_Denonville,_Marquis_de_Denonville" title="Jacques-René de Brisay de Denonville, Marquis de Denonville">M. de Denonville</a> in 1688, recites that the lower Ohio, at least from its confluence with the Wabash to the Mississippi, was a familiar trade route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[24]</a></sup>  In 1692, Arnout Viele, a Dutchman from New York, traversed the length of the Ohio from the headwaters of the Allegheny in Pennsylvania to it's mouth on the Mississippi, though the hydrography of the Allegheny remained opaque for at least several decades thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Lakes_forts">Great Lakes forts</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="555" decoding="async" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg/220px-La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg/330px-La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg/440px-La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Salle_Fort_Frontenac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of La Salle inspecting the reconstruction of Fort Frontenac, 1675. Painting by John David Kelly.</div></div></div>
<p>On July 12, 1673, the Governor of New France, <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Buade_de_Frontenac" title="Louis de Buade de Frontenac">Louis de Buade de Frontenac</a>, arrived at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Cataraqui_River" title="Cataraqui River">Cataraqui River</a> to meet with leaders of the Five Nations of the Iroquois to encourage them to trade with the French. While the groups met and exchanged gifts, Frontenac's men, led by La Salle, hastily constructed a rough wooden palisade on a point of land by a shallow, sheltered bay. Originally the fort was named Fort Cataraqui but was later renamed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frontenac" title="Fort Frontenac">Fort Frontenac</a> by La Salle in honor of his patron. The purpose of Fort Frontenac was to control the lucrative fur trade in the Great Lakes Basin to the west. The fort was also meant to be a bulwark against the English and Dutch, who were competing with the French for control of the fur trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[26]</a></sup> La Salle was left in command of the fort in 1673.
</p><p>Thanks to his powerful protector, the discoverer managed, during a voyage to France in 1674–75, to secure for himself the grant of Fort Cataraqui and acquired letters of nobility for himself and his descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-4">[3]</a></sup> With Frontenac's support, he received not only a <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> concession, with permission to establish frontier forts, but also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_of_nobility" title="Title of nobility">title of nobility</a>. He returned and rebuilt Frontenac in stone. An <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Heritage_Trust" title="Ontario Heritage Trust">Ontario Heritage Trust</a> plaque describes La Salle at Cataraqui as "[a] major figure in the expansion of the French fur trade into the Lake Ontario region,  Using the fort as a base, he undertook expeditions to the west and southwest in the interest of developing a vast fur-trading empire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Tonti" title="Henri de Tonti">Henri de Tonti</a> joined his explorations as his lieutenant.
</p><p>In early 1679, La Salle's expedition built <a href="/wiki/Fort_Conti" title="Fort Conti">Fort Conti</a> at the mouth of the Niagara River on Lake Ontario. There they loaded supplies from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frontenac" title="Fort Frontenac">Fort Frontenac</a> into smaller boats (<a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bateaux" title="Bateaux">bateaux</a>), so they could continue up the shallow and swiftly flowing lower <a href="/wiki/Niagara_River" title="Niagara River">Niagara River</a> to what is now the location of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewiston,_New_York" title="Lewiston, New York">Lewiston, New York</a>. There the Iroquois had a well-established <a href="/wiki/Portage" title="Portage">portage</a> route which bypassed the rapids and the <a href="/wiki/Waterfall#Types" title="Waterfall">cataract</a> later known as <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a>.
</p><p>The first ship built by La Salle, called the <i>Frontenac</i>, a 10-ton single-decked brigantine or barque was lost in Lake Ontario, on January 8, 1679.  Afterward, La Salle built <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Griffon" title="Le Griffon">Le Griffon</a></i>, a seven-cannon, 45-ton <a href="/wiki/Barque" title="Barque">barque</a>, on the upper Niagara River at or near <a href="/wiki/Cayuga_Creek" title="Cayuga Creek">Cayuga Creek</a>. She was launched on August 7, 1679.
</p><p>La Salle sailed in <i>Le Griffon</i> up <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, then up Huron to <a href="/wiki/Michilimackinac" title="Michilimackinac">Michilimackinac</a> and on to present-day <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay,_Wisconsin" title="Green Bay, Wisconsin">Green Bay, Wisconsin</a>. <i>Le Griffon</i> left for Niagara with a load of furs, but was never seen again. He continued with his men in canoes down the western shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>, rounding the southern end to the mouth of the Miami River (now <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_River_(Lake_Michigan)" title="St. Joseph River (Lake Michigan)">St. Joseph River</a>), where they built a stockade in January 1680. They called it <a href="/wiki/Fort_Miami_(Michigan)" title="Fort Miami (Michigan)">Fort Miami</a> (now known as <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph,_Michigan" title="St. Joseph, Michigan">St. Joseph, Michigan</a>). There they waited for Tonti and his party, who had crossed the Lower Michigan peninsula on foot.
</p><p>Tonti arrived on November 20; on December 3, the entire party set off up the St. Joseph, which they followed until they had to take a portage at present-day <a href="/wiki/South_Bend,_Indiana" title="South Bend, Indiana">South Bend, Indiana</a>.  They crossed to the <a href="/wiki/Kankakee_River" title="Kankakee River">Kankakee River</a> and followed it to the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_River" title="Illinois River">Illinois River</a>.  There they built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Cr%C3%A8vecoeur" title="Fort Crèvecoeur">Fort Crèvecoeur</a>, which later led to the development of present-day <a href="/wiki/Peoria,_Illinois" title="Peoria, Illinois">Peoria, Illinois</a>. La Salle set off on foot for Fort Frontenac for supplies. While he was gone, the soldiers at Ft. Crevecoeur, led by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Chartier" title="Martin Chartier">Martin Chartier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mutiny" title="Mutiny">mutinied</a>, destroyed the fort, and exiled Tonti, whom he had left in charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FC_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FC-33">[28]</a></sup> He<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2019)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> later captured most of the mutineers on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, before rendezvousing with Tonti at <a href="/wiki/St._Ignace,_Michigan" title="St. Ignace, Michigan">St. Ignace, Michigan</a>.
</p>

<p>La Salle reassembled a party for another major expedition.  In 1682 he departed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Crevecoeur" title="Fort Crevecoeur">Fort Crevecoeur</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (January 2019)">disputed</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle" title="Talk:René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> with a group of Frenchmen and Indians and canoed down the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>.  He named the Mississippi basin <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)"><i>La Louisiane</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-34">[29]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2019)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> and claimed it for France.  At what later became the site of <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>, he built the small <a href="/wiki/Fort_Prudhomme" title="Fort Prudhomme">Fort Prudhomme</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The reason for this is unclear. (January 2019)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> On April 9, 1682, at the mouth of the Mississippi River near modern <a href="/wiki/Venice,_Louisiana" title="Venice, Louisiana">Venice, Louisiana</a>, he buried an engraved plate and a cross, claiming the territory for France.
</p><p>In 1683, on his return voyage, La Salle established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Saint_Louis_(Illinois)" title="Fort Saint Louis (Illinois)">Fort Saint-Louis</a> of Illinois, at <a href="/wiki/Starved_Rock_State_Park" title="Starved Rock State Park">Starved Rock</a> on the Illinois River, to replace Fort Crevecoeur. He appointed Tonti to command the fort while he traveled to France for supplies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas_expedition">Texas expedition</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1026" data-file-width="1400" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG/220px-LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG/330px-LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG/440px-LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaSallesExpeditiontoLouisiana.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodore_Gudin" title="Theodore Gudin">Theodore Gudin</a> titled <i>La Salle's Expedition to Louisiana in 1684</i>. The ship on the left is <i><a href="/wiki/La_Belle_(ship)" title="La Belle (ship)">La Belle</a></i>, in the middle is <i>Le Joly</i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Aimable&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L'Aimable (page does not exist)">L'Aimable</a></i> is to the right. They are at the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a></div></div></div>
<p>On July 24, 1684,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-34">[29]</a></sup> He departed France and returned to America with a large expedition designed to establish a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_colony" title="French colony">French colony</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, at the mouth of the Mississippi River. They had four ships and 300 colonists. The expedition was plagued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">pirates</a>, hostile Indians, and poor navigation. One ship was lost to pirates in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, a second sank in the inlets of <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a>. They founded a <a href="/wiki/French_colonization_of_Texas" title="French colonization of Texas">settlement</a>, near the bay which they called the Bay of Saint Louis, on <a href="/wiki/Garcitas_Creek" title="Garcitas Creek">Garcitas Creek</a> in the vicinity of present-day <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Texas" title="Victoria, Texas">Victoria, Texas</a>. La Salle led a group eastward on foot on three occasions to try to locate the mouth of the Mississippi.  In the meantime, the flagship La Belle, the only remaining ship, ran aground and sank into the mud, stranding the colony on the Texas coast.
</p><p>During a final search for the Mississippi River, some of La Salle's remaining 36 men mutinied, near the site of present <a href="/wiki/Navasota,_Texas" title="Navasota, Texas">Navasota, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[f]</a></sup>
On March 19, 1687, he was slain by Pierre Duhaut during an ambush while talking to Duhaut's decoy, <a href="/wiki/Jean_L%27Archev%C3%AAque" title="Jean L'Archevêque">Jean L'Archevêque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[31]</a></sup> They were "six leagues" from the westernmost village of the <a href="/wiki/Hasinai" title="Hasinai">Hasinai</a> (Tejas) Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTsalle_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-HTsalle-34">[29]</a></sup> Duhaut was killed to avenge La Salle.  The remaining men in the party, afraid of retribution, killed each other, except for two.
</p><p>The colony lasted only until 1688, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karankawa" title="Karankawa">Karankawa</a>-speaking Native Americans killed the 20 remaining adults and took five children as captives. Tonti sent out search missions in 1689 when he learned of the settlers' fate, but failed to find survivors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[32]</a></sup>  The children of the colony were later recovered by the Spanish.
</p>

<p>In addition to the forts, which also served as authorized agencies for the extensive fur trade, La Salle's visits to Illinois and other Indians cemented the French policy of alliance with Indians in the common causes of containing both Iroquois influence and Anglo-American settlement.  He also gave the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_territory" title="Louisiana territory">Louisiana</a> to the interior North American territory he claimed for France, which lives on in the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">name of a US state</a>. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span></h2>
<p>In 1995, La Salle's primary ship <i><a href="/wiki/La_Belle_(ship)" title="La Belle (ship)">La Belle</a></i> was discovered in the muck of <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a>.  It has been the subject of archeological research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[34]</a></sup> Through an international treaty, the artifacts excavated from La Belle are owned by France and held in trust by the Texas Historical Commission. The collection is held by the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a> Museum of Science and History.  Artifacts from <i>La Belle</i> are shown at nine museums across Texas. The wreckage of his ship <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L%27Aimable&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L'Aimable (page does not exist)">L'Aimable</a></i> has yet to be located.
</p><p>The possible remains of <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Griffon" title="Le Griffon">Le Griffon</a></i> were found in 1898 by lighthouse keeper Albert Cullis, on a beach on the western edge of <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin Island</a> in northern Lake Huron. Results of testing some of the artifacts were disputed. Many of the recovered artifacts were lost and the wreck was washed away in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[36]</a></sup> A possible shipwreck of <i>Le Griffon</i> near <a href="/wiki/Poverty_Island" title="Poverty Island">Poverty Island</a> at the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay_(Lake_Michigan)" title="Green Bay (Lake Michigan)">Green Bay</a> in northern Lake Michigan was located by Steve Libert of the Great Lakes Exploration Group in 2001.  The organization prevailed in a lawsuit against the state of Michigan over ownership of artifacts in 2012, and in 2013 was issued a permit to excavate the wreck.  Only one artifact, a wood pole, was recovered, and it is indeterminate whether it was from a shipwreck.
</p>
