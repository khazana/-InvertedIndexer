<p><b>Edwin Powell Hubble</b> (November 20, 1889 – September 28, 1953)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASABio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASABio-1">[1]</a></sup> was an American astronomer. He played a crucial role in establishing the fields of <a href="/wiki/Extragalactic_astronomy" title="Extragalactic astronomy">extragalactic astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Observational_cosmology" title="Observational cosmology">observational cosmology</a> and is regarded as one of the most important astronomers of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-space.com-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-futurism_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-futurism-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble discovered that many objects previously thought to be clouds of dust and gas and classified as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebulae" title="Nebulae">nebulae</a>" were actually <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> beyond the Milky Way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He used the strong direct relationship between a classical <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variable's</a> <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">pulsation period</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udalski99_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-udalski99-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sos08_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sos08-6">[6]</a></sup> (discovered in 1908 by <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt" title="Henrietta Swan Leavitt">Henrietta Swan Leavitt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) for scaling <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">galactic and extragalactic distances</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2001_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2001-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2010-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble provided evidence that the <a href="/wiki/Recessional_velocity" title="Recessional velocity">recessional velocity</a> of a galaxy increases with its distance from the earth, a property now known as "<a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>", despite the fact that it had been both proposed and demonstrated observationally two years earlier by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lemaître">Georges Lemaître</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Hubble-Lemaître's Law implies that the universe is expanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubbles_Law_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubbles_Law-11">[11]</a></sup> A decade before, the American astronomer <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a> had provided the first evidence that the light from many of these nebulae was strongly red-shifted, indicative of high recession velocities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble's name is most widely recognized for the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> which was named in his honor, with a model prominently displayed in his hometown of <a href="/wiki/Marshfield,_Missouri#Notable_people" title="Marshfield, Missouri">Marshfield, Missouri</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edwin Hubble was born to Virginia Lee Hubble (née James) (1864–1934)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and John Powell Hubble, an insurance executive, in <a href="/wiki/Marshfield,_Missouri" title="Marshfield, Missouri">Marshfield, Missouri</a>, and moved to <a href="/wiki/Wheaton,_Illinois" title="Wheaton, Illinois">Wheaton, Illinois</a>, in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In his younger days, he was noted more for his athletic prowess than his intellectual abilities, although he did earn good grades in every subject except for spelling. Edwin was a gifted athlete, playing baseball, football, basketball, and running track in both high school and college. He played a variety of positions on the basketball court from center to shooting guard. In fact, Hubble even led the University of Chicago's basketball team to their first conference title in <a href="/wiki/1906%E2%80%9307_Chicago_Maroons_men%27s_basketball_team" title="1906–07 Chicago Maroons men's basketball team">1907</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_E._Christianson_1996_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gale_E._Christianson_1996-16">[16]</a></sup> He won seven first places and a third place in a single high school track and field meet in 1906.
</p><p>His studies at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> were concentrated on law, which led to a bachelor of science degree in 1910. Hubble also became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kappa_Sigma_Fraternity" title="Kappa Sigma Fraternity">Kappa Sigma Fraternity</a>. He spent the three years at <a href="/wiki/The_Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" title="The Queen's College, Oxford">The Queen's College, Oxford</a> after earning his bachelor's as one of the university's first <a href="/wiki/Rhodes_Scholarship" title="Rhodes Scholarship">Rhodes Scholars</a>, initially studying <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a> instead of science (as a promise to his dying father),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> and later added literature and Spanish,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Times-17">[17]</a></sup> and earning his master's degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1909, Hubble's father moved his family from <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shelbyville,_Kentucky" title="Shelbyville, Kentucky">Shelbyville, Kentucky</a>, so that the family could live in a small town, ultimately settling in nearby <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a>. His father died in the winter of 1913, while Edwin was still in England, and in the summer of 1913, Edwin returned to care for his mother, two sisters, and younger brother, as did his brother William. The family moved once more to Everett Avenue, in Louisville's Highlands neighborhood, to accommodate Edwin and William.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble was also a dutiful son, who despite his intense interest in astronomy since boyhood, acquiesced to his father's request to study law, first at the University of Chicago and later at Oxford, though he managed to take a few math and science courses. After the death of his father in 1913, Edwin returned to the Midwest from Oxford but did not have the motivation to practice law. Instead, he proceeded to teach Spanish, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_Albany_High_School_(Indiana)" title="New Albany High School (Indiana)">New Albany High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Albany,_Indiana" title="New Albany, Indiana">New Albany, Indiana</a>, where he also coached the boys' basketball team. After a year of high-school teaching, he entered graduate school with the help of his former professor from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> to study astronomy at the university's <a href="/wiki/Yerkes_Observatory" title="Yerkes Observatory">Yerkes Observatory</a>, where he received his Ph.D. in 1917. His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation">dissertation</a> was titled "Photographic Investigations of Faint Nebulae".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  In Yerkes, he had access to one of the most powerful telescopes in the world at the time, which had an innovative 24 inch (61 cm) reflector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_identity_card.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="350" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hubble_identity_card.jpg/220px-Hubble_identity_card.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hubble_identity_card.jpg/330px-Hubble_identity_card.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/05/Hubble_identity_card.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_identity_card.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hubble's identity card in the <a href="/wiki/American_Expeditionary_Forces" title="American Expeditionary Forces">American Expeditionary Forces</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>After the United States declared war on <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in 1917, Hubble rushed to complete his Ph.D. dissertation so he could join the military. Hubble volunteered for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and was assigned to the newly created <a href="/wiki/86th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="86th Infantry Division (United States)">86th Division</a>, where he served in 2nd Battalion, 343 Infantry Regiment. He rose to the rank of lieutenant colonel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and was found fit for overseas duty on July 9, 1918, but the 86th Division never saw combat. After the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Hubble spent a year in Cambridge, where he renewed his studies of astronomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1919, Hubble was offered a staff position at the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Institution_for_Science" title="Carnegie Institution for Science">Carnegie Institution for Science</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">Mount Wilson Observatory</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena, California</a>, by <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">George Ellery Hale</a>, the founder and director of the observatory. Hubble remained on staff at Mount Wilson until his death in 1953. Shortly before his death, Hubble became the first astronomer to use the newly completed giant 200-inch (5.1 m) reflector <a href="/wiki/Hale_Telescope" title="Hale Telescope">Hale Telescope</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">Palomar Observatory</a> near San Diego, California.
</p><p>Hubble also worked as a civilian for <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> at <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Proving_Ground" title="Aberdeen Proving Ground">Aberdeen Proving Ground</a> in Maryland during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as the Chief of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=External_Ballistics_Branch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="External Ballistics Branch (page does not exist)">External Ballistics Branch</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistics_Research_Laboratory" title="Ballistics Research Laboratory">Ballistics Research Laboratory</a> during which he directed a large volume of research in exterior <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a> which increased the effective firepower of bombs and projectiles. His work was facilitated by his personal development of several items of equipment for the instrumentation used in exterior ballistics, the most outstanding development being the high-speed clock camera, which made possible the study of the characteristics of bombs and low-velocity projectiles in flight. The results of his studies were credited with greatly improving design, performance, and military effectiveness of bombs and rockets. For his work there, he received the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Merit" title="Legion of Merit">Legion of Merit</a> award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble was raised as a Christian but some of his later statements suggest uncertainty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble married Mrs. Grace Lillian (Burke) Leib (1889–1980), daughter of John Patrick and Luella (Kepford) Burke, on February 26, 1924.
</p><p>Hubble had a heart attack in July 1949 while on vacation in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. He was taken care of by his wife and continued on a modified diet and work schedule. He died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerebral_thrombosis" title="Cerebral thrombosis">cerebral thrombosis</a> (a spontaneous blood clot in his brain) on September 28, 1953, in <a href="/wiki/San_Marino,_California" title="San Marino, California">San Marino, California</a>. No funeral was held for him, and his wife never revealed his burial site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PK_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PK-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discoveries">Discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe_goes_beyond_the_Milky_Way_galaxy">Universe goes beyond the Milky Way galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Universe goes beyond the Milky Way galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:100inchHooker.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="904" data-file-width="554" decoding="async" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/200px-100inchHooker.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/300px-100inchHooker.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/100inchHooker.jpg/400px-100inchHooker.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:100inchHooker.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 100-inch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hooker_telescope" title="Hooker telescope">Hooker telescope</a> at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">Mount Wilson Observatory</a> that Hubble used to measure galaxy distances and a value for the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expanding_universe" title="Expanding universe">expansion of the universe</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Edwin Hubble's arrival at Mount Wilson Observatory, California in 1919 coincided roughly with the completion of the 100-inch (2.5 m) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hooker_Telescope" title="Hooker Telescope">Hooker Telescope</a>, then the world's largest. At that time, the prevailing view of the cosmos was that the universe consisted entirely of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a>. Using the Hooker Telescope at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">Mt. Wilson</a>, Hubble identified <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variables</a> (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> that is used as a means to determine the distance from the galaxy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> – see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_candle" title="Standard candle">standard candle</a>) in several <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy#Spiral_nebula" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral nebulae</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Nebula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">Triangulum</a>. His observations, made in 1924, proved conclusively that these nebulae were much too distant to be part of the Milky Way and were, in fact, entire galaxies outside our own, suspected by researchers at least as early as 1755 when <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>'s General History of Nature and Theory of the Heavens appeared. This idea had been opposed by many in the astronomy establishment of the time, in particular by <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a>. Despite the opposition, Hubble, then a thirty-five-year-old scientist, had his findings first published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> on <span class="nowrap">November 23</span>, 1924,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SharovNovikov1993_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SharovNovikov1993-32">[32]</a></sup> then presented them to other astronomers at the January 1, 1925 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MB2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-MB2-33">[33]</a></sup> Hubble's results for Andromeda were not formally published in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> until 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HubbleTuningFork.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="850" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/HubbleTuningFork.jpg/190px-HubbleTuningFork.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/HubbleTuningFork.jpg/285px-HubbleTuningFork.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/HubbleTuningFork.jpg/380px-HubbleTuningFork.jpg 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HubbleTuningFork.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hubble's <a href="/wiki/Hubble_sequence" title="Hubble sequence">classification scheme</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hubble's findings fundamentally changed the scientific view of the universe. Supporters state that Hubble's discovery of nebulae outside of our galaxy helped pave the way for future astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Although some of his more renowned colleagues simply scoffed at his results, Hubble ended up publishing his findings on nebulae. This published work earned him an award titled the American Association Prize and five hundred dollars from Burton E. Livingston of the Committee on Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_E._Christianson_1996_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gale_E._Christianson_1996-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble also devised the most commonly used <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification" title="Galaxy morphological classification">system for classifying galaxies</a>, grouping them according to their appearance in photographic images. He arranged the different groups of galaxies in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_sequence" title="Hubble sequence">Hubble sequence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redshift_increases_with_distance">Redshift increases with distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Redshift increases with distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hubble went on to estimate the distances to 24 extra-galactic nebulae, using a variety of methods. In 1929, in his first published paper, Hubble examined the relation between these distances and their <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocities</a> as determined from their <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifts</a>. His estimated distances are now known to all be too small, by up to a factor of about 7. This was due to factors such as the fact that there are two kinds of Cepheid variables or confusing bright gas clouds with bright stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirshner_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirshner-37">[37]</a></sup> However, his distances were more or less proportional to the true distances, and combining his distances with measurements of the redshifts of the galaxies by <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a>, and by his assistant <a href="/wiki/Milton_L._Humason" title="Milton L. Humason">Milton L. Humason</a>, he found a roughly linear relation between  the distances of the galaxies and their radial velocities (corrected for solar motion),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubbles_Law_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hubbles_Law-11">[11]</a></sup> a discovery that later became known as <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>.
</p><p>This meant, the greater the distance between any two galaxies, the greater their relative speed of separation. If interpreted that way, Hubble's measurements on 46 galaxies lead to a value for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_Constant" title="Hubble Constant">Hubble Constant</a> of 500 km/s/Mpc, which is much higher than the currently accepted value of 70 km/s/Mpc due to errors in their distance calibrations.
</p><p>Yet the reason for the redshift remained unclear. <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lemaître">Georges Lemaître</a>, a Belgian Catholic priest and physicist, predicted on theoretical grounds based on Einstein's equations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Relativity" title="General Relativity">general relativity</a> the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>-distance relation, and published observational support for it, two years before the discovery of Hubble's law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature.com-38">[38]</a></sup> However, many cosmologists and astronomers (including Hubble himself) failed to recognize the work of Lemaître; Hubble remained doubtful about Lemaître's interpretation for his entire life.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="the paragraph up to this point is not cited (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although he used the term "velocities" in his paper (and "apparent radial velocities" in the introduction), he later expressed doubt about interpreting these as real velocities. In 1931 he wrote a letter to the Dutch cosmologist <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Sitter" title="Willem de Sitter">Willem de Sitter</a> expressing his opinion on the theoretical interpretation of the redshift-distance relation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirshner_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kirshner-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Mr. Humason and I are both deeply sensible of your gracious appreciation of the papers on velocities and distances of nebulae. We use the term 'apparent' velocities to emphasize the empirical features of the correlation. The interpretation, we feel, should be left to you and the very few others who are competent to discuss the matter with authority."
</p></blockquote>
<p>Today, the "apparent velocities" in question are usually thought of as an increase in <a href="/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances" title="Comoving and proper distances">proper distance</a> that occurs due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expansion of the universe</a>. Light traveling through an expanding metric will experience a Hubble-type redshift, a mechanism somewhat different from the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler effect</a> (although the two mechanisms become equivalent descriptions related by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate_transformation" title="Coordinate transformation">coordinate transformation</a> for nearby galaxies).
</p><p>In the 1930s, Hubble was involved in determining the distribution of galaxies and <a href="/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe" title="Shape of the universe">spatial curvature</a>. These data seemed to indicate that the universe was <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">flat</a> and homogeneous, but there was a deviation from flatness at large redshifts. According to <a href="/wiki/Allan_Sandage" title="Allan Sandage">Allan Sandage</a>,
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Hubble believed that his count data gave a more reasonable result concerning spatial curvature if the redshift correction was made assuming no recession. To the very end of his writings he maintained this position, favouring (or at the very least keeping open) the model where no true expansion exists, and therefore that the redshift "represents a hitherto unrecognized principle of nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>There were methodological problems with Hubble's survey technique that showed a deviation from flatness at large redshifts. In particular, the technique did not account for changes in luminosity of galaxies due to <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">galaxy evolution</a>.
Earlier, in 1917, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> had found that his newly developed theory of general relativity indicated that the universe must be either expanding or contracting. Unable to believe what his own equations were telling him, Einstein introduced a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> (a "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fudge_factor" title="wiktionary:fudge factor">fudge factor</a>") to the equations to avoid this "problem". When Einstein learned of Hubble's redshifts, he immediately realized that the expansion predicted by general relativity must be real, and in later life he said that changing his equations was "the biggest blunder of [his] life." In fact, Einstein apparently once visited Hubble and tried to convince him that the universe was expanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hubble also discovered the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> <a href="/wiki/1373_Cincinnati" title="1373 Cincinnati">1373 Cincinnati</a> on August 30, 1935. In 1936 he wrote <i>The Observational Approach to Cosmology</i> and <i>The Realm of the Nebulae</i> which explained his approaches to extra-galactic astronomy and his view of the subject's history.
</p><p>In December 1941, Hubble reported to the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> that results from a six-year survey with the Mt. Wilson telescope did not support the expanding universe theory. According to an LA Times article reporting on Hubble's remarks, "The nebulae could not be uniformly distributed, as the telescope shows they are, and still fit the explosion idea. Explanations which try to get around what the great telescope sees, he said, fail to stand up. The explosion for example would have had to start long after the earth was created, and possibly even after the first life appeared here."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harnisch_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harnisch-42">[42]</a></sup> (Hubble's estimate of what we now call the Hubble constant would put the Big Bang only 2000 million years ago.)
</p>
<h2><span id="Accusations_concerning_Lema.C3.AEtre.27s_priority"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_concerning_Lemaître's_priority">Accusations concerning Lemaître's priority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accusations concerning Lemaître's priority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011 the journal <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a> reported claims that Hubble had played a role in the <a href="/wiki/Redaction" title="Redaction">redaction</a> of key parts of the translation of Lemaître's 1927 paper, which stated what is now called Hubble's Law and also gave observational evidence for it.  Historians quoted in the article were sceptical that the redactions were part of a campaign to ensure Hubble retained priority. However, the observational astronomer <a href="/wiki/Sidney_van_den_Bergh" title="Sidney van den Bergh">Sidney van den Bergh</a> published a paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bergh_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bergh-43">[43]</a></sup> suggesting that while the omissions may have been made by a translator, they may still have been deliberate.
</p><p>In November 2011, the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Mario_Livio" title="Mario Livio">Mario Livio</a> reported in <i>Nature</i> that documents in the Lemaître archive demonstrated that the redaction had indeed been carried out by Lemaître himself, who apparently saw little point in including scientific content which had already been reported by Hubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature.com_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature.com-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="No_Nobel_Prize">No Nobel Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: No Nobel Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time, the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> did not recognize work done in astronomy. Hubble spent much of the later part of his career attempting to have astronomy considered an area of physics, instead of being its own science. He did this largely so that astronomers—including himself—could be recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_Committee" title="Nobel Prize Committee">Nobel Prize Committee</a> for their valuable contributions to <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>. This campaign was unsuccessful in Hubble's lifetime, but shortly after his death, the Nobel Prize Committee decided that astronomical work would be eligible for the physics prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_E._Christianson_1996_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gale_E._Christianson_1996-16">[16]</a></sup> However, the prize is not one that can be awarded posthumously.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stamp">Stamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 6, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> released a 41-cent stamp honoring Hubble on a sheet titled "American Scientists" designed by artist <a href="/wiki/Victor_Stabin" title="Victor Stabin">Victor Stabin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PK_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-PK-28">[28]</a></sup> His citation reads:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Often called a "pioneer of the distant stars," astronomer Edwin Hubble (1889–1953) played a pivotal role in deciphering the vast and complex nature of the universe. His meticulous studies of spiral nebulae proved the existence of galaxies other than our own Milky Way. Had he not died suddenly in 1953, Hubble would have won that year's Nobel Prize in Physics.
</p></blockquote>
<p><i>(Note that the assertion that he would have won the Nobel Prize in 1953 is likely false, although he was nominated for the prize that year.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>)
</p><p>The other scientists on the "American Scientists" sheet include <a href="/wiki/Gerty_Cori" title="Gerty Cori">Gerty Cori</a>, biochemist; <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a>, chemist, and <a href="/wiki/John_Bardeen" title="John Bardeen">John Bardeen</a>, physicist.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwin_Hubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The play <i>Creation's Birthday</i>, written by Cornell physicist Hasan Padamsee, tells Hubble's life story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>

</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Thanks to Edwin P. Hubble
</p><p>Our static cosmology was in serious trouble--
</p><p>When we saw a wavelength
</p><p>Of such tiny strength,
</p><p>It proved the universe was an expanding bubble.
</p>
